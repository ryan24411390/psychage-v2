# Signs and Experiences

*Part 2 of 5 · F-PORTRAIT · 1417 words (declared) · 12 citations*

**Thesis:** The symptoms that define schizophrenia in the public mind — voices and delusions — are the ones that matter least for how a life actually goes; the negative and cognitive symptoms nobody pictures are what predict whether someone works, studies, and connects.

**The risk:** Positive symptoms own the public image but are the weakest predictor of functional outcome; the negative and cognitive symptoms nobody pictures carry the weight.

> I was simply sectioned again, detached from my real self, observing what was being done to me in a third-person perspective. ... My thoughts, my emotions, and my actions, none of them belong to me any more.
>
> — Kean C, 2009 (Kean C. Silencing the self: schizophrenia as a self-disturbance. Schizophrenia Bulletin. 2009. [1])

### The symptoms you already know

Ask most people to describe schizophrenia and they name two things: hearing voices, and holding beliefs that are not true. These are the positive symptoms — 'positive' meaning added to experience, not good. From the outside they look like the whole condition. From the inside they are stranger, and more specific, than the cliche. A hallucination is a genuine perception, not an overactive imagination. The voice is heard the way a doorbell is heard; the brain delivers it with the full authority of the senses, which is exactly why it cannot be waved away. And it is not always a voice. In the largest phenomenological survey of voice-hearing, fewer than half of participants described experiences that were literally auditory — 46% reported thought-like or mixed voices instead [2]. A delusion, in turn, is not defined by being false. Plenty of false beliefs are ordinary. What marks a delusion is that it holds firm against evidence that should dismantle it — a failure, in two-factor accounts of delusional belief, to reject an idea in the face of disconfirmation [3].

### The symptoms mistaken for character

If positive symptoms are additions, negative symptoms are subtractions — and they are the ones that get read as personality. A consensus of researchers groups them into distinct domains: blunted affect (a face and voice that stop showing feeling), alogia (speech that thins to almost nothing), anhedonia (pleasure gone flat), asociality (withdrawal from other people), and avolition (the collapse of drive and initiative) [4]. Named this way, they sound clinical. Lived from the outside, they look like flaws. The person who stops calling reads as cold. The person who cannot start a task reads as lazy. The person whose face has gone still reads as rude, or simply absent. None of these readings is what is actually happening — and every one of them is what the world sees.

### The symptoms nobody pictures

There is a third domain that almost never makes the picture at all: cognition. This is not about intelligence, and not about insight into any single belief. It is the machinery underneath — working memory (holding information in mind long enough to use it), processing speed (how fast the brain takes in and acts on information), and executive function (planning, switching tasks, keeping a goal in view). Across roughly a hundred studies, people with schizophrenia perform about a full standard deviation below healthy peers on cognitive testing overall — a grand mean effect size of g = -1.03 [5]. The single largest gap is in processing speed: on digit-symbol coding, a plain timed matching task, the deficit reaches g = -1.57, larger than the gaps in memory or executive function [6]. A standard deviation is not a rounding error. It is the distance between keeping up and falling behind, and it is completely invisible in a waiting room.

**[What it looks like]** She said yes to dinner on Friday, then canceled an hour before — again. Last month she went quiet in the group chat and never came back. She seems checked out. Maybe rude. Maybe just not that invested in keeping you in her life. After a while you stop reaching out, because it starts to feel like you are the only one still trying.

**[What it is]** Getting to Friday dinner is not one decision; it is dozens of small ones — deciding to shower, tracking the time, holding the plan against the pull to stay still. Avolition flattens the drive to begin. Cognitive load makes the sequencing exhausting. Blunted affect means her face never shows the wanting that is still there underneath [4]. The withdrawal is not a verdict on you. It is the symptom, wearing the mask of a choice.

> **[Highlight]** Here is the part the public image gets exactly backwards. The voices and delusions that define schizophrenia in the popular mind are the weakest predictors of how a life actually goes. In a landmark review of the evidence, positive symptoms were not significantly associated with functional outcome in a single one of the studies examined [7]. And when researchers analyzed CATIE — one of the largest schizophrenia trials ever run, with 1,447 participants — day-to-day functioning tracked negative symptoms more closely than any other domain, more than the positive symptoms that dominate both the stigma and the prescription pad [8]. Negative and cognitive symptoms carry the weight. The symptoms nobody pictures are the ones that decide whether a person works, studies, and stays connected.

### Before it has a name

Almost none of this arrives on a single day. Before a first episode there is usually a prodrome — a slow slide of months or years in which sleep frays, grades slip, friends drift off, and thinking gets subtly harder to trust. It is a genuine at-risk state: among people identified as being at clinical high risk, about 25% go on to develop psychosis within three years [9]. That number cuts both ways. It means the early warning signs are real. It also means that roughly three out of four people flagged as high-risk do not transition on that timeline — which is why the prodrome is almost always recognized backwards, obvious in hindsight and ambiguous while it is happening.

### No two the same

One more thing the single word 'schizophrenia' hides: the people it names may have almost nothing in common. The diagnosis is polythetic — built from a menu. You meet it by having some number of symptoms from a list, with no single feature required of everyone. Work through the combinations and the result is startling: two people with the same diagnosis can share no symptoms at all, and any two possible symptom pictures overlap, on average, by less than half [10]. There is no single face of this condition. There is a family of very different presentations, wearing one name.

**Myth:** Hallucinations in schizophrenia are always voices.

**Fact:** Voices are the most common form — auditory hallucinations occur in roughly 60-80% of people with a schizophrenia-spectrum diagnosis — though that figure counts everyone classified as hearing voices, even the many who, as noted earlier, experience them as thought-like rather than literally heard. And they are rarely the whole story. More than half of those who hallucinate do so in more than one sense at once (about 53% report hallucinations in multiple modalities); sight, touch, smell, and taste can all be genuinely involved [11].  
*(Lim et al., 2016)*

**Myth:** People with schizophrenia have no insight into their own illness.

**Fact:** Insight is not simply absent — it is variable, and it fluctuates. In one outpatient sample, about half (49.5%) had at least moderately impaired insight and a quarter (25%) had severe deficits, which means many retained partial or intact awareness — and that awareness itself shifts as symptoms rise and fall [12].  
*(Dickerson et al., 1997)*

**FAQ / glossary:**

- **Positive, negative, cognitive — what do those words mean?** — Positive symptoms are experiences added on: hallucinations and delusions. Negative symptoms are capacities taken away: drive, emotional expression, pleasure, speech, and sociability. Cognitive symptoms are changes in the mental machinery — memory, processing speed, and planning. All three belong to the condition; only the first tends to reach the screen.

- **If the voices aren't the worst part, why is so much attention on them?** — Because they are the most visible, and the most alarming to people watching from outside, they dominate the public story and much of the clinical conversation. But visibility is not the same as importance. The negative and cognitive symptoms that shape daily life draw far less attention, even though they predict how a life goes more strongly [7].

- **Does everyone with schizophrenia hear voices?** — No. Auditory hallucinations are common — roughly 60-80% — but not universal, and many people who hallucinate experience more than one sense at once [11]. Some people carry the diagnosis with delusions or disorganized thinking and few voices, or none.

- **Can you see it coming?** — Sometimes — but usually only in hindsight. The prodrome, the gradual change before a first episode, is real, and clinicians can identify an at-risk state. Even so, only about a quarter of those flagged go on to psychosis within three years, so the signs are meaningful without being a verdict [9].
