# Why It Happens

**Thesis:** The 'split mind' folk-model is the exact inverse of the mechanism, this is a failure of integration, not a multiplication of selves.

**The risk:** Taught as the mechanism, the dopamine hypothesis is alone insufficient, D2 blockade barely moves the negative and cognitive symptoms, glutamate/NMDA hypofunction is co-equal rather than a footnote, and there is no biomarker, no single gene, and no one chain, only a set of converging partial accounts.

---

Here is the question that should bother you first: why does this illness wait? It almost never arrives in childhood. It gathers, instead, in late adolescence and early adulthood, the very years a person is meant to be leaving home, choosing work, falling in love [1][2]. A brain that has worked well for two decades begins, in a narrow window, to misfire. Any honest account of why schizophrenia happens has to explain that timing, because the timing is not incidental; it is the fingerprint of the mechanism. Hold on to the puzzle, because the answer runs against the word itself. 'Schizophrenia' means 'split mind,' and the culture heard 'split' as multiplication: a second self, a hidden twin, a personality that comes and goes. The biology says almost the opposite. This is not a mind divided into many. It is a mind whose ordinary machinery for binding perception, thought, and meaning into a single stream begins to come apart. Not more selves. Less integration. Everything that follows here is, in one way or another, a description of that fraying, and of the several forces that converge to cause it.

### The injury is early; the illness is late

The leading explanation for that strange timing is the neurodevelopmental model, and it is nearly forty years old. Its core claim is counterintuitive: the injury comes early, but the illness comes late [2]. Something in how the brain is assembled, genes shaping its wiring before birth and across childhood, lays down a vulnerability that then stays silent for years. The circuits most affected are precisely the ones that finish maturing last, in the late teens and twenties. So the fault is present the whole time, like a hairline crack in a bridge, but it reveals itself only when weight is finally placed on the parts that mature latest. Modern genetics has, if anything, hardened this picture rather than softened it. When researchers read gene activity in post-mortem brain tissue, the risk genes for schizophrenia are most active during early development, as though the illness were written in a language the brain speaks mainly while it is still under construction [3]. That reframes a diagnosis made in the twenties as the late echo of something set in motion decades earlier, which is part of why prevention research keeps drifting toward windows most people never think of as psychiatric at all.

None of that yet tells us what, concretely, goes wrong. Four partial mechanisms carry the most evidence, and the honest way to hold them is side by side rather than in a single line. Each is real. None is sufficient on its own. Read them as four instruments in one arrangement, not four soloists competing for the melody.

**1. Adolescent synaptic pruning and complement C4** - The brain spends adolescence deleting connections it no longer needs, a housecleaning called synaptic pruning that sharpens circuits by removing the weakest links. In the prefrontal cortex, the seat of planning and self-monitoring, this remodeling continues into the third decade of life [1]. One of the molecules that tags a synapse for removal is an immune protein, complement component 4; people who carry higher-expressing versions of the C4 gene are at greater risk, and in mice the same molecule physically drives synapse elimination [4]. This matters more than it may sound, because the region of the genome most strongly associated with schizophrenia at the population level owes part of that signal to C4 [4]. The influential hypothesis is that this pruning, in some people, cuts too deep, thinning the network past the point of coherence during the exact years symptoms emerge. Not too few connections from the start; too many removed, too late.

**2. The dopamine signal misfires** - As the network is remodeled, its chemistry drifts. The oldest theory is the dopamine hypothesis, real but partial. Pooling dozens of imaging studies shows the largest abnormality is not where the medications act. It is presynaptic, too much dopamine made and released in the striatum, a large effect (Cohen's d = 0.79), while the D2 receptor that antipsychotics block is only slightly altered (d = 0.26) and not altered at all in people who have never been medicated [5]. The most useful way to read this is a single idea: aberrant salience. Diverse risk factors appear to converge on one final common pathway, an over-active dopamine signal that stamps ordinary events with false urgency [13]. A passing car, a stranger's glance, a song on the radio, each arrives loaded with private meaning it should not carry. Seen this way, a delusion is not a failure of logic but the mind's reasonable attempt to explain a signal that will not stop feeling important.

**3. Glutamate and NMDA hypofunction** - Dopamine cannot be the whole story, because blocking it barely touches the negative and cognitive symptoms, the flattening, the withdrawal, the fog, that most decide how a life actually goes. Here a co-equal account matters: glutamate, the brain's main excitatory messenger, and specifically the under-functioning of one of its receptors, the NMDA receptor [6]. The evidence is genuinely startling. Give a healthy volunteer a low, sub-anesthetic dose of ketamine, which blocks that receptor, and you reproduce not only hallucination-like experiences but the negative and cognitive symptoms too, along with perceptual and memory changes [7]. No dopamine-blocking drug does that. One influential reading places glutamate upstream, shaping the very dopamine signaling the older theory describes, which would make these two mechanisms not rivals but links in one chain [6]. This is not an academic distinction. If a large share of the mechanism sits outside the dopamine system, it helps explain why medications built almost entirely around dopamine leave so much of the illness untouched, and why the search for treatments that act on glutamate has been pursued so stubbornly and for so long. Either way, glutamate is not a footnote to dopamine. It is a second door into the same room.

**4. Environment leaves fingerprints** - None of this happens in a vacuum. Adversity in childhood, abuse, neglect, bullying, the loss of a parent, roughly triples the odds of later psychosis (OR 2.78), and by one estimate a third of cases would not occur without it [8]. Cannabis shows a dose-response signature that is hard to explain away: the heaviest users carry nearly four times the odds, and more use tracks with more risk [9]. Growing up in a dense city, and being a first-generation migrant, each raise risk as well, as the chart below lays out. These are population-level associations, not verdicts on any individual, and most people exposed to every one of them never develop the illness. But they show, unmistakably, that the mechanism is not sealed inside the skull. What happens to a developing brain is part of the biology, not a separate story told alongside it.

### So is it genetic?

Overwhelmingly, and not remotely simply. Twin studies put the heritability, the share of variation in risk across a population that tracks with genes, at roughly 79% [10]. That sounds like destiny until you look at identical twins, who share every gene: when one has schizophrenia, the other does so only about a third of the time, 33%, against 7% for fraternal twins [10]. Genes load the dice heavily; they do not throw them. And when the field finally had the statistical power to find the genes, it found not one but a crowd, 287 distinct regions of the genome, with roughly 120 genes prioritized, each contributing only a sliver of risk [11]. There is no 'schizophrenia gene.' The absence of one is not a gap in the research; it is one of the field's real discoveries. The condition is polygenic, spread across hundreds of small effects, much as height or blood pressure is. And even added together, the known variants explain only part of the heritability the twin studies imply, a gap that tells us the map is still being drawn. This is exactly why a genetic test cannot tell an individual what a twin study tells a population.

### Genes and world, together

The cleanest way to see that nature and nurture are not two columns to be added is a study of gene-by-environment interaction. In one long-running birth cohort, young people who used cannabis in adolescence were far more likely to develop psychotic symptoms if they carried a particular common variant of the COMT gene, while those with a different version of the same gene appeared largely unaffected by the same exposure [14]. The exposure did not act on everyone equally; the genotype set who was vulnerable to it. That is what interaction means, and it is why the old question, is it nature or nurture, is less hard to answer than badly posed. A single early finding like this deserves caution, and replication across studies has been uneven. But the shape of the result keeps recurring across the field: the same environment lands differently on different genomes, and the same genome expresses itself differently in different worlds. Risk is not a sum. It is a conversation.

> **[Highlight]** Notice what the four accounts have in common. Pruning that thins a network too far, a dopamine signal that mislabels what matters, a glutamate system that cannot hold its circuits in balance, a childhood that shapes a still-developing brain, each is, at bottom, a failure of the mind to keep its parts working as one. That is the thread the folk-model gets exactly backward. The trouble is not a self that has split into several. It is a single self whose capacity to integrate perception, thought, and meaning has been pushed, from several directions at once, past what it can hold together.

> **[Highlight]** Here is the honest limit, offered without apology: there is no blood test, no brain scan, no biomarker that diagnoses schizophrenia. People with the condition differ from healthy volunteers on many biological measures, yet almost none of those differences has become a clinical test [12]. What we have is not one clean chain running from gene to symptom, but a set of converging partial accounts, each robust in its own right and none complete. Saying so plainly is more interesting, and more useful, than any confident hedge, and it is the difference between teaching the science and selling a story.

**Myth:** Cold, distant, or 'schizophrenogenic' mothering causes schizophrenia.

**Fact:** This was a real mid-twentieth-century theory, and it was wrong. Its own former promoters walked it back from a 'half-truth' to an outright psychiatric 'myth.' Parenting style does not cause schizophrenia; blaming mothers produced decades of needless guilt and no cures.  
*(Hartwell 1996)*

**Myth:** There is a single 'schizophrenia gene', or the condition is purely genetic.

**Fact:** Risk is spread across at least 287 genomic loci with no single causal gene [11], and even identical twins, who share all of it, both develop the condition only about a third of the time [10]. Genes and environment interact; neither writes the outcome alone.

**FAQ / glossary:**

- **Why does synaptic pruning matter so much?** - Adolescence is when the brain deletes weak or unused connections to make its circuits faster and sharper. In the prefrontal cortex this continues into the third decade of life [1]. The leading hypothesis is that in some people the process cuts too deep, thinning networks past the point where they can hold information together, which fits both the timing of onset and the trouble with planning and memory that so often comes with it. It also reframes the disorder: the problem may be less what the brain builds than what it takes apart, and when.

- **If dopamine is the answer, why isn't blocking it a cure?** - Because it was never the whole answer. Dopamine-blocking drugs quiet hallucinations and delusions but barely move the negative and cognitive symptoms [5]. A co-equal account points to glutamate: blocking its NMDA receptor with ketamine reproduces both positive AND negative symptoms in healthy volunteers [7], something no dopamine drug does [6]. Two mechanisms, not one, and treatment has mostly addressed only the first, which is a large part of why so much of the illness remains under-treated.

- **If it's about 79% genetic, why don't identical twins always share it?** - Heritability measures how much of the variation in risk across a population tracks with genes, not whether any one person is fated. Identical twins share every gene yet are concordant only about a third of the time [10], because development, chance, and environment all get a vote. Genes set the odds; they do not settle the outcome, and heritability describes populations, not individuals.

- **What does 'aberrant salience' actually mean?** - Salience is the brain's tag for 'pay attention, this matters.' Normally it lands on the things that genuinely do, a friend's face, a sudden noise, a threat. The influential idea is that an over-active dopamine signal fires that tag at the wrong moments, so neutral events, a license plate, a glance, a coincidence, start to feel urgently significant [5][13]. Delusions and hallucinations can be understood, at least in part, as the mind's effort to make sense of a stream of misplaced significance it cannot switch off. It is not that reasoning breaks first; it is that the data feeding reasoning have been mislabeled.

- **Does cannabis cause schizophrenia, or just reveal it?** - The data cannot fully settle causation, but they are not neutral either. Heavier use tracks with higher odds in a dose-dependent way [9], the association survives many attempts to explain it as reverse causation, and at least one study suggests the effect is stronger in people carrying a particular gene variant [14]. The most defensible reading is that cannabis is a contributing cause in vulnerable people rather than a simple switch in anyone, one push among several, not the whole shove. That is also why it is a population-level risk factor and not a verdict on any individual who has ever used it.
