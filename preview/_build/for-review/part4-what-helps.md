**Thesis:** The most powerful things that help in schizophrenia are the least-discussed, coordinated care and family support rival medication for preventing relapse, and 'non-adherence' is usually a rational response to real side-effect burden, not a failure of will.

**The risk:** Psychosocial care, family intervention (relapse RR 0.55) and coordinated specialty care (better on all 13 outcomes), rivals medication for preventing relapse, and the 44-56% called 'non-adherent' are usually making a rational response to real side-effect burden and impaired insight, not a moral failure.

---

Ask what helps in schizophrenia and most people picture a pill. The evidence tells a stranger story, and it begins not with a drug but with a clock. Before any treatment is even chosen, time is already at work. Across 33 samples pooled in one systematic review, the longer psychosis went untreated before care began, the worse the long-term outcome tended to be, a small but stubbornly consistent link, with correlations of 0.13 to 0.18 running across symptoms, remission, and social functioning [1]. Those numbers are modest, and meant to be. Duration of untreated psychosis is one thread among many, tangled up with how severe the illness was to begin with and how much support already surrounded the person; on its own it never tells the whole story. But the direction almost never reverses. And it carries an uncomfortable implication: some of what looks like the illness itself deteriorating may in fact be care arriving late. If that is even partly true, then what helps turns as much on when and how as on which, a point the rest of this map keeps making. The parts that matter most are often the ones left off the prescription.

### What the medications do, and do not, do

Start with the drugs, because that is where the public conversation stays, and because the evidence for them is genuinely real. Antipsychotics are the class clinicians most often reach for first. The largest network meta-analysis of them pooled 402 trials and more than 53,000 participants across 32 oral agents, and every single one beat placebo for reducing acute symptoms, with standardised effects ranging from -0.89 for the strongest down to -0.03, statistically indistinguishable from nothing, for the weakest [3]. An earlier multiple-treatments analysis of 15 drugs, drawing on 212 trials and some 43,000 people, found the same shape: all beat placebo, from 0.88 down to 0.33 [4]. Two facts sit side by side in those ranges, and a careful clinician holds both at once. The first is that the differences from one agent to the next are gradual, a slope and not a cliff, which is why guidelines rarely crown a single winner and lean instead on how well a particular person tolerates a particular drug. The second is that the average benefit is small-to-medium: reviewers put the typical acute effect near 0.4 versus placebo [3][5]. These medicines lower the intensity of hallucinations and fixed beliefs for many people, sometimes dramatically for one individual even where the group average looks modest. What they do not do is cure, and they do not touch every symptom equally. Their effect falls mainly on the positive symptoms, the voices and the convictions, while the negative and cognitive difficulties that most shape whether a person works, studies, and connects are barely moved by any drug in the class. The honest summary is a class that works, modestly and unevenly, on one part of the problem.

### The strongest case: staying well

The strongest argument for medication is not about feeling better today. It is about not relapsing tomorrow. Pooling 65 trials and 6,493 people, one meta-analysis found that over a single year 27% of those kept on an antipsychotic relapsed, against 64% of those switched to placebo, a risk ratio of 0.40, and roughly one relapse prevented for every three people treated [6]. Hospital readmission moved in step, falling from 26% down to 10% [6]. As effects go in this field, that is among the larger and more reliable ones on record, and it is the reason maintenance is discussed at all. It is also worth being precise about what the sentence does and does not say. It reports what the trials found; it is not a direction to any reader. The maintenance question, whether, how long, and at what cost, is one a person and their clinician weigh together, because the protection is not free. And it describes groups, not individuals. A risk ratio is a population average; no average can tell one person how their own body will answer, or place them in advance on the lucky or the unlucky side of the line.

### The bill that comes with it

That protection is bought with a real bill, and naming it plainly is part of respecting the trade. The same drugs that quiet symptoms can bring metabolic weight gain, up to roughly 3.2 kg more than placebo for some agents, a shift that carries its own long-run cardiac and metabolic risk [3]. They can bring movement problems: the earlier analysis found the odds of extrapyramidal side-effects running as high as 4.76 times placebo for some agents, even as others sat gentler on movement than placebo itself [4]. And they can bring tardive dyskinesia, an involuntary and often lasting movement disorder, at a pooled prevalence near 25% across 41 studies and more than 11,000 people [7]. That burden is not evenly spread: it ran lower during second-generation treatment (20.7%) than first-generation (30.0%), one reason the choice of agent is rarely a trivial one [7]. None of this cancels the benefit; the relapse figures stand on their own. It sets that benefit against a cost that is concrete, and borne by the person rather than the prescriber. A plan that ignores the cost is not, in any real sense, a plan the person has agreed to.

### Where the rest of the evidence lives

If medication addresses one part of the problem, and addresses it modestly, the parts it leaves untouched are where the rest of the evidence lives, in talk, in families, in the way care is organised. These are the interventions the public conversation forgets, and more than one of them turns out to matter far more than its low profile would suggest.

**1. CBT for psychosis** - Talking therapy adapted for psychosis is where guidelines place a modest adjunct, never a substitute. Across 34 studies its pooled effect on overall symptoms was small, -0.33, and, tellingly, it shrank to -0.15 in the trials that blinded their raters, against -0.62 in those that did not [8]. That gap is a lesson in miniature: expectation inflates results, and honest appraisal means trusting the blinded number over the flattering one. What the therapy targets is less the presence of symptoms than the distress wrapped around them, the grip a voice holds, the certainty of a belief, the retreat into isolation that tends to follow. Real and useful, then, but a supporting role rather than the headline act.

**2. Family intervention & psychoeducation** - Here is the asymmetry almost no one hears. A Cochrane review of dozens of trials found that working with families cut relapse with a risk ratio of 0.55, about one relapse prevented for every seven families helped, and improved medication adherence as well (RR 0.60) [9]. That rivals maintenance medication for keeping people well, and it does so by treating the household as part of the care rather than a backdrop to it. Yet it remains among the least-offered and least-discussed interventions in the field. Why so rare? It asks for trained clinicians, several sessions, and patience, none of it as quick to deliver as writing a prescription, which may say more about how services are built than about what actually works.

### Care as a system, not a script

Step back far enough and a pattern comes into focus: the interventions with the strongest evidence are less individual products than ways of organising care around a person, early and all at once. That is the logic of coordinated specialty care, and unlike most good ideas in this field, it has been tested head to head.

**Before - Treatment as usual:** Care that arrives piecemeal: a prescription here, an appointment there, little coordination between the people meant to help, and long waits before anyone connects the parts. Each piece can look defensible on its own; together they often add up to less than their sum.

**After - Coordinated specialty / early intervention:** Team-based early care that folds therapy, family work, supported work or school, and medication management into one service working from a shared plan. A meta-analysis of 10 trials and 2,176 people found these programmes beat usual care on all 13 outcomes measured, fewer hospitalisations (RR 0.74), lower symptom severity (SMD -0.32), fewer people dropping out of care, and more of them in work or school [10]. In the US RAISE trial, 404 people in first-episode care were randomised; the 223 who received the coordinated NAVIGATE programme stayed in treatment longer and gained more in quality of life, symptoms, and involvement in work or school than the 181 in ordinary community care [2]. Timing sharpened everything: the gains were largest for those whose psychosis had gone untreated for a shorter span before the programme began, the clock from the opening, surfacing again in the results [2].

### What modern care is not

It helps to name what standard care is not, because the culture's picture of it is decades out of date. The asylum image, locked wards and lifelong institutionalisation, belongs to a receding past, not to treatment today. Electroconvulsive therapy still exists, and it still occupies an outsized place in the public imagination, but its evidenced role is narrow. A network meta-analysis of 59 trials in people whose psychosis had not responded to antipsychotics found that adding ECT on top might help, with an effect of -0.51, yet the certainty behind that number was very low, and the authors urged that it be considered only with substantial caution, as a limited next step for a specific and difficult situation [11]. It is not the dramatic centrepiece the movies keep staging. Most of the real work happens somewhere far less cinematic: in clinics and living rooms, in conversations and coordination, in the slow business of keeping a plan intact.

### The word we use most and understand least

Which brings us to the word the field uses most and understands least: 'non-adherence.' Between 44% and 56% of people prescribed antipsychotics do not take them as directed [12]. The reflex is to read that as denial, or weakness of will, or one more symptom to be managed. The evidence reads it differently. When a class of drugs can bring weight gain, movement disorders, and tardive dyskinesia at a pooled prevalence near 25% [7], deciding to stop is often a rational weighing, real costs a person is paying now against benefits they may not consciously feel. Layer on impaired insight, which is a feature of the illness itself and not a character flaw, and 'non-adherence' begins to look less like a moral failure than a signal: that the trade-off has tipped, or was never fully explained, or was never really the person's to negotiate in the first place. The stakes are not abstract. Inadequate treatment has been linked, again and again, with a markedly higher risk of relapse (RR up to 2.70) and higher mortality (RR up to 1.83, in a pooled sample of more than 270,000 people) [12]. But the direction the evidence points is not toward blame. It is toward better-tolerated care and better conversations. Reframed this way, the clinical task itself changes shape. The question stops being how to make a person comply, and becomes why the bargain feels unliveable, and what, in the drug or the relationship or the scaffolding around both, would make it liveable. That is a harder question. It is also a more honest one, and it carries the whole subject back to where it began: what helps depends less on the pill than on nearly everything arranged around it.
