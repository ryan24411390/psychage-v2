Emotional agility isn't about controlling or suppressing emotions—it's about being flexible with them, allowing difficult feelings while choosing actions aligned with your values.

Psychologist Susan David developed the concept of emotional agility to describe the ability to navigate life's challenges with self-acceptance, clear values, and adaptive actions . Research shows this flexibility is a core component of psychological health .

Think about how water flows around obstacles rather than crashing against them. Emotional agility works the same way—it's about moving with your emotions rather than fighting them or letting them control you. When you're emotionally agile, you can feel anxious about a presentation and still deliver it, experience grief while also finding moments of gratitude, or notice anger without immediately acting on it.

What Emotional Agility Looks Like

Emotional agility involves four key skills :

1

Showing Up

Facing emotions rather than avoiding them. Acknowledging what you feel without judgment or immediate reaction. This means noticing when you're sad, anxious, or angry instead of numbing with distractions or pushing feelings away.

2

Stepping Out

Creating distance between you and your emotions. "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure" rather than "I am a failure." You are not your emotions—you're the person experiencing them.

3

Walking Your Why

Using values as your compass. Making choices based on what matters to you, not just how you feel in the moment. Your values become the North Star when emotions pull you in different directions.

4

Moving On

Taking small, committed actions toward what you value, even when emotions pull you elsewhere. Willingness to experience discomfort for meaningful goals. This is where change happens.

Emotional Agility in Real Life

Consider these concrete examples of emotional agility in action:

Example 1: The Job Interview

Maria feels intense anxiety before a crucial job interview. Instead of canceling (avoidance) or trying to "think positive" (suppression), she shows up to the anxiety: "I notice my heart racing and catastrophic thoughts about failing." She steps out: "I'm having anxious thoughts, but thoughts aren't facts." She walks her why: "I value career growth and challenging myself." She moves on: She goes to the interview despite the discomfort, bringing the anxiety along like a passenger in the car rather than letting it drive.

Example 2: The Difficult Conversation

James needs to address a hurtful comment his partner made. He feels both angry and scared of conflict. Emotional rigidity would say "pick one—either you're angry and confront them, or you're scared and stay quiet." Emotional agility allows both: "I'm angry about what was said AND I'm nervous about this conversation. Both feelings make sense." He has the conversation anyway because he values honest communication in relationships.

Example 3: The Creative Block

A writer sits down to work and immediately feels overwhelmed by self-doubt. Emotional rigidity: "I feel like I can't write today, so I won't." Emotional agility: "I'm feeling that 'I can't do this' feeling. That's my inner critic showing up—thanks for trying to protect me from failure. AND I value creative expression, so I'm going to write one paragraph anyway." The feeling doesn't go away, but it doesn't stop the action either.

Agility vs. Emotional Rigidity

Emotional Rigidity
  • Avoiding or suppressing difficult emotions
  • Being controlled by feelings: "I feel anxious, so I can't do it"
  • Black-and-white thinking about emotions (good/bad)
  • Letting feelings dictate actions
  • Struggling against reality
  • Fusing with thoughts: "I think it, therefore it's true"
Emotional Agility
  • Accepting all emotions as data, not directives
  • Acting on values despite discomfort: "I feel anxious AND I can do it"
  • Holding emotions lightly, not letting them define you
  • Choosing actions aligned with goals
  • Adapting to reality with flexibility
  • Observing thoughts: "I'm having the thought that..."

Research shows that people with higher emotional agility have better mental health, stronger relationships, and greater career success . They're not less emotional—they're just more flexible in how they respond to emotions.

Cognitive Defusion: Stepping Back from Thoughts

A core skill of emotional agility is defusion—loosening the grip of thoughts and feelings . Fusion means your thoughts feel like absolute truth; defusion creates space.

When you're fused with a thought like "I'm not good enough," it feels like an undeniable fact. Your mind doesn't distinguish between "I'm having the thought that I'm not good enough" and "I am not good enough." Defusion helps you see thoughts as mental events—sometimes useful, sometimes not—rather than reality itself.

Important Distinction

Defusion isn't about positive thinking or challenging thoughts. It's about changing your relationship with thoughts—holding them more lightly rather than arguing with them or trying to replace them.

Values as Your Compass

When emotions are unreliable guides (anxiety says avoid, but growth requires courage), values provide direction . Values are chosen life directions, not goals to achieve.

Key Takeaway

Values vs. Goals: A goal is "run a marathon" (achievable, then done). A value is "being active and challenging myself" (ongoing direction). Goals can be met or missed; values guide you continuously.

Common core values include:

  • Connection and relationships: Being present with loved ones, building community
  • Growth and learning: Expanding your understanding and capabilities
  • Creativity and self-expression: Bringing your unique perspective into the world
  • Health and vitality: Taking care of your body and mind
  • Service and contribution: Making a positive difference for others
  • Authenticity and integrity: Living aligned with your true self
  • Adventure and novelty: Exploring new experiences and ideas
  • Peace and acceptance: Finding calm amid life's challenges

Identifying your values helps you make hard choices: "I feel like avoiding (emotion), but I value connection, so I'll reach out (action)."

Willing Discomfort

Emotional agility requires willingness—actively choosing to experience discomfort when it serves your values . This isn't suffering for its own sake; it's accepting that meaningful lives include difficult emotions.

Every important life domain requires willingness to feel uncomfortable sometimes. You can't have deep relationships without risking rejection. You can't grow without facing uncertainty. You can't create without tolerating self-doubt. The question isn't whether you'll feel discomfort—it's whether that discomfort is in service of something you care about.

Practical Exercises: Building Emotional Agility

Exercise 1

The Emotion Labeling Practice (5 minutes daily)

Step 1: Set a timer for 5 minutes. Close your eyes and scan your body for sensations.

Step 2: When you notice a feeling, name it: "I'm noticing sadness," "I'm noticing tension," "I'm noticing excitement." Use the phrase "I'm noticing..." to create distance.

Step 3: Get specific with emotion words. Instead of just "bad," try "disappointed," "overwhelmed," "frustrated," or "lonely." The more precise your label, the better you understand what you're experiencing.

Step 4: Notice where the emotion sits in your body. "The anxiety is in my chest as tightness." "The sadness is behind my eyes as pressure."

Step 5: Simply observe without trying to change anything. You're building the "observer" muscle—the part of you that can notice emotions without being consumed by them.

Practice this daily for two weeks and notice if you feel less overwhelmed by emotions. You're training yourself to be with feelings rather than fight them.

Exercise 2

Values-Aligned Action (Weekly practice)

Step 1: Write down 3-5 core values that matter to you. What kind of person do you want to be? What life directions feel meaningful? Examples: "being a supportive friend," "learning new things," "being creative," "taking care of my health."

Step 2: For each value, identify one small action you could take this week—even if it feels uncomfortable. If you value connection but feel anxious about reaching out, the action might be: "Text one friend to ask how they're doing."

Step 3: Notice what emotions show up when you think about this action. Anxiety? Fear of rejection? Uncertainty? Write them down: "When I think about texting my friend, I notice fear that they won't respond."

Step 4: Use this sentence: "I'm feeling [emotion] AND I'm going to [action] because I value [value]." Example: "I'm feeling anxious about rejection AND I'm going to text my friend because I value connection."

Step 5: Take the action. Bring the emotion along. Notice that you can feel fear and still send the text. You can feel doubt and still create. You can feel tired and still go for the walk.

This exercise teaches you that emotions don't have to determine your actions. You build confidence in your ability to move toward what matters, regardless of how you feel.

Exercise 3

Thought Defusion Techniques (As needed)

When you notice a sticky, unhelpful thought, try these defusion techniques:

Technique A - Add "I'm having the thought that...": Instead of "I'm a failure," say "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure." This tiny shift creates space between you and the thought.

Technique B - Thank your mind: When your mind offers unhelpful thoughts, say "Thanks, Mind, for that thought" or "Thanks for trying to protect me." This acknowledges the thought without buying into it.

Technique C - Name the story: If you notice a familiar thought pattern, name it. "Oh, there's my 'I'm not good enough' story again." Or "That's the 'everyone will judge me' thought." Naming it helps you see it as a pattern, not reality.

Technique D - Sing it: Take the thought and sing it to the tune of "Happy Birthday" or another familiar song. This sounds silly, but it works—it's hard to take a thought seriously when you're singing it like a show tune.

The goal isn't to make thoughts go away. It's to loosen their grip so they don't dictate your behavior. You're building psychological flexibility.

Building Emotional Agility Over Time

Emotional agility is a skill, not a trait. You build it through consistent practice:

  • Practice mindfulness: Regular observation of thoughts and feelings without judgment builds the observer muscle . Even five minutes daily makes a difference.
  • Clarify values: Write down what matters most. Review when making decisions. Your values are your true North.
  • Start small: Take one values-aligned action despite discomfort. Build willingness gradually. Small wins create momentum.
  • Notice fusion: Catch moments when you believe thoughts absolutely. Practice defusion techniques. This gets easier with repetition.
  • Expand your range: Experience more emotions without immediate reaction. Build tolerance for discomfort like building physical strength—gradually increase the weight.
  • Practice self-compassion: You'll mess up. You'll avoid emotions sometimes. You'll get fused with thoughts. That's part of being human. Notice it, name it, and try again.

When to Seek Professional Help

If emotions consistently overwhelm your functioning despite attempts at agility, consider therapy—especially Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), which directly teaches these skills . A therapist can provide structured practice and personalized guidance.

Seek professional support if:

  • You avoid important life activities due to difficult emotions (social situations, work responsibilities, healthcare)
  • Emotions frequently escalate to a level where you lose control of behavior
  • You rely heavily on substances, food, shopping, or other behaviors to avoid feelings
  • You feel stuck in rigid patterns despite wanting to change
  • Emotional struggles interfere with relationships, work, or daily functioning

Emotional agility isn't about being calm all the time or never feeling negative emotions. It's about building the flexibility to have difficult feelings without letting them control your life. It's choosing actions based on your values, not your momentary emotions. And it's developing the capacity to be fully human—experiencing the whole range of feelings while still moving toward what matters.
