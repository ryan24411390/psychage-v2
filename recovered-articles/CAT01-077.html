Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT) is an evidence-based approach that treats emotions not as problems to eliminate, but as valuable information to understand and transform for healing.

Developed by psychologist Leslie Greenberg, EFT is grounded in the understanding that emotions contain adaptive wisdom—they tell us what matters and what needs to change . The therapy helps people access, explore, and reshape emotional experience. Unlike approaches that focus on changing thoughts or behaviors, EFT works directly with feelings themselves.

Core Principles of EFT

  • Emotions as adaptive: Feelings evolved to guide behavior and signal needs. They're information, not pathology.
  • Accessing primary emotions: Surface emotions (anger, anxiety) often protect underlying vulnerable feelings (hurt, shame, fear). Therapy helps reach the core.
  • Emotion transformation: You don't eliminate difficult emotions by avoiding them—you transform them by fully experiencing and understanding them.
  • The body knows: Emotional wisdom lives in physical sensation and intuition, not just cognition. EFT uses embodied awareness.

Key Takeaway

Central idea: The only way out of difficult emotions is through them. Avoidance maintains distress; experiencing and processing emotions resolves them.

Real-World Example 1: Sarah's Anger

Sarah enters therapy feeling chronically irritable and snapping at her partner. Her therapist helps her notice that beneath her anger lies hurt—she feels unseen and unappreciated. Deeper still is fear: "If I'm not valuable to him, he'll leave." Rather than teaching Sarah to "manage her anger," EFT helps her access the underlying hurt and fear, express these vulnerable emotions, and discover what she truly needs (reassurance, connection). When the core emotions are addressed, the surface anger naturally diminishes.

How EFT Works

EFT follows a structured process :

1

Awareness: Arriving at Emotion

Noticing what you're feeling in the moment. Slowing down to identify emotional experience. "What are you feeling right now?"

2

Expression: Allowing Emotion

Giving voice and space to the feeling. The therapist creates safety to fully experience emotions without judgment or rushing to fix them.

3

Regulation: Containing Emotion

Building capacity to tolerate intense feelings. Therapist helps you stay with emotion without being overwhelmed. Co-regulation supports self-regulation.

4

Reflection: Making Sense of Emotion

Understanding what the emotion means. "What is this feeling telling you about what you need?" Extracting the adaptive information.

5

Transformation: Changing Emotion

Accessing new emotional experiences that shift maladaptive patterns. Bringing compassion to shame, assertiveness to fear, grief to unfinished anger.

Key Techniques: Two-Chair and Empty-Chair Work

EFT uses experiential techniques to access and work with emotions:

Two-Chair Dialogue: You literally sit in different chairs to embody different parts of yourself. One chair holds your critical inner voice; the other holds your vulnerable self. By speaking from each position, you access emotions and needs that get buried in internal conflict. This externalizes the internal struggle, making it workable .

Example 2: James and His Inner Critic

James struggles with self-criticism. In therapy, he sits in one chair as his harsh inner critic: "You're lazy and worthless. You'll never succeed." Then he moves to the other chair and speaks as his vulnerable self: "I'm trying so hard... I feel scared I'm not enough." The therapist guides James to notice what each part needs. The critic is trying to motivate him (but harshly); the vulnerable part needs compassion. By dialoguing between the parts, James develops self-compassion and transforms the internal relationship.

Empty-Chair Work: An empty chair represents someone important—a parent, ex-partner, or even a younger version of yourself. You speak to the empty chair, expressing unfinished emotions. Then you sit in the chair and respond as that person, accessing their perspective. This helps process unresolved relational pain and brings closure.

Focusing on Felt Sense: "Felt sense" is Eugene Gendlin's term for the vague, hard-to-describe bodily feeling that carries emotional meaning. EFT therapists help you notice and stay with these felt senses until they reveal their meaning. "What does that heaviness in your chest want to say?"

Evocative Responding: The therapist reflects not just your words, but the emotion beneath them, bringing it into sharper focus. If you say flatly, "My mom never listened to me," the therapist might respond, "There's deep sadness in that—feeling unseen by the person who mattered most." This evocative reflection helps you contact the emotion more fully.

Conditions effectively treated:

  • Depression: Especially when linked to unresolved grief, shame, or self-criticism
  • Anxiety disorders: Social anxiety, generalized anxiety
  • Trauma and PTSD: Processing traumatic emotions and creating new emotional experiences
  • Relationship problems: Emotionally Focused Couples Therapy (EFT-C) has strong evidence
  • Emotion regulation difficulties: Alexithymia, chronic shame, anger problems
  • Eating disorders: Addressing underlying emotional issues

Example 3: Couples Therapy Success

Maria and Tom are stuck in a pursue-withdraw pattern. Maria criticizes; Tom shuts down. In EFT couples therapy, the therapist helps Maria access the fear beneath her criticism: "I'm terrified you don't care about me anymore." Tom, hearing this vulnerable emotion instead of criticism, softens and shares his own fear: "I withdraw because I feel like I always fail you." Accessing and sharing these primary emotions—fear of abandonment, fear of inadequacy—allows them to reconnect emotionally. The negative pattern breaks when core emotions are expressed and responded to.

What to Expect in EFT Sessions

  • Session length: Typically 50-60 minutes weekly or biweekly
  • Duration: Short-term (16-20 sessions) for focused issues; longer for complex trauma or personality patterns
  • Active participation: More experiential and emotional than traditional talk therapy. You'll be asked to notice feelings, speak from different parts of yourself, or engage in chair work.
  • Safe emotional exploration: Therapist creates environment to access vulnerable emotions without judgment
  • Between-session practice: Noticing emotions, journaling, self-compassion exercises

Clinical Note

For therapist-shoppers: Look for therapists trained in EFT/EFT-C (certificate programs exist). Ask: "Are you trained in Emotion-Focused Therapy?" and "Do you use experiential techniques like chair work?"

Practical Exercises to Try at Home

Exercise 1: Self-Compassion Chair Work (15 minutes)

  1. Set up two chairs facing each other. Label one "Inner Critic" and one "Compassionate Self."
  2. Sit in the Critic chair. Speak aloud as your harshest inner voice. "You're not good enough. You always mess up." Let it say everything it usually says in your head.
  3. Move to the Compassionate Self chair. Take a breath. Respond as your kindest, wisest self. "I hear that you're scared I'll fail. But I'm doing my best. I deserve kindness, not punishment."
  4. Go back and forth 3-4 times. Let the critic voice its fears; let the compassionate voice respond with understanding.
  5. End in the Compassionate Self chair. Notice how it feels to offer yourself kindness. Write down one compassionate statement to remember.

Exercise 2: Felt Sense Exploration (10 minutes)

  1. Think of a current problem or worry. Don't analyze it—just bring it to mind.
  2. Notice where you feel it in your body. Chest? Stomach? Throat? Shoulders?
  3. Describe the physical sensation. Is it tight? Heavy? Hot? Fluttery? Use descriptive words.
  4. Ask the sensation: "What are you about?" Wait. Don't force an answer. Let an image, word, or feeling emerge. Maybe it's "trapped" or "afraid" or "alone."
  5. Stay with it gently. Ask: "What do you need?" Again, wait for what emerges. Maybe "space" or "to be heard" or "safety."
  6. Acknowledge it. "I hear you. You need to be heard." Notice if the sensation shifts—even slightly. This is emotion transformation beginning.

Exercise 3: Unfinished Business Letter (20 minutes)

  1. Think of someone you have unresolved feelings toward. A parent, ex-partner, friend, boss—anyone.
  2. Write them a letter you'll never send. Express everything you wish you'd said. "I felt hurt when..." "I needed you to..." Don't censor. This is for you.
  3. Read it aloud to an empty chair. Imagine them sitting there. Speak the emotions you wrote.
  4. Sit in the empty chair and respond as them. What would they say? Try to step into their perspective, even if it's painful or defensive.
  5. Return to your chair. How do you feel after hearing their (imagined) response? Write down any insights or shifts in your emotional experience.

Who Benefits Most from EFT

Good fit if you:

  • Feel disconnected from your emotions or have alexithymia
  • Experience chronic shame, self-criticism, or emotional blocks
  • Have unresolved grief or traumatic experiences
  • Struggle with depression linked to emotional suppression
  • Want to improve emotional intimacy in relationships
  • Are willing to engage experientially (not just talk about feelings)

May not be ideal if you:

  • Are in acute crisis requiring immediate stabilization (safety first)
  • Prefer highly structured, protocol-driven therapy (CBT might fit better)
  • Find emotional exploration too overwhelming (may need trauma-focused prep first)
  • Want primarily cognitive/behavioral strategies

Example 4: When EFT Helps Most

David has tried CBT for depression. He learned thought-challenging skills, but still feels empty. His therapist suggests EFT. In EFT, David discovers beneath his "empty" feeling is deep grief about his father's death five years ago—grief he never fully processed. By accessing, expressing, and working through this grief emotionally (not just cognitively), David's depression lifts. He needed to feel the feelings, not just change thoughts about them.

Emotion-Focused Therapy offers a powerful path for those ready to turn toward emotions rather than away from them. By accessing, understanding, and transforming emotional experience, EFT helps people heal from the inside out. The journey through emotions—rather than around them—leads to lasting change.
