You dread Monday mornings. Team meetings make you nauseous. You rehearse every email ten times before sending. Lunch in the break room feels like a social minefield. You're competent at your job—but social anxiety at work makes every day exhausting.

Workplace social anxiety is the fear of judgment, embarrassment, or negative evaluation in professional settings . Unlike general social anxiety, this is specific to work contexts where professional reputation and livelihood feel at stake.

Common Workplace Triggers

  • Meetings: Speaking up, presenting, being put on the spot
  • Performance reviews: Being evaluated, receiving feedback
  • Email/communication: Worrying about tone, being misunderstood
  • Networking events: Small talk, self-promotion
  • Collaboration: Working closely with others, asking for help
  • Visibility: Being noticed by leadership, recognition

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Chen is brilliant at data analysis but stays silent in meetings. He has valuable insights but fears saying something stupid. His manager thinks he's disengaged. His career suffers not from lack of skill, but from social anxiety preventing him from showcasing his expertise.

Example 2: Lisa spends hours composing simple work emails, agonizing over every word. She reads sent emails repeatedly, searching for things that might be misinterpreted. What should take five minutes takes an hour. Her productivity suffers because social anxiety extends into written communication.

Example 3: David turns down a promotion because it requires more client-facing work. He's qualified, but the thought of increased social interaction triggers panic. He stays in a role below his skill level, sacrificing career advancement to avoid anxiety.

Managing Social Anxiety at Work

1. Prepare, But Don't Over-Prepare

Preparation reduces anxiety, but perfectionism increases it. For meetings, prepare 2-3 key points you want to make. For presentations, know your material well but don't memorize scripts word-for-word. Balance builds confidence without rigidity .

2. Start Small with Exposure

Build tolerance gradually. Ask one question in a meeting. Send an email without re-reading it five times. Make small talk with one colleague. Each small success builds confidence .

3. Challenge Catastrophic Thinking

Social anxiety predicts disaster: "If I speak up, I'll sound stupid and everyone will think less of me." Reality-test these predictions. How often do people actually respond negatively? What's the actual worst-case scenario, and how likely is it?

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Meeting Participation Challenge

  1. Set a goal: Speak once in every meeting this week
  2. Prepare one comment or question beforehand
  3. Speak within the first 10 minutes (waiting makes anxiety worse)
  4. After speaking, resist ruminating—note that you did it and move on
  5. Track weekly: How many times did you speak? Did catastrophic predictions come true?

Exercise 2: Email Efficiency Protocol

  1. Write email draft
  2. Read through once for typos
  3. Set a timer for 2 minutes—make any final edits
  4. When timer goes off, send immediately without re-reading
  5. Resist the urge to check sent emails—each time you want to, take three deep breaths instead

Exercise 3: Visibility Micro-Exposures

  1. Week 1: Say good morning to 3 colleagues daily
  2. Week 2: Make one comment in team chat each day
  3. Week 3: Ask one colleague about their weekend
  4. Week 4: Share one work success (however small) with your team
  5. Track anxiety levels—notice how they decrease with repetition

When to Seek Professional Help

If social anxiety is limiting your career advancement, causing you to avoid necessary work interactions, or creating severe distress, consider therapy. CBT is effective for workplace social anxiety .
