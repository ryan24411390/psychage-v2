Some people struggle to identify what they're feeling beyond basic categories like "good" or "bad." This isn't emotional suppression or avoidance—it's alexithymia, a trait involving difficulty recognizing and describing emotions.

The term alexithymia comes from Greek: "a" (without), "lexis" (words), "thymos" (emotions)—literally "no words for feelings." It affects an estimated 10% of the general population and is significantly higher in certain conditions . For someone with alexithymia, being asked "How do you feel?" can trigger genuine confusion rather than resistance.

What Alexithymia Looks Like

Alexithymia involves three core features:

  • Difficulty identifying feelings: Unable to distinguish between different emotions. "I feel... something. I don't know what."
  • Difficulty describing feelings: Struggle to put emotional experiences into words, even when aware something is happening
  • Externally-oriented thinking: Focus on external facts and events rather than internal emotional experiences. Detail-oriented but emotionally disconnected.

Key Takeaway

Alexithymia is a trait, not a disorder. It exists on a spectrum—some people have mild difficulty with emotional awareness, while others have profound disconnection from their internal emotional states.

What It Feels Like Inside

People with alexithymia describe experiences like:

  • "I know something's wrong, but I can't tell if I'm sad, angry, or just tired"
  • "People ask how I feel and I go blank—I honestly don't know"
  • "I notice physical sensations (heart racing, stomach tight) but don't connect them to emotions"
  • "I'm better at talking about what happened than how I felt about it"
  • "I feel numb or disconnected from emotional situations"
  • "I don't understand why people make such a big deal about feelings"

Example 1: The Job Interview

Sarah just finished a major job interview. Her colleague asks, "How did it go? How are you feeling?" Sarah responds factually: "The interviewer asked six questions. I answered them. The office had blue chairs." When pressed about her feelings, she genuinely doesn't know—she noticed her palms were sweaty and her heart was beating fast, but she can't connect these sensations to "nervous" or "excited." She's not being evasive; she simply lacks the internal awareness to label the emotion.

Example 2: The Relationship Conversation

Marcus's partner says, "You seem upset. What's wrong?" Marcus responds, "Nothing's wrong." His partner grows frustrated: "You've been quiet all evening. Talk to me." Marcus feels pressured but confused. He knows something feels "off" in his body—tension in his shoulders, a heaviness—but he can't identify it as sadness, frustration, or hurt. He's not withholding; he genuinely cannot access the emotional label .

Causes and Risk Factors

Alexithymia has multiple potential origins, often involving a combination of biological, developmental, and environmental factors:

Neurological Differences: Brain imaging studies show reduced activity in the anterior cingulate cortex (involved in emotional awareness) and decreased connectivity between brain regions that process emotions and language . Some people may be neurologically wired with less emotional signal transmission.

Developmental Factors: Growing up in environments where emotions were dismissed, punished, or ignored can impair emotional vocabulary development. If caregivers never helped you name feelings ("You seem frustrated" or "That must have felt scary"), you may never develop the internal classification system for emotions.

Trauma Response: Severe or repeated trauma can lead to emotional numbing as a protective mechanism. Disconnecting from feelings becomes a survival strategy that persists even when safety is restored.

Associated Conditions: Alexithymia rates are significantly elevated in autism spectrum disorder (50%), PTSD (40-60%), eating disorders (40-60%), and substance use disorders (45-50%) .

Interoceptive Deficit: Interoception is awareness of internal body signals. People with alexithymia often have poor interoceptive accuracy—they can't reliably detect their own heartbeat, stomach sensations, or muscle tension. Since emotions create bodily sensations, poor interoception means the primary emotional signals go undetected.

Specific challenges include:

  • Relationship difficulties: Partners want emotional sharing; alexithymia makes this nearly impossible. "I don't know how I feel" frustrates both people.
  • Mental health treatment challenges: Therapy relies on identifying and processing emotions—core struggles for those with alexithymia
  • Somatic symptoms: Emotions may manifest as physical complaints (headaches, stomach issues) without conscious emotional awareness
  • Emotional regulation problems: Can't regulate what you can't identify. May use maladaptive coping (substance use, avoidance) to manage undefined distress
  • Empathy limitations: Difficulty recognizing others' emotions when you struggle with your own

Example 3: The Medical Mystery

Jennifer visits her doctor for the fifth time this year with migrain headaches, digestive issues, and chronic fatigue. Medical tests show nothing wrong. Her doctor gently suggests stress might be a factor. Jennifer is confused—she doesn't feel stressed. She can't identify that her body is expressing anxiety about her demanding job through physical symptoms. Without emotional awareness, the mind-body signals get crossed.

Assessment and Diagnosis

The Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20) is the most widely used assessment tool . It measures:

  1. Difficulty identifying feelings
  2. Difficulty describing feelings to others
  3. Externally-oriented thinking style

Scores range from 20-100:

  • ≤51: No alexithymia
  • 52-60: Possible alexithymia
  • ≥61: Alexithymia

Clinical Note

Alexithymia is not a psychiatric diagnosis—it's a personality trait or characteristic that can exist independently or alongside other conditions. It's dimensional, not categorical.

Treatment and Management

Alexithymia can improve with targeted intervention :

1

Interoceptive Awareness Training

Learning to notice body sensations. Body scans, mindfulness, biofeedback. Connecting physical cues to emotional states .

2

Emotion Education

Building emotional vocabulary. Learning emotion categories, what they feel like physically, what causes them. Like learning a new language.

3

Emotion-Focused Therapy

Therapy specifically designed to help access and process emotions. Gentler approach than traditional talk therapy for those with alexithymia.

4

Expressive Arts

Art, music, movement can access emotions before words are available. Alternative pathways to emotional awareness.

Practical Exercises for Building Emotional Awareness

Exercise 1: Body Scan for Emotion Clues (5 minutes daily)

  1. Sit comfortably and close your eyes. Take three deep breaths.
  2. Scan your body from head to toe. Notice any sensations: tension, warmth, tightness, heaviness, lightness, tingling. Don't judge—just notice.
  3. Ask: "Where do I feel something?" Maybe your chest feels tight, or your shoulders are tense, or your stomach feels uneasy.
  4. Name the sensation. Use physical descriptors: "heavy," "tight," "fluttery," "burning."
  5. Try connecting to an emotion. Reference an emotion wheel or list. Does "tight chest" match "anxious"? Does "heavy shoulders" match "sad" or "overwhelmed"? Don't force it—just try.
  6. Record your observations. Write down: "Tight chest - maybe anxiety about the meeting tomorrow." You're building the body-emotion connection over time.

Exercise 2: Emotion Vocabulary Builder (10 minutes, 3x per week)

  1. Get an emotion wheel or list. Search "emotion wheel" online and print one, or use a feelings list.
  2. Pick one emotion from the wheel. Start simple: "angry," "sad," "afraid," "happy."
  3. Research its physical signature. What does anger feel like in the body? (Hot face, clenched fists, tight jaw). What causes it? (Boundary violation, unfairness).
  4. Recall a time you might have felt it. Think back to situations where this emotion would make sense. What was happening? What did you notice in your body?
  5. Practice the label. Next time you notice those physical cues, try applying the label: "This might be anger."
  6. Repeat with a new emotion each session. Slowly build your internal dictionary.

Exercise 3: "Thoughts, Body, Guess" Journaling (5 minutes daily)

  1. Set a timer for 5 minutes. Grab a notebook or phone.
  2. Write: "What happened today?" Describe events factually. "Had a meeting. Boss gave feedback. Came home."
  3. Write: "What did I notice in my body?" Even small things count. "Stomach felt weird. Shoulders tight."
  4. Write: "What thoughts went through my head?" "I thought about whether I did a good job."
  5. Take a guess: "This might have been..." Use your emotion vocabulary. "Maybe I felt nervous or worried about the feedback?"
  6. No pressure to be right. The goal is practice connecting events → body signals → emotion labels. Over time, patterns emerge.

Supporting Someone with Alexithymia

  • Don't assume withholding: They genuinely don't know how they feel, not refusing to share
  • Ask about thoughts and body sensations: "What are you thinking?" and "What do you notice in your body?" work better than "How do you feel?"
  • Provide vocabulary: "When that happened to me, I felt scared and angry. Does any of that resonate?"
  • Be patient: Building emotional awareness takes time. Progress is slow.
  • Value other forms of connection: Actions, shared activities, problem-solving together—these matter too

Example 4: The Patient Partner

When Devon's partner Jamie says "I don't know how I'm feeling," Devon has learned not to push. Instead, Devon asks: "What's going on in your body right now?" Jamie might say, "My stomach feels tight." Devon responds, "That happens to me when I'm worried. Are you worried about something?" This gentle offering of vocabulary—without insisting Jamie accept it—helps Jamie start building emotional language over time.

When to Seek Professional Help

Consider therapy if :

  • Alexithymia significantly impairs relationships or functioning
  • You experience unexplained physical symptoms
  • Depression or anxiety develops (common co-occurrences)
  • You want to develop greater emotional awareness
  • Substance use or other maladaptive coping emerges

Alexithymia isn't a life sentence. While challenging, emotional awareness can improve with patience, practice, and appropriate support. Learning to identify and name feelings opens doors to richer emotional experience and deeper connections. The journey from "I don't know what I feel" to "I think this might be sadness" is profound—and possible.
