> **Summary:** LGBTQ+ individuals experience eating disorders at significantly elevated rates compared to the general population — yet they are less likely to be screened, less likely to receive culturally competent care, and more likely to encounter treatment environments that do not affirm their identity. The intersection of minority stress, body ideals specific to LGBTQ+ subcultures, gender dysphoria, discrimination, internalized stigma, and the trauma of identity-related rejection creates a unique risk landscape. Research has begun to document these elevated rates and their drivers, though the field remains years behind where it should be. This article examines what is known about eating disorders across LGBTQ+ communities, the unique risk factors that different populations face, the treatment gaps that persist, and what affirming care looks like.

**Key Facts:**
1. Gay and bisexual men are approximately 7 times more likely to report binge eating and 12 times more likely to report purging than heterosexual men [1].
2. Transgender individuals report eating disorder symptoms at rates 2–4 times higher than cisgender peers, with gender dysphoria identified as a significant mediating factor [2].
3. Lesbian and bisexual women report elevated rates of binge eating disorder compared to heterosexual women, though the relationship with restrictive eating disorders is more complex [3].
4. Minority stress — the chronic stress of living as a marginalized person in a hostile or indifferent society — is a primary framework for understanding elevated eating disorder risk in LGBTQ+ populations [4].
5. Fewer than 10% of eating disorder treatment programs in the United States offer LGBTQ+-specific programming or staff training on affirming care for sexual and gender minorities [5].

## The Elevated Risk: What the Numbers Show

The data on eating disorders in LGBTQ+ populations, while still developing, consistently demonstrates elevated risk across multiple identities.

**Gay and bisexual men** represent one of the highest-risk demographic groups for eating disorders. Research from the Growing Up Today Study, a large prospective cohort study conducted by Feldman and Meyer (2007), found that sexual minority men were significantly more likely to report binge eating, purging, and fasting for weight control than heterosexual men [1]. A meta-analysis by Calzo and colleagues (2017) confirmed these findings across studies, estimating that gay and bisexual men have approximately 3–12 times the risk of specific eating disorder behaviors compared to heterosexual men [6].

**Transgender and gender-diverse individuals** face uniquely elevated risk. A study by Diemer and colleagues (2015) using data from the National College Health Assessment found that transgender students were 4 times more likely to report an eating disorder diagnosis and 2 times more likely to report compensatory behaviors compared to cisgender women [2]. Subsequent research has documented similar patterns across community and clinical samples.

**Lesbian and bisexual women** show a more nuanced pattern. While early research suggested that lesbian women might be "protected" from eating disorders due to less investment in thin-ideal body standards, more recent and methodologically rigorous research has challenged this assumption. Watson and colleagues (2017) found that sexual minority women reported elevated rates of binge eating, body dissatisfaction, and disordered eating behaviors compared to heterosexual women, though the patterns varied by specific sexual identity and other intersecting identities [3].

**Non-binary and gender-fluid individuals** are the least studied group, but emerging research suggests they face significant body image distress related to navigating a body that does not fit binary categories — a form of distress that may manifest through disordered eating as an attempt to control or reshape the body [7].

## Minority Stress: The Psychological Driver

The minority stress model, developed by Ilan Meyer (2003), provides the primary theoretical framework for understanding elevated mental health risk in LGBTQ+ populations [4]. The model identifies several stress processes unique to minority group members:

**External stressors.** Discrimination, violence, harassment, microaggressions, and social rejection based on sexual orientation or gender identity create chronic stress that the individual must manage alongside the ordinary stressors of daily life.

**Expectation of rejection.** The anticipation that one may face discrimination in any given interaction — at work, in healthcare, in social settings, in the family — creates a state of vigilance that is itself psychologically taxing.

**Concealment.** The management of identity disclosure — deciding who to tell, what to reveal, and how to navigate environments where one's identity may not be safe — requires constant cognitive and emotional effort.

**Internalized stigma.** The internalization of societal negative attitudes toward one's own identity produces shame, self-hatred, and diminished self-worth.

Each of these stress processes is independently associated with eating disorder risk. Discrimination experiences predict body dissatisfaction. Concealment predicts emotional dysregulation (which drives binge eating). Internalized stigma predicts restriction, purging, and negative body image. The cumulative burden of minority stress creates a psychological environment in which eating disorders are more likely to develop and more difficult to recover from.

## Gender Dysphoria and the Body

For transgender and gender-diverse individuals, the relationship between body image and eating disorders has an additional dimension: gender dysphoria — the distress that can arise from the incongruence between one's gender identity and one's physical body.

Gender dysphoria can manifest through food and eating in several ways:

**Restriction to suppress secondary sex characteristics.** A transgender man may restrict food intake to reduce body fat, suppress breast development, and create a more masculine-appearing body. A transgender woman may restrict to create a thinner, more "feminine" frame. These restriction behaviors may meet criteria for anorexia nervosa but are driven by gender incongruence rather than by the typical body image disturbance described in clinical literature [8].

**Binge eating as coping.** The distress of living in a body that does not match one's identity can trigger binge eating as an emotional regulation strategy — particularly when access to gender-affirming medical care (hormones, surgery) is limited, delayed, or denied.

**Exercise compulsivity** aimed at reshaping the body toward one's gender identity — building muscle for transmasculine individuals, or reducing muscle and increasing leanness for transfeminine individuals.

**Complex body image.** Transgender individuals may experience body dissatisfaction that is simultaneously about gender (this body does not match my gender) and about the same appearance concerns that affect the general population (this body does not match cultural beauty standards within my gender). These two sources of body distress can compound each other.

Access to gender-affirming care (hormone therapy, surgery) has been associated with improvements in both body image and eating disorder symptoms in some studies — suggesting that addressing gender dysphoria may reduce the body distress that drives disordered eating [9]. However, gender-affirming care does not automatically resolve eating disorders, and individuals may continue to need eating disorder-specific treatment alongside gender-affirming care.

## Body Ideals Within LGBTQ+ Subcultures

LGBTQ+ communities are not free from body ideals — they have their own, and these ideals can exert pressure comparable to mainstream cultural standards.

**Gay male culture** has been documented to emphasize muscularity, leanness, and youth as markers of attractiveness and social status. Research by Tylka and Andorka (2012) found that objectification experiences — being evaluated and valued primarily for physical appearance — were elevated in gay men compared to heterosexual men, and that objectification predicted body dissatisfaction and disordered eating [10]. Dating apps that emphasize physical appearance (displaying body photos, height, weight) may amplify appearance-based comparison and discrimination.

**Lesbian communities** have historically been characterized as more size-accepting than heterosexual culture — but this characterization is increasingly recognized as an oversimplification. Body image pressures exist within lesbian communities, though they may center on different ideals (such as specific presentations of femininity or masculinity) depending on subcultural context.

**Transgender communities** face the additional pressure of "passing" — being perceived as one's gender identity by others — which can create intense focus on achieving specific body characteristics.

## Treatment: The Need for Affirming Care

The eating disorder treatment system remains poorly equipped to serve LGBTQ+ populations. Most treatment programs were designed with cisgender, heterosexual women as the default patient, and have not adapted their clinical approaches, treatment environments, or staff training to serve the full diversity of people who experience eating disorders.

Specific gaps include:

**Language and intake processes** that assume heterosexuality and cisgender identity — using gendered language, collecting sex data without collecting gender data, and housing patients by sex assigned at birth rather than gender identity.

**Treatment content** that focuses exclusively on thin-ideal internalization and heteronormative body image without addressing muscularity ideals, gender dysphoria, minority stress, or the specific body image concerns of LGBTQ+ populations.

**Staff training.** Clinicians who lack training in LGBTQ+ cultural competency may inadvertently create hostile or invalidating treatment environments — misgendering transgender patients, dismissing the role of minority stress, or pathologizing sexual orientation.

**Lack of affirming community.** LGBTQ+ patients in treatment programs that are predominantly cisgender and heterosexual may feel isolated, invisible, and unable to discuss the identity-related dimensions of their eating disorder.

Affirming eating disorder treatment should include: routine screening for eating disorders in LGBTQ+ healthcare settings, assessment of minority stress and gender dysphoria as factors in eating disorder development, treatment approaches that address LGBTQ+-specific body image concerns, staff training in cultural humility and affirming practices, inclusive intake processes and physical environments, and connection to LGBTQ+-affirming support communities.

> 💡 **Spark:** An eating disorder in an LGBTQ+ person is not just about food and body. It is about food, body, identity, safety, belonging, and the accumulated weight of living in a world that has not yet decided to fully accept you. Treatment that does not see all of these dimensions will not reach the root of the suffering.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are LGBTQ+ and struggling with eating or body image,** know that your experience is documented, recognized, and treatable. You are not alone, and you deserve care that affirms all of who you are.

- **Seek affirming treatment.** The National Alliance for Eating Disorders can help connect you with LGBTQ+-affirming providers. When evaluating a treatment provider, ask specifically about their experience with LGBTQ+ patients and their approach to identity-related body image concerns.

- **If you are a clinician,** screen for eating disorders in your LGBTQ+ patients. Use inclusive language. Ask about minority stress. Recognize that body image concerns in LGBTQ+ populations may not map onto the standard thin-ideal framework.

- **Connect with community.** LGBTQ+-specific eating disorder support groups and peer support networks provide a space where identity-related dimensions of the eating disorder can be openly discussed.
