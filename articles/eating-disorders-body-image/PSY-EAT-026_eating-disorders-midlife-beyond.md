> **Summary:** The stereotype of eating disorders as conditions that affect only teenagers and young adults is one of the most persistent misconceptions in mental health — and one of the most clinically damaging. Eating disorders can develop at any age, and a significant number of adults in their 40s, 50s, 60s, and beyond live with clinical eating disorders that are undiagnosed, untreated, and unrecognized. Some have struggled since adolescence. Others develop eating disorders for the first time in midlife, triggered by life transitions such as divorce, menopause, bereavement, retirement, medical illness, or the loss of youth-associated identity. This article examines the evidence on eating disorders in midlife and older adulthood, the unique risk factors and clinical presentations in these age groups, the barriers to diagnosis, and what appropriate care looks like.

**Key Facts:**
1. Research by Mangweth-Matzek and colleagues found that 3.8% of women over 60 met criteria for a clinical eating disorder, with an additional 4.4% exhibiting subclinical disordered eating [1].
2. Approximately 13% of women over 50 report engaging in eating disorder behaviors including restriction, purging, and binge eating [2].
3. Eating disorders in older adults carry higher mortality risk due to reduced physiological resilience, comorbid medical conditions, and delayed detection [3].
4. Life transitions — menopause, divorce, bereavement, retirement, empty nest, medical diagnosis — are frequently identified as triggers for eating disorder onset or relapse in midlife [4].
5. Older adults with eating disorders are significantly less likely to be screened, diagnosed, or referred to specialized treatment compared to younger adults with identical presentations [5].

## The Invisible Population

Eating disorders in midlife and older adulthood exist in a diagnostic blind spot. Clinicians are trained to look for eating disorders in adolescents and young adults. Screening tools are validated on younger populations. Awareness campaigns feature young faces. Treatment programs are designed for and marketed to younger demographics. The result is a population of older adults whose eating disorders go unseen — by themselves, by their families, and by the healthcare system.

Research tells a different story from the stereotype. A landmark study by Mangweth-Matzek and colleagues (2014) assessed eating disorders in community-dwelling women over 60 and found that 3.8% met full diagnostic criteria for an eating disorder, with an additional 4.4% meeting criteria for subclinical eating disturbance [1]. When you consider that the global population of women over 60 exceeds 600 million, these percentages represent tens of millions of people.

The Gender and Body Image (GABI) study by Midlarsky and Nitzburg (2008) found that body dissatisfaction remains high throughout the lifespan — contrary to the assumption that body image concerns naturally diminish with age [6]. Women in their 60s reported levels of body dissatisfaction comparable to women in their 20s, though the specific content of the dissatisfaction shifted: younger women focused on being thinner, while older women focused on both weight and the visible effects of aging (wrinkles, sagging, loss of firmness).

For men, the data is even more sparse, but emerging research suggests that eating disorders in older men are significantly underrecognized. The combination of age-related stigma, gender-based barriers to eating disorder recognition (as discussed in PSY-EAT-023), and the assumption that eating disorders simply do not occur in older men creates a triply invisible population.

## Two Pathways: Chronic and New-Onset

Eating disorders in midlife and older adulthood follow two distinct pathways, each with different clinical implications.

**Chronic eating disorders** that have persisted since adolescence or young adulthood into midlife and beyond. Some individuals with eating disorders never fully recover and carry the condition — actively or in partial remission — for decades. Research suggests that approximately 20–30% of individuals with anorexia nervosa develop a chronic course [7]. These individuals face the cumulative medical consequences of prolonged eating disorders (osteoporosis, cardiac damage, nutritional deficiency, dental loss) compounded by the normal physiological changes of aging. Their eating disorders may have become so deeply embedded in their routines and identities that they — and those around them — have stopped seeing the behaviors as pathological.

**Late-onset eating disorders** that develop for the first time in midlife or later life, typically in response to life transitions or stressors. These new-onset presentations challenge the assumption that eating disorders are conditions of youth. Research by Scholtz and colleagues (2010) documented that approximately 25% of women presenting to eating disorder services over age 40 reported first onset of their eating disorder after age 40 [4].

The triggers for late-onset eating disorders frequently involve loss or transition:
- **Menopause** — hormonal changes that alter body composition, mood, and metabolism
- **Divorce or relationship dissolution** — loss of relational identity, potential return to dating
- **Bereavement** — loss of a partner, parent, or close friend
- **Retirement** — loss of professional identity and daily structure
- **Empty nest** — children leaving home, loss of parenting role
- **Medical illness** — loss of physical capability, confrontation with mortality
- **Aging-related body changes** — cultural messages that aging bodies are undesirable

These transitions share a common psychological thread: the disruption of identity and the experience of loss of control. When the anchors of identity shift — when a person is no longer "the mother of young children" or "the professional at work" or "the spouse in a partnership" — the body can become the remaining domain in which control and self-definition feel possible. The same psychological dynamic that drives eating disorders in adolescents (using food and body control as a response to overwhelming life changes) applies in midlife, with different triggering events.

## Why Older Adults Are Missed

The barriers to eating disorder identification in older adults are systematic and multilayered.

**Age-based clinical assumptions.** Healthcare providers often do not screen for eating disorders in older patients because they do not expect to find them. Weight loss in an older adult may be attributed to medical illness, medication side effects, depression, or "natural" age-related changes without exploring the possibility of an eating disorder. Research by Lapid and colleagues (2010) found that clinicians were significantly less likely to include eating disorders in their differential diagnosis for older patients presenting with weight loss, even when the clinical vignette was identical to one for a younger patient [5].

**Overlap with medical conditions.** Many symptoms of eating disorders in older adults overlap with symptoms of common medical conditions: weight loss (cancer, thyroid disease, diabetes), decreased appetite (depression, medication effects), gastrointestinal symptoms (gastroparesis, inflammatory bowel disease), and fatigue (anemia, heart failure). This overlap makes it easy for the eating disorder to hide behind a medical explanation — particularly when clinicians are not thinking about eating disorders in this population.

**Social isolation.** Older adults who live alone — which becomes increasingly common after bereavement, divorce, or children leaving home — may have no one who observes their eating behavior closely enough to notice patterns of restriction, bingeing, or food avoidance. The eating disorder exists in private, without witnesses.

**Shame and generational stigma.** Older adults may carry additional shame about having a condition they perceive as "a young person's problem." The cultural narrative that eating disorders affect teenage girls can make a 65-year-old woman feel that her condition is illegitimate, embarrassing, or too trivial to warrant professional help.

**Treatment system gaps.** Many eating disorder treatment programs — particularly residential and intensive outpatient programs — are designed for adolescents and young adults. Older adults may feel out of place in these programs, and program staff may lack training in the medical complexities of treating eating disorders in the context of age-related comorbidities.

## Medical Risks Are Amplified

Eating disorders in older adults carry higher medical risk than identical conditions in younger individuals, because:

**Reduced physiological reserve.** The body's capacity to compensate for nutritional deficiency decreases with age. Cardiac function, renal function, hepatic function, and bone density are all already in age-related decline — malnutrition accelerates these declines and reduces the margin for safe recovery.

**Medication interactions.** Older adults are more likely to take multiple medications. The electrolyte disturbances produced by eating disorders (particularly purging) can interact dangerously with cardiac medications, diuretics, and other commonly prescribed drugs.

**Bone fragility.** Osteoporosis is already more prevalent in older adults. An eating disorder that further depletes bone density in someone with age-related bone loss can produce devastating fracture risk — including hip fractures, which carry significant mortality in older populations [8].

**Cognitive effects.** Malnutrition in older adults can produce cognitive impairment that may be mistakenly attributed to early dementia or normal age-related cognitive decline. Adequate nutrition is essential for cognitive maintenance in aging, and an eating disorder that compromises nutritional status may hasten cognitive deterioration.

**Refeeding risk.** Older adults are at higher risk for refeeding syndrome when nutrition is restored after prolonged restriction, due to reduced cardiac reserve and altered electrolyte handling. Refeeding must be managed with particular caution in this population [3].

## Treatment: Adapting Evidence-Based Approaches

The evidence base for eating disorder treatment in older adults is limited compared to younger populations, but clinical experience and preliminary research suggest that standard evidence-based treatments can be effectively adapted.

**CBT-E** remains appropriate for older adults with bulimia, BED, and anorexia, with adaptations that address the specific cognitive themes relevant to aging — including grief, identity transition, health anxiety, and aging-related body image concerns [9].

**Medical management** requires close collaboration between the eating disorder treatment team and the patient's primary care physician, cardiologist, endocrinologist, or other specialists managing age-related conditions. Medication dosing for eating disorders (e.g., SSRIs for bulimia or BED) may need adjustment for altered drug metabolism in older adults.

**Nutritional rehabilitation** should be tailored to the specific nutritional needs of aging — including attention to calcium, vitamin D, protein adequacy, and micronutrients that are particularly important for maintenance of bone, muscle, and cognitive function in later life.

**Addressing isolation.** Many older adults with eating disorders lack the social support networks that facilitate recovery. Treatment may need to include connecting the patient with community resources, support groups, or peer support. Online support groups can be particularly valuable for older adults who may have mobility limitations or live in areas without local eating disorder services.

**IPT (Interpersonal Psychotherapy)** may be particularly well-suited to older adults with eating disorders, given the prominence of interpersonal loss, role transition, and relational grief in late-onset presentations [10].

> 💡 **Spark:** An eating disorder does not expire. It does not age out, grow out, or become irrelevant because the person carrying it has gray hair. Every person, at every age, who is suffering from a disordered relationship with food and body deserves to be seen, assessed, and offered effective care.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are experiencing eating or body image distress in midlife or beyond,** know that your experience is clinically recognized and treatable. You are not "too old" for an eating disorder, and you are not "too old" for treatment.

- **Ask for screening.** If your doctor has not asked about your relationship with food, initiate the conversation. "I've been struggling with eating and I'd like to be assessed" is a direct and effective way to begin.

- **If you are a healthcare provider,** screen for eating disorders in older patients who present with unexplained weight loss, nutritional deficiency, or body image distress — regardless of their age.

- **Connect with support.** The National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235) supports people of all ages.

- **Use Psychage's Mood Journal** at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) to track patterns between life transitions, emotional state, and eating behavior.
