> **Summary:** Eating disorders are serious psychiatric conditions that affect an estimated 9% of the global population across every demographic — every age, gender, ethnicity, body size, and socioeconomic background. Far from lifestyle choices or phases of vanity, these conditions involve profound disruptions in neurobiology, emotional regulation, and cognitive processing. They carry the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition, yet remain among the most misunderstood. This article explores what eating disorders actually are, who they affect, the forces that drive them, and why the stereotypes surrounding them cause real harm to real people seeking help.

**Key Facts:**
1. Eating disorders affect approximately 9% of the global population — roughly 720 million people — making them far more prevalent than most people assume [1].
2. Anorexia nervosa has the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder, with a standardized mortality ratio estimated between 5.2 and 5.86 [2].
3. Fewer than 6% of people diagnosed with eating disorders are medically classified as "underweight," dismantling the myth that eating disorders have a visible appearance [3].
4. Genetic factors account for 50–80% of the risk for developing an eating disorder, according to twin studies and genome-wide association analyses [4].
5. The average delay between eating disorder onset and treatment is 5–7 years, partly driven by stereotypes about who "looks like" they have an eating disorder [5].

## What the Diagnostic Landscape Actually Looks Like

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision (DSM-5-TR) recognizes several distinct feeding and eating disorders, each with its own clinical profile. These include anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder (BED), avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder (ARFID), pica, rumination disorder, and other specified feeding or eating disorders (OSFED) [6]. What unites them is not a single behavior but a shared disruption in the relationship between a person, food, and their body — a disruption that causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

Anorexia nervosa involves persistent restriction of energy intake leading to significantly low body weight, accompanied by intense fear of weight gain or persistent behavior that interferes with weight gain, and a disturbance in how one's body weight or shape is experienced [6]. Bulimia nervosa involves recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors — such as self-induced vomiting, laxative misuse, fasting, or excessive exercise — with self-evaluation unduly influenced by body shape and weight [6]. Binge eating disorder, the most prevalent eating disorder worldwide, involves recurrent binge eating episodes marked by a sense of loss of control, without regular compensatory behaviors [7].

But here is where the stereotypes begin to fracture: ARFID involves significant restriction or avoidance of food that is not driven by body image concerns at all. It may stem from sensory sensitivities, fear of aversive consequences of eating (such as choking or vomiting), or apparent lack of interest in food [6]. Pica involves the persistent eating of non-nutritive substances. Rumination disorder involves the repeated regurgitation of food. These conditions challenge the reductive narrative that eating disorders are fundamentally about wanting to be thin.

OSFED — previously called "eating disorder not otherwise specified" or EDNOS — captures presentations that cause significant distress and impairment but do not meet the full criteria for another specific diagnosis. Research by Thomas, Vartanian, and Brownell (2009) found that OSFED is actually the most commonly diagnosed eating disorder in many clinical settings, yet it receives a fraction of the research attention and public awareness [8]. This diagnostic category alone illustrates how much the public conversation about eating disorders misses.

## The Biology That Stereotypes Ignore

One of the most damaging misconceptions about eating disorders is that they are choices — acts of vanity, bids for attention, or failures of willpower. Decades of neuroscience research have thoroughly dismantled this idea.

Twin studies conducted by Bulik and colleagues at the University of North Carolina have consistently demonstrated that genetic heritability accounts for 50–80% of the risk for anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa [4]. The Psychiatric Genomics Consortium's landmark 2019 genome-wide association study of anorexia nervosa identified eight significant genetic loci and found genetic correlations with other psychiatric disorders including obsessive-compulsive disorder and major depression, as well as metabolic traits including glycemic regulation and lipid metabolism [9]. This finding was groundbreaking: it revealed that anorexia nervosa is not purely a psychiatric condition but a metabo-psychiatric one, with biological roots in both brain function and metabolic processing.

Neuroimaging studies have shown that individuals with eating disorders display altered activity in brain regions responsible for reward processing, interoception (the ability to sense internal body signals like hunger and fullness), and executive function. Dr. Walter Kaye's research at the University of California, San Diego, has demonstrated that individuals with anorexia nervosa show heightened activity in the dorsal striatum — a brain area involved in habit formation — and reduced responsiveness in reward circuits when processing food-related stimuli [10]. In simpler terms, the brain of someone with anorexia may experience restriction as neurologically rewarding in ways that are not under conscious control.

For binge eating disorder and bulimia nervosa, research points to dysregulation in serotonin and dopamine pathways that influence impulse control and reward sensitivity. The neurobiological profile differs across eating disorder types, but the throughline is consistent: these are conditions with measurable, observable brain-based differences, not character flaws.

## Who Actually Gets Eating Disorders (Everyone)

The stereotypical image of a person with an eating disorder — a young, affluent, thin, white woman — is not just incomplete. It is actively dangerous. It prevents recognition, delays diagnosis, and denies treatment to millions of people who do not match the prototype.

Research published in the International Journal of Eating Disorders has consistently documented that eating disorders occur across all genders. A 2019 study by Nagata and colleagues found that approximately one in three people with eating disorders is male, yet men are significantly less likely to seek treatment and clinicians are less likely to screen for eating disorders in male patients [11]. Muscle dysmorphia — a condition involving preoccupation with perceived insufficient muscularity — is increasingly recognized as a male-predominant eating disorder variant that often goes undiagnosed because it doesn't fit traditional screening tools designed around restriction and thinness.

Age is another shattered stereotype. While onset commonly occurs during adolescence, eating disorders are diagnosed in children as young as five and in adults well into their seventies and eighties. A 2012 study by Mangweth-Matzek and colleagues in the International Journal of Eating Disorders found that 3.8% of women over 60 met criteria for a clinical eating disorder, with an additional 4.4% exhibiting subclinical disordered eating [12]. Life transitions — retirement, bereavement, medical illness, menopause — can trigger or reactivate eating disorders at any stage of life.

The racial and ethnic demographics are equally misrepresented. Research by Marques and colleagues (2011) demonstrated that eating disorders are at least as prevalent in Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous communities as in white populations, yet people of color are significantly less likely to be asked about eating behaviors by healthcare providers and less likely to receive specialist referrals [3]. The assumption that eating disorders are a "white woman's problem" is not just incorrect — it functions as a barrier to care for communities that are already underserved.

Eating disorders also occur across all body sizes. Atypical anorexia nervosa — in which a person meets all criteria for anorexia nervosa except being at a low weight — can be equally or even more medically dangerous than its "typical" presentation. A 2019 study by Sawyer and colleagues found that individuals with atypical anorexia experienced comparable rates of medical complications including bradycardia, orthostatic instability, and electrolyte disturbances [3]. When clinicians rely on visual assessment or weight-based thresholds to screen for eating disorders, they systematically miss these patients.

## Why the Stereotypes Cause Concrete Harm

The harm caused by eating disorder stereotypes is not abstract. It is measurable in delayed diagnoses, denied insurance claims, and deaths that could have been prevented.

The average gap between symptom onset and first treatment for eating disorders is estimated at 5–7 years [5]. For men, people of color, individuals in larger bodies, and older adults, that gap is often longer. When someone's internal experience — the terror around food, the compulsive exercise, the sense of losing control — does not match the cultural image of what an eating disorder "looks like," they are less likely to recognize their own condition, less likely to be identified by clinicians, and less likely to be taken seriously when they do seek help.

Insurance and healthcare systems compound the problem. Many treatment programs still use weight-based criteria to determine the "severity" of eating disorders, a practice that the Academy for Eating Disorders has publicly criticized as clinically unsupported and discriminatory [5]. A person in a larger body who is losing weight rapidly through severe restriction may be congratulated by their doctor rather than screened for an eating disorder. This is not a hypothetical scenario — it is documented repeatedly in clinical literature and patient advocacy reports.

The consequences of untreated eating disorders are severe. Beyond the psychiatric toll — depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, social isolation — the medical complications include cardiac arrhythmias, electrolyte imbalances that can cause sudden death, kidney failure, osteoporosis, gastrointestinal damage, and infertility. Eating disorders do not resolve on their own. They are progressive conditions that, without intervention, tend to become more entrenched and more dangerous over time.

> 💡 **Spark:** An eating disorder is not something you can see. It is not a body type, a demographic, or a lifestyle choice. It is a psychiatric condition with neurobiological roots — and the most important step toward better outcomes is dismantling the stereotypes that prevent people from being seen, believed, and treated.

## What You Can Try

If any part of this article resonated with your own experience, here are concrete next steps:

- **Take a self-assessment.** The SCOFF questionnaire is a validated five-question screening tool used by clinicians worldwide. It is not a diagnosis, but it can help you identify patterns worth exploring with a professional. Psychage offers guided self-assessments at [psychage.com/tools/clarity-score](psychage.com/tools/clarity-score).

- **Name what you are experiencing — to yourself first.** Many people with eating disorders spend years describing their behaviors in euphemisms: "I'm just being healthy," "I'm just not hungry," "I just ate too much." Naming the pattern — restriction, bingeing, purging, food fear — without judgment can be a powerful first act of self-recognition.

- **Talk to a professional who specializes in eating disorders.** General therapists and primary care physicians may lack training in eating disorder identification and treatment. The Academy for Eating Disorders (aedweb.org) and the National Alliance for Eating Disorders maintain directories of specialized providers.

- **Challenge one stereotype this week.** If you find yourself thinking eating disorders only affect certain people, revisit the evidence. Share what you have learned. Stereotypes lose power when they are named and contradicted.

- **Use Psychage's mood tracking tools** to begin noticing connections between your emotions and your eating patterns. The Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) can help make those patterns visible over time.
