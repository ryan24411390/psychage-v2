> **Summary:** Diagnosing an eating disorder is not as simple as checking a weight chart or observing a single behavior. It requires a comprehensive assessment that integrates psychological evaluation, medical examination, nutritional analysis, and structured clinical tools. Yet despite the availability of validated screening instruments and diagnostic criteria, eating disorders remain among the most underdiagnosed psychiatric conditions — with average delays of 5–7 years between symptom onset and first treatment. This article examines how eating disorders are actually diagnosed, the screening tools that clinicians use, the barriers that prevent timely identification, and what individuals can do to advocate for proper assessment.

**Key Facts:**
1. The Eating Disorder Examination (EDE), a structured clinical interview, is considered the gold standard for eating disorder diagnosis in research and clinical settings [1].
2. The SCOFF questionnaire — a five-question screening tool — has sensitivity of 72–100% and specificity of 73–94% for detecting eating disorders in primary care settings [2].
3. The average delay between eating disorder onset and first treatment is estimated at 5–7 years across diagnoses [3].
4. Primary care physicians correctly identify eating disorders in clinical vignettes only approximately 40% of the time, suggesting significant gaps in clinical training [4].
5. Eating disorder screening is recommended by the American Academy of Pediatrics at every adolescent well-visit, yet implementation rates remain low [5].

## The Diagnostic Framework: DSM-5-TR Criteria

Eating disorder diagnosis in most clinical settings is guided by the diagnostic criteria established in the DSM-5-TR (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition, Text Revision), published by the American Psychiatric Association [6]. Internationally, the ICD-11 (International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision), published by the World Health Organization, provides a parallel diagnostic framework that is broadly consistent with the DSM-5-TR but differs in some specifics.

The DSM-5-TR recognizes several feeding and eating disorders, each defined by a specific constellation of behavioral, psychological, and sometimes physiological criteria. What these criteria share is the requirement that the eating disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in functioning — a threshold that distinguishes clinical eating disorders from subclinical or normative eating concerns.

However, diagnostic criteria are clinical endpoints, not screening mechanisms. A person does not walk into a doctor's office and announce which DSM-5-TR criteria they meet. The path from symptom to diagnosis requires clinical assessment — and this is where the process often breaks down.

## The Comprehensive Assessment: What It Should Include

A thorough eating disorder assessment, as recommended by the Academy for Eating Disorders and the NICE guidelines for eating disorders, should include multiple components [7]:

**Psychological assessment.** This is the core of eating disorder diagnosis. It involves detailed exploration of the person's relationship with food, eating behaviors, body image, weight history, compensatory behaviors, and the emotional and cognitive context of their eating patterns. A skilled clinician will assess not just what behaviors are present but what psychological functions those behaviors serve — whether restriction is driven by body image concerns or sensory aversion (distinguishing anorexia from ARFID), whether binge episodes involve true loss of control or subjective loss of control, and whether compensatory behaviors are present.

**Medical assessment.** Because eating disorders carry serious physical consequences, medical evaluation is a critical component of diagnosis. This typically includes vital signs (heart rate, blood pressure, orthostatic measurements), blood work (complete metabolic panel including electrolytes, liver function, thyroid function, complete blood count), assessment of menstrual function, bone density evaluation (for individuals with prolonged restriction), and cardiac evaluation if indicated. The medical assessment serves both diagnostic and safety purposes — some eating disorder presentations constitute medical emergencies requiring immediate stabilization.

**Nutritional assessment.** A registered dietitian with eating disorder expertise can evaluate the adequacy and patterns of the individual's nutritional intake, identify deficiencies, assess behaviors around food preparation and consumption, and provide a nutritional baseline against which progress can be measured.

**Differential diagnosis.** Clinicians must rule out medical conditions that can produce eating disorder-like symptoms — including gastrointestinal conditions (celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, gastroparesis), endocrine disorders (hyperthyroidism, Addison's disease), neurological conditions, and other psychiatric conditions that may include appetite changes (major depression, anxiety disorders).

**Functional assessment.** Understanding how the eating disturbance affects the person's daily life — relationships, work or school performance, social participation, physical functioning — helps establish clinical significance and guides treatment intensity decisions.

## Structured Clinical Instruments: The Tools of Diagnosis

Clinical assessment of eating disorders benefits from structured instruments that provide standardized measurement and reduce the influence of clinician bias. Several validated tools are used across research and clinical settings:

**The Eating Disorder Examination (EDE).** Developed by Christopher Fairburn and Zafra Cooper, the EDE is a structured clinical interview that assesses the key behavioral and psychological features of eating disorders over the preceding 28 days [1]. It generates subscale scores for dietary restraint, eating concern, shape concern, and weight concern, along with diagnostic outputs. The EDE is considered the gold standard for eating disorder assessment in research settings, though its administration time (approximately 45–60 minutes) limits its use in some clinical contexts.

**The Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q).** A self-report version of the EDE, the EDE-Q can be completed by patients independently in approximately 15 minutes [8]. While it does not replace the clinical interview, it serves as an efficient screening and monitoring tool. Research by Mond and colleagues (2004) established that the EDE-Q has good sensitivity and specificity for identifying clinically significant eating disorder symptoms.

**The SCOFF Questionnaire.** Developed by Morgan, Reid, and Lacey (1999), the SCOFF is a five-question screening tool designed for use in primary care and non-specialist settings [2]. The questions address core features of eating disorders: Do you make yourself **S**ick because you feel uncomfortably full? Do you worry you have lost **C**ontrol over how much you eat? Have you recently lost more than **O**ne stone (14 pounds/6.4 kg) in a three-month period? Do you believe yourself to be **F**at when others say you are too thin? Would you say that **F**ood dominates your life? A score of 2 or more "yes" responses warrants further assessment. The SCOFF's brevity makes it practical for routine screening, though it is more sensitive for anorexia and bulimia than for binge eating disorder or ARFID.

**The Eating Attitudes Test (EAT-26).** Developed by Garner and colleagues (1982), the EAT-26 is a self-report questionnaire widely used in screening programs and research studies [9]. It assesses attitudes toward food, body image concerns, and behavioral indicators of eating disorders. A score of 20 or above suggests the need for further clinical evaluation.

**The Nine Item ARFID Screen (NIAS).** Developed by Zickgraf and Ellis (2018), the NIAS specifically screens for the three ARFID presentation profiles — sensory-based avoidance, fear of aversive consequences, and lack of interest in eating [10]. This tool addresses a significant gap in screening, as most traditional eating disorder instruments do not capture ARFID presentations because they are focused on body image-driven pathology.

## Why Eating Disorders Are Still Missed

Despite the availability of these validated instruments, eating disorders continue to be dramatically underdiagnosed. Understanding the barriers to timely identification is essential for improving detection.

**Weight-based assumptions.** Perhaps the most pervasive barrier is the assumption that eating disorders are visible — that they produce a characteristic body appearance, usually extreme thinness. This assumption causes clinicians to overlook eating disorders in individuals whose bodies do not match the stereotype. A person in a larger body with severe restriction may receive praise for weight loss rather than screening for anorexia. A person with binge eating disorder may be counseled to diet rather than assessed for a psychiatric condition.

**Clinician training gaps.** Research by Currin, Schmidt, and Waller (2007) found that primary care physicians identified eating disorders in clinical vignettes only about 40% of the time [4]. Medical training typically devotes minimal time to eating disorder identification and assessment. A survey of medical schools in the United States found that the average curriculum included fewer than 2 hours of eating disorder-specific training across the entire program.

**Patient concealment and shame.** Eating disorders are associated with profound shame, and many individuals actively conceal their symptoms. Binge-purge behaviors occur in private. Restriction may be masked by claims of food allergies, dietary preferences, or busy schedules. Individuals may present for medical consequences (dental erosion, electrolyte abnormalities, amenorrhea) without disclosing the eating behaviors that produced them.

**Demographic blind spots.** Screening practices are often concentrated on populations that match the eating disorder stereotype — young white women — while men, older adults, people of color, individuals in larger bodies, and people with disabilities are systematically under-screened. Research by Marques and colleagues (2011) demonstrated that people of color were significantly less likely to be asked about eating behaviors by healthcare providers, despite comparable prevalence rates [11].

**Normalization of disordered eating.** In cultures where dieting, fasting, cleansing, and exercise-based weight control are normalized — sometimes celebrated — early eating disorder symptoms may be indistinguishable from behaviors that are socially encouraged. The line between culturally normative dieting and clinical restriction can be genuinely difficult to draw, which contributes to both patient and clinician failure to recognize the problem.

## Screening as Routine Practice: What Guidelines Recommend

Leading clinical organizations have increasingly called for routine eating disorder screening, particularly in high-risk populations:

The **American Academy of Pediatrics** recommends eating disorder screening at every adolescent well-visit, using validated instruments or targeted clinical questions [5]. The recommendation reflects evidence that early identification and intervention significantly improve outcomes.

**NICE guidelines** (National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) for eating disorders recommend that healthcare professionals in primary care, mental health, and pediatric settings should be alert to eating disorder symptoms in at-risk populations and should use screening questions when concerns arise [7].

The **Academy for Eating Disorders** has published guidelines for medical practitioners emphasizing that eating disorder screening should not be limited to patients who appear underweight, and that rapid weight change — in any direction — warrants inquiry about eating behaviors [3].

Implementation of these recommendations remains inconsistent. Barriers include time constraints in clinical appointments, lack of reimbursement for screening, insufficient clinician training, and the absence of standardized screening protocols in many healthcare systems.

## Self-Assessment: What Individuals Can Do

While self-screening is not a substitute for clinical assessment, it can be a valuable step in recognizing patterns that warrant professional evaluation.

The SCOFF questionnaire can be self-administered. The EAT-26 is freely available online for self-screening. These tools are not diagnostic — they identify risk, not definitive diagnoses. But for individuals who may not yet recognize their eating patterns as potentially clinical, a screening tool can provide the cognitive reframing that prompts help-seeking.

Equally important is paying attention to functional impact. If your relationship with food causes significant distress, interferes with your ability to eat socially, limits your daily activities, occupies a disproportionate amount of your mental energy, or has produced physical symptoms — these are signals that professional assessment is warranted, regardless of your weight or what you believe an eating disorder "should" look like.

> 💡 **Spark:** The most important screening tool for an eating disorder is not a questionnaire — it is the willingness to ask the question. If your relationship with food feels like a source of suffering rather than sustenance, that experience deserves clinical attention. Not because of what you weigh. Because of what you feel.

## What You Can Try

- **Complete a self-screening.** The SCOFF questionnaire takes 30 seconds. The EAT-26 takes about 5 minutes. Neither is a diagnosis, but both can help you determine if professional assessment is warranted.

- **Use Psychage's Clarity Score** at [psychage.com/tools/clarity-score](psychage.com/tools/clarity-score) as an additional self-assessment tool that can help identify patterns across multiple domains of mental health, including eating and body image.

- **Request screening at your next medical visit.** If your doctor does not routinely ask about eating behaviors, you can initiate the conversation. Saying "I have some concerns about my eating patterns and would like to be screened" is a direct, effective way to begin.

- **Seek an eating disorder specialist for assessment.** If you suspect an eating disorder, a specialist will conduct a more thorough and nuanced assessment than a generalist. The Academy for Eating Disorders (aedweb.org) and the National Alliance for Eating Disorders maintain searchable provider directories.

- **Bring information to your appointment.** If you have completed self-screening tools, kept food or mood journals, or noted specific patterns, bring this information to your clinical assessment. It helps the clinician understand your experience more accurately and reduces the likelihood of important information being missed.
