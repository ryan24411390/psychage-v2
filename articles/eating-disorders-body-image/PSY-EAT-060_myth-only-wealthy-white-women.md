> **Summary:** The belief that eating disorders exclusively or primarily affect affluent white women is one of the most consequential myths in mental health. It is not merely inaccurate — it is a product of research bias, clinical blind spots, and systemic racism in healthcare that has systematically excluded people of color, people from lower socioeconomic backgrounds, and non-Western populations from eating disorder research, diagnosis, and treatment for decades. Epidemiological data consistently demonstrates that eating disorders occur across all racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic groups. In many cases, prevalence rates are equivalent or even higher in communities of color compared to white populations. The disparity is not in who gets eating disorders — it is in who gets diagnosed, who gets referred, who gets treated, and who gets studied. This myth does not describe the epidemiology of eating disorders. It describes the epidemiology of clinical attention.

**Key Facts:**
1. Marques and colleagues (2011) found that eating disorders are at least as prevalent in Black, Hispanic, and Asian American populations as in white populations, yet people of color are significantly less likely to be asked about eating behaviors or referred for specialist treatment [1].
2. A landmark study by Becker and colleagues (2003) documented the rapid emergence of eating pathology in Fiji following the introduction of Western television, demonstrating that eating disorders are not culturally confined to Western societies [2].
3. Food insecurity — which disproportionately affects lower-income communities — is associated with elevated rates of binge eating disorder and bulimia nervosa, directly contradicting the myth that eating disorders require affluence [3].
4. Approximately 80% of published eating disorder research has been conducted on white, female, Western samples, creating a body of evidence that reflects researcher attention rather than population prevalence [4].
5. Black teenagers are 50% more likely to engage in bulimic behaviors than white teenagers, according to data from national surveys, yet Black individuals with eating disorders are less than half as likely to be diagnosed or referred for treatment [5].

## The Myth

The myth constructs a narrow demographic profile of eating disorders: the affected person is young, female, white, thin, and affluent. She lives in a suburban or urban environment, comes from an educated family, and develops her eating disorder in response to pressures from fashion magazines, social media, and the beauty industry. Under this framework, eating disorders are diseases of privilege — products of affluence and whiteness, conditions that require the luxury of caring about appearance in a world where other people have "real problems." The myth implicitly argues that people of color, people experiencing poverty, people from non-Western cultures, and men are too busy surviving or too disconnected from beauty culture to develop eating disorders.

This myth does double harm: it erases the suffering of millions of people who do not match the demographic profile, and it provides a false explanation for eating disorders that reduces them to cultural vanity rather than recognizing their biological, psychological, and environmental complexity.

## Where This Comes From

The origins of this myth can be traced to specific, identifiable biases in the history of eating disorder research and clinical practice.

**Research sampling bias** is the primary driver. When eating disorder research accelerated in the 1970s and 1980s, researchers drew their samples overwhelmingly from university-based treatment centers and private psychiatric hospitals — settings that served predominantly white, affluent, educated populations. The resulting data described the demographics of treatment-seeking, not the demographics of illness. Because white women were overrepresented in treatment settings, they were overrepresented in research samples, which produced findings that appeared to confirm eating disorders as a white, female, affluent phenomenon [4]. This was not a finding — it was a sampling artifact. But it was reported as a finding, cited as a finding, and used to construct diagnostic frameworks, screening practices, and public health messages that treated the demographic profile of the sample as the demographic profile of the disease.

**Clinical referral bias** reinforced the pattern. Becker, Franko, Speck, and Herzog (2003) documented that clinicians are significantly less likely to screen for eating disorders or refer for specialist treatment when the patient is a person of color, even when symptom presentations are identical to those in white patients [6]. This finding has been replicated across multiple studies and clinical settings. The mechanism is cognitive: when clinicians hold an implicit prototype of who gets eating disorders (young, white, female, thin), they are less likely to recognize the condition in patients who do not match that prototype. The result is systematic under-diagnosis in communities of color — which further reduces the representation of people of color in treatment settings and research samples, perpetuating the cycle.

**Cultural narratives about eating disorders** compounded the bias. Media coverage of eating disorders from the 1970s through the 2000s overwhelmingly featured white women. Public awareness campaigns used white female imagery. Celebrity eating disorder disclosures involved white female celebrities. The cultural face of eating disorders became synonymous with white femininity, creating a public perception that was mistaken for epidemiological truth [7].

**Socioeconomic assumptions** added another layer. The idea that eating disorders require affluence rests on the assumption that these conditions are driven primarily by exposure to beauty culture, fashion, and dieting — pursuits associated with leisure and disposable income. This assumption ignores the biological, neurobiological, and psychological drivers of eating disorders (genetics, reward processing alterations, emotional regulation dysfunction, trauma) and the environmental factors that affect low-income populations specifically (food insecurity, weight stigma in healthcare, limited access to mental health services).

## What Research Actually Shows

The evidence that eating disorders cross all demographic boundaries is extensive, consistent, and drawn from national and international datasets.

**Racial and ethnic prevalence data** demonstrates that eating disorders are not white conditions. Marques, Alegria, Becker, Chen, Fang, Chosak, and Diniz (2011), analyzing data from the National Latino and Asian American Study and the National Survey of American Life, found that the lifetime prevalence of eating disorders among racial and ethnic minorities was comparable to — and in some cases exceeded — rates in white populations [1]. Among Black Americans, the prevalence of binge eating disorder was comparable to that in white Americans, and bulimic behaviors were actually more common. Among Hispanic Americans, rates of binge eating were elevated compared to white populations. Among Asian Americans, rates of anorexia nervosa and restrictive eating disorders were significant though less studied.

Striegel-Moore and colleagues (2003), in a study published in the *International Journal of Eating Disorders*, found that Black adolescent girls reported rates of recurrent binge eating and purging that were equivalent to or higher than those reported by white adolescent girls [5]. A critical distinction emerged: Black individuals with equivalent or greater eating disorder pathology were significantly less likely to have been asked about eating behaviors by a healthcare provider and significantly less likely to have received a diagnosis. The disparity was not in the illness — it was in the clinical response to the illness.

**Global prevalence data** further dismantles the Western-only framework. Becker, Burwell, Gilman, Herzog, and Hamburg (2002) conducted landmark research in Fiji, documenting the rapid emergence of disordered eating behaviors among ethnic Fijian girls following the introduction of Western television [2]. Before television exposure, purging for weight control was virtually nonexistent in this population. Within three years of television introduction, 11.3% of participants reported purging behaviors and 29% were at risk for clinical eating disorders. This study demonstrated that eating disorders are not confined to Western societies — but it also revealed that cultural factors (including media exposure) interact with biological vulnerability across all cultural contexts.

Research by Hoek and colleagues (2005) examined eating disorder prevalence in Curacao, a Caribbean nation, and found rates of anorexia nervosa comparable to those documented in Western European countries [8]. Pike and Dunne (2015), reviewing global eating disorder research published in *Current Opinion in Psychiatry*, concluded that eating disorders have been documented on every continent, in both developed and developing nations, and across all major cultural and religious contexts [9]. The conditions are genuinely global.

**Socioeconomic data** contradicts the affluence myth with particular clarity. Becker and colleagues (2009) reviewed the relationship between socioeconomic status and eating disorders and found no consistent association between higher socioeconomic status and elevated eating disorder prevalence [6]. For binge eating disorder specifically, the association runs in the opposite direction: food insecurity — the condition of not having reliable access to sufficient food — is a significant risk factor. Rasmusson, Lydecker, Coffino, White, and Grilo (2019) found that food insecurity was associated with elevated rates of binge eating and bulimic behaviors, independent of other socioeconomic variables [3]. The mechanism is not paradoxical: the experience of food scarcity triggers physiological and psychological responses — including preoccupation with food, loss-of-control eating when food becomes available, and cycles of restriction and overeating — that map directly onto binge eating disorder symptomatology.

This means that the myth does not just fail to describe reality — it describes reality backwards. Some of the highest-risk populations for eating disorders are not the affluent but the food-insecure, not the privileged but the marginalized.

**Research bias documentation** quantifies the extent of the problem. Halmi (2009) and others have noted that approximately 80% of published eating disorder studies have been conducted on predominantly white, Western, female samples [4]. This body of literature does not demonstrate that eating disorders primarily affect white Western women — it demonstrates that eating disorder researchers have primarily studied white Western women. The distinction is critical. When a research field systematically excludes certain populations from study, the resulting evidence base will inevitably underrepresent those populations. The absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

**Treatment access disparities** confirm that the demographic distribution of treatment does not match the demographic distribution of illness. Cachelin, Rebeck, Veisel, and Striegel-Moore (2001) found that Mexican American and African American women with clinically significant eating disorders were less likely to seek treatment, less likely to be referred by healthcare providers, and less likely to receive specialist care compared to white women with equivalent presentations [10]. Gordon, Brattole, Wingate, and Joiner (2006) documented similar disparities, finding that racial and ethnic minorities faced multiple barriers to eating disorder treatment including lack of culturally competent providers, financial barriers, stigma within communities, and the perception — reinforced by the myth — that eating disorders are not relevant to their communities [11].

## Why This Matters

The myth that eating disorders only affect wealthy white women functions as a structural barrier to care for everyone who does not fit that description. Its harms are specific and quantifiable.

For people of color with eating disorders, the myth creates invisibility. When the cultural image of eating disorders is white, a person of color experiencing the same condition may not recognize it as an eating disorder, may not feel that available treatment resources are "for them," and may face family and community members who dismiss their condition as a "white people's problem." This invisibility compounds the already significant barrier of having less access to culturally competent mental health providers.

For individuals from lower socioeconomic backgrounds, the myth implies that their eating problems are not clinical conditions but natural consequences of their circumstances — that binge eating during food insecurity is a survival behavior rather than a psychiatric condition that warrants treatment. This framing denies clinical attention to people who are experiencing genuine eating disorder pathology, often compounded by the physiological and psychological effects of poverty and food scarcity.

For the field of eating disorder research and treatment, the myth perpetuates the sampling biases that created it. When researchers, clinicians, and treatment programs assume a white, affluent patient base, they design studies, screening protocols, and treatment environments that reflect and reinforce that assumption. The result is a self-perpetuating system that continues to underserve the populations it has always underserved.

At the level of public health policy, the myth distorts resource allocation. If eating disorders are perceived as conditions of privilege, they are unlikely to be prioritized in healthcare systems serving low-income or racially diverse communities — precisely the communities where eating disorders are prevalent but under-treated.

## The More Accurate Picture

Eating disorders are psychiatric conditions with biological, psychological, and environmental determinants that operate across all demographics. Genetic vulnerability, neurobiological differences in reward processing and emotional regulation, trauma exposure, sociocultural pressures, and interpersonal stressors do not respect racial, ethnic, or socioeconomic boundaries. The accurate epidemiological picture is one in which eating disorders are globally distributed, racially and ethnically non-discriminating, and present across the full socioeconomic spectrum.

The disparities are real — but they are disparities of diagnosis, treatment, research attention, and healthcare access, not disparities of illness. Reframing the conversation from "who gets eating disorders" to "who gets diagnosed with eating disorders" reveals that the myth is not a description of the disease. It is a description of who the healthcare system was designed to see.

An accurate, equitable approach to eating disorders requires: universal screening regardless of demographic profile; culturally adapted assessment and treatment approaches; intentional diversification of research samples; and active dismantling of the clinical biases that cause providers to see eating disorders in white patients and miss them in everyone else.

> 💡 **Spark:** The myth that eating disorders only affect wealthy white women does not describe who is sick. It describes who has been allowed to be seen as sick, studied as sick, and treated as sick. Every person excluded from that narrow frame — every Black teenager with bulimia, every food-insecure mother with binge eating disorder, every first-generation immigrant son battling restriction — deserves the same recognition, the same diagnosis, and the same chance at recovery.

## What You Can Try

- **Recognize that eating disorders have no demographic boundaries.** If you or someone in your community is experiencing disordered eating, do not dismiss it because it does not match the cultural stereotype. Eating disorders are psychiatric conditions, not demographic conditions.

- **Seek culturally competent providers.** The National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235), the Association of Black Psychologists, the National Latino Behavioral Health Association, and the Asian American Psychological Association can help connect you with providers who understand the intersection of culture, race, and eating disorders.

- **Advocate for universal screening in healthcare settings.** If you are a healthcare provider, screen every patient for eating disorders — not just those who match the demographic prototype. Use validated screening tools and apply them consistently.

- **Support research diversification.** Look for and support organizations that are actively working to include diverse populations in eating disorder research, including the Strategic Training Initiative for the Prevention of Eating Disorders (STRIPED) and the Eating Disorders Coalition.

- **Track your own emotional and behavioral patterns around food.** Psychage's Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) provides a private, non-judgmental space to notice connections between emotions and eating, regardless of your background.

- **Challenge the myth in your own community.** When eating disorders are dismissed as "not our problem" — whether in communities of color, lower-income communities, or male-dominated spaces — push back with the evidence. These conditions affect everyone, and everyone deserves access to care.
