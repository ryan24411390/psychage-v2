> **Summary:** The belief that eating disorders are women's conditions — that men are somehow exempt from developing disordered relationships with food, body, and eating — is contradicted by decades of epidemiological, clinical, and neurobiological evidence. Approximately one in three people with eating disorders is male. Men develop anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, and conditions like muscle dysmorphia that are disproportionately prevalent in male populations. Yet men with eating disorders face a compounded burden: the illness itself, plus a cultural narrative that tells them their condition does not exist. Screening tools designed around female presentations miss male symptoms. Clinicians fail to ask men about disordered eating. Men themselves internalize the myth and resist seeking help for a condition they have been told they cannot have. The result is a population of millions who are ill, invisible, and systematically underserved.

**Key Facts:**
1. Approximately one in three individuals with an eating disorder is male, representing millions of men worldwide — yet men account for only 10% of treatment-seeking populations, indicating massive under-identification and under-treatment [1].
2. Nagata and colleagues (2020) found that male eating disorders are frequently characterized by muscularity-oriented disordered eating — including excessive protein consumption, supplement misuse, and compulsive exercise — presentations that standard screening tools are not designed to detect [2].
3. Muscle dysmorphia, a condition involving preoccupation with perceived insufficient muscularity, affects an estimated 10-15% of regular gym users in some samples and carries significant psychiatric comorbidity including depression, anxiety, and substance use [3].
4. The EAT-26 and SCOFF screening tools — the most widely used eating disorder screening instruments — were developed and validated primarily on female samples and contain items that map poorly to male eating disorder presentations [4].
5. Men with eating disorders experience longer delays before diagnosis, higher rates of misdiagnosis, and lower rates of specialist referral compared to women with equivalent symptom severity [5].

## The Myth

The myth is blunt: eating disorders are women's problems. Men do not develop them. If a man appears preoccupied with food, body, or weight, there must be another explanation — he is just dedicated to fitness, concerned about athletic performance, or going through a temporary period of stress. The myth does not merely claim that eating disorders are less common in men (which is true, though the gap is narrower than most people assume). It claims that men are categorically excluded — that something about maleness confers immunity. Under this framework, a man who restricts food is disciplined. A man who exercises compulsively is committed. A man who obsesses over protein intake and muscle definition is health-conscious. The same behaviors that would trigger clinical concern in a woman are reframed as masculine virtues in a man.

The myth is powerful because it operates at every level: in the man who cannot name his own suffering because he lacks the cultural permission to have this particular illness, in the clinician who does not think to screen a male patient for disordered eating, and in the treatment system that offers programs designed around female experiences and female bodies.

## Where This Comes From

The gendering of eating disorders as female conditions has its roots in both the history of diagnosis and the culture of gender.

Historically, the clinical study of eating disorders focused almost exclusively on women. When Richard Morton described what was likely anorexia nervosa in 1689 and when Sir William Gull formalized the diagnosis in 1874, their published cases were female. Through the 20th century, the clinical literature, the research samples, the treatment populations, and the public narratives about eating disorders were overwhelmingly female. This created a self-reinforcing cycle: eating disorders were studied in women, diagnostic criteria were calibrated to female presentations, screening tools were validated on female samples, and treatment programs were designed for female patients — all of which produced data showing that eating disorders were primarily female conditions, which further discouraged the study, screening, and treatment of men [6].

Cultural constructions of masculinity reinforced the clinical blind spot. Western masculine norms prescribe emotional stoicism, self-reliance, and physical toughness. Acknowledging vulnerability — particularly vulnerability related to body image and food — conflicts with these norms. Eating disorders are culturally coded as feminine, which means that a man who acknowledges having one faces not only the stigma of mental illness but the additional stigma of having a "feminine" illness. This double stigma suppresses help-seeking, self-recognition, and disclosure, keeping male eating disorders hidden from clinical view [7].

The fitness and bodybuilding industries added another layer of concealment. Male disordered eating frequently manifests through muscularity-oriented behaviors — restrictive eating designed to reduce body fat, excessive protein consumption, supplement and steroid use, and compulsive exercise aimed at building muscle. These behaviors are not only normalized in male-dominated fitness culture; they are celebrated. A man who eats according to rigid macronutrient calculations, exercises for hours daily, and uses substances to alter his physique is culturally positioned as aspirational, not ill. The illness hides inside the cultural ideal.

## What Research Actually Shows

The epidemiological, clinical, and neurobiological evidence demonstrates that eating disorders in men are common, serious, and distinct in ways that demand recognition.

**Prevalence data** directly contradicts the "men don't get eating disorders" myth. Hudson, Hiripi, Pope, and Kessler (2007), analyzing data from the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, found that the lifetime prevalence of binge eating disorder was 2.0% in men versus 3.5% in women — a ratio far closer to parity than the cultural stereotype suggests [8]. For bulimia nervosa, the ratio was approximately 0.5% in men versus 1.5% in women. For anorexia nervosa, male prevalence was lower but not negligible: approximately 0.3% versus 0.9% in women. Across all eating disorder diagnoses combined, the widely cited statistic is that approximately one in three affected individuals is male [1]. This means millions of men worldwide are living with eating disorders. The myth erases them.

**Male-specific presentations** explain why standard screening misses men. Nagata, Ganson, and Murray (2020), writing in *Current Opinion in Pediatrics*, identified a distinct male eating disorder phenotype characterized by muscularity-oriented disordered eating (MODE) [2]. This includes excessive exercise aimed at building muscle rather than losing weight, rigid dietary patterns focused on protein and macronutrient ratios rather than caloric restriction, use of anabolic steroids or other appearance and performance enhancing drugs (APEDs), and preoccupation with lean muscularity rather than thinness. These presentations are eating disorders — they involve distorted body image, compulsive behaviors, significant distress, and impaired functioning — but they do not map onto the diagnostic frameworks and screening items designed around female presentations of thinness pursuit.

**Muscle dysmorphia** represents the most prominent male-specific body image condition. First described by Pope, Katz, and Hudson (1993) and later termed "muscle dysmorphia" by Pope, Gruber, Choi, Olivardia, and Phillips (1997), this condition involves a persistent belief that one's body is insufficiently muscular — a conviction that persists despite objective evidence to the contrary [3]. Individuals with muscle dysmorphia engage in compulsive exercise, rigid dietary control, avoidance of situations that expose their body, and significant distress about their perceived inadequacy. The condition is associated with elevated rates of depression, anxiety, social phobia, and substance use disorders. Olivardia, Pope, and Hudson (2000) found that men with muscle dysmorphia reported quality of life impairments comparable to individuals with OCD and anorexia nervosa [9]. Yet because the condition does not involve thinness pursuit, it is frequently unrecognized as an eating and body image disorder.

**Screening tool bias** is a structural barrier to male eating disorder detection. The most widely used eating disorder screening instruments — including the EAT-26 (Eating Attitudes Test) and the SCOFF questionnaire — were developed and initially validated on predominantly or exclusively female samples [4]. Their items are calibrated to detect restriction motivated by weight loss and thinness pursuit: "Do you find yourself preoccupied with a desire to be thinner?" "Do you feel terrified of being overweight?" These questions capture female-typical eating disorder presentations but miss male-typical presentations involving muscularity pursuit, compulsive exercise, and supplement misuse. A man who is severely restricting food to achieve a lean, muscular physique — who is medically compromised and psychologically consumed by his body — may answer "no" to items about wanting to be thinner and therefore screen negative for an eating disorder he clearly has.

Murray, Nagata, Griffiths, Calzo, Brown, Mitchison, and colleagues (2017) developed the Muscularity-Oriented Eating Test (MOET) specifically to address this gap, demonstrating that muscularity-oriented disordered eating is a measurable, valid construct that existing tools fail to capture [10]. The existence of this validated instrument confirms what clinicians and researchers have argued for years: the tools we use to find eating disorders were built to find them in women, and they systematically miss them in men.

**Help-seeking barriers** compound the detection problem. Griffiths, Mond, Murray, and Touyz (2015) examined barriers to treatment-seeking among men with eating disorders and identified several male-specific obstacles: the perception that eating disorders are female conditions (leading to self-exclusion), fear of stigma related to having a "feminine" illness, lack of awareness that their symptoms constitute an eating disorder, and difficulty finding treatment providers experienced in male presentations [7]. Men who do seek treatment frequently report feeling out of place in treatment settings designed around female experiences, with group therapy composed entirely of women, educational materials featuring female examples, and clinical language oriented toward thinness rather than muscularity.

**Mortality and medical severity** in male eating disorders are at least as serious as in female presentations. Research suggests that men with anorexia nervosa may actually have higher mortality rates than women with the condition, partly because their illness is identified later and they present to treatment at more advanced stages of medical compromise [5]. Strother, Lemberg, Stanford, and Turberville (2012), writing in *Eating Disorders*, documented that men with eating disorders are at elevated risk for all the same medical complications as women — cardiac arrhythmias, electrolyte imbalances, bone density loss, endocrine disruption, gastrointestinal damage — plus testosterone suppression and its associated consequences [11].

## Why This Matters

The myth that men do not get eating disorders produces a specific cascade of harm. At the individual level, it denies men the language to name their experience. A man experiencing the terror, compulsion, and despair of an eating disorder may not recognize what is happening to him because everything he has absorbed about eating disorders tells him this is a condition for women. Without language, there is no self-recognition. Without self-recognition, there is no help-seeking. Without help-seeking, there is no treatment.

At the clinical level, the myth produces systematic under-detection. When clinicians — who are trained using textbooks, case studies, and screening tools oriented toward female presentations — encounter a male patient with disordered eating, they are less likely to identify the condition. The male patient who presents with compulsive exercise, rigid dietary patterns, supplement misuse, and body image distress is more likely to receive a diagnosis of depression, anxiety, OCD, or substance use disorder than an eating disorder — even when the eating disorder is the primary condition [5].

At the treatment level, the myth produces systems that are structurally inhospitable to men. Treatment programs designed for women may inadvertently exclude or alienate male patients through gendered language, female-only group compositions, and clinical approaches that do not address male-specific concerns about muscularity, athleticism, and masculine identity.

At the public health level, the myth suppresses research. Male eating disorders remain under-studied relative to female presentations, creating gaps in evidence-based treatment approaches specifically validated for men. This research gap perpetuates the clinical blind spot, which perpetuates the myth.

## The More Accurate Picture

Eating disorders are human conditions, not gendered conditions. They occur in men at rates that, while lower than in women, are far higher than the cultural narrative acknowledges. Male eating disorders often present differently than female eating disorders — with greater emphasis on muscularity, leanness, and athletic performance rather than thinness — but these different presentations are no less serious, no less painful, and no less deserving of clinical attention and effective treatment.

The accurate picture requires expanding diagnostic frameworks to recognize muscularity-oriented disordered eating as a legitimate eating disorder presentation. It requires developing and deploying screening tools that capture male-typical symptoms. It requires training clinicians to screen for eating disorders in male patients with the same diligence they apply to female patients. And it requires creating treatment environments where men feel seen, understood, and welcome.

Recognition is the first step toward treatment, and treatment is the first step toward recovery. Millions of men are currently ill and invisible. The myth that protects their invisibility needs to end.

> 💡 **Spark:** A man who cannot eat a meal without calculating its protein content, who exercises through injury because missing a workout feels intolerable, who avoids social situations where his body might be seen, who uses substances to change his physique — this man has an eating disorder. The fact that his eating disorder does not look like a woman's eating disorder does not make it less real. It makes it less recognized. And that is exactly the problem.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are a man experiencing distress about food, body, exercise, or eating, recognize that eating disorders are not gender-restricted.** Your experience is valid regardless of whether it matches the cultural prototype. The National Alliance for Eating Disorders helpline (1-866-662-1235) supports people of all genders.

- **Examine whether your relationship with exercise, food rules, or supplementation has become compulsive.** Key indicators include: inability to skip workouts without significant distress, rigid food rules that cause anxiety when broken, secretive use of supplements or substances, and body checking behaviors (mirror, scales, measurements) that consume significant time.

- **Seek providers experienced with male eating disorders.** The Academy for Eating Disorders (aedweb.org) and the National Alliance for Eating Disorders can help connect you with clinicians who understand male-specific presentations.

- **Use Psychage's Mood Journal** at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) to track emotional states connected to exercise, body image, and eating patterns. Noticing the connection between distressing emotions and compulsive behaviors is a powerful step toward change.

- **Talk about this with other men.** Silence perpetuates the myth. When men share their experiences with eating and body image distress, they give other men permission to recognize and name their own struggles.

- **If you are a clinician, integrate eating disorder screening into all assessments regardless of patient gender.** Consider using the MOET (Muscularity-Oriented Eating Test) alongside traditional screening tools to capture male-typical presentations.
