> **Summary:** Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), originally developed by Marsha Linehan for borderline personality disorder, has been adapted for eating disorders with a specific focus on binge eating disorder and bulimia nervosa. DBT for eating disorders targets the emotion dysregulation that drives binge-purge cycles — the pattern in which overwhelming emotions trigger binge eating as a maladaptive coping strategy, which then triggers compensatory behaviors driven by shame and distress. By teaching four core skill sets — mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness — DBT equips individuals with alternative ways to manage the emotional states that previously drove them to food. This article explains how DBT is adapted for eating disorders, what the evidence shows, and what distinguishes it from other treatment approaches.

**Key Facts:**
1. DBT was originally developed by Marsha Linehan in the 1990s for borderline personality disorder and was adapted for eating disorders by Debra Safer, Christy Telch, and Eunice Chen [1].
2. The affect regulation model of binge eating — which proposes that binge episodes function as attempts to escape or regulate negative emotional states — is the theoretical foundation for DBT's application to eating disorders [2].
3. Randomized controlled trials show that DBT produces binge abstinence rates of approximately 64% at end of treatment for binge eating disorder, compared to 36% in active comparison conditions [3].
4. DBT's four skill modules — mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, and interpersonal effectiveness — address the specific emotional vulnerabilities that maintain binge-purge cycles [1].
5. DBT is particularly appropriate for individuals with eating disorders who also experience significant emotional dysregulation, self-harm, or comorbid personality pathology [4].

## Why Emotion Regulation Matters for Eating Disorders

The connection between emotions and eating is universal — everyone has eaten for comfort, eaten less when anxious, or reached for food when bored. But for individuals with binge eating disorder and bulimia nervosa, the relationship between emotions and eating has become pathologically entrenched: overwhelming emotions trigger binge episodes with a reliability and intensity that overrides other coping strategies.

The affect regulation model, articulated by Heatherton and Baumeister (1991) and supported by ecological momentary assessment research, proposes that binge eating functions as an escape from aversive self-awareness [2]. When negative emotions — sadness, anxiety, shame, anger, loneliness, boredom — exceed the person's perceived capacity to cope, their attention narrows from abstract self-evaluation to immediate sensory experience. Eating provides the sensory focus that displaces the intolerable emotion. The binge is not about hunger or food preference — it is about emotional escape.

DBT addresses this mechanism directly. Rather than focusing primarily on eating behavior (as CBT-E does) or on interpersonal patterns (as IPT does), DBT targets the emotional dysregulation that makes binge eating feel necessary. The goal is not just to change eating behavior but to build the emotional skills that make disordered eating less functionally necessary [1].

## The Four Skill Modules

DBT for eating disorders teaches four interrelated skill sets, each addressing a different dimension of the emotional vulnerability that drives disordered eating.

### Mindfulness: Observing Without Reacting

Mindfulness is the foundational skill of DBT. It involves the ability to observe internal experiences — thoughts, emotions, physical sensations, urges — without automatically reacting to them. For someone with binge eating disorder, mindfulness creates a space between the emotional trigger and the binge response.

In practice, mindfulness training for eating disorders includes: noticing the urge to binge without acting on it; observing emotions without labeling them as intolerable; bringing non-judgmental awareness to the experience of eating (taste, texture, fullness); and recognizing the difference between physical hunger and emotional hunger [5].

The "wise mind" concept — DBT's term for the integration of emotional experience and rational thinking — is particularly relevant for eating disorder treatment. The emotional mind says "I need to eat right now to feel better." The rational mind says "I shouldn't eat because I'll feel worse." The wise mind integrates both: "I am in pain. I can acknowledge this pain without either numbing it with food or punishing myself for feeling it."

### Distress Tolerance: Surviving the Moment Without Making It Worse

Distress tolerance skills address the moments of acute emotional crisis that typically trigger binge episodes. These are not emotion regulation skills (which aim to change the emotion) — they are survival skills for moments when the emotion cannot be changed and the goal is to get through it without resorting to destructive behavior.

DBT distress tolerance techniques adapted for eating disorders include: the TIPP skills (Temperature — ice water on the face to activate the dive reflex and reduce physiological arousal; Intense exercise — brief intense movement to metabolize stress hormones; Paced breathing; Paired muscle relaxation); self-soothing through the five senses (engaging each sense with something comforting that is not food); the STOP skill (Stop, Take a step back, Observe, Proceed mindfully); and radical acceptance — the practice of accepting reality as it is, without demanding that it be different, which reduces the suffering that resistance to reality produces [6].

### Emotion Regulation: Changing What You Feel

Emotion regulation skills aim to reduce the intensity and frequency of unwanted emotions — not by suppressing them but by understanding and managing them more effectively.

Key emotion regulation skills in DBT for eating disorders include: identifying and labeling emotions accurately (many individuals with eating disorders experience emotions as undifferentiated distress and lack the vocabulary to specify what they are feeling); understanding the function of emotions (emotions provide information — anxiety signals potential threat, sadness signals loss, anger signals boundary violation); reducing emotional vulnerability through the ABC PLEASE skills (Accumulating positive experiences, Building mastery, Coping ahead for difficult situations, treating PhysicaL illness, balanced Eating, avoiding mood-Altering substances, getting adequate Sleep, and Exercising); and opposite action — deliberately acting opposite to the behavioral urge that an emotion produces when the emotion is not justified by the situation [7].

For eating disorders, opposite action is particularly powerful. When shame says "hide and eat in secret," opposite action says "reach out to someone." When anxiety says "restrict to regain control," opposite action says "eat the planned meal." When sadness says "binge to numb the pain," opposite action says "engage in an activity that brings genuine connection."

### Interpersonal Effectiveness: Getting What You Need From Others

Interpersonal effectiveness skills address the relational patterns that contribute to emotional distress and, by extension, to disordered eating. Many individuals with eating disorders struggle with assertiveness, have difficulty asking for what they need, say yes when they want to say no, and prioritize others' needs at the expense of their own — patterns that generate chronic emotional deprivation and frustration.

DBT interpersonal skills include the DEAR MAN technique (Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce, Mindful, Appear confident, Negotiate) for making effective requests; the GIVE skills (Gentle, Interested, Validate, Easy manner) for maintaining relationships; and the FAST skills (Fair, no Apologies, Stick to values, Truthful) for maintaining self-respect in interactions [8].

## The Evidence for DBT in Eating Disorders

The evidence base for DBT in eating disorders has grown substantially since the first adaptation was published.

**Binge eating disorder.** Telch, Agras, and Linehan (2001) conducted the first randomized controlled trial of DBT for BED and found that 89% of participants in the DBT group had stopped binge eating by end of treatment, compared to 12.5% in the waitlist control group. At six-month follow-up, 56% of the DBT group maintained binge abstinence [3].

Safer, Robinson, and Jo (2010) compared DBT to an active comparison therapy and found that DBT produced significantly greater reductions in binge eating frequency and eating disorder psychopathology [9].

**Bulimia nervosa.** Safer, Telch, and Agras (2001) demonstrated that DBT adapted for bulimia nervosa produced significant reductions in binge-purge frequency. Subsequent studies have supported DBT as an effective treatment for bulimia, particularly for individuals with comorbid emotional dysregulation [4].

**Comorbid presentations.** DBT is particularly well-suited for individuals with eating disorders who also experience borderline personality features, chronic self-harm, suicidal ideation, or severe emotional dysregulation — populations for whom CBT-E alone may be insufficient. The comprehensive DBT program (individual therapy, skills group, phone coaching, and consultation team) provides a higher level of support than standard outpatient CBT-E [10].

## DBT vs. CBT-E: Which Is Right?

DBT and CBT-E are not competing treatments — they address different aspects of eating disorder psychopathology and may be more appropriate for different patient profiles.

**CBT-E** is the first-line choice for most individuals with eating disorders, targeting the cognitive and behavioral maintaining mechanisms (over-evaluation of shape and weight, dietary restraint, checking/avoidance). It is most effective when the primary maintaining mechanism is the eating disorder psychopathology itself.

**DBT** is particularly appropriate when: emotional dysregulation is the primary driver of binge eating or purging; the individual has not responded to CBT-E; there is significant comorbid personality pathology or chronic suicidality; or the individual identifies emotional triggers as central to their eating disorder experience.

In clinical practice, elements of both approaches are sometimes integrated. A therapist might use CBT-E's approach to dietary restraint and regular eating while incorporating DBT skills training for emotion regulation. This integration is clinically pragmatic, though not yet extensively tested in formal trials.

> 💡 **Spark:** Binge eating is not about the food. It is about the feeling that came before the food — the feeling that felt so overwhelming that disappearing into eating seemed like the only option. DBT teaches that there are other options. Not easy options. But real ones.

## What You Can Try

- **Learn to label your emotions.** Before, during, and after urges to binge, ask: "What am I actually feeling right now?" Developing emotional vocabulary is the first step in DBT's approach.

- **Try the TIPP skills** during moments of acute distress: splash cold water on your face, do 60 seconds of intense movement, practice paced breathing. These physiological interventions can reduce the intensity of the urge.

- **Practice the "urge surfing" technique.** When the urge to binge arises, set a timer for 15 minutes and observe the urge without acting on it. Notice its intensity, its physical location in your body, and its natural rise and fall. Many urges peak and subside within 15–20 minutes if not acted upon.

- **Seek a DBT-trained therapist** if emotional dysregulation is central to your eating disorder. Ask specifically about DBT training and experience with eating disorders.
