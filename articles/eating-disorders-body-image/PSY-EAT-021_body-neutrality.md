> **Summary:** Body positivity has been a powerful cultural movement, challenging the idea that only certain bodies are worthy of visibility, love, and respect. But for many people — particularly those in active recovery from eating disorders, body dysmorphic disorder, or chronic body dissatisfaction — the instruction to "love your body" can feel like another impossible standard to meet. Body neutrality offers an alternative: rather than demanding that you feel positively about your body, it asks you to reduce the centrality of appearance in defining your worth. The goal is not to love how you look. The goal is to care less about how you look and more about what your life contains. This article explores what body neutrality is, how it differs from body positivity, what the evidence suggests about its psychological utility, and how to begin practicing it.

**Key Facts:**
1. Body neutrality as a named concept was popularized around 2015 by eating disorder recovery coach Anne Poirier and has since gained significant traction in clinical and wellness communities [1].
2. Body positivity has been critiqued for becoming commercialized, for centering appearance as the axis of self-worth (even if in a positive direction), and for being experienced as invalidating by individuals who cannot access positive feelings about their bodies [2].
3. Research on self-compassion — a closely related construct — shows that treating oneself with kindness regardless of body feelings produces better body image outcomes than forcing positive self-evaluations [3].
4. Body neutrality is increasingly integrated into eating disorder treatment frameworks, particularly for individuals in early recovery who find body positivity triggering or unachievable [4].
5. The core shift in body neutrality is from "how do I feel about my body?" to "what is my body enabling me to do?" — moving the axis of evaluation from appearance to function [1].

## The Limits of Loving Your Body

Body positivity emerged as a radical political and social movement — rooted in fat activism, disability rights, and the fight against appearance-based discrimination. In its original form, body positivity challenged structural inequities: demanding that larger-bodied people, disabled people, people of color, and gender-nonconforming people be treated with dignity regardless of their proximity to culturally idealized appearance.

Over the past decade, however, body positivity has been absorbed into mainstream consumer culture in ways that have diluted its political content and, for some individuals, created a new form of pressure. The commercial version of body positivity — found in advertising campaigns, social media hashtags, and wellness content — often reduces a structural critique to a personal mandate: "love your body." This framing, while well-intentioned, can inadvertently:

**Create a new standard of achievement.** For someone struggling with body dysmorphic disorder, an active eating disorder, or deep-seated body shame, being told to "love your body" can feel like being told to complete a task they are neurologically and psychologically unable to perform. The gap between their lived experience and the expectation of self-love becomes another source of failure and self-criticism [2].

**Center appearance as the axis of self-worth.** Even in its positive framing, commercial body positivity still positions the body as the primary object of self-evaluation. "Love your body" and "hate your body" are opposite conclusions reached from the same premise: that your body is the most important thing about you. For many people — particularly those in eating disorder recovery — reducing the significance of the body as a source of self-evaluation is more therapeutic than changing its valence [4].

**Exclude experiences of genuine distress.** Body positivity messaging can feel invalidating to people whose bodies are sources of chronic pain, disability, medical illness, or trauma. Being told to love a body that causes suffering can feel dismissive. Body neutrality acknowledges that the relationship with one's body does not need to be loving to be functional and peaceful.

## What Body Neutrality Actually Proposes

Body neutrality does not oppose body positivity. It offers a complementary framework — one that may be more accessible for people who cannot currently access positive feelings about their appearance.

The central proposition of body neutrality is a shift in the axis of evaluation:

**From appearance to function.** Instead of "how does my body look?", the question becomes "what does my body enable me to do?" The focus shifts from aesthetics to capacity: the body carries you through the world, senses your environment, connects you to other people, creates, heals, and adapts. These functions are independent of appearance.

**From evaluation to acceptance.** Body neutrality does not require you to evaluate your body at all — positively or negatively. Some days you may feel good about your body. Some days you may feel distressed. Body neutrality does not demand either response. It invites you to notice the body's state without attaching moral weight to that state.

**From centrality to periphery.** In body neutrality, the body moves from the center of self-definition to the periphery. Your identity is not organized around how your body looks. It is organized around your values, your relationships, your interests, your contributions, and your experiences. The body is the vehicle for these things, not the measure of them.

Anne Poirier, who popularized the concept in her work with eating disorder recovery, describes body neutrality as "a space where you can exist without thinking about your body one way or another" [1]. This is a radical proposition in a culture that devotes enormous energy — time, money, emotional bandwidth — to body evaluation.

## The Evidence for Decentering Appearance

While body neutrality as a named concept is relatively new, the psychological principles underlying it have substantial empirical support.

**Self-compassion research.** Dr. Kristin Neff's self-compassion framework — which involves treating oneself with kindness, recognizing common humanity in suffering, and maintaining mindful awareness of emotional states without over-identifying with them — has been shown to produce significant improvements in body image. A randomized controlled trial by Albertson, Neff, and Dill-Shackleford (2015) found that a three-week self-compassion meditation intervention produced significant reductions in body dissatisfaction, body shame, and appearance-contingent self-worth [3]. Self-compassion did not produce body love — it produced a reduction in the suffering associated with body evaluation.

**Functional body image.** Research by Alleva and colleagues (2015) developed and tested the "Expand Your Horizon" program, a body functionality-based intervention that asks participants to write about what their body does rather than how it looks [5]. Across multiple randomized controlled trials, this approach produced significant improvements in body image, reduced appearance-related anxiety, and improved overall psychological wellbeing — effects that were maintained at follow-up.

**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT).** ACT's approach to body image distress aligns closely with body neutrality. Rather than trying to change body-related thoughts or feelings, ACT teaches individuals to notice these internal events without being controlled by them — creating psychological flexibility. Research by Pearson, Follette, and Hayes (2012) found that ACT-based interventions for body image produced improvements in body satisfaction and reduced disordered eating [6].

**Defusion from appearance-based self-worth.** Research on contingent self-worth — self-esteem that depends on meeting specific standards — shows that individuals whose self-worth is contingent on appearance experience greater mood instability, lower overall self-esteem, and greater vulnerability to body image disturbance [7]. Decentering appearance from the self-worth equation — as body neutrality proposes — directly addresses this vulnerability.

## Body Neutrality in Eating Disorder Recovery

Body neutrality has found particular resonance in eating disorder treatment and recovery communities, where the relationship with the body is often the most damaged and the most clinically sensitive.

In early eating disorder recovery, body image is frequently the last symptom to improve. A person may have restored their weight, normalized their eating patterns, and developed effective coping strategies — while still experiencing significant body distress. Being told to "love your body" at this stage can feel not just difficult but actively harmful: it can trigger comparison to their pre-illness body, reactivate distorted body perception, and create a sense that recovery is incomplete because they do not feel positively about their appearance [4].

Body neutrality offers recovery a different benchmark. Recovery does not require loving your body. It requires being able to live in your body — to feed it adequately, move it comfortably, dress it functionally, and engage in life without your body being the central preoccupation of your day. This is a more achievable and more clinically realistic goal for many people in recovery.

Clinicians including Jennifer Rollin, Lexie Kite, and Lindsay Kite have advocated for integrating body neutrality into eating disorder treatment as a bridge — a resting point between body hatred and body acceptance that does not demand emotional states that the person cannot yet access [8].

## Practicing Body Neutrality

Body neutrality is not a technique you perform once. It is a practice — a gradual shift in how you relate to your body and how much mental energy you devote to appearance-based evaluation.

**Functional gratitude.** Instead of a gratitude practice focused on how your body looks, practice noticing what your body does. "My legs carried me to work." "My hands made dinner." "My eyes watched the sunset." This is not forced positivity about appearance — it is factual acknowledgment of function.

**Reducing body talk.** Notice how much of your internal dialogue and external conversation is about bodies — your own and others'. Body neutrality involves gradually reducing the volume of body talk, creating mental space for other dimensions of experience.

**Choosing comfort over aesthetics.** When selecting clothing, prioritize how the clothes feel on your body rather than how they make your body look. This small shift redirects attention from appearance to experience.

**Tolerating ambivalence.** Body neutrality does not require you to feel any particular way about your body. It asks you to tolerate whatever you feel without acting on it — without restricting because you feel dissatisfied, without avoiding situations because you feel self-conscious, without spending hours evaluating because you feel uncertain.

**Engaging in embodied activities.** Activities that produce body awareness through sensation rather than appearance — walking in nature, dancing to music you love, stretching after sitting, swimming, playing with a pet — rebuild the relationship with the body on experiential rather than evaluative terms [9].

> 💡 **Spark:** You do not have to love your body. You do not have to hate it either. You can simply live in it — noticing what it does, caring for it adequately, and directing your attention toward the parts of life that have nothing to do with how you look. That is not resignation. That is freedom.

## What You Can Try

- **For one day, redirect every body-evaluation thought.** When you catch yourself thinking about how your body looks, replace it with a thought about what your body is doing. "I hate my stomach" becomes "my body is digesting my lunch right now."

- **Write a body function inventory.** List 20 things your body does that have nothing to do with appearance. This exercise, adapted from Alleva's research, shifts the cognitive framework from evaluative to functional.

- **Remove one mirror.** If you have multiple mirrors in your home, remove one that you check frequently. Notice how the absence changes your morning routine and mental state.

- **Set a body-talk boundary.** For one week, decline to participate in conversations about diets, weight, or body evaluation — your own or others'. Notice how much conversational space this opens up.
