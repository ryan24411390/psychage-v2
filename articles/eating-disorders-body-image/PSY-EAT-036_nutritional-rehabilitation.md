> **Summary:** Nutritional rehabilitation — the medical process of restoring adequate nutrition and, when necessary, weight after a period of malnutrition — is the foundation of eating disorder recovery. Without adequate nutrition, the brain cannot function effectively enough to engage in psychological treatment, the body cannot repair the damage caused by energy deficit, and the neurochemical disruptions that maintain the eating disorder persist. Yet nutritional rehabilitation is not simply "eating more." It is a medical process that must be carefully managed to avoid dangerous complications, including refeeding syndrome. This article explains what nutritional rehabilitation involves, why it is medically necessary, how it is structured, the role of the registered dietitian, and what patients and families should expect.

**Key Facts:**
1. Nutritional rehabilitation is considered a medical prerequisite for effective psychological treatment in severe eating disorders — the malnourished brain cannot fully engage in psychotherapy [1].
2. Refeeding syndrome — a potentially fatal metabolic complication of restoring nutrition too rapidly — affects an estimated 0.8–34% of patients beginning refeeding, depending on severity and clinical setting [2].
3. Weight restoration in anorexia nervosa is associated with improvements in brain volume, cognitive function, mood, and eating disorder-specific psychopathology [3].
4. The "meal-based" approach to nutritional rehabilitation — in which patients eat regular meals rather than receiving caloric supplements — is associated with better long-term outcomes and reduced relapse [4].
5. A registered dietitian specializing in eating disorders is an essential member of the treatment team for nutritional rehabilitation [5].

## Why Nutrition Comes First

The relationship between nutritional status and psychological functioning is not metaphorical — it is biological. The brain is the most metabolically demanding organ in the body, consuming approximately 20% of total energy intake. When energy intake is insufficient, brain function is compromised: concentration decreases, cognitive flexibility diminishes, mood deteriorates, and the ability to engage in complex cognitive processes — including the cognitive restructuring central to CBT-E — is impaired [1].

The landmark Minnesota Starvation Experiment, conducted by Ancel Keys and colleagues in 1944–1945, demonstrated that semi-starvation in previously healthy men produced psychological symptoms virtually identical to those observed in eating disorders: obsessive preoccupation with food, social withdrawal, irritability, depression, cognitive rigidity, and difficulty concentrating [6]. These symptoms were not caused by a pre-existing psychiatric condition — they were caused by malnutrition. And they resolved with refeeding.

This finding has profound implications for eating disorder treatment. Many of the psychological symptoms that maintain eating disorders — rigid thinking, food preoccupation, emotional blunting, impaired interoception — are partially products of the malnutrition that the eating disorder produces. Psychological treatment that attempts to address these symptoms without first addressing the malnutrition is working against biology. This is why clinical guidelines consistently recommend that nutritional rehabilitation precede or accompany psychological treatment rather than being deferred until psychological readiness is achieved [3].

## The Medical Process of Refeeding

Refeeding — the reintroduction of adequate nutrition after a period of starvation or severe restriction — is a medical process that carries its own risks if not managed carefully.

**Refeeding syndrome** is the most dangerous complication. During prolonged energy restriction, the body adapts by shifting from carbohydrate metabolism to fat and protein metabolism. Intracellular stores of phosphate, potassium, and magnesium are depleted. When carbohydrates are reintroduced, insulin surges drive these electrolytes from the bloodstream into cells, causing dangerously low serum levels. Hypophosphatemia — low blood phosphate — is the hallmark finding and can cause cardiac failure, respiratory failure, rhabdomyolysis, seizures, and death [2].

The risk of refeeding syndrome is highest in patients who are severely malnourished, who have been restricting for a prolonged period, or who have very low BMI. Current clinical guidelines recommend:

- **Start low, advance slow.** Initial caloric intake for severely malnourished patients typically begins at 1,000–1,200 calories per day (or sometimes lower for the most severe cases), advancing by 200–300 calories every 2–3 days as tolerated.
- **Electrolyte monitoring.** Daily monitoring of phosphate, potassium, magnesium, and sodium during the first 1–2 weeks of refeeding, with supplementation as needed.
- **Thiamine supplementation.** Thiamine (vitamin B1) should be supplemented before and during refeeding to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy.
- **Cardiac monitoring.** ECG monitoring is recommended for patients at high risk of refeeding syndrome, given the cardiac complications of electrolyte disturbance [7].

However, recent research has challenged the overly cautious approach to refeeding that has characterized clinical practice for decades. The landmark NICE guidelines revision and research by Garber and colleagues (2016) found that starting at higher caloric levels (1,500–1,800 calories) in medically supervised settings did not increase refeeding complications and produced faster weight restoration — which is associated with better long-term outcomes [8]. The optimal approach appears to be adequately aggressive refeeding with close medical monitoring, rather than extremely conservative caloric advancement.

## What Weight Restoration Involves

For individuals with anorexia nervosa and other restrictive eating disorders, weight restoration — returning to a weight that supports full physiological functioning — is a central component of nutritional rehabilitation.

**Target weight** is typically determined by the treatment team based on the individual's growth history, pre-illness weight trajectory, and physiological indicators including menstrual function (for those with menstrual cycles), bone density, cardiac function, and metabolic markers. The common clinical approach is to target a weight at which all physiological functions have normalized rather than a specific BMI number [4].

**Rate of weight gain** in inpatient and residential settings typically targets 1–2 kg (2.2–4.4 lbs) per week. In outpatient settings, 0.5–1 kg (1.1–2.2 lbs) per week is more typical. These rates require caloric intake that significantly exceeds what the patient may expect — often 2,500–3,500 calories per day or more for active weight restoration, depending on the individual's metabolic rate, activity level, and degree of metabolic adaptation [9].

**Hypermetabolism during refeeding** is a phenomenon that catches many patients and families by surprise. During early weight restoration, the body's metabolic rate often increases dramatically — sometimes to 1.5–2 times expected levels — as the body repairs tissue, restores organ function, and rebuilds depleted energy stores. This means that the caloric intake needed for weight restoration may be much higher than what would normally maintain that weight. The hypermetabolism typically normalizes as weight stabilization is achieved.

**Body composition changes.** During early weight restoration, weight gain often includes significant fluid retention and preferential fat deposition in the trunk — which can be psychologically distressing for patients. Research shows that body composition normalizes over time, with more even fat distribution and increased lean mass as weight stabilization continues. Communicating this timeline to patients is important for managing the body image distress that early weight restoration can produce [10].

## The Role of the Eating Disorder Dietitian

A registered dietitian (RD) specializing in eating disorders is a critical member of the treatment team. The eating disorder dietitian's role differs significantly from the role of a general nutrition counselor.

**Meal planning** that is therapeutic, not restrictive. The eating disorder dietitian helps patients develop meal plans that provide adequate nutrition while gradually expanding food variety and flexibility. Unlike conventional diet counseling — which often focuses on what to reduce or avoid — eating disorder nutrition therapy focuses on adequacy, variety, and the development of a non-restrictive relationship with food [5].

**Nutritional education** that corrects the misinformation the eating disorder has produced. Many individuals with eating disorders hold extensive but distorted nutritional knowledge — they may know the calorie content of hundreds of foods while understanding nothing about the body's actual nutritional needs. The dietitian provides accurate nutritional education within a therapeutic frame.

**Challenging food rules.** Gradually expanding the range of foods the patient is willing to eat — reintroducing "fear foods," breaking rigid eating patterns, and developing flexibility around food choices — is a behavioral component of nutrition therapy that directly complements the psychological work of CBT-E.

**Medical monitoring.** The dietitian monitors nutritional status through dietary assessment, weight monitoring, and collaboration with the medical team on laboratory values and physical signs.

## The Psychological Experience of Refeeding

Nutritional rehabilitation is not just a medical process — it is a psychological one, and understanding the patient's experience is essential for supportive care.

**Terror.** For many patients, particularly those with anorexia nervosa, eating and weight gain feel genuinely life-threatening — not just uncomfortable but existentially terrifying. The eating disorder has rewritten the brain's threat-detection system so that food registers as danger. This terror is real and must be acknowledged rather than dismissed.

**Gastrointestinal distress.** Early refeeding commonly produces bloating, abdominal pain, constipation, and early satiety — side effects of the gastrointestinal deconditioning that occurs during prolonged restriction. These symptoms are temporary but can feel overwhelming and may reinforce the eating disorder's message that eating is harmful.

**Emotional flooding.** As nutrition improves, the emotional blunting produced by malnutrition lifts — and emotions that were suppressed by starvation return, often with intense force. Patients may experience sudden waves of anxiety, sadness, anger, or grief as their brain's emotional processing comes back online. This is a sign of neurological recovery, though it feels like emotional deterioration.

**Body image distress.** The body changes associated with weight restoration — particularly the early trunk-preferential fat distribution and fluid retention — can produce severe body image distress. This distress is one of the primary drivers of relapse during early recovery and must be addressed proactively through body image therapy, psychoeducation, and ongoing psychological support.

> 💡 **Spark:** Nutritional rehabilitation is not punishment. It is medicine. The malnourished brain cannot think clearly, feel fully, or recover meaningfully. Every calorie consumed during refeeding is rebuilding the organ that will eventually make full recovery possible.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are in nutritional rehabilitation,** trust the process — even when it feels wrong. The discomfort of refeeding is temporary. The damage of continued malnutrition is progressive.

- **Work with an eating disorder dietitian.** General nutritional advice is insufficient for eating disorder recovery. Seek a dietitian with specific training in eating disorders.

- **Expect and prepare for gastrointestinal symptoms.** Bloating, discomfort, and fullness during early refeeding are common and temporary. Discuss these with your treatment team.

- **Understand hypermetabolism.** The high caloric intake during weight restoration is medically necessary — your body is using that energy to repair itself.
