> **Summary:** Eating disorders are not adolescent conditions that occasionally appear in younger children. They are conditions that can and do develop in children as young as five — and the age of onset appears to be decreasing. Pediatric eating disorder presentations often differ from adolescent and adult presentations in ways that can delay recognition: children may not articulate body image concerns, may present primarily with physical symptoms (weight loss, abdominal pain, food refusal), and may receive medical workups for gastrointestinal or endocrine conditions before an eating disorder is considered. Early identification and intervention are critical because eating disorders in children can disrupt physical growth, bone development, brain maturation, and social-emotional development during periods that cannot be fully recovered later. This article examines what eating disorders look like in children, the unique risk factors and clinical considerations of pediatric onset, and why parents, pediatricians, and teachers need to be alert.

**Key Facts:**
1. Hospital admissions for eating disorders in children under 12 have increased significantly over the past two decades, with some studies showing increases of 119% in the 5–11 age group [1].
2. ARFID is the most commonly diagnosed eating disorder in children under 12, followed by anorexia nervosa and unspecified feeding or eating disorders [2].
3. Children with eating disorders may not endorse classic body image concerns ("I want to be thinner") — their restriction may be driven by fear of choking, stomach pain, sensory aversion, or non-specific anxiety [3].
4. Growth faltering — failure to follow expected growth curves — may be the first and most visible sign of an eating disorder in a child, even before significant weight loss occurs [4].
5. Family-based treatment (FBT/Maudsley approach) is the leading evidence-based treatment for pediatric eating disorders, with strong evidence for early-onset anorexia nervosa [5].

## The Changing Face of Pediatric Eating Disorders

The clinical landscape of eating disorders in children has shifted dramatically over the past two decades. Conditions once considered rare in prepubertal children are now documented with increasing frequency, and the COVID-19 pandemic appears to have accelerated this trend — with multiple studies reporting sharp increases in pediatric eating disorder presentations beginning in 2020 and persisting through subsequent years [1].

Research from children's hospitals in Australia, the UK, Canada, and the United States has documented significant increases in admissions for eating disorders among children under 12. A study by Toulany and colleagues (2022) found that pediatric eating disorder hospitalizations in Ontario, Canada, increased by 66% during the pandemic compared to pre-pandemic levels, with the largest proportional increase occurring in the youngest age groups [6].

These increases are not simply the result of better detection, though awareness has improved. They reflect a genuine epidemiological shift driven by multiple factors: earlier exposure to appearance-focused social media, increased social isolation during pandemic lockdowns, disruption of normal eating routines, heightened anxiety in the home environment, and increased parental stress.

The clinical implications are significant. Pediatric eating disorders that develop during critical periods of physical and neurological development carry risks that are qualitatively different from those in adolescents and adults — including disruption of linear growth, permanent bone density deficits if they occur before peak bone mass is achieved, and interference with brain development during the period of most rapid neural maturation.

## How Pediatric Presentations Differ

Eating disorders in children often look different from the "textbook" presentations that clinicians are trained to recognize in adolescents and adults. These differences are one of the primary reasons that pediatric eating disorders are missed or misdiagnosed.

**Absence of classic body image language.** Younger children may not articulate body dissatisfaction in the terms that screening tools are designed to detect. A child may not say "I want to be thinner" or "I'm afraid of gaining weight." Instead, they may say "my stomach hurts when I eat," "I'm not hungry," "food makes me feel weird," or simply refuse to eat without providing an explanation. The cognitive and linguistic development required to articulate body image concerns may not yet be present, even when body-related distress is driving the behavior [3].

**ARFID as the most common presentation.** In children under 12, ARFID — avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder — is the most commonly diagnosed eating disorder. ARFID in children may present as extreme selective eating, refusal of foods based on sensory properties (texture, smell, appearance), fear of choking or vomiting after a traumatic food-related experience, or apparent lack of interest in food. These presentations are not driven by body image concerns and may not trigger the "eating disorder" category in a clinician's differential diagnosis [2].

**Somatic presentation.** Children with eating disorders frequently present with physical complaints rather than psychological ones: abdominal pain, nausea, constipation, fatigue, dizziness, or headaches. These symptoms may trigger extensive gastrointestinal workups — endoscopies, blood panels, imaging — before an eating disorder is considered. Research by Nicholls and colleagues (2011) found that the average time from first medical presentation to eating disorder diagnosis in children was significantly longer than in adolescents, partly because medical explanations were pursued first [4].

**Growth faltering as a primary sign.** In growing children, failure to gain expected weight or height may be the first clinical indicator of an eating disorder — before any absolute weight loss occurs. A child who was previously tracking along the 50th percentile for height and weight and drops to the 25th percentile has experienced relative malnutrition even if their absolute weight is still within the "normal" range. Pediatricians who monitor growth trajectories are in a unique position to identify these changes early [4].

**Food-related anxiety without weight concern.** Some children develop acute food avoidance following a specific traumatic event — choking, severe vomiting, witnessing someone else choke or have an allergic reaction. The resulting food restriction can be severe enough to cause significant weight loss and nutritional deficiency but is driven by fear of the aversive experience rather than by body image. This presentation is recognized under ARFID but may be classified as an anxiety disorder if the food-specific component is not identified.

## Risk Factors Specific to Childhood

While many eating disorder risk factors are shared across age groups (genetic predisposition, perfectionism, anxiety), several factors are particularly salient in childhood onset:

**Family food environment.** Children's eating is shaped by their family's food practices — what is served, how meals are structured, the emotional atmosphere around mealtimes, and the language used about food and bodies. Research by Rodgers and colleagues (2013) found that maternal dietary restraint, maternal comments about the child's weight, and parental modeling of disordered eating predicted body dissatisfaction and eating disturbance in children as young as five [7].

**Peer influence and bullying.** Appearance-related teasing and weight-based bullying are among the strongest risk factors for eating disorders in children. The impact is disproportionate at younger ages because children's self-concepts are less stable and more permeable to external feedback. A study by Haines and colleagues (2006) found that weight-based teasing in childhood predicted disordered eating five years later [8].

**Early puberty.** Children who enter puberty earlier than their peers face a unique body image challenge: their bodies are changing at a time when their peers' bodies are not, creating visible difference and potential social discomfort. The natural fat gain associated with puberty may trigger body dissatisfaction and restriction in a child who has internalized thin-ideal messaging.

**Neurodevelopmental conditions.** ARFID co-occurs at elevated rates with autism spectrum conditions and ADHD. Children with sensory processing differences, anxiety disorders, or rigid cognitive styles are at higher risk for food avoidance and restriction. Pediatric eating disorder assessment should routinely include neurodevelopmental screening [9].

**Media exposure at younger ages.** Children now encounter appearance-focused media content at earlier ages than any previous generation. Research by Harriger and colleagues (2010) documented that children as young as three show preferences for thinner body types after exposure to media imagery [10]. Smartphones, tablets, and social media have lowered the age at which children begin to internalize cultural body ideals.

## Medical Consequences in Growing Bodies

The medical consequences of eating disorders in children are qualitatively different from those in adults because they occur during periods of active growth and development.

**Linear growth.** Energy restriction during childhood can slow or halt linear growth. If the eating disorder is identified and treated early, catch-up growth may occur — but prolonged restriction during critical growth periods can result in permanently reduced adult height.

**Bone development.** Childhood and early adolescence are the periods during which approximately 90% of peak bone mass is established. Energy restriction during these years — particularly when combined with the hormonal disruptions that eating disorders produce — can create bone density deficits that are never fully recovered, predisposing the individual to osteoporosis and fractures throughout life.

**Brain development.** The brain undergoes extensive development throughout childhood, with myelination, synaptic pruning, and prefrontal cortex maturation continuing into the mid-twenties. Malnutrition during childhood can affect cognitive development, academic performance, and emotional regulation capacity. While many neurological effects are reversible with nutritional rehabilitation, the long-term impact of childhood malnutrition on brain development is an area of active research.

**Pubertal development.** Energy restriction can delay puberty, interrupt pubertal progression, or cause pubertal arrest. In girls, this may manifest as primary amenorrhea (never starting menstruation) rather than the secondary amenorrhea (loss of previously established periods) more commonly seen in adolescent and adult eating disorders. Delayed puberty affects bone, reproductive, and emotional development.

## Treatment: Family-Based Treatment as the Gold Standard

Family-based treatment (FBT), also known as the Maudsley approach, is the most extensively researched and most strongly recommended treatment for eating disorders in children and young adolescents [5].

FBT positions parents as the primary agents of change in their child's eating disorder recovery. Rather than treating the child individually while parents wait in the lobby, FBT actively empowers parents to take charge of their child's eating — ensuring adequate nutritional intake, disrupting eating disorder behaviors, and reducing mealtime conflict — while the clinician coaches and supports the family through the process.

The three phases of FBT are:
- **Phase 1 (weight restoration):** Parents take full control of their child's eating. The therapist supports the parents in refeeding, validates their efforts, and helps the family manage the distress that the eating disorder produces during this phase.
- **Phase 2 (returning control):** As the child stabilizes medically and nutritionally, control over eating is gradually returned to the child in an age-appropriate manner.
- **Phase 3 (identity work):** With the eating disorder in remission, treatment addresses developmental and identity issues that may have been disrupted by the illness.

Randomized controlled trials have shown that FBT produces full remission in approximately 40–50% of adolescents with anorexia nervosa at end of treatment, with rates increasing at follow-up [5]. For younger children, the evidence is more limited but consistently supportive.

FBT is effective because it addresses the reality that children cannot be expected to recover from eating disorders independently. A child does not have the developmental capacity, the nutritional knowledge, or the psychological resources to manage their own refeeding. Parents — when supported, educated, and empowered — do [11].

> 💡 **Spark:** A child with an eating disorder is not choosing to be difficult at mealtimes. They are trapped in a condition that they may not have the words to describe, the understanding to recognize, or the capacity to fight alone. The adults around them — parents, doctors, teachers, coaches — are the early warning system and the recovery team. Paying attention is the intervention.

## What You Can Try

- **Monitor growth trajectories, not just weight.** If your child is falling off their growth curve — in either height or weight — raise this with their pediatrician. Growth deceleration can be an early indicator of energy deficiency.

- **Pay attention to food-related behavior changes.** Sudden refusal of previously accepted foods, new rules about eating, avoidance of family meals, rituals around food preparation, or visible anxiety around mealtimes all warrant attention.

- **Do not comment on your child's weight or body size.** Research consistently shows that parental comments about weight — even well-intentioned ones — increase the risk of body dissatisfaction and eating disorders in children.

- **Seek assessment from a pediatric eating disorder specialist.** If you suspect an eating disorder, do not wait. Early intervention produces significantly better outcomes. Ask your pediatrician for a referral to a specialist who works with children.

- **Learn about FBT.** If your child is diagnosed with an eating disorder, ask the treating clinician about family-based treatment. The approach empowers you as the parent to be an active part of your child's recovery.
