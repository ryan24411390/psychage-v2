> **Summary:** Coaches and trainers occupy a unique position in the lives of athletes: they see them daily, observe their bodies in motion, notice their energy levels, and hold significant authority over their relationship with food, exercise, and physical performance. This proximity makes coaches among the first adults likely to notice when an athlete develops disordered eating — but it also means that a coach's words and actions can either accelerate the development of an eating disorder or create the conditions for early intervention. Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), a syndrome resulting from insufficient energy intake relative to exercise expenditure, affects athletes across all sports, genders, and competitive levels. This article equips coaches and trainers with the knowledge to recognize warning signs of eating disorders and RED-S, guides them on how to approach an athlete they are concerned about, clarifies what not to do, explains the referral process, and outlines how to create a team culture that reduces eating disorder risk.

**Key Facts:**

1. Eating disorders are 2-3 times more prevalent in athletes than in the general population, with the highest rates in aesthetic sports (gymnastics, figure skating, dance), weight-class sports (wrestling, rowing, martial arts), and endurance sports (distance running, cycling) [1].
2. Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) affects an estimated 22-58% of female athletes and 13-18% of male athletes across competitive levels, though exact prevalence depends on the sport and the screening method used [2].
3. RED-S impairs athletic performance — reduced endurance, decreased training response, impaired judgment, increased injury risk, and slower recovery — making it a performance issue as well as a health issue [3].
4. A 2018 survey found that only 19% of coaches had received any formal training in eating disorder recognition, despite 73% reporting that they had coached an athlete they believed had disordered eating [4].
5. Athletes are more likely to disclose disordered eating to a coach who has explicitly stated that health takes priority over performance than to a coach who has not made this value clear, regardless of the coach's actual concern for the athlete [5].

## Why Athletes Are at Elevated Risk

The athletic environment, by its nature, intensifies several of the core risk factors for eating disorders. Understanding these risk factors helps coaches recognize why their athletes are vulnerable and how their own behavior can amplify or mitigate that vulnerability.

**Performance pressure tied to body composition.** In many sports, there is a real or perceived relationship between body size and performance. Lighter runners are expected to be faster. Leaner gymnasts are assumed to achieve higher scores. Smaller wrestlers compete in lower weight classes against opponents they might overpower. These associations — some evidence-based, many exaggerated or oversimplified — create a logical pathway from competitive motivation to body manipulation. An athlete who is praised for "leaning out" and simultaneously performs well has received the most powerful reinforcement possible: a direct link between food restriction and the thing they care about most [6].

**Normalization of extreme eating behaviors.** Athletic cultures often normalize eating practices that would be considered disordered in any other context. "Making weight" by dehydrating and restricting before a weigh-in, following rigidly prescribed meal plans, categorizing foods as "clean" and "dirty," exercising through injury, and treating rest as laziness are all practices that exist on the continuum of disordered eating but are framed as discipline, commitment, and competitive edge within sport [7]. This normalization makes it difficult for athletes to recognize when their behavior has crossed a line, and difficult for coaches to distinguish between "dedicated" and "disordered."

**Compulsive exercise as hidden symptom.** In the general population, compulsive exercise is recognized as a symptom of eating disorders. In athletes, it is rewarded. The athlete who trains twice a day, who adds extra workouts beyond what is prescribed, who becomes anxious or distressed when a training session is cancelled — these patterns mirror the compulsive exercise seen in clinical eating disorders, but they are indistinguishable from the behavior of a highly motivated athlete. Coaches must learn to look beyond the behavior itself and assess its rigidity, its relationship to food intake, and its effect on the athlete's physical and psychological wellbeing [8].

**Coach authority over eating.** Coaches wield enormous influence over athletes' relationships with food and their bodies. A single comment — "You'd be faster if you lost a few pounds," "You're looking a bit heavy this season," "Are you sure you need that?" — can initiate a cascade of restriction in a psychologically vulnerable athlete. Research by Kerr and colleagues documented multiple cases in which athletes traced the onset of their eating disorder to a specific comment by a coach about weight or body composition [6]. The power differential means that athletes may comply with weight-related directives even when they know the directives are harmful, because the coach controls playing time, team selection, and the athlete's competitive future.

## Warning Signs Coaches Should Watch For

Coaches see athletes in contexts that no other adult does — training, competition, locker rooms, travel, team meals. This access provides opportunities to notice changes that parents, teachers, and even physicians may miss.

**Changes in energy and performance.** An athlete whose performance plateaus or declines despite maintained or increased training may be in energy deficit. Fatigue, decreased endurance, slower recovery between sessions, increased susceptibility to illness, and recurrent injuries (particularly stress fractures) are all performance-related indicators of RED-S. The athlete may attribute these changes to overtraining or bad luck, but the underlying cause may be inadequate fueling [3].

**Changes in eating behavior around the team.** Watch for athletes who stop eating at team meals, who make excuses to eat separately, who develop new food restrictions that progressively narrow, or who become preoccupied with reading ingredient lists and calculating nutritional content. An athlete who previously ate freely and now approaches food with visible anxiety or rigid rules has likely changed their relationship with eating.

**Overtraining beyond the program.** An athlete who consistently adds unauthorized training sessions, who exercises on rest days, who becomes agitated or distressed when training is cancelled, or who continues training through illness or injury is displaying patterns associated with compulsive exercise. This is particularly concerning when combined with changes in eating behavior or visible weight changes.

**Mood and personality shifts.** Irritability, withdrawal from teammates, decreased enjoyment of the sport, increased perfectionism about performance, and emotional volatility — particularly mood swings associated with eating or not eating — are psychological indicators. Malnutrition directly affects neurotransmitter function, producing cognitive rigidity, anxiety, depressed mood, and difficulty concentrating. An athlete who was previously sociable and engaged but has become withdrawn and tense may be experiencing the neuropsychological effects of energy deficiency [9].

**Physical signs.** Cold intolerance, lanugo (fine body hair that develops in response to energy deficit), dental erosion (in athletes who purge), parotid gland swelling (chipmunk cheeks, also associated with purging), brittle nails, thinning hair, dizziness or lightheadedness during training, and menstrual irregularity or cessation in female athletes are all physical indicators. Coaches should not conduct body checks or medical assessments, but they can observe signs that are visible in normal training contexts and flag them for medical follow-up [10].

## How to Approach an Athlete You Are Concerned About

The conversation between a coach and an athlete about potential disordered eating is one of the most important and most delicate interactions in sport. Done well, it can be the turning point that leads to recovery. Done poorly, it can drive the athlete deeper into secrecy and illness.

**Have the conversation privately.** Never raise concerns in front of teammates, other coaches, or parents in the first instance. Request a private meeting: "I'd like to talk with you one-on-one about how you're doing. Can we find a time this week?"

**Lead with performance and wellbeing observations.** Frame your concern in terms of what you've observed about the athlete's functioning, not their body: "I've noticed your energy has been lower in practice this month, and you've had two more injuries than usual this season. I'm concerned about how you're doing overall." Starting with observable, non-body-focused observations is less threatening and harder to dismiss.

**Express care, not judgment.** The athlete needs to know that you are raising this topic because you care about them as a person, not because you are evaluating their body or their discipline. "I'm bringing this up because I care about your health and I want to make sure you're getting what you need to feel good and perform your best."

**Ask open-ended questions.** "How have you been feeling about training lately?" "How's your energy been?" "Has anything changed with your eating or recovery?" These questions invite disclosure without cornering the athlete. Avoid yes/no questions that are easy to deflect ("Are you eating enough?" — "Yes").

**Listen more than you talk.** If the athlete opens up, your job is to listen, validate, and guide toward support — not to diagnose, provide nutritional advice, or prescribe solutions. "Thank you for telling me that. That sounds really difficult. I'd like to help you get the right support."

## What NOT to Say or Do

Coaches' well-intentioned but misguided responses can cause real harm. Here are the most important things to avoid.

**Do not comment on the athlete's weight, shape, or body composition.** Ever. Not positively ("You look great — you've really leaned out"), not negatively ("You've put on weight this offseason"), and not as a performance directive ("You need to get down to X to compete in that weight class"). Weight-related comments from coaches are among the most commonly cited precipitating factors in athlete eating disorder development [6].

**Do not conduct weigh-ins** unless medically supervised and clinically necessary. Mandatory team weigh-ins are associated with increased body dissatisfaction, disordered eating, and eating disorder onset in athletes. If weight monitoring is required for medical or safety reasons (as in combat sports), it should be conducted by medical staff in a private, supportive setting — never by coaches in front of teammates [11].

**Do not try to be the therapist.** Coaching and clinical treatment require different skills and different boundaries. A coach who attempts to counsel an athlete through an eating disorder risks deepening the athlete's dependence on the coaching relationship, crossing ethical boundaries, and providing guidance that is clinically inappropriate. Your role is to notice, express concern, and facilitate connection with qualified professionals.

**Do not make return to play contingent on visible weight gain.** While medical clearance should guide return-to-play decisions, framing those decisions in terms of weight targets reinforces the idea that the eating disorder is about weight rather than the complex psychological, neurobiological, and social factors that drive it. Defer to the treatment team on return-to-play criteria and support their recommendations.

**Do not ignore what you see because the athlete is performing well.** Some athletes maintain high performance levels well into the progression of an eating disorder — compensating through talent, adrenaline, and sheer determination. Performance is not a reliable indicator of health. An athlete can win competitions while being seriously medically compromised, and the physiological toll of RED-S accumulates even when performance metrics remain stable [3].

## The Referral Process

When a coach suspects disordered eating or RED-S, the appropriate response is to connect the athlete with qualified professionals — not to manage the situation independently.

**Step 1: Consult with athletic training staff.** If your program has certified athletic trainers or sports medicine personnel, they should be your first point of contact. Share your observations (behavioral, performance-based) and ask for their assessment. Athletic trainers are often more comfortable raising medical concerns with athletes and can facilitate medical evaluation.

**Step 2: Connect with medical and psychological professionals.** The athlete should be evaluated by a physician familiar with RED-S and eating disorders, ideally a sports medicine physician. Simultaneously, referral to a psychologist or therapist specializing in eating disorders (and, ideally, in athlete populations) is appropriate. A registered dietitian with expertise in sports nutrition and eating disorders should be part of the team — general nutritionists without eating disorder training may inadvertently reinforce restriction through rigid meal planning.

**Step 3: Communicate with the family (for minors).** For athletes under 18, parents or guardians should be informed of concerns and referrals. Frame the conversation collaboratively: "I've noticed some changes in your child's energy and behavior at practice, and I want to make sure they have the support they need. I've connected with our athletic trainer, who recommends a medical check-up."

**Step 4: Follow the treatment team's guidance.** Once an athlete is engaged in treatment, the coach's role shifts to supporting the treatment plan — which may include modified training loads, rest days, excusal from weigh-ins or body composition testing, and flexible return-to-play timelines. Advocating for the athlete's health within the competitive structure of the program is one of the most important things a coach can do [12].

## Creating a Team Culture That Reduces Risk

The most effective prevention happens before any individual athlete develops symptoms. Coaches who build team cultures that reduce eating disorder risk protect all of their athletes simultaneously.

**Separate performance from body size.** Train athletes to evaluate their performance by what their bodies can do — speed, strength, endurance, skill execution — rather than what their bodies look like. Eliminate language that ties performance to body composition. Focus feedback on technique, strategy, effort, and recovery rather than appearance.

**Normalize fueling as performance support.** Frame adequate nutrition as a training essential, no different from sleep, hydration, or technique work. Celebrate athletes who eat well and recover fully. Make food available at training and competition, and model non-restrictive eating as a coach. The team culture around food starts with the coaching staff.

**Educate about RED-S.** Provide annual education for athletes (and parents, for youth sport) about RED-S, its effects on performance and long-term health, and its prevalence in sport. Frame this education in performance terms: under-fueling makes you slower, weaker, more injury-prone, and more cognitively impaired. Athletes who understand the performance costs of energy deficiency are more likely to prioritize adequate fueling [3].

**Establish clear anti-weight-talk policies.** Create explicit team norms against body commentary — from coaches, staff, and athletes. "On this team, we don't comment on each other's bodies. We focus on what our bodies can do." This norm protects athletes who are vulnerable and signals that the team values people over appearance.

> 💡 **Spark:** A coach will never regret pulling an athlete aside to say, "I've noticed you don't seem like yourself — how are you doing?" They may regret staying silent. Eating disorders in sport thrive in cultures of silence, performance pressure, and body scrutiny. Every coach has the power to build a different kind of culture — one where health comes first, fueling is valued, and struggling athletes know they can ask for help.

## What You Can Try

If you are a coach, trainer, or sport administrator, here are concrete steps to take:

**Complete eating disorder education.** The National Eating Disorders Association (NEDA), the National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA), and the International Olympic Committee (IOC) all offer free or low-cost educational resources specifically designed for coaches. The IOC's RED-S Clinical Assessment Tool (RED-S CAT) provides a structured framework for evaluating energy availability concerns.

**Build your referral network before you need it.** Identify a sports medicine physician, an eating disorder therapist, and a sports dietitian in your area who can accept referrals from your program. Having names and contact information ready eliminates delay when a concern arises.

**Audit your program's culture.** Honestly assess: Does your program conduct mandatory weigh-ins? Do coaches or staff comment on athletes' bodies? Is there pressure to "make weight" or "lean out"? Are rest days genuinely supported, or subtly discouraged? Address any practices that elevate eating disorder risk.

**Model healthy behaviors.** Athletes observe what their coaches do, not just what they say. A coach who skips meals, makes disparaging comments about their own body, or glorifies extreme exercise is modeling the same attitudes that drive eating disorders — regardless of what they tell their athletes.
