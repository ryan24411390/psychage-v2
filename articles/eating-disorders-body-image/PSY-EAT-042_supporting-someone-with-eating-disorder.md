> **Summary:** Watching someone you love struggle with an eating disorder is one of the most painful and helpless experiences a person can have. The condition may seem incomprehensible from the outside — why can't they just eat? Why do they keep purging when they know it's dangerous? Why do they refuse help? The answer is that eating disorders are serious psychiatric conditions with neurobiological mechanisms that override conscious choice. Your loved one is not choosing this. But you can play a meaningful role in their recovery — not by fixing the eating disorder yourself, but by creating the conditions in which recovery becomes possible. This article provides practical, evidence-informed guidance for supporting someone with an eating disorder: how to start the conversation, what to say and what to avoid, how to encourage treatment without being controlling, and how to take care of yourself through the process.

**Key Facts:**
1. Family and social support is consistently identified as one of the strongest predictors of eating disorder recovery outcomes [1].
2. Research shows that expressed emotion — particularly criticism and emotional overinvolvement — in families is associated with worse eating disorder outcomes, while warmth and support are associated with better outcomes [2].
3. The "New Maudsley Approach" provides a skills-based framework for carers of individuals with eating disorders, teaching communication skills and self-care strategies [3].
4. Carers of individuals with eating disorders experience significant psychological distress, including anxiety, depression, and caregiver burnout — their own wellbeing requires attention [4].
5. Comments about weight, food, and appearance — even well-intentioned ones — can trigger or worsen eating disorder symptoms [5].

## Starting the Conversation

One of the most difficult moments for a loved one is raising the concern for the first time. The fear of saying the wrong thing, being rejected, damaging the relationship, or making things worse can paralyze well-intentioned family members and friends into silence.

Research and clinical experience suggest the following approach [3, 5]:

**Choose the right moment.** Have the conversation in a private, calm setting — not during a meal, not in public, not during an argument, and not when either of you is rushed or distressed. Express that you want to talk about something important and ask if now is a good time.

**Lead with care, not diagnosis.** Do not open with "I think you have an eating disorder." Instead, describe what you have observed and express how it makes you feel: "I've noticed that you seem anxious around mealtimes and you've been skipping dinners with us. I'm worried about you because I care about you."

**Use "I" statements.** Frame your observations in terms of your own experience: "I've noticed..." "I'm concerned because..." "I feel worried when..." This reduces defensiveness and avoids sounding accusatory.

**Do not comment on weight or appearance.** Never mention weight loss, weight gain, how thin someone looks, or how their body has changed. Even comments that seem positive ("you look like you've lost weight") can reinforce eating disorder behavior. Comments that seem concerned ("you look too thin") can trigger shame and concealment.

**Be prepared for denial or anger.** The person may deny having a problem, become angry, accuse you of overreacting, or refuse to discuss it. This is common and does not mean you should not have raised the concern. Plant the seed and let them know you are available when they are ready.

**Offer to help, not ultimatums.** "I'd like to help you find someone to talk to about this. Would that be okay?" is more effective than "You need to get help or else."

## What Helps: Evidence-Based Approaches for Carers

Professor Janet Treasure and colleagues at King's College London developed the "New Maudsley Approach" for carers of individuals with eating disorders — a skills-based framework that teaches family members how to support recovery without inadvertently maintaining the eating disorder [3].

The approach uses animal metaphors to describe common carer responses and their effects:

**The Kangaroo** is overprotective, doing everything for the person with the eating disorder, accommodating every demand, and tiptoeing around the illness. While motivated by love, kangaroo behavior can inadvertently enable the eating disorder by removing the natural consequences that might motivate change.

**The Rhinoceros** charges at the eating disorder head-on — confronting, demanding, criticizing, and trying to force change through sheer determination. While understandable, rhinoceros behavior typically increases resistance, damages the relationship, and worsens the eating disorder through heightened conflict and stress.

**The Dolphin** — the approach the New Maudsley model encourages — is calm, consistent, and strategically supportive. The dolphin nudges gently and persistently, maintains warmth and connection, sets appropriate boundaries without hostility, and communicates concern without criticism [6].

Key communication skills from the New Maudsley Approach:

**Motivational interviewing techniques.** Ask open-ended questions about what the person wants for their life. Reflect their own words back to them. Express empathy. Highlight discrepancies between their values and their eating disorder behavior — without judgment.

**Externalization.** Separate the person from the eating disorder. Talk about the eating disorder as something that is happening to them, not something they are: "The anorexia is telling you not to eat" rather than "You are refusing to eat."

**Reduce expressed emotion.** Research by Eisler and colleagues consistently found that high expressed emotion (criticism, hostility, emotional overinvolvement) in families predicted worse eating disorder outcomes, while low expressed emotion predicted better outcomes [2]. This does not mean suppressing emotions — it means learning to express concern without criticism and care without control.

## What to Avoid

Certain well-intentioned behaviors can inadvertently worsen eating disorders:

**Do not become the food police.** Monitoring every bite, commenting on what they eat, scrutinizing their plate, or interrogating them about meals creates a surveillance dynamic that increases shame, secrecy, and resistance. Unless you are participating in family-based treatment under clinical guidance, meal supervision should be left to professionals.

**Do not offer diet advice or nutritional guidance.** You may be tempted to share information about "healthy eating" or suggest dietary changes. For someone with an eating disorder, any external dietary input — even well-intentioned — can be co-opted by the eating disorder as new food rules. Leave nutritional guidance to the eating disorder dietitian [7].

**Do not comment on their body.** Any body comment — positive, negative, or neutral — can trigger eating disorder cognitions. "You look great" can be interpreted as "you look like you've gained weight" or "I'm being monitored." "You look thin" can reinforce restriction. The safest approach is to not comment on their body at all.

**Do not take it personally.** Eating disorder behaviors — lying about food, hiding food, becoming angry at mealtimes, withdrawing from the family — are symptoms of the illness, not personal attacks. The person is not trying to hurt you. They are trapped in a condition that distorts their behavior.

**Do not threaten or issue ultimatums.** "If you don't eat, I'm leaving" or "You have to stop this or I won't support you anymore" may feel like you are drawing a line, but ultimatums typically increase shame and secrecy rather than motivating change [8].

## Encouraging Treatment

If your loved one has not yet sought professional help, you can gently encourage treatment:

**Offer to help with logistics.** Finding a therapist, making a phone call, driving to an appointment, researching insurance coverage — these practical steps can reduce the barriers that prevent help-seeking.

**Normalize professional support.** "A lot of people get help for this kind of thing, and it makes a real difference" reduces the perception that seeking help is a sign of weakness or failure.

**Share information without lecturing.** Leaving a book, article, or website (like this one) where they can find it — without demanding they read it — provides information without pressure.

**Respect their autonomy (for adults).** For adult loved ones, you cannot force treatment. You can express concern, offer help, maintain the relationship, and set boundaries around your own wellbeing — but the decision to enter treatment ultimately belongs to them.

**For parents of minors,** the dynamic is different. Parents have the authority and responsibility to arrange treatment for their children. Family-based treatment (FBT) explicitly empowers parents to take charge of their child's recovery (see PSY-EAT-033).

## Taking Care of Yourself

Supporting someone with an eating disorder is emotionally exhausting. Research by Treasure and colleagues (2007) found that carers of individuals with eating disorders experience levels of psychological distress comparable to carers of individuals with psychotic disorders — including anxiety, depression, social isolation, and burnout [4].

Your wellbeing is not a luxury — it is a necessity. You cannot support someone else's recovery if you are depleted yourself.

**Seek your own support.** Therapy, carer support groups, and peer support networks provide spaces where you can process your own emotions without burdening the person you are caring for. Organizations like FEAST (Families Empowered and Supporting Treatment of Eating Disorders) and Beat (UK) offer dedicated carer programs [9].

**Set boundaries.** You can love someone with an eating disorder while also setting boundaries around your own wellbeing: "I love you and I'm here for you, but I cannot monitor your eating at every meal without it affecting my own mental health."

**Educate yourself.** Understanding eating disorders — their neurobiology, their maintaining mechanisms, their treatment — reduces the helplessness and frustration that come from not understanding what is happening. This article series is designed to provide that understanding.

**Maintain your own life.** Continue to engage in your own activities, relationships, and interests. Your life should not be entirely consumed by the eating disorder — for your sake and for the sake of the person you are supporting, who benefits from having a loved one who is emotionally resourced [10].

> 💡 **Spark:** You cannot recover for someone. But you can be the reason they believe recovery is possible — by staying present, staying warm, staying informed, and staying well enough to be there for the long haul.

## What You Can Try

- **Learn about eating disorders.** Reading this article series is a strong start. Understanding the condition transforms your ability to respond effectively.

- **Practice the dolphin approach.** Calm, consistent, warm, and strategic. Nudge gently. Express care without criticism. Set boundaries without hostility.

- **Join a carer support group.** FEAST (feast-ed.org), Beat (beateatingdisorders.org.uk), and the National Alliance for Eating Disorders all offer carer-specific support.

- **Never comment on weight or appearance.** Make this a firm personal rule.

- **Take care of yourself.** Your wellbeing is not selfish — it is essential. Seek your own therapy. Maintain your friendships. Protect your sleep. This is a marathon, not a sprint.
