> **Summary:** One in three people with an eating disorder is male. Yet men remain drastically underdiagnosed, undertreated, and underrepresented in eating disorder research, clinical practice, and public awareness. The stereotype that eating disorders are "women's conditions" is one of the most dangerous myths in mental health — it prevents recognition, delays treatment, and denies help to millions of men who are suffering in silence. Male eating disorders often present differently from the stereotypical image: the drive for muscularity may coexist with or replace the drive for thinness, exercise compulsivity may be more prominent than dietary restriction, and body image concerns may center on leanness and muscle definition rather than weight alone. This article examines the evidence on eating disorders in men, the barriers to diagnosis and treatment, and what it will take to close the gender gap in eating disorder care.

**Key Facts:**
1. Approximately one in three people with an eating disorder is male — a ratio that has been consistently documented across epidemiological studies [1].
2. Men with eating disorders wait significantly longer than women to seek treatment, with average delays exceeding those already lengthy averages for women [2].
3. Screening tools for eating disorders were predominantly developed and validated on female samples, reducing their sensitivity for detecting male presentations [3].
4. Male eating disorders are more likely to involve the drive for muscularity, excessive exercise, and anabolic steroid use than female eating disorders, though restriction, bingeing, and purging also occur [4].
5. Mortality risk for men with eating disorders is at least as high as — and in some studies higher than — mortality risk for women with eating disorders [5].

## The Scale of the Problem

The belief that eating disorders predominantly affect women has persisted for decades despite consistent epidemiological evidence to the contrary. Research from the National Comorbidity Survey Replication found that the lifetime prevalence of anorexia nervosa in men was 0.3%, bulimia nervosa 0.5%, and binge eating disorder 2.0% — and these figures almost certainly represent undercounts, given the barriers to identification and self-report in male populations [1].

When you aggregate across all eating disorder diagnoses, approximately 10 million men in the United States alone will experience a clinically significant eating disorder during their lifetime. Globally, the figures are in the hundreds of millions. These are not small numbers hidden in a footnote — they represent a population-scale public health crisis that has been systematically overlooked because it does not match the cultural image of who gets eating disorders.

Dr. Jason Nagata at the University of California, San Francisco, has led groundbreaking research on eating disorders in adolescent boys and young men, documenting prevalence rates, risk factors, and health consequences that have been largely absent from the clinical literature until recent years [4]. His work, along with that of Dr. Stuart Murray, Dr. Scott Griffiths, and others, has established that male eating disorders are not rare variants of female conditions but a distinct clinical reality with its own characteristics, risk factors, and treatment needs.

## How Male Eating Disorders Present Differently

While men can and do develop eating disorders that look identical to those described in women — including classic anorexia nervosa with restriction and fear of weight gain, bulimia nervosa with binge-purge cycling, and binge eating disorder — male presentations frequently involve additional or alternative features that current screening tools are not designed to detect.

**The drive for muscularity.** Research by McCreary and Sasse (2000) developed the Drive for Muscularity Scale and documented that body dissatisfaction in men often centers on perceived insufficient muscularity rather than (or in addition to) perceived excessive weight [6]. A man with an eating disorder may not report "wanting to be thinner" — the question most screening tools ask — but instead report wanting to be "leaner," "more defined," "bigger," or "more shredded." These goals can drive the same restrictive, compensatory, and compulsive behaviors that characterize eating disorders in women, but framed in muscularity language that clinicians may not recognize as eating disorder pathology.

**Exercise compulsivity.** Compulsive exercise — exercise that is driven by obligation rather than enjoyment, that produces distress when interrupted, and that continues despite injury or illness — is more prominent in male than female eating disorder presentations. Research by Murray and colleagues (2017) found that exercise compulsivity in men with eating disorders was associated with greater overall psychopathology and worse treatment outcomes [7]. Compulsive exercise may be the presenting symptom that is most visible, yet it is rarely screened for in clinical settings because it is culturally encouraged and associated with "health" rather than pathology.

**Anabolic steroid use.** As discussed in PSY-EAT-019, use of anabolic-androgenic steroids to enhance muscularity is significantly elevated in men with body image disturbance and eating disorders. AAS use carries serious medical risks (cardiac, hepatic, hormonal, psychiatric) that are often not assessed by eating disorder treatment programs.

**Binge eating disorder as the most common presentation.** While anorexia nervosa receives the most public attention, binge eating disorder is the most prevalent eating disorder in men — and is particularly underdiagnosed because binge eating in men is often normalized or dismissed as "overeating." The emotional distress, loss of control, and functional impairment associated with BED in men are comparable to those in women.

## Why Men Are Underdiagnosed

The underdiagnosis of eating disorders in men is not a single failure but a cascade of barriers that compound at every level of the identification and treatment system.

**Cultural stereotype.** The dominant cultural image of eating disorders is a young, thin, white woman. This image is pervasive in media, educational materials, awareness campaigns, and even clinical training. When a man — particularly a man in a larger body or a muscular body — presents with eating disorder symptoms, the cultural prototype does not activate, and the condition may go unrecognized by both the individual and their healthcare provider.

**Screening tool bias.** Many eating disorder screening instruments were developed using exclusively or predominantly female samples and use language that maps poorly onto male eating disorder presentations. Questions about "wanting to be thinner" may miss men who want to be "leaner" or "bigger." Questions about dietary restriction may miss men whose primary pathology is compulsive exercise. Questions about body dissatisfaction may use body areas (hips, thighs) that are less salient for men than for women [3].

Research by Darcy and colleagues (2012) found that when screening tools were adapted to include muscularity-related items alongside thinness-related items, detection rates for eating disorder symptoms in men increased significantly [3]. This suggests that the tools themselves have been structurally biased toward female presentations.

**Male help-seeking barriers.** Men face additional barriers to seeking help for eating disorders related to gender role expectations. Admitting to an eating disorder — a condition culturally coded as "feminine" — may feel like an admission of emasculation. Research by Griffiths and colleagues (2015) found that men with eating disorders reported significantly higher levels of stigma and lower rates of help-seeking than women with equivalent symptom severity [8].

**Clinical training gaps.** Medical and psychology training programs devote limited attention to eating disorders generally, and even less to male presentations specifically. A clinician who received training on eating disorders primarily through case examples featuring women may not recognize the same condition when it presents in a man wearing gym clothes who reports that his protein intake is "a little rigid."

**The "fit" versus "sick" paradox.** A man who exercises compulsively, controls his diet rigidly, and monitors his body composition obsessively may be perceived — by himself and others — as healthy, disciplined, and "into fitness" rather than as someone with an eating disorder. Fitness culture actively celebrates the behaviors that eating disorder clinicians would identify as pathological, creating a cultural camouflage that is particularly effective for men.

## The Mortality Question

The mortality risk associated with eating disorders in men is at least as high as — and in some studies higher than — the risk for women. A meta-analysis by Arcelus and colleagues (2011) found that the standardized mortality ratio for eating disorders was elevated across genders, and subsequent studies have found that men with anorexia nervosa may have higher mortality than women with the same condition, possibly because they are diagnosed later and with greater medical severity [5].

The mortality risk extends beyond the eating disorder itself. Suicide rates are elevated in men with eating disorders, as is the risk of medical complications from behaviors that are frequently unmonitored — including the cardiovascular risks of anabolic steroid use, the electrolyte disturbances of purging, and the organ damage of prolonged restriction.

These mortality figures are particularly tragic given that they occur in a population that could be identified and treated earlier — if the clinical system were designed to see them.

## What Better Care Looks Like

Addressing the gap in eating disorder care for men requires changes at every level of the system.

**Screening.** Universal eating disorder screening should include muscularity-related items, exercise compulsivity items, and questions about body composition concerns alongside traditional thinness and weight questions. The Male Body Attitudes Scale (MBAS), developed by Tylka, Bergeron, and Schwartz (2005), is one of the few validated instruments specifically designed for male body image assessment [9].

**Clinical training.** Training programs for mental health and medical professionals should include explicit education on male eating disorder presentations, including muscle dysmorphia, exercise compulsivity, AAS use, and binge eating disorder in men.

**Treatment adaptation.** While the core evidence-based treatments for eating disorders (CBT-E, FBT, IPT) are applicable to men, therapeutic language and framing may need adaptation. Discussing "the drive for muscularity" rather than "the drive for thinness," addressing exercise compulsivity as a primary target, and acknowledging the gender-specific shame and stigma that men experience can improve therapeutic alliance and outcomes [10].

**Representation.** Awareness campaigns, educational materials, and media representation of eating disorders should systematically include male images, male voices, and male stories. When men see themselves reflected in eating disorder messaging, they are more likely to recognize their own experience and seek help.

**Research inclusion.** Eating disorder research has historically excluded or undersampled men. Future clinical trials, epidemiological studies, and treatment development efforts must include adequate male representation to generate evidence that applies to the full population of people affected [11].

> 💡 **Spark:** An eating disorder does not check your gender before it takes hold. But the healthcare system, the screening tools, and the cultural narrative have been checking — and dismissing millions of men whose suffering does not match the stereotype. Every man with an eating disorder who was told he couldn't have one because "men don't get eating disorders" represents a failure of the system, not a failure of the man.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are a man experiencing distress around eating, body image, or exercise,** know that your experience is clinically recognized, common, and treatable. You are not alone, and you are not "making it up."

- **Seek an eating disorder specialist.** General therapists may lack familiarity with male presentations. Look for clinicians who explicitly mention treating men, muscle dysmorphia, or exercise compulsivity in their areas of expertise.

- **Challenge the fitness culture narrative.** If your relationship with exercise feels compulsive rather than joyful, if missing a workout produces intense anxiety, or if your dietary rules create significant distress, these patterns warrant professional attention regardless of how "healthy" they might look from the outside.

- **Talk to someone.** Breaking the silence is the most difficult and most important step. Tell one person — a friend, a partner, a doctor, a helpline. The National Alliance for Eating Disorders helpline (1-866-662-1235) supports people of all genders.
