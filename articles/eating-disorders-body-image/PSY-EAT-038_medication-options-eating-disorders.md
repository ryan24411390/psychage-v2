> **Summary:** Medication is not a first-line treatment for most eating disorders — psychological therapy remains the primary evidence-based approach. However, pharmacotherapy can play a meaningful adjunctive role for specific eating disorder diagnoses and for the psychiatric comorbidities (depression, anxiety, OCD) that frequently accompany them. The evidence landscape varies dramatically by diagnosis: fluoxetine has robust evidence for bulimia nervosa, lisdexamfetamine is FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe binge eating disorder, and no medication has demonstrated consistent efficacy for anorexia nervosa itself. This article reviews what the evidence supports, what it does not, and how medication fits into a comprehensive eating disorder treatment plan.

**Key Facts:**
1. Fluoxetine (Prozac) at 60mg/day is the only FDA-approved medication for bulimia nervosa, with evidence showing 50–67% reduction in binge-purge frequency [1].
2. Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) is the only FDA-approved medication for binge eating disorder, shown to reduce binge days by approximately 3.5 days per week compared to placebo [2].
3. No medication has demonstrated consistent efficacy for the core symptoms of anorexia nervosa (restriction, weight phobia, body image disturbance) in randomized controlled trials [3].
4. SSRIs are generally ineffective during the acute underweight phase of anorexia nervosa, likely because the malnourished brain lacks adequate serotonin precursors to respond to the medication [4].
5. Medication is most effective when combined with evidence-based psychotherapy — medication alone produces lower remission rates than combination treatment for bulimia and BED [5].

## Bulimia Nervosa: The Strongest Medication Evidence

Bulimia nervosa has the most robust evidence base for pharmacological treatment of any eating disorder.

**Fluoxetine (Prozac)** is the gold standard. The landmark Fluoxetine Bulimia Nervosa Collaborative Study Group trial (1992) demonstrated that fluoxetine at 60mg/day — notably higher than the typical dose for depression (20mg) — produced a 67% reduction in binge frequency and a 56% reduction in purge frequency compared to placebo [1]. This finding has been replicated in multiple subsequent trials. Fluoxetine remains the only FDA-approved medication for bulimia nervosa.

The mechanism is thought to involve serotonin's role in satiety signaling, impulse control, and mood regulation — all of which are dysregulated in bulimia. By enhancing serotonergic transmission, fluoxetine may reduce the urge to binge, improve impulse control, and stabilize mood in ways that make the binge-purge cycle less compelling [6].

**Other SSRIs** (sertraline, fluvoxamine, citalopram) have also shown efficacy in smaller trials, though the evidence is not as robust as for fluoxetine. In clinical practice, other SSRIs are sometimes used when fluoxetine is not tolerated.

**Important limitation:** Medication alone produces lower remission rates for bulimia than CBT-E alone, and CBT-E plus fluoxetine does not consistently outperform CBT-E alone in randomized trials [5]. This means that while fluoxetine is a useful adjunct — particularly for patients who do not respond fully to psychotherapy — it should not be used as a standalone treatment. The APA and NICE guidelines recommend psychotherapy as the primary treatment, with medication as an adjunctive option.

## Binge Eating Disorder: Expanding Options

The pharmacological evidence for binge eating disorder has expanded significantly in recent years.

**Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse)** was approved by the FDA in 2015 for the treatment of moderate-to-severe binge eating disorder in adults — the first and, as of this writing, only medication specifically approved for BED. Clinical trials demonstrated that lisdexamfetamine at 50–70mg/day reduced binge eating days by approximately 3.5 days per week compared to placebo [2]. The mechanism is thought to involve dopamine and norepinephrine modulation, improving impulse control and reducing the reward salience of binge eating.

Important considerations: lisdexamfetamine is a controlled substance (Schedule II) with potential for abuse and dependence. It is contraindicated in patients with a history of stimulant abuse, cardiovascular disease, or concurrent monoamine oxidase inhibitor use. Weight loss is a common side effect, which complicates its use in a population where the relationship with weight is already pathological — clinicians must monitor for the development of restrictive eating as a secondary effect.

**SSRIs** have shown moderate efficacy for BED. Meta-analyses indicate that SSRIs reduce binge frequency and improve mood, though the effect sizes are smaller than for lisdexamfetamine. Fluoxetine, sertraline, and escitalopram have all been studied with positive results [7].

**Topiramate** has demonstrated efficacy for reducing binge eating frequency and producing weight loss in BED trials, but side effects — including cognitive impairment ("brain fog"), paresthesias, and kidney stones — limit its acceptability. It is used off-label when other options are insufficient [8].

**As with bulimia,** CBT-E alone produces comparable or superior outcomes to medication alone for BED, and the combination of CBT-E plus medication does not consistently outperform CBT-E alone. Medication is a useful adjunct, not a substitute for psychotherapy.

## Anorexia Nervosa: The Medication Disappointment

Despite decades of research, no medication has demonstrated consistent efficacy for the core symptoms of anorexia nervosa — the drive for thinness, fear of weight gain, body image disturbance, and dietary restriction.

**SSRIs** are frequently prescribed to individuals with anorexia nervosa, but the evidence for their efficacy during the acute, underweight phase of illness is weak. The Kaye hypothesis explains why: SSRIs work by blocking serotonin reuptake, but serotonin synthesis depends on dietary tryptophan, which is depleted during malnutrition. A malnourished brain lacks the raw materials to produce the serotonin that the SSRI is designed to modulate [4]. Research by Walsh and colleagues (2006) confirmed that fluoxetine did not improve outcomes for anorexia nervosa in a randomized controlled trial, even when combined with psychotherapy [3].

**After weight restoration,** SSRIs may have a role in relapse prevention. Kaye and colleagues (2001) found that fluoxetine reduced relapse rates in weight-restored patients with anorexia nervosa compared to placebo [9]. However, a larger subsequent trial by Walsh and colleagues did not replicate this finding, leaving the evidence inconclusive.

**Olanzapine** (an atypical antipsychotic) has shown modest evidence for promoting weight gain and reducing anxiety in anorexia nervosa in small trials. A randomized controlled trial by Attia and colleagues (2019) found that olanzapine produced significantly greater weight gain than placebo, though the magnitude of the effect was modest [10]. Olanzapine is sometimes used off-label as an adjunct during the weight restoration phase, particularly for patients with severe anxiety or agitation around eating.

**Other medications studied for anorexia** — including zinc supplementation, dronabinol (synthetic THC), oxytocin, and D-cycloserine — have shown mixed or preliminary results that do not yet support routine clinical use.

The bottom line for anorexia nervosa: nutritional rehabilitation and evidence-based psychotherapy (FBT for adolescents, CBT-E or MANTRA for adults) are the primary treatments. Medication may address comorbid conditions (depression, anxiety, OCD) but does not treat the core eating disorder.

## Medication for Comorbid Conditions

Even when medication does not effectively target the eating disorder itself, it can play an important role in treating the psychiatric comorbidities that frequently accompany eating disorders:

**Depression** co-occurs with eating disorders at rates of 50–75%. SSRIs (after weight restoration in anorexia) are the first-line pharmacological treatment for comorbid depression. However, clinicians should be aware that many depressive symptoms in eating disorders are consequences of malnutrition rather than independent depressive illness — and may resolve with nutritional rehabilitation alone.

**Anxiety disorders** (generalized anxiety, social anxiety, panic disorder) co-occur at rates of 50–60%. SSRIs and SNRIs are appropriate treatments for comorbid anxiety. Benzodiazepines should be used with caution due to the risk of dependence and the potential for disinhibition of eating behavior.

**OCD** co-occurs at elevated rates, particularly with anorexia nervosa. High-dose SSRIs or clomipramine may be appropriate for comorbid OCD, following the same treatment guidelines as for OCD without an eating disorder.

**ADHD** co-occurs with BED at elevated rates. Stimulant medications may serve a dual purpose — treating ADHD and reducing binge eating — but must be monitored carefully given the potential for appetite suppression and weight loss.

## Principles for Medication Use in Eating Disorders

Several principles guide appropriate medication use in eating disorder treatment:

**Psychotherapy first.** Medication should generally be considered as an adjunct to evidence-based psychotherapy, not as a standalone treatment. The evidence consistently shows that psychotherapy alone outperforms medication alone for most eating disorders.

**Wait for nutritional adequacy.** In anorexia nervosa, medication trials are generally more appropriate after weight restoration, when the brain's neurochemistry has stabilized enough to respond to pharmacological intervention.

**Target specific symptoms.** Use medication to target specific, defined symptoms: binge frequency, purge frequency, comorbid depression, comorbid anxiety. Avoid prescribing medication for vague indications.

**Monitor for adverse effects.** Individuals with eating disorders may be more vulnerable to medication side effects due to malnutrition, electrolyte disturbances, and altered drug metabolism.

**Communicate openly.** Patients should understand what the medication is expected to do, what it cannot do, and what side effects to expect. Shared decision-making improves adherence and reduces the sense of being "medicated" without consent.

> 💡 **Spark:** There is no pill that cures an eating disorder. But there are medications that can quiet the neurological noise — the relentless binge urges, the crashing mood, the spiraling anxiety — enough for the real work of psychological recovery to take hold.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are considering medication,** discuss it with a psychiatrist experienced in eating disorders — not a general practitioner. Eating disorder pharmacotherapy requires specialized knowledge.

- **Do not expect medication to replace therapy.** The most effective treatment for most eating disorders is evidence-based psychotherapy, with medication as an adjunct when needed.

- **If you have been prescribed an SSRI while severely underweight,** discuss the timing with your treatment team. SSRIs may be more effective after nutritional rehabilitation.
