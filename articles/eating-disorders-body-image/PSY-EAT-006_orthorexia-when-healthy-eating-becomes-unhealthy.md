> **Summary:** Orthorexia nervosa — a term coined in 1997 by physician Steven Bratman — describes an obsessive fixation on eating foods perceived as pure, correct, or virtuous that paradoxically becomes a source of malnutrition, social isolation, and profound psychological distress. Unlike anorexia or bulimia, the primary preoccupation in orthorexia is not with how much one eats or with body size, but with the perceived quality and purity of food. While orthorexia is not yet a formal diagnosis in the DSM-5-TR, a growing body of clinical research recognizes it as a distinct pattern of disordered eating with significant health consequences. In a culture that relentlessly equates dietary restriction with virtue, orthorexia sits at a dangerous intersection of wellness ideology and psychiatric vulnerability.

**Key Facts:**
1. The term "orthorexia nervosa" was coined by physician Steven Bratman in 1997 to describe an unhealthy obsession with eating "pure" or "correct" foods [1].
2. Orthorexia is not a formal DSM-5-TR diagnosis, but proposed diagnostic criteria have been published by Dunn and Bratman (2016) and are used in clinical research [2].
3. Prevalence estimates range from 1% to 7% of the general population, with rates as high as 35–58% in specific populations such as nutrition professionals, fitness enthusiasts, and healthcare workers [3].
4. Orthorexia can lead to severe malnutrition, osteoporosis, anemia, and cardiac complications when dietary rules eliminate entire food groups over extended periods [4].
5. Social media use — particularly exposure to wellness, fitness, and "clean eating" content — is significantly correlated with orthorexic tendencies in multiple studies across countries [5].

## The Paradox: How Pursuit of Health Becomes Its Opposite

Orthorexia begins where good intentions meet psychological vulnerability. The initial desire is often reasonable: to eat more nutritiously, to avoid processed foods, to feel better physically. But for some individuals, this desire escalates into an all-consuming preoccupation that narrows dietary variety to an increasingly restricted set of "permitted" foods, generates intense anxiety or guilt when food rules are violated, and progressively damages both physical health and quality of life.

Dr. Steven Bratman, who first described the phenomenon — and who experienced it himself — emphasized that the defining feature of orthorexia is not what someone eats, but how the pursuit of dietary purity functions psychologically [1]. A person with orthorexia may spend hours researching food sources, planning meals, and evaluating the "cleanliness" of ingredients. They may refuse to eat food prepared by others due to uncertainty about ingredients. They may progressively eliminate entire food groups — first processed foods, then sugar, then grains, then oils, then cooked foods — until their diet is severely restricted.

The paradox is precise: the obsession with eating for health produces malnutrition. Research by Moroze and colleagues (2015) documented cases in which individuals presenting with severe malnutrition — including anemia, pancytopenia, and metabolic acidosis — attributed their restrictive diets to health optimization rather than weight control [4]. Their commitment to dietary purity had become the mechanism of their physical decline.

This paradox is difficult to see from the inside. Because the behaviors of orthorexia align with cultural values about health and discipline, the person and their social network may initially celebrate the restriction. Comments like "you're so disciplined" and "I wish I could eat that well" reinforce the behavior precisely when intervention would be most effective. By the time the consequences become visible — social isolation, nutritional deficiency, psychological rigidity — the pattern is deeply entrenched.

## What Drives Orthorexia: Beyond Food, Into Psychology

The psychological profile of orthorexia overlaps significantly with obsessive-compulsive disorder, perfectionism, and anxiety — more so than with the body image disturbance that characterizes anorexia and bulimia. Research by Barrada and Roncero (2018) found that orthorexic tendencies were more strongly predicted by obsessive-compulsive symptoms and perfectionism than by drive for thinness or body dissatisfaction [6].

This finding reshapes how we understand orthorexia. The core psychological driver is not "I want to be thin" but "I need to do this correctly." The food rules in orthorexia function similarly to compulsions in OCD — they are performed to reduce anxiety, provide a sense of order and control, and prevent a feared outcome (in this case, consuming something "impure" or "harmful"). Violating these rules produces intense distress, guilt, and self-punishment — often including further dietary restriction as atonement.

The need for control is a recurring theme. Research by Koven and Abry (2015) proposed that orthorexia emerges when individuals who are psychologically predisposed to rigidity and perfectionism encounter environmental triggers — such as a health scare, a wellness documentary, a social media community, or a stressful life transition — that activate dietary restriction as a coping mechanism [7]. The food rules provide structure, predictability, and a sense of moral superiority in a world that feels chaotic or threatening.

Identity is deeply implicated. For many individuals with orthorexia, their dietary practices become central to their sense of self. They identify as "clean eaters," "whole food advocates," or adherents of specific dietary philosophies. Their social networks may be organized around these identities. Challenging the dietary restriction threatens not just a behavior but a core component of identity — which helps explain why individuals with orthorexia often resist clinical intervention even when they acknowledge that their health is suffering.

## The Wellness Industry: Cultural Soil for Orthorexia

Orthorexia cannot be understood in isolation from the cultural environment that produces it. The contemporary wellness industry — worth an estimated $4.4 trillion globally, according to the Global Wellness Institute — provides the ideological substrate on which orthorexia grows [8].

The wellness narrative operates through several mechanisms that are directly relevant to orthorexia:

**Moralization of food.** The language of wellness routinely frames food in moral terms — foods are "pure" or "toxic," "whole" or "processed," "nourishing" or "inflammatory." This moralization transforms eating from a biological activity into a moral performance, in which choosing the "right" foods signals virtue and choosing the "wrong" foods signals failure. For a person with perfectionistic tendencies, this framing is psychologically activating.

**Fear-based messaging.** Wellness content frequently employs fear to motivate dietary changes — warnings about "hidden toxins," "inflammation," "gut damage," and "chemicals in your food." While some of these concerns have scientific basis, the wellness industry often amplifies them far beyond what evidence supports, creating a pervasive sense of danger associated with ordinary eating. For a person with anxiety-based vulnerability, this fear messaging can trigger and maintain restrictive food rules.

**The authority of influencers.** Social media has created an ecosystem in which individuals without clinical training can present themselves as dietary authorities. Research by Turner and Lefevre (2017) found that higher Instagram use was significantly associated with greater orthorexic symptoms, particularly when individuals followed appearance-focused "healthy eating" accounts [5]. The curated, aesthetically idealized presentation of restrictive diets on social media normalizes eating patterns that would be clinically concerning if assessed by a qualified professional.

**The blurred line between healthy and disordered.** Perhaps the most dangerous feature of the wellness environment is that it makes it genuinely difficult to distinguish healthy dietary attention from orthorexia. This is by design — the wellness industry profits from the anxiety that drives people to seek increasingly stringent dietary solutions. Bratman himself acknowledged that the line between conscientious eating and orthorexia is not fixed; it depends on the degree to which food rules generate distress, restrict dietary variety, impair social functioning, and undermine physical health [1].

## Clinical Recognition: Where the Field Stands

Despite growing clinical and research attention, orthorexia nervosa remains without a formal diagnosis in either the DSM-5-TR or the ICD-11. This absence has practical consequences: it complicates insurance coverage, limits research funding, and contributes to clinical uncertainty about when and how to intervene.

The most widely cited proposed diagnostic criteria were published by Dunn and Bratman (2016) in the journal *Eating Behaviors* [2]. Their criteria include: an obsessive focus on dietary practices believed to promote health, accompanied by exaggerated emotional distress when food rules are violated; escalating dietary restrictions over time; and clinically significant impairment in physical health, psychological wellbeing, or social functioning as a consequence of the dietary preoccupation.

Measurement has been a persistent challenge. The most widely used assessment tool, the ORTO-15, developed by Donini and colleagues (2005), has been criticized for poor psychometric properties and for pathologizing ordinary health-conscious eating [9]. More recent instruments — including the Düsseldorf Orthorexia Scale (DOS) developed by Barthels and colleagues (2015) and the Teruel Orthorexia Scale (TOS) — have shown improved validity and reliability [10].

The absence of a formal diagnosis creates a clinical paradox: clinicians encounter orthorexic presentations regularly, but they must classify them under existing diagnostic categories — typically OSFED (Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder) or OCD — to justify treatment. Some researchers argue that orthorexia should be classified as a subtype of OCD rather than an eating disorder, given its psychological overlap with obsessive-compulsive processes. Others argue that its relationship to food and nutrition makes it fundamentally an eating disorder. The debate remains unresolved.

What is not debated is that the clinical presentations are real, the suffering is genuine, and the health consequences can be severe. Whether or not orthorexia receives its own diagnostic code, clinicians need frameworks for identifying and treating it.

## Treatment Approaches: What Shows Promise

Treatment for orthorexia draws from evidence-based approaches for both eating disorders and OCD, reflecting its position at the intersection of these conditions.

**Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)** adapted for orthorexia targets the rigid food rules, the catastrophic beliefs about dietary purity, and the emotional distress associated with rule violation. Treatment typically involves identifying and challenging specific food beliefs ("if I eat this, I will become sick"), behavioral experiments in which the person deliberately introduces previously forbidden foods, and developing flexibility around eating [11].

**Exposure and response prevention (ERP)**, the gold-standard treatment for OCD, has been applied to orthorexia with promising results. In ERP, the person is gradually exposed to anxiety-provoking food situations — eating a food they consider impure, eating at a restaurant where they cannot control ingredients, eating food prepared by someone else — while refraining from performing their typical anxiety-reducing rituals (checking labels, researching ingredients, restricting further).

**Nutritional rehabilitation and education** with a dietitian experienced in eating disorders helps restore nutritional adequacy while gradually broadening dietary variety. This must be done sensitively — for a person whose entire belief system is organized around food quality, being told to eat foods they consider harmful requires careful therapeutic framing.

**Addressing the underlying psychological drivers** — perfectionism, anxiety, need for control, identity fragility — is essential for lasting recovery. Without addressing these root vulnerabilities, the person may abandon one set of food rules only to adopt another.

> 💡 **Spark:** When the pursuit of health becomes the source of harm — when food rules shrink your world instead of nourishing it — that is not wellness. It is a form of suffering that deserves clinical attention, compassion, and effective treatment. Eating well should make life larger, not smaller.

## What You Can Try

If your relationship with "healthy eating" has become a source of anxiety, rigidity, or social isolation:

- **Notice whether your food rules have expanded over time.** If the list of foods you consider unacceptable has grown while the list of foods you allow has shrunk, that trajectory is worth examining honestly.

- **Ask yourself: does my way of eating enhance my life or restrict it?** Can you eat at a friend's house? Can you travel without extreme planning? Can you eat something outside your rules without significant distress? If the answer to these is no, the dietary pattern may be causing more harm than the foods you are avoiding.

- **Seek a clinician who understands orthorexia.** This may be an eating disorder specialist or an OCD specialist — ideally someone familiar with both. Bring this article if it helps start the conversation.

- **Gently diversify.** With professional guidance, begin reintroducing foods you have eliminated. Start with foods that generate the least anxiety and work gradually. Track your emotional responses using Psychage's Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal).

- **Audit your media diet.** Unfollow or mute social media accounts that amplify food anxiety, moralize eating, or promote increasingly restrictive dietary rules. What you consume digitally shapes what feels safe to consume physically.
