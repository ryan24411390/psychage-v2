> **Summary:** The overlap between autism spectrum conditions and eating disorders is one of the most actively researched and clinically significant intersections in contemporary mental health. Research consistently finds elevated rates of eating disorders among autistic individuals and elevated rates of autistic traits among individuals with eating disorders — particularly anorexia nervosa and ARFID. The shared features are striking: sensory processing differences that affect food tolerance, cognitive rigidity that produces rule-bound eating patterns, difficulties with interoception that impair hunger and fullness recognition, and social communication differences that affect mealtime behavior. Understanding this overlap is critical for accurate diagnosis and effective treatment — because interventions designed for neurotypical eating disorder patients may be ineffective or even harmful for autistic individuals, and eating disorder behaviors in autistic people may be misattributed to autism alone.

**Key Facts:**
1. Research estimates that 20–35% of individuals with anorexia nervosa score above clinical cutoffs on measures of autistic traits, compared to approximately 1–2% in the general population [1].
2. ARFID co-occurs with autism at significantly elevated rates, with sensory-based food avoidance being particularly prevalent among autistic individuals [2].
3. Autistic individuals are 5 times more likely to be diagnosed with an eating disorder than non-autistic peers, according to large-scale population studies [3].
4. Cognitive rigidity — difficulty shifting between mental sets — is a shared trait that contributes to the maintenance of both autism-related food patterns and eating disorder behaviors [4].
5. Standard eating disorder treatments may need significant adaptation for autistic individuals, with emerging evidence supporting modified CBT approaches that account for neurodevelopmental differences [5].

## The Overlap: What the Data Shows

The co-occurrence of autism and eating disorders has been documented from two directions: elevated eating disorder rates in autistic populations, and elevated autistic traits in eating disorder populations.

**From the autism direction:** A Swedish population-based study by Huke and colleagues (2013) analyzed national registry data and found that autistic individuals had significantly elevated risk for anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and OSFED compared to non-autistic individuals [3]. Subsequent studies in the UK, Denmark, and the United States have replicated these findings, with risk ratios ranging from 3 to 5 times the general population rate.

**From the eating disorder direction:** Dr. Janet Treasure's research group at King's College London has consistently found that individuals with anorexia nervosa score significantly higher on measures of autistic traits — including the Autism Spectrum Quotient (AQ) and the Systemizing Quotient — than healthy controls [1]. Baron-Cohen and colleagues estimated that approximately 20–35% of individuals with anorexia nervosa score above clinical cutoffs on autism screening measures, a figure dramatically higher than the 1–2% base rate in the general population.

These findings have generated substantial scientific discussion about the nature of the overlap: Is the co-occurrence driven by shared genetic risk factors? Do autism and anorexia share neurobiological mechanisms? Does autism increase vulnerability to eating disorders through specific pathways? Or does prolonged malnutrition in anorexia produce cognitive and behavioral features that mimic autism?

The emerging consensus is that all of these mechanisms likely contribute, to varying degrees, in different individuals. The overlap is not artifactual — it is clinically real and has direct implications for how both conditions are identified and treated.

## Shared Features: Where the Conditions Converge

Several specific features are shared between autism and eating disorders, particularly anorexia nervosa and ARFID.

**Sensory processing differences.** Autistic individuals frequently experience sensory input differently from neurotypical individuals — including taste, texture, smell, temperature, and visual appearance of food. These differences can produce intense aversive responses to specific food characteristics that drive food avoidance. Research by Koomar and colleagues (2021) found that sensory-based food avoidance was significantly more prevalent in autistic children than in neurotypical children, with many meeting or approaching ARFID criteria [2].

In anorexia nervosa, sensory sensitivity may also play a role — though it is often overshadowed by the more visible body image component. Some individuals with anorexia report that foods feel "wrong" in texture or produce aversive oral sensations, contributing to restriction alongside weight-related fears.

**Cognitive rigidity.** Both autism and anorexia nervosa are characterized by cognitive inflexibility — difficulty shifting between mental sets, strong preference for routines and predictability, and discomfort with novel or unstructured situations. In autism, this manifests as insistence on sameness, repetitive behaviors, and rigid adherence to routines. In anorexia, it manifests as rule-bound eating, inflexible food rules, and inability to adapt eating behavior to changing circumstances [4].

Dr. Kate Tchanturia's research at King's College London has been instrumental in documenting cognitive rigidity as a shared feature. Her set-shifting studies using tasks like the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test show impaired performance in both autistic individuals and individuals with anorexia nervosa, with the deficit persisting after weight restoration in anorexia — suggesting it is a trait characteristic rather than a consequence of malnutrition [4].

**Interoceptive difficulties.** Interoception — the ability to perceive and interpret internal body signals such as hunger, fullness, pain, and emotional states — is disrupted in both autism and eating disorders. Autistic individuals frequently report difficulty recognizing when they are hungry, when they are full, and when they are experiencing specific emotions. These same interoceptive difficulties are documented in eating disorders, where impaired hunger and fullness recognition contributes to disordered eating patterns [6].

**Detail-focused processing.** Both autism and anorexia are associated with a cognitive style that emphasizes local detail at the expense of global context — sometimes called "weak central coherence." This processing style may contribute to the hyperawareness of food-specific details (calorie numbers, ingredient lists, precise textures) while losing sight of the broader picture (overall health, quality of life, social connection) [7].

**Social difficulties around eating.** Mealtimes are social events, and both autism and eating disorders can make social eating challenging — though for different reasons. Autistic individuals may struggle with the sensory environment of restaurants, the social demands of conversation during meals, or the unpredictability of shared eating. Individuals with eating disorders may avoid social eating due to fear of observation, inability to control food selection, or shame about eating behavior. When both conditions coexist, the barriers to social eating compound.

## Diagnostic Challenges

The overlap between autism and eating disorders creates significant diagnostic challenges that can lead to missed diagnoses, misdiagnoses, and inappropriate treatment.

**Autism masking eating disorders.** When an autistic individual develops an eating disorder, the eating disorder behaviors may be attributed to autism — "she's always been a picky eater because of her autism" — without recognizing that the eating patterns have escalated to clinical eating disorder severity. Food avoidance that was previously manageable may have intensified into ARFID. Routine-based eating may have rigidified into restriction. The gradual shift from autistic food preferences to clinical eating disorder may go undetected because clinicians attribute all food-related behavior to the autism [8].

**Eating disorders masking autism.** Conversely, autistic traits in individuals with eating disorders may go unrecognized because the autism screening was never conducted. A woman hospitalized for anorexia who shows significant cognitive rigidity, sensory sensitivity, social communication differences, and restricted interests may be treated solely for anorexia — without recognizing that underlying autism is contributing to the eating disorder's maintenance and to the difficulty of treatment. Research suggests that undiagnosed autism in eating disorder patients is associated with poorer treatment response and higher relapse rates [5].

**The malnutrition confound.** Prolonged malnutrition produces cognitive and behavioral effects that resemble autistic features — including social withdrawal, rigid thinking, preoccupation with food-related topics, and reduced cognitive flexibility. This creates a diagnostic challenge: are the autistic-like features observed in an acutely malnourished person genuine autistic traits, or are they consequences of malnutrition that will resolve with nutritional rehabilitation? Longitudinal assessment — evaluating autistic traits both during acute illness and after weight restoration — is often necessary to distinguish trait autism from state effects of malnutrition [9].

## Treatment Adaptations for Autistic Individuals With Eating Disorders

Standard eating disorder treatments were developed for neurotypical populations and may need significant adaptation to be effective for autistic individuals.

**Modified CBT.** Standard CBT-E relies on cognitive restructuring, flexible thinking, and tolerance of uncertainty — all of which may be more challenging for autistic individuals whose cognitive rigidity is a trait characteristic rather than solely a product of the eating disorder. Modified CBT approaches for autistic individuals with eating disorders may include: more concrete and structured session formats, visual aids and written materials to supplement verbal processing, explicit instruction in skills that neurotypical patients may acquire implicitly, longer treatment duration to accommodate slower behavioral change, and reduced emphasis on cognitive flexibility as a treatment target when rigidity is neurologically based rather than eating-disorder-maintained [5].

**Sensory-informed approaches.** Treatment must distinguish between food avoidance that is driven by eating disorder cognitions (fear of weight gain, calorie counting) and food avoidance that is driven by genuine sensory aversion. For an autistic individual with ARFID, forcing exposure to sensorily aversive foods is not therapeutic — it is harmful. Gradual, respectful sensory desensitization, conducted at the individual's pace and with their consent, is more appropriate.

**Structured environments.** Autistic individuals may benefit from more predictable, structured treatment environments — clear schedules, advance notice of changes, reduced sensory stimulation in dining areas, and explicit communication about expectations and procedures.

**Addressing interoceptive awareness.** Interoceptive training — helping the individual learn to recognize and respond to hunger, fullness, and emotional signals — may be a particularly valuable treatment component for autistic individuals whose interoceptive difficulties predate and contribute to the eating disorder [6].

**Family-based treatment adaptations.** FBT for autistic adolescents with eating disorders may need to incorporate understanding of the child's sensory profile, communication style, and need for predictability into the refeeding process. Parents may need education about both the eating disorder and autism to provide effective support.

Research by Tchanturia and colleagues has begun developing and testing autism-informed eating disorder treatment approaches, with preliminary evidence suggesting improved engagement and outcomes compared to standard protocols [10].

> 💡 **Spark:** When autism and an eating disorder coexist, they cannot be treated as two separate conditions stacked on top of each other. They are intertwined — shared features feeding both conditions simultaneously. Treatment that understands both is not a luxury. It is the only treatment that will reach the root of both.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are autistic and struggling with eating,** know that the overlap between autism and eating disorders is recognized and increasingly understood. You deserve care that addresses both.

- **Request autism-informed eating disorder treatment.** Ask providers about their experience with neurodivergent clients. If they lack familiarity, organizations like FEAST (Families Empowered and Supporting Treatment of Eating Disorders) maintain resources for autism-eating disorder co-occurrence.

- **If you are a clinician treating eating disorders,** screen for autistic traits, especially when patients show persistent cognitive rigidity, sensory-based food avoidance, and difficulty with standard CBT. The Autism Spectrum Quotient (AQ-10) is a brief screening tool.

- **Distinguish between sensory needs and eating disorder behaviors.** Food avoidance driven by genuine sensory aversion requires a different approach than food avoidance driven by fear of weight gain.
