> **Summary:** Diabulimia — a colloquial term for the deliberate restriction or omission of insulin by individuals with type 1 diabetes as a method of weight control — sits at the intersection of two serious medical conditions and produces outcomes that are more dangerous than either condition alone. When a person with type 1 diabetes restricts their insulin, their body cannot use glucose for energy and instead breaks down fat and muscle, producing rapid weight loss. This weight loss comes at a catastrophic medical cost: chronically elevated blood sugar, diabetic ketoacidosis, accelerated microvascular damage, and dramatically increased risk of blindness, kidney failure, limb amputation, and premature death. Diabulimia is not a formal diagnosis in the DSM-5-TR, but it is increasingly recognized by diabetes organizations and eating disorder professionals as one of the most medically dangerous eating disorder behaviors in existence. This article explores what diabulimia involves, why it develops, and why integrated treatment addressing both the diabetes and the eating disorder is essential.

**Key Facts:**
1. An estimated 30–40% of women with type 1 diabetes report insulin restriction for weight control at some point during their lives, with rates highest during adolescence and young adulthood [1].
2. Insulin omission is the most medically dangerous compensatory behavior in any eating disorder — associated with a three-fold increase in mortality risk compared to individuals with type 1 diabetes who do not restrict insulin [2].
3. Diabulimia is not a formal DSM-5-TR diagnosis, but the behavior may be classified under OSFED or as an eating disorder co-occurring with a medical condition [3].
4. The discovery that insulin omission causes weight loss often occurs during normal diabetes management — when a person notices they lost weight during a period of poor glucose control [4].
5. Treatment requires integration of diabetes management and eating disorder treatment — approaches that treat only one condition typically fail [5].

## How Insulin Restriction Becomes a Weight Loss Tool

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the body's immune system destroys the insulin-producing beta cells of the pancreas. Without insulin, the body cannot move glucose from the bloodstream into cells for energy. Treatment requires lifelong insulin administration.

When insulin is provided adequately, glucose enters cells, is used for energy or stored, and blood sugar remains within a manageable range. But when insulin is deliberately restricted or omitted, glucose accumulates in the blood and is excreted through the kidneys in the urine — a process called glycosuria. The body, unable to access glucose for energy, begins breaking down fat and muscle for fuel. The result is rapid weight loss — but at the cost of uncontrolled hyperglycemia and metabolic crisis.

The discovery that insulin restriction produces weight loss is often inadvertent. A young person with type 1 diabetes may notice that they lose weight during a period when they forgot or skipped insulin doses — during travel, illness, or the disruptions of adolescence. In a culture that relentlessly rewards weight loss and equates thinness with success, this observation can become a behavioral pattern: deliberate under-dosing or omission of insulin for the purpose of weight control [4].

The behavior typically escalates. What begins as occasional under-dosing can progress to routine insulin omission, with increasingly severe metabolic consequences. The person may maintain just enough insulin to prevent acute diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) while keeping blood sugar chronically elevated to promote ongoing caloric wasting through glycosuria.

## The Medical Consequences: Why This Is the Most Dangerous ED Behavior

Insulin restriction for weight control produces medical consequences that exceed those of any other eating disorder behavior. The combination of chronic hyperglycemia and the metabolic derangements of insulin deficiency accelerates every complication associated with diabetes — often by decades.

**Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA).** When insulin is severely restricted, the body's reliance on fat breakdown produces ketone bodies in quantities that overwhelm the body's buffering capacity, resulting in metabolic acidosis. DKA is a medical emergency that can produce coma and death. Recurrent DKA episodes — common in individuals who cycle between insulin restriction and insulin resumption — cause cumulative organ damage [6].

**Microvascular complications.** Chronic hyperglycemia accelerates damage to small blood vessels throughout the body. Diabetic retinopathy (leading to blindness), diabetic nephropathy (leading to kidney failure and dialysis), and diabetic neuropathy (leading to nerve damage, pain, and potentially limb amputation) all progress more rapidly in individuals who restrict insulin. Research by Goebel-Fabbri and colleagues (2008) found that women with type 1 diabetes who reported insulin restriction had significantly higher rates of retinopathy and nephropathy than those who did not, even after controlling for diabetes duration [2].

**Cardiovascular risk.** The combination of chronic hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia, and the metabolic stress of repeated DKA episodes accelerates cardiovascular disease.

**Mortality.** The mortality risk associated with insulin restriction is staggering. Goebel-Fabbri's 11-year follow-up study found that women with type 1 diabetes who reported insulin restriction had a three-fold increase in mortality compared to those who did not — dying at a median age of 45 [2]. This makes diabulimia one of the most lethal psychiatric conditions in existence.

## The Psychological Trap

The psychology of diabulimia involves the intersection of eating disorder psychology and the unique psychological burdens of living with type 1 diabetes.

**Diabetes-specific distress.** Managing type 1 diabetes is a relentless cognitive and behavioral task: monitoring blood sugar, calculating insulin doses, counting carbohydrates, managing supplies, navigating exercise and illness. Research by Fisher and colleagues (2012) documented that diabetes distress — the emotional burden of continuous disease management — is common and is associated with worse glycemic control and reduced quality of life [7].

**Weight gain at diagnosis.** When type 1 diabetes is diagnosed and insulin treatment begins, weight gain often follows — as the body, previously wasting calories through glycosuria, begins to properly metabolize food. This weight gain, while medically appropriate, can be psychologically distressing — particularly for adolescents who are already navigating body image development. The temporal association between starting insulin and gaining weight creates a cognitive link between insulin and weight gain that can persist as a belief: "insulin makes me fat" [4].

**Unique compensatory tool.** Unlike purging, laxative abuse, or excessive exercise — which are available to anyone with an eating disorder — insulin restriction is available only to people with insulin-dependent diabetes. It is a uniquely accessible compensatory behavior for this population, requiring no additional supplies or effort beyond simply not doing something (taking insulin). This accessibility makes the behavior particularly difficult to resist.

**Shame and concealment.** Individuals with diabulimia typically experience profound shame about both their eating disorder and their diabetes mismanagement. They may present to their endocrinologist with persistently elevated HbA1c levels without disclosing the reason, receiving clinical puzzlement or frustration rather than screening for an eating disorder. They may present to an eating disorder clinician without disclosing their diabetes. The concealment perpetuates the condition by preventing either treatment team from seeing the full picture [5].

## Warning Signs

Clinicians, family members, and individuals with type 1 diabetes should be alert to the following warning signs of insulin restriction for weight control:

- Persistently elevated HbA1c (>9%) without clear medical explanation
- Recurrent DKA episodes
- Reluctance to perform blood glucose monitoring in front of others
- Secretive behavior around insulin administration
- Preoccupation with weight, body shape, or food
- Rapid or unexplained weight loss
- Frequent urination and excessive thirst (signs of hyperglycemia)
- Deteriorating diabetes self-management despite adequate knowledge and resources
- Development of diabetic complications at earlier ages than expected
- Anxiety or resistance around insulin dose increases [8]

## Integrated Treatment: The Only Effective Approach

Treating diabulimia requires integration of eating disorder treatment and diabetes management — an integration that the healthcare system is poorly structured to provide. Endocrinologists typically have limited training in eating disorders. Eating disorder specialists typically have limited training in diabetes management. The patient falls in the gap between two specialties that rarely communicate.

**What integrated treatment looks like:**

A multidisciplinary team including an endocrinologist (or diabetes specialist), an eating disorder therapist, a diabetes educator, and a dietitian with expertise in both eating disorders and diabetes. The team communicates regularly and coordinates treatment goals.

**Psychological treatment** uses adapted CBT-E or DBT approaches that address both the eating disorder cognitions (body image, weight concern, compensatory behavior) and the diabetes-specific cognitions (beliefs about insulin and weight, diabetes distress, management burden). The therapeutic targets include reducing insulin restriction, improving glycemic control, and addressing the emotional distress that drives the behavior [9].

**Nutritional rehabilitation** must account for the glycemic effects of food and the need for insulin dose adjustment as eating patterns normalize. A dietitian who understands both eating disorders and diabetes can help the patient develop a meal plan that is nutritionally adequate, supports stable blood sugar, and does not trigger eating disorder behaviors.

**Medical monitoring** must be more frequent and more comprehensive than for either condition alone, given the elevated risk of DKA, electrolyte disturbance, and organ damage.

**Diabetes technology** — including continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) and insulin pumps — can be helpful in reducing the cognitive burden of diabetes management and providing real-time feedback that supports insulin adherence. However, technology must be introduced carefully, as some individuals with diabulimia experience CGM data as triggering or anxiety-provoking [10].

**Gradual insulin normalization.** Abruptly restoring full insulin doses after a period of restriction can produce rapid weight gain and fluid retention that is psychologically devastating for someone with an eating disorder. Treatment protocols typically involve gradual insulin dose increases with psychological support throughout the process.

> 💡 **Spark:** Diabulimia exploits a unique vulnerability: the intersection of a body that requires insulin to survive and a mind that has learned to withhold it in pursuit of thinness. It is one of the most dangerous conditions in medicine — and one of the most treatable, when the right professionals work together.

## What You Can Try

- **If you have type 1 diabetes and are restricting insulin for weight control,** you are not alone and this behavior has a name. It is recognized by medical professionals, and integrated treatment exists. The first step is telling someone.

- **Talk to your endocrinologist or diabetes team.** If you are not comfortable disclosing to your current provider, the National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235) can help you find a provider experienced in diabulimia.

- **If you are a diabetes clinician,** screen for eating disorders in all patients with persistently elevated HbA1c. Ask directly about insulin omission in a non-judgmental way. A question like "some people with diabetes find they lose weight when they take less insulin — has this ever been your experience?" opens the conversation.

- **If you are a parent of a young person with type 1 diabetes,** be alert to unexplained weight loss, frequent DKA, and secretive behavior around insulin. Avoid commenting on your child's weight.
