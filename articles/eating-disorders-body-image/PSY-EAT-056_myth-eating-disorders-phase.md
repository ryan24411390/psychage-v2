> **Summary:** The belief that eating disorders are temporary phases — teenage quirks that people naturally outgrow — is among the most dangerous misconceptions in mental health. Eating disorders are serious psychiatric conditions with the highest mortality rate of any mental health diagnosis. Without treatment, they do not spontaneously resolve; they entrench, escalate, and often persist for decades. Research on duration of illness, mortality, and long-term outcomes consistently demonstrates that eating disorders are progressive conditions that become more treatment-resistant over time. The "phase" myth delays intervention during the critical early window when treatment is most effective, and it dismisses the suffering of people at every stage of illness as something that will simply pass on its own.

**Key Facts:**
1. Anorexia nervosa has the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder, with a standardized mortality ratio of approximately 5.86 — meaning individuals with anorexia are nearly six times more likely to die than age-matched peers [1].
2. The average duration of illness before first treatment for eating disorders is 5-7 years, with many individuals remaining symptomatic for a decade or more before receiving intervention [2].
3. Longitudinal research by Eddy and colleagues (2017) found that only 31.4% of individuals with anorexia nervosa and 68.2% of individuals with bulimia nervosa achieved full recovery within 22 years of follow-up [3].
4. Early intervention within the first three years of illness onset is associated with significantly better outcomes, including higher rates of full recovery and lower rates of relapse [4].
5. Eating disorders that begin in adolescence frequently persist into adulthood, with research documenting active eating disorder pathology in individuals in their 40s, 50s, 60s, and beyond [5].

## The Myth

The myth frames eating disorders as developmental blips — temporary disruptions that belong to a particular life stage, usually adolescence, and that naturally resolve as a person matures. Under this framework, a teenager who restricts food is "going through a phase." A young adult who binges and purges is "experimenting." A college student whose relationship with food has become rigid and fearful is "stressed" and will settle down after graduation. The implicit message is that eating disorders are not serious enough to warrant urgent intervention because time itself will provide the cure.

This framing treats eating disorders the way many people treat teenage moodiness or identity experimentation — as features of a developmental period that will self-correct. It assumes that growing older, gaining life experience, or simply reaching a certain birthday will be sufficient to resolve conditions that involve measurable neurobiological differences, deeply entrenched cognitive patterns, and progressively worsening medical complications. The myth is comforting. It is also wrong.

## Where This Comes From

The "phase" framing has multiple origins. The first is the association between eating disorders and adolescence. Because eating disorders frequently onset during puberty and early adulthood — a period already characterized by rapid physical and psychological change — it is tempting to interpret them as part of the developmental turbulence rather than as distinct illnesses that happen to emerge during that period.

The second origin is a misunderstanding of how psychiatric conditions present. Many people have limited exposure to the chronic, progressive nature of eating disorders. The public narrative tends to focus on either the acute crisis (hospitalization, dramatic weight loss) or the recovery story (the person who "beat" their eating disorder). The long middle — the years of cycling between partial recovery and relapse, the decades of subclinical symptoms that never fully resolve, the quiet persistence of disordered thinking even after behaviors stabilize — is largely invisible in popular discourse.

A third origin is minimization. Framing eating disorders as phases serves the psychological function of reducing the perceived severity of the problem. If a parent can believe that their child's restriction is a phase, they do not have to confront the terrifying possibility that their child has a life-threatening psychiatric illness. If a clinician can frame a patient's binge eating as situational stress, they do not have to navigate the complexities of eating disorder treatment. The "phase" label is a cognitive shortcut that protects the observer from the full weight of what the person is experiencing — at the direct expense of the person who needs help [6].

Cultural attitudes toward adolescence also contribute. Western culture has a long tradition of dismissing adolescent distress as inherently transient — "they'll grow out of it" is applied broadly to everything from depression to self-harm to substance use. This generalized minimization of adolescent suffering creates a context in which eating disorders are easily absorbed into the category of "things teenagers do" rather than being recognized as the psychiatric emergencies they often are.

## What Research Actually Shows

The evidence on eating disorder chronicity, mortality, and long-term outcomes directly contradicts the "phase" narrative.

**Mortality data** provides the starkest rebuttal. Arcelus, Mitchell, Wales, and Nielsen (2011) conducted a meta-analysis of 36 studies examining mortality in eating disorders, published in *Archives of General Psychiatry*. They found that anorexia nervosa carries a standardized mortality ratio (SMR) of 5.86 — nearly six times the expected mortality rate for the general population of the same age and gender [1]. Approximately one in five deaths in individuals with anorexia nervosa is by suicide. Bulimia nervosa carries an SMR of 1.93, and eating disorder not otherwise specified (now OSFED) carries an SMR of 1.92. These are not the mortality profiles of conditions that resolve spontaneously. These are the mortality profiles of serious, chronic illnesses.

**Longitudinal outcome studies** reveal that recovery from eating disorders is neither automatic nor rapid. Eddy, Tabri, Thomas, Murray, Franko, Attia, and colleagues (2017) published a 22-year prospective follow-up study in the *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* tracking individuals diagnosed with anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa [3]. At 22 years of follow-up, only 62.8% of the anorexia nervosa group had achieved recovery at any point during the study period, and only 31.4% were in sustained recovery at the final assessment. For bulimia nervosa, 68.2% had achieved recovery. The median time to recovery for anorexia nervosa was approximately nine years. These numbers describe a condition measured in decades, not months — a condition that persists through adolescence, young adulthood, middle age, and beyond.

Steinhausen's (2002) meta-analysis of outcome studies in anorexia nervosa, published in the *American Journal of Psychiatry*, found that approximately 46.9% of individuals recovered, 33.5% improved but remained symptomatic, and 20.8% developed a chronic course [7]. One in five individuals with anorexia nervosa will carry the illness for the rest of their lives. This is not a phase.

**Duration of illness and treatment response** are directly linked. Research by Treasure, Stein, and Maguire (2015) in *The Lancet* demonstrated that duration of illness is one of the strongest predictors of treatment outcome in eating disorders [4]. Individuals who receive treatment within the first three years of illness onset show significantly higher rates of full recovery compared to those who are treated later. Each additional year of illness before treatment reduces the probability of full recovery and increases the probability of a chronic course. The neurobiological changes associated with prolonged malnutrition, the entrenchment of cognitive patterns, and the progressive erosion of social functioning all contribute to increasing treatment resistance over time.

This evidence has a direct clinical implication: the "wait and see" approach that the "phase" myth encourages is the exact opposite of what the evidence supports. Early, aggressive intervention is associated with the best outcomes. Waiting for a person to "grow out of" an eating disorder is not a neutral decision — it is a decision to allow the illness to become more entrenched, more medically dangerous, and more difficult to treat.

**Research on eating disorders across the lifespan** further demolishes the age-limited "phase" framework. Mangweth-Matzek and colleagues (2014) found that 3.8% of women over 60 met full diagnostic criteria for an eating disorder, with an additional 4.4% exhibiting subclinical disordered eating [5]. Midgley, Adams, Herle, Solmi, and Micali (2021) documented eating disorder presentations across the lifespan, including new-onset cases in individuals in their 50s, 60s, and 70s triggered by life transitions such as bereavement, retirement, medical illness, or menopause [8]. Eating disorders do not have an expiration date. They do not recognize birthdays. They persist for as long as they are untreated, and in some cases, they persist despite treatment.

**Relapse data** adds another dimension. Even among individuals who achieve recovery, relapse rates are substantial. Khalsa, Portnoff, McCurdy-McKinnon, and Feusner (2017) found that approximately 31% of individuals who recover from anorexia nervosa experience relapse, with the highest risk occurring in the first 18 months after weight restoration [9]. Relapse does not mean failure — but it does mean that eating disorders are chronic conditions that require ongoing monitoring and support, not one-time interventions.

## Why This Matters

The "phase" myth causes harm at every level of the system.

For the individual, it creates a framework in which seeking help feels illegitimate. If eating disorders are phases, then asking for treatment is an overreaction. The person who is struggling learns to minimize their own experience: "I should be able to handle this." "It will pass." "I am not sick enough for treatment." These internal narratives, reinforced by external dismissal, delay help-seeking during the window when intervention is most effective.

For families, the "phase" framing provides a seductive alternative to confronting a frightening diagnosis. Parents who believe their child is going through a phase may wait months or years before seeking professional evaluation — months and years during which the eating disorder is becoming neurobiologically entrenched. Research by Le Grange and colleagues on family-based treatment (the Maudsley method) has demonstrated that family engagement within the first years of illness is associated with significantly better outcomes [10]. Every month of the "wait and see" approach reduces the effectiveness of this evidence-based intervention.

For healthcare systems, the "phase" framing contributes to systematic under-identification of eating disorders in primary care, pediatric, and emergency settings. When clinicians share the cultural assumption that eating disorders are temporary, they are less likely to screen for them, less likely to refer to specialists, and more likely to attribute symptoms to other causes.

The mortality data makes the stakes of this myth unambiguous: eating disorders kill people. They do not kill people because those people failed to grow out of them. They kill people because the conditions were not identified and treated while intervention could have changed the trajectory.

## The More Accurate Picture

Eating disorders are psychiatric conditions with variable but often chronic trajectories. Like other serious mental illnesses — major depression, bipolar disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder — they can be effectively treated, but treatment requires professional intervention and often sustained, long-term support. Recovery is possible. For many people, full recovery is achievable. But recovery is the result of treatment, support, and sustained effort — not the passive passage of time.

The accurate picture also recognizes that eating disorders exist on a spectrum of severity and chronicity. Some individuals experience a relatively acute episode that responds well to early treatment. Others cycle between periods of active illness and partial recovery for years. Still others develop a chronic course that persists for decades. None of these trajectories is the "phase" that the myth describes. All of them require clinical attention.

The most useful reframing is this: eating disorders are medical emergencies with psychiatric origins. The earlier they are identified and treated, the better the prognosis. Waiting is not a treatment strategy. It is a risk factor.

> 💡 **Spark:** Every year of untreated eating disorder reduces the probability of full recovery. The most important thing anyone can do — a parent, a friend, a clinician, a person who is struggling — is to reject the "phase" narrative and treat the eating disorder as what it is: a serious illness that requires intervention now, not later.

## What You Can Try

- **Do not wait.** If you or someone you know is exhibiting signs of an eating disorder — restriction, bingeing, purging, compulsive exercise, food rigidity, intense body distress — seek professional evaluation. The evidence is clear: earlier treatment produces better outcomes.

- **Challenge the "phase" framing when you encounter it.** When someone dismisses eating disorder symptoms as a phase, provide the mortality and chronicity data. Facts change conversations.

- **Connect with specialized providers, not general practitioners alone.** The National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235) maintains a helpline and referral service. The Academy for Eating Disorders (aedweb.org) provides provider directories.

- **Track patterns over time using Psychage's Mood Journal** at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal). Documenting the persistence of distressing thoughts and behaviors can help counter the internal narrative that "this will pass on its own."

- **Learn about evidence-based treatments.** Family-based treatment (FBT/Maudsley), cognitive behavioral therapy for eating disorders (CBT-E), and dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) have strong evidence bases. Understanding what effective treatment looks like can help you or your loved one access it faster.
