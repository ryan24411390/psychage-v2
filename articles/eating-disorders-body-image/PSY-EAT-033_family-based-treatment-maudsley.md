> **Summary:** Family-based treatment (FBT), also known as the Maudsley approach, is the most extensively researched and most strongly recommended treatment for adolescent anorexia nervosa — and increasingly for other eating disorders in young people. FBT fundamentally reframes the family's role: rather than viewing parents as part of the problem, it positions them as the most powerful resource in their child's recovery. Parents are empowered to take temporary charge of their child's eating, interrupt eating disorder behaviors, and guide nutritional restoration — with the therapist serving as coach and consultant. The evidence is compelling: randomized controlled trials consistently show FBT producing full remission in 40–50% of adolescents with anorexia at end of treatment, with rates improving further at follow-up. This article explains how FBT works, what the research supports, what families can expect, and why this approach has transformed eating disorder treatment for young people.

**Key Facts:**
1. FBT was developed at the Maudsley Hospital in London in the 1980s and manualized by James Lock and Daniel Le Grange in 2001 [1].
2. Randomized controlled trials show FBT produces full remission in approximately 40–50% of adolescents with anorexia nervosa at end of treatment, compared to 20–25% for individual adolescent therapy [2].
3. FBT is the first-line treatment recommended by NICE guidelines, the APA, and the Academy for Eating Disorders for adolescent anorexia nervosa [3].
4. The approach has three distinct phases: parent-led refeeding, gradual return of control to the adolescent, and identity and developmental work [1].
5. FBT is increasingly adapted for bulimia nervosa, binge eating disorder, ARFID, and adult eating disorders, with preliminary evidence supporting these adaptations [4].

## The Revolutionary Idea: Parents as the Solution

Before FBT, the dominant paradigm in eating disorder treatment positioned parents as contributors to the problem. Family dynamics, parenting styles, and mother-child relationships were investigated as causes of the eating disorder, and treatment often involved separating the adolescent from the family — through individual therapy, residential treatment, or hospitalization — while the family "worked on their issues" separately.

FBT reversed this paradigm. Drawing on the observation that parents are the people most motivated to help their child recover and most present in the child's daily eating environment, FBT asks: what if we harness parental love, determination, and authority as the primary therapeutic resource?

The theoretical foundation rests on several principles developed by Christopher Dare, Ivan Eisler, and colleagues at the Maudsley Hospital, and later manualized by James Lock at Stanford and Daniel Le Grange at the University of Chicago [1]:

**Agnosticism about cause.** FBT explicitly does not blame parents for the eating disorder. The therapist communicates clearly that the eating disorder is a serious illness — not caused by bad parenting — and that the family's job is to fight the illness together, not to investigate how it started.

**Externalization of the illness.** FBT separates the eating disorder from the child. The child is not "being difficult" or "refusing to eat" — the eating disorder is preventing them from eating. This externalization reduces parental frustration and family conflict, and it protects the child from experiencing recovery as a battleground between themselves and their parents.

**Parental empowerment.** Parents are positioned as experts on their own child and as the most capable agents of change. The therapist does not tell parents what to feed their child or how to structure meals — they empower parents to figure this out using their knowledge of their child, their family culture, and their practical circumstances.

**Developmental sensitivity.** FBT recognizes that adolescents are not adults and should not be expected to manage their own recovery from a serious psychiatric illness. Just as parents would manage a child's insulin for diabetes or medication for epilepsy, parents manage the eating for an adolescent with an eating disorder — temporarily, until the child demonstrates the capacity to resume age-appropriate eating independence.

## The Three Phases

FBT unfolds across three distinct phases, typically over 15–20 sessions spread across 6–12 months [1]:

### Phase 1: Weight Restoration (Sessions 1–10)

Phase 1 is the most intensive and most distinctive phase of FBT. Parents take full control of their child's eating — deciding what is eaten, how much, and when. The adolescent is temporarily relieved of all decisions about food.

**Session 1** typically includes a dramatic therapeutic moment: the therapist describes the medical dangers of the eating disorder, conveys the seriousness of the condition, and charges the parents with the responsibility of saving their child's life. This session is deliberately emotional — it is designed to mobilize parental urgency and commitment.

**Session 2** is the family meal, in which the family brings a meal to the therapy session and the therapist observes the family's eating dynamics in real time. The therapist coaches parents to encourage their child to eat one additional bite beyond what the eating disorder allows — a small behavioral step that demonstrates that parental authority can override the eating disorder.

**Subsequent Phase 1 sessions** support parents as they implement refeeding at home. The therapist troubleshoots mealtime challenges, reinforces parental authority, manages sibling dynamics, and maintains the externalization frame ("the anorexia is fighting back — keep going"). Weight is monitored at each session.

Phase 1 continues until the adolescent has achieved consistent weight gain and the eating disorder's grip on mealtimes has loosened. This phase is often the most difficult for families — meals can be distressing, the adolescent may resist intensely, and parents may feel exhausted and uncertain. The therapist's role is to sustain parental resolve and problem-solve obstacles.

### Phase 2: Returning Control (Sessions 11–16)

Once weight is being restored consistently and the eating disorder behaviors have diminished, Phase 2 begins the gradual transfer of eating control back to the adolescent. This is not an all-or-nothing transition — it is a negotiated, stepwise process in which the adolescent demonstrates readiness to manage progressively more eating decisions.

The adolescent might begin by choosing their own snacks, then their own lunches, then their own dinners — with parents maintaining oversight and the ability to step back in if eating disorder behaviors resurface. The pace is determined by the adolescent's demonstrated capacity, not by a calendar.

Phase 2 also addresses the developmental impact of the eating disorder — the social withdrawal, academic disruption, and identity constriction that the illness may have produced.

### Phase 3: Identity and Developmental Work (Sessions 17–20)

Phase 3 addresses the broader developmental tasks of adolescence that may have been disrupted or complicated by the eating disorder. This phase focuses on age-appropriate autonomy, identity development, peer relationships, and future planning. The eating disorder is no longer the central focus — it has been pushed to the background by successful refeeding and behavioral change.

Phase 3 also addresses relapse prevention, helping the family develop a plan for recognizing and responding to early warning signs of eating disorder recurrence.

## The Evidence

FBT has the strongest evidence base of any treatment for adolescent anorexia nervosa.

The landmark randomized controlled trial by Lock and colleagues (2010) compared FBT to adolescent-focused individual therapy (AFT) and found that FBT produced significantly higher full remission rates at end of treatment (42% vs. 23%) and at 12-month follow-up (49% vs. 23%) [2]. These findings have been replicated in multiple subsequent trials.

A Cochrane review by Fisher and colleagues (2019) concluded that family-based approaches were more effective than individual therapy for weight restoration in adolescents with anorexia nervosa, with moderate-quality evidence supporting FBT as the first-line treatment [5].

Research by Le Grange and colleagues (2007) extended FBT to adolescent bulimia nervosa and found that FBT produced significantly higher abstinence from binge-purge behaviors than supportive individual therapy at both end of treatment and 6-month follow-up [4].

NICE guidelines (2017), APA guidelines, and the Academy for Eating Disorders all recommend FBT as the first-line treatment for adolescent anorexia nervosa, and as a recommended treatment for adolescent bulimia nervosa [3].

## What FBT Asks of Families

FBT is demanding. It asks parents to reorganize their lives around their child's eating — supervising every meal and snack, preparing food that the eating disorder resists, sitting with their child through the distress of eating, and maintaining calm authority in the face of opposition that can be intense.

The emotional toll on parents is well-documented. Research by Stillar and colleagues (2016) found that parents providing FBT experienced significant psychological distress, particularly during Phase 1, including anxiety, guilt, helplessness, and relationship strain [6]. Parental self-care, spousal support, and access to parent support groups are important adjuncts to FBT.

Siblings are also affected. The family's focus on the ill child's eating can produce feelings of neglect, resentment, and anxiety in siblings. FBT therapists are trained to attend to sibling experience and to help parents balance the needs of all family members [7].

Despite these demands, most families who complete FBT report that the experience — while one of the most difficult of their lives — was ultimately empowering. The act of fighting the eating disorder together, and seeing their child return to health, produces a sense of family efficacy and closeness that many describe as transformative [8].

## Limitations and Adaptations

FBT is not a universal solution. Some important limitations and ongoing developments:

**Non-response.** Approximately 50–60% of adolescents do not achieve full remission with FBT alone, suggesting that additional or alternative approaches are needed for a significant proportion of patients. Predictors of non-response include higher eating disorder severity at baseline, longer duration of illness, comorbid psychiatric conditions, and family factors including high parental criticism [9].

**Single-parent and non-traditional families.** FBT was originally developed with two-parent families as the assumed structure. Adaptations for single-parent families, blended families, foster families, and families with limited support networks are ongoing. Research suggests that FBT can be effective in single-parent households, though the burden on the sole parent is intensified [10].

**Autism co-occurrence.** As discussed in PSY-EAT-032, autistic adolescents with eating disorders may need FBT adaptations that account for sensory processing differences, cognitive rigidity, and communication style.

**Adult adaptation.** The Maudsley Model of Anorexia Nervosa Treatment for Adults (MANTRA) and other family-inclusive adult treatments draw on FBT principles while adapting them for the developmental context of adulthood — where parental authority over eating is neither appropriate nor feasible.

**Cultural adaptation.** FBT's emphasis on family authority and family meals may need cultural adaptation for families whose food practices, family structures, and help-seeking patterns differ from the Western nuclear family model in which FBT was developed [11].

> 💡 **Spark:** FBT asks families to do something extraordinary: to fight a psychiatric illness at their own kitchen table, three times a day, every day, until it releases its grip on their child. It is exhausting, frightening, and sometimes heartbreaking. It is also the most effective treatment we have for adolescent anorexia nervosa — because it harnesses the most powerful force in a young person's world: the love of their family.

## What You Can Try

- **If your child has been diagnosed with an eating disorder,** ask the treating clinician about family-based treatment. If they are not trained in FBT, request a referral to a clinician who is.

- **Learn about FBT before starting.** The books *Help Your Teenager Beat an Eating Disorder* by Lock and Le Grange and *Brave Girl Eating* by Harriet Brown provide accessible introductions for families.

- **Connect with other FBT families.** Organizations like FEAST (Families Empowered and Supporting Treatment of Eating Disorders) provide peer support, education, and advocacy for families using FBT.

- **Take care of yourself.** FBT is demanding on parents. Ensure you have your own emotional support — therapy, peer support, spousal communication — throughout the process.
