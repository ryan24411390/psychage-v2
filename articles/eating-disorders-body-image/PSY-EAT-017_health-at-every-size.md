> **Summary:** Health at Every Size (HAES) is a paradigm that challenges the dominant assumption that body weight is a reliable indicator of health and that weight loss should be a primary clinical goal. Rooted in research on weight stigma, the biological regulation of body weight, and the psychological harms of dieting, HAES promotes health-enhancing behaviors — joyful movement, nutritious eating, stress management, adequate sleep — without centering weight loss as the metric of success. HAES has attracted both passionate advocacy and vigorous criticism. This article presents the scientific evidence that underlies the HAES framework, the areas where that evidence is strong, the areas where it remains contested, and what HAES means in practical terms for individuals and healthcare providers.

**Key Facts:**
1. The Health at Every Size approach was formalized by Linda Bacon (now Lindo Bacon) in 2008 and is supported by the Association for Size Diversity and Health (ASDAH) [1].
2. Randomized controlled trials of HAES-aligned interventions have demonstrated improvements in blood pressure, blood lipids, psychological wellbeing, and eating behavior — often without significant weight change [2].
3. The central claim of HAES — that health-promoting behaviors improve health outcomes regardless of whether they produce weight loss — is supported by multiple lines of evidence, including the observation that physical fitness is a stronger predictor of mortality than body weight [3].
4. Weight stigma — discrimination and prejudice based on body size — is independently associated with increased cortisol, inflammation, avoidance of healthcare, and worse health outcomes, irrespective of weight itself [4].
5. Critics of HAES argue that it underestimates the health risks associated with very high body weight and that some HAES messaging discourages individuals from pursuing evidence-based medical interventions [5].

## The Core Principles of HAES

HAES is built on several core principles, as articulated by the Association for Size Diversity and Health [1]:

**Weight inclusivity.** Accept and respect the inherent diversity of body shapes and sizes and reject the idealizing or pathologizing of specific weights.

**Health enhancement.** Support health policies that improve access to information and services, and personal practices that improve wellbeing — attending to individual physical, economic, social, spiritual, and emotional needs.

**Respectful care.** Acknowledge our biases, and work to end weight discrimination, weight stigma, and weight bias. Provide information and services from an understanding that socioeconomic status, race, gender, sexual orientation, age, and other identities impact weight stigma.

**Eating for wellbeing.** Promote flexible, individualized eating based on hunger, satiety, nutritional needs, and pleasure, rather than externally regulated eating plans focused on weight control.

**Life-enhancing movement.** Support physical activities that allow people of all sizes, abilities, and interests to engage in enjoyable movement.

These principles represent a fundamental reframing of the relationship between body weight and health — from "weight as the problem and weight loss as the solution" to "health-enhancing behaviors as the focus, regardless of their effect on weight."

## What the Evidence Supports

The evidence base for HAES principles has grown substantially since the paradigm was formalized.

### Health behaviors predict health outcomes better than body weight

A landmark study by Blair and colleagues at the Cooper Institute tracked over 25,000 men over eight years and found that cardiorespiratory fitness was a significantly stronger predictor of all-cause mortality than body weight [3]. Men who were classified as "fit" by treadmill testing had lower mortality risk regardless of their weight category, while men who were "unfit" had higher mortality regardless of being in a lower weight category. This finding has been replicated in multiple large cohort studies and across genders.

A 2012 meta-analysis by Barry and colleagues, published in *Progress in Cardiovascular Diseases*, analyzed data from over 43,000 participants and found that individuals classified as "metabolically healthy" (normal blood pressure, blood lipids, and blood glucose) had no elevated mortality risk regardless of their weight category [6]. Conversely, individuals classified as "metabolically unhealthy" had elevated risk regardless of weight. The conclusion: metabolic health, not body weight per se, is the more clinically meaningful indicator.

### Dieting does not produce lasting weight loss for most people

As discussed in PSY-EAT-014, meta-analytic evidence consistently shows that dietary restriction produces short-term weight loss followed by long-term weight regain for the majority of individuals. Mann and colleagues (2007) found that one-third to two-thirds of dieters regain more weight than they lost [7]. The body's homeostatic defense of its weight "set point" — through metabolic adaptation, hormonal changes, and neurological shifts in reward sensitivity — makes sustained weight loss through dieting unlikely for most people.

This evidence is foundational to HAES: if weight loss is not achievable on a sustained basis for the majority of people, then centering weight loss as the primary health goal is both ineffective and potentially harmful.

### HAES-aligned interventions improve health outcomes

Several randomized controlled trials have directly tested HAES-aligned interventions against conventional diet-based approaches.

Bacon and colleagues (2005) randomized women in larger bodies to either a HAES-based intervention (emphasizing intuitive eating, body acceptance, and joyful movement) or a traditional diet program. At two-year follow-up, the HAES group showed significant and sustained improvements in blood pressure, total cholesterol, LDL cholesterol, depression, self-esteem, and body image. The diet group showed initial improvements in some measures that did not persist at follow-up, and the diet group experienced significant weight cycling [2].

Provencher and colleagues (2009) found that a HAES-based intervention produced improvements in eating behavior, psychological wellbeing, and metabolic markers comparable to a calorie-restricted diet — without the adverse psychological effects of dieting and without weight cycling [8].

### Weight stigma itself causes health harm

One of the most important contributions of HAES research is the documentation that weight stigma — not just body weight — contributes to negative health outcomes.

Tomiyama and colleagues (2018) published a comprehensive review in *BMC Medicine* documenting that weight stigma is independently associated with increased cortisol, systemic inflammation, binge eating, reduced physical activity, avoidance of healthcare, and increased mortality risk [4]. These effects are independent of body weight — meaning that the experience of being stigmatized for one's weight produces physiological stress responses that themselves damage health.

This finding reframes the public health conversation. If stigmatizing larger-bodied people produces the very health problems that the stigma claims to address, then stigma is not a motivator for health improvement — it is a health risk factor.

## Where the Evidence Is Contested

HAES is not without legitimate scientific debate. Presenting the paradigm honestly requires acknowledging the areas of contention.

### Very high body weight and health risk

While the "metabolically healthy" research demonstrates that many individuals in larger bodies have no elevated metabolic risk, this finding is less consistent at the very high end of the weight spectrum. Some longitudinal studies suggest that the "metabolically healthy" phenotype in individuals with the highest body weights may be less stable over time — with a proportion transitioning to metabolically unhealthy over 10–20-year follow-up periods [5].

Critics of HAES, including some obesity medicine researchers, argue that HAES messaging can be interpreted as denying any health risk associated with very high body weight — a position that is not supported by the totality of evidence. The relationship between body weight and health is complex, non-linear, and heavily confounded by fitness, socioeconomic status, weight cycling history, and weight stigma — but it is not zero at all weight levels.

### Medical interventions for weight-related conditions

For specific medical conditions in which body weight plays a biomechanical or metabolic role — such as osteoarthritis, sleep apnea, or type 2 diabetes — some evidence supports that weight reduction (through any means that achieves it sustainably) can produce clinical benefits. GLP-1 receptor agonist medications, which produce sustained weight reduction in many individuals, have shown significant improvements in cardiometabolic outcomes in clinical trials [9].

HAES advocates would note that these interventions target specific medical conditions and should be evaluated on their clinical merits — not used to justify population-wide weight stigma or to imply that all larger-bodied people need medical intervention.

### The "set point" debate

The concept of a body weight "set point" — a physiologically defended weight range that the body seeks to maintain — is central to the HAES argument against dieting. While there is strong evidence for homeostatic weight defense mechanisms, some researchers argue that the set point concept oversimplifies the biology and may discourage individuals from pursuing health-enhancing behaviors that happen to produce weight change [10].

## HAES in Practice: What It Means for Individuals

For individuals navigating their own relationship with food, body, and health, HAES offers a practical framework:

**Focus on what you do, not what you weigh.** Eating a variety of nutritious foods, engaging in physical activity you enjoy, managing stress, getting adequate sleep, and maintaining social connections — these behaviors improve health outcomes regardless of their effect on body weight.

**Reject weight-based self-worth.** Your value as a person is not determined by a number on a scale. Cultivating a self-concept that is not organized around body weight reduces the psychological burden of weight fluctuation and the emotional damage of weight stigma.

**Listen to your body.** Intuitive eating — eating in response to hunger and fullness cues rather than external dietary rules — is associated with improved metabolic markers, reduced disordered eating, and improved psychological wellbeing.

**Demand respectful healthcare.** You deserve medical care that addresses your specific health concerns without automatically prescribing weight loss as the first-line intervention. If your healthcare provider recommends weight loss, it is reasonable to ask: "What would you recommend for a thin patient with the same symptoms?"

## HAES in Practice: What It Means for Clinicians

For healthcare providers, HAES principles translate to concrete clinical practices:

**Screen for eating disorders before recommending weight loss.** Many patients in larger bodies have undiagnosed eating disorders that will be worsened by dietary restriction. The Academy for Eating Disorders recommends screening for disordered eating before initiating any weight-loss intervention [11].

**Address health behaviors directly.** Rather than prescribing weight loss and hoping health behaviors improve as a side effect, target health behaviors directly: "Let's talk about adding more vegetables to your meals" rather than "You need to lose weight."

**Recognize weight stigma as a clinical variable.** Patients in larger bodies who report negative healthcare experiences may be avoiding preventive care, delaying treatment, or providing incomplete medical histories due to anticipated stigma. Creating a weight-inclusive clinical environment improves the therapeutic relationship and clinical outcomes.

> 💡 **Spark:** HAES does not claim that weight never affects health. It claims that health is better served by promoting behaviors than by pursuing numbers — and that the stigma, shame, and failed dieting that define weight-centric healthcare cause harm that weight-inclusive care avoids.

## What You Can Try

- **Shift your health metrics.** For one month, evaluate your health by how you feel, how you sleep, how you move, and how you eat — not by what the scale says. Notice whether this shift changes your relationship with health-related behaviors.

- **Ask your doctor different questions.** Instead of "how much should I weigh?", ask "what can I do to improve my blood pressure/energy/mood/sleep?" Health-behavior-focused questions often produce more actionable and less stigmatizing clinical guidance.

- **Try intuitive eating.** A registered dietitian trained in intuitive eating can help you rebuild trust with your body's hunger and fullness signals. Research supports this approach for improving both metabolic and psychological outcomes.

- **Examine your own weight biases.** Notice when you make assumptions about people based on their body size — including yourself. Weight bias is deeply internalized, and awareness is the first step toward change.
