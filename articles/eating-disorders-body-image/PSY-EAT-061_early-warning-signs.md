> **Summary:** Eating disorders frequently begin in childhood and adolescence, yet the average delay between symptom onset and treatment is between three and five years — a gap that worsens prognosis and deepens entrenchment. Parents and teachers occupy a critical vantage point: they observe young people's behavior around food, their emotional shifts, and their physical changes on a daily basis. Recognizing early warning signs is not about diagnosing — it is about noticing patterns that warrant professional evaluation. This article maps the behavioral, emotional, and physical red flags across developmental stages, offers guidance on how to approach a child or adolescent you are concerned about, and outlines pathways to professional assessment.

**Key Facts:**

1. The median age of eating disorder onset is 12.3 years for anorexia nervosa and 12.4 years for bulimia nervosa, with many cases beginning before the child or family recognizes a problem [1].
2. Early intervention within the first three years of illness is associated with significantly higher recovery rates — up to 80% for adolescents receiving family-based treatment, compared to roughly 50% when treatment is delayed beyond five years [2].
3. A 2019 meta-analysis found that parents correctly identified disordered eating in their children only 13-37% of the time, with boys' symptoms being recognized even less frequently than girls' [3].
4. Teachers interact with students approximately 30 hours per week, and research identifies school personnel as the second most likely adults (after parents) to notice early behavioral changes associated with disordered eating [4].

## Why Early Recognition Matters

The trajectory of an eating disorder is shaped profoundly by how quickly it is identified and addressed. Eating disorders are not phases that children grow out of on their own. They are psychiatric illnesses with the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition, and their neurobiological entrenchment deepens with every month they go untreated [5]. The neural pathways that maintain restrictive eating, binge-purge cycles, or compulsive exercise become more rigid over time — the brain literally rewires itself around disordered patterns, making them progressively harder to interrupt.

This is why the window of early intervention is so valuable. Research from the Maudsley Hospital's eating disorder service has demonstrated that adolescents who enter treatment within three years of symptom onset respond significantly better to family-based treatment (FBT) than those with longer illness duration [2]. The illness has had less time to reorganize the young person's identity, social world, and neurochemistry around its demands.

Yet the gap between onset and treatment remains stubbornly wide. A large epidemiological study published in the *International Journal of Eating Disorders* found that fewer than half of individuals with eating disorders ever receive treatment, and those who do typically wait years before accessing it [6]. For children and adolescents, this delay is almost always mediated by adult recognition — or the lack of it. Young people rarely self-refer. They depend on parents, teachers, coaches, and pediatricians to notice that something has shifted.

The challenge is that eating disorders are secretive by nature. They are ego-syntonic in their early stages, meaning the individual often does not perceive the behavior as problematic — and may actively resist having it identified. A child who is restricting food intake may insist they "already ate" or "aren't hungry." An adolescent developing bulimia may become more private about bathroom use but show no obvious physical changes for months. The warning signs are there, but they require a specific kind of attention to see.

## Behavioral Warning Signs by Age Group

Warning signs manifest differently depending on the child's developmental stage, and recognizing this variation is critical for parents and teachers who interact with multiple age groups.

**Children ages 5-10.** At this age, eating disorders often present as food avoidance that goes beyond typical childhood pickiness. Watch for a child who progressively narrows their accepted foods, who becomes distressed or panicky at mealtimes, who begins refusing foods they previously enjoyed, or who complains of stomachaches or nausea before meals without a medical explanation. Some children develop rituals around food — cutting it into tiny pieces, eating in a rigid order, or insisting that foods not touch each other — that intensify over time. Physical signs may include falling off their growth curve (height or weight percentile dropping relative to their personal trajectory), persistent fatigue, or complaints of feeling cold. Teachers may notice that a child stops eating their packed lunch, trades away food items, or becomes unusually preoccupied with what other children are eating [7].

**Pre-teens ages 11-13.** The transition to middle school coincides with puberty, making this a high-risk window. Pre-teens may begin expressing dissatisfaction with their body — pinching skin, standing sideways in mirrors, comparing themselves to peers or to images online. Behaviorally, watch for the sudden adoption of "healthy eating" rules that become progressively rigid: eliminating food groups, reading nutrition labels obsessively, refusing to eat foods prepared by others, or insisting on "clean" or "whole" versions of previously accepted meals. Exercise may increase — but secretly or compulsively, such as doing exercises in their room at night or becoming distressed when a workout is interrupted. Socially, pre-teens developing eating disorders often begin withdrawing from group eating situations: avoiding the cafeteria, skipping birthday parties, or becoming anxious about family dinners [8].

**Adolescents ages 14-18.** By high school, eating disorder behaviors may be more sophisticated and harder to detect. Adolescents are better at concealment — wearing layered clothing to hide body changes, using water loading before weigh-ins, disposing of food without eating it, or using social media to find communities that reinforce disordered behaviors (often disguised as "fitness" or "clean eating" accounts). Behavioral red flags include dramatic changes in eating patterns (suddenly becoming vegan, doing intermittent fasting, or eliminating entire macronutrient groups), disappearing to the bathroom immediately after meals, excessive exercise that continues through injury or illness, and increasing rigidity about food timing and composition. Teachers may notice declining academic performance (malnutrition impairs concentration and memory), social withdrawal, irritability, difficulty concentrating in class, or an unusual fixation on food-related topics in schoolwork or conversation [9].

## Emotional and Psychological Warning Signs

Beyond observable behavior, there are emotional and psychological shifts that often precede or accompany the development of an eating disorder. These are harder to measure but equally important to notice.

**Perfectionism that intensifies.** Many children who develop eating disorders already have perfectionistic tendencies, but the perfectionism sharpens and narrows. They may become more self-critical, more rigid about rules (not just food rules — any rules), more distressed by grades below their standard, and less able to tolerate mistakes. This cognitive rigidity is a well-documented feature of eating disorder neuropsychology and often predates the eating pathology itself [10].

**Increased body comparison and checking.** Repeated negative comments about their own body, frequent requests for reassurance about appearance, or visible distress after looking in mirrors or seeing photographs of themselves. This is distinct from normal developmental self-consciousness — it is persistent, distressing, and worsening over time.

**Mood volatility tied to food and eating.** Anxiety before meals, irritability when meal plans change, guilt after eating, or depressed mood that correlates with food intake. When a child's emotional state becomes noticeably organized around eating events, this is a significant warning signal.

**Social withdrawal.** Pulling away from friends, declining invitations that involve food, or losing interest in activities they previously enjoyed. Eating disorders are isolating illnesses, and the withdrawal often begins early — driven by shame, the need to maintain rigid eating routines, or the cognitive narrowing that malnutrition produces.

**Loss of menstrual periods.** In adolescents who have begun menstruating, the cessation of periods (amenorrhea) is a medical red flag that the body is under significant physiological stress. This can occur even in the absence of visible changes to the adolescent's body size and should always prompt medical evaluation [11].

## How to Approach a Young Person You Are Concerned About

Recognizing warning signs is the first step. The second — and often the one that stops adults in their tracks — is knowing how to raise the concern without causing harm or pushing the young person further into secrecy.

**Choose a private, low-pressure setting.** Do not raise concerns at the dinner table, in front of peers, or during a moment of conflict. Find a quiet time when neither of you is rushed. A walk, a car ride, or a calm moment at home often works better than a face-to-face sit-down, which can feel confrontational.

**Lead with observation, not accusation.** Use specific, nonjudgmental observations rather than labels or conclusions. "I've noticed you seem stressed at mealtimes and you've been skipping lunch" is concrete and caring. "I think you have an eating disorder" is diagnostic and likely to provoke defensiveness. Focus on what you have seen and express concern for their wellbeing rather than their eating behavior specifically.

**Avoid commenting on weight, shape, or appearance.** Do not say "You've lost weight" or "You look thin" — even if intended as an expression of concern, comments about the body can be experienced as reinforcing or triggering. Keep the focus on emotions, behavior, and wellbeing.

**Expect denial or anger.** The vast majority of young people with early eating disorders will deny that anything is wrong — and they may genuinely believe this, because the illness is ego-syntonic at this stage. Do not argue or try to convince them. Simply express your concern, let them know you are available, and follow up with action (scheduling a medical appointment, informing the school counselor, or consulting a specialist) regardless of whether the young person agrees that there is a problem. Parental authority to seek evaluation does not require the child's agreement [12].

**Do not wait for the "right" level of severity.** One of the most dangerous patterns in adult response to eating disorders is the belief that the young person is not "sick enough" for concern — that they need to lose a certain amount of weight, or be engaging in a specific behavior, before intervention is warranted. This is categorically false. Eating disorders at every stage of severity benefit from early intervention, and waiting for the illness to become visibly severe means waiting for it to become more entrenched and harder to treat.

## Referral Pathways: What to Do Next

Once you have noticed warning signs and expressed concern, the next step is connecting the young person with professional evaluation.

**Start with the pediatrician or primary care provider.** A medical evaluation can assess growth trajectory, vital signs, nutritional status, and screen for medical complications. Request that the provider use a validated screening tool such as the SCOFF questionnaire or the Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q) adapted for adolescents. Be specific about the behaviors you have observed — do not rely on the child to report them accurately, as minimization is common.

**Request a referral to an eating disorder specialist.** General therapists without eating disorder training may miss early presentations or inadvertently reinforce disordered patterns. Seek therapists or programs that specialize in eating disorders and, for adolescents, that offer family-based treatment (FBT), the gold-standard intervention for adolescent eating disorders [2].

**For teachers and school staff.** Your role is to observe, document, and refer — not to diagnose or treat. If you notice warning signs, bring your observations to the school counselor or psychologist, who can then coordinate with the family. Document specific behaviors and dates rather than interpretations. Many schools have protocols for referral to community mental health resources.

**If the young person is in immediate medical danger** — fainting, chest pain, inability to stand without dizziness, or expressing suicidal thoughts — seek emergency medical care immediately.

> 💡 **Spark:** The adults who catch eating disorders early are rarely the ones with clinical training. They are the parents who notice their child pushing food around the plate for the third week in a row, the teachers who see that a student has stopped eating lunch, the coaches who realize an athlete is exercising through injury. You do not need expertise in eating disorders to notice that something has changed. You need to trust what you are seeing — and act on it before the window narrows.

## What You Can Try

If you are a parent or teacher concerned about a young person, here are concrete next steps:

**Keep a written log of observations.** For one to two weeks, note specific behaviors related to food, exercise, mood, and social engagement that concern you, along with dates. This documentation is invaluable for medical providers and can help you distinguish between a bad week and a sustained pattern.

**Educate yourself on age-appropriate eating.** Understanding what is developmentally normal — childhood food neophobia peaks around ages 2-6, adolescents naturally eat more during growth spurts, and some fluctuation in eating patterns is expected during transitions — helps you calibrate your concern accurately.

**Create a meal environment that is low-pressure.** Family meals without commentary on food choices, portion sizes, or body shape reduce mealtime anxiety and make it safer for a young person to eat without scrutiny. Remove diet talk from the household.

**Connect with support for yourself.** Organizations like the National Eating Disorders Association (NEDA) and F.E.A.S.T. (Families Empowered and Supporting Treatment of Eating Disorders) offer parent-specific resources, support groups, and helplines. You do not have to navigate this alone.
