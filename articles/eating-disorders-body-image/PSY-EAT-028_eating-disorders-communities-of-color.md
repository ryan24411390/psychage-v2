> **Summary:** Eating disorders do not discriminate by race or ethnicity — but the healthcare system, the research enterprise, and the public narrative absolutely do. For decades, eating disorders have been framed as conditions affecting primarily white, affluent women, and this framing has created a self-fulfilling prophecy: people of color with eating disorders are less likely to be screened, less likely to be diagnosed, less likely to be referred to specialist treatment, and less likely to be included in the research that shapes clinical understanding. The reality, documented by an increasingly robust body of evidence, is that eating disorders are at least as prevalent in Black, Hispanic/Latine, Asian, Indigenous, and other communities of color as in white populations — and in some cases more prevalent. This article examines the evidence on eating disorders across racial and ethnic communities, the structural barriers to equitable care, the cultural factors that shape how eating disorders present and are understood, and what must change.

**Key Facts:**
1. Research by Marques and colleagues (2011) found that eating disorders are at least as prevalent in Black and Hispanic populations as in white populations in the United States, yet people of color are significantly less likely to receive treatment [1].
2. Black teenagers are 50% more likely to engage in binge eating and purging behaviors than white teenagers, according to data from the Youth Risk Behavior Survey [2].
3. People of color with eating disorders are significantly less likely to be asked about eating behaviors by healthcare providers, even when presenting with identical symptoms to white patients [3].
4. Asian Americans report rates of body dissatisfaction and disordered eating that are comparable to or higher than white Americans, yet receive eating disorder diagnoses at significantly lower rates [4].
5. Eating disorder research has historically underrepresented people of color, with clinical trials composed of 80–90% white participants in many landmark studies [5].

## The Myth of the "White Girl's Disease"

The characterization of eating disorders as conditions of white, affluent women is not a neutral misconception — it is a structural barrier that has shaped research priorities, clinical training, screening practices, and treatment access for decades. This characterization did not emerge from epidemiological evidence. It emerged from who was studied, who was published about, and who had access to the academic medical centers where eating disorder research was conducted.

Early eating disorder research — conducted primarily in university-affiliated clinics serving predominantly white, higher-socioeconomic-status populations — produced samples that reflected the demographics of the researchers' clinical settings rather than the demographics of eating disorders themselves. When these biased samples were generalized to the population, the result was a clinical literature that described eating disorders as white conditions and, in doing so, created the very disparities in detection and treatment that perpetuated the myth.

The evidence accumulated over the past two decades has systematically dismantled this characterization. Marques and colleagues (2011), analyzing data from the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, found that lifetime prevalence of binge eating disorder was higher in Hispanic and comparable in Black populations to white populations [1]. Striegel-Moore and colleagues (2003) found that Black women had rates of recurrent binge eating comparable to white women and rates of purging that exceeded those in white women in some age groups [6].

The data is clear: eating disorders are global, multicultural, cross-class conditions. The disparities that exist are not in prevalence — they are in recognition, diagnosis, and treatment.

## How Structural Racism Shapes Eating Disorder Care

The disparities in eating disorder care for people of color are structural — embedded in the systems that determine who gets screened, diagnosed, and treated.

**Clinical screening bias.** A pivotal study by Gordon, Brattole, Wingate, and Joiner (2006) presented clinicians with identical clinical vignettes describing eating disorder symptoms, varying only the race of the hypothetical patient. Clinicians were significantly less likely to identify the presentation as an eating disorder when the patient was described as Black or Hispanic compared to when the patient was described as white [3]. This finding demonstrates that clinician bias — whether explicit or implicit — directly reduces diagnostic accuracy for patients of color.

**Research exclusion.** The underrepresentation of people of color in eating disorder research has cascading consequences. Treatment approaches are developed and validated on predominantly white samples. Screening tools are normed on predominantly white populations. Diagnostic criteria reflect the presentations most common in white patients. When a person of color presents with eating disorder symptoms that do not precisely match the white-normed prototype, the condition may go unrecognized.

**Treatment access barriers.** Eating disorder treatment — particularly intensive outpatient, residential, and inpatient treatment — is expensive, often inadequately covered by insurance, and disproportionately located in affluent, predominantly white communities. These structural barriers reduce access for people of color, who are more likely to be uninsured or underinsured, to live in areas with fewer mental health resources, and to face transportation and work-schedule barriers to treatment attendance [7].

**Culturally incongruent treatment environments.** Even when people of color access eating disorder treatment, they may encounter environments in which they are one of few or no people of their racial or ethnic background — among patients and staff alike. Treatment content that centers white cultural experiences of body image, food, and family may not resonate with the lived experiences of patients from different cultural backgrounds.

## Cultural Contexts: How Race and Ethnicity Shape the Eating Disorder Experience

Eating disorders do not exist in a cultural vacuum. Race, ethnicity, and culture shape how body image is experienced, how food-related distress is expressed, and how help is sought (or not sought).

**Black communities.** Research has identified several factors that influence eating disorder presentation in Black populations. Weight-based discrimination intersects with racial discrimination, creating compound stigma. The cultural value placed on curvier bodies in some Black communities may offer some protection against thin-ideal internalization, but this protection is incomplete — Black women are increasingly exposed to thin-ideal messaging through mainstream and social media, and they experience binge eating and purging at rates that equal or exceed those in white populations [2]. Additionally, the stereotype that eating disorders are "white diseases" may prevent Black individuals from recognizing their own symptoms as eating disorders, and may prevent their clinicians from screening for them.

**Hispanic/Latine communities.** Eating disorders in Hispanic/Latine populations are influenced by acculturation stress — the psychological burden of navigating between cultural identities — as well as by immigration-related trauma, language barriers in healthcare, and the specific body ideals of both origin and host cultures. Research by Alegria and colleagues (2007) found that US-born Hispanic individuals had higher rates of eating disorders than foreign-born Hispanic individuals, suggesting that acculturation to US body ideals plays a role in risk [8].

**Asian American communities.** Asian Americans face a particularly severe version of the "invisible eating disorder" phenomenon. The model minority stereotype — which attributes health and academic success to Asian populations — can mask psychiatric distress, including eating disorders. Research by Franko and colleagues (2007) found that Asian American women reported body dissatisfaction and eating disorder symptoms at rates comparable to white American women, but were significantly less likely to be diagnosed or treated [4]. Cultural values around family honor, emotional restraint, and stigma associated with mental illness may further reduce help-seeking.

**Indigenous communities.** Eating disorders in Indigenous populations are among the least studied and most underserved. The intersection of historical trauma, food insecurity, limited healthcare access, and cultural disruption creates a unique risk environment. Research by Cummins and colleagues (2005) found elevated rates of binge eating and body dissatisfaction among Native American adolescents, occurring in a context of intergenerational trauma and cultural loss [9].

## What Equitable Care Requires

Closing the racial disparities in eating disorder care requires changes at every level of the system:

**Universal screening.** Eating disorder screening should be routine for all patients regardless of race or ethnicity. Screening tools should be validated across diverse populations. Clinicians should receive training in recognizing eating disorders across demographic groups and in examining their own biases.

**Diversifying the research base.** Clinical trials and epidemiological studies should be designed to include adequate representation of all racial and ethnic groups. Funding agencies should require diversity in study populations as a condition of grant approval.

**Culturally responsive treatment.** Treatment should be adapted to reflect the cultural contexts of patients' lives — including culturally specific food practices, family structures, body ideals, and expressions of distress. This requires both diversifying the treatment workforce and training existing clinicians in cultural humility.

**Addressing structural barriers.** Expanding insurance coverage for eating disorder treatment, increasing the geographic distribution of treatment programs, offering telehealth options, and providing treatment in languages other than English are structural changes that would disproportionately benefit communities of color.

**Community education.** Eating disorder awareness campaigns should be developed by and for communities of color, using culturally relevant language, imagery, and channels. The message that eating disorders affect all communities — and that help is available — needs to reach the communities that have been historically excluded from the conversation [10].

> 💡 **Spark:** The most dangerous eating disorder myth is not that they are about vanity or choice. It is that they only happen to white women. This myth has cost millions of people of color — years of undiagnosed suffering, untreated illness, and preventable death. Eating disorders do not see race. The healthcare system does — and until it changes, the disparities will persist.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are a person of color experiencing eating or body image distress,** your condition is real, recognized, and treatable — regardless of whether it matches the stereotypical image of an eating disorder.

- **Seek culturally competent care.** Ask potential providers about their experience treating patients from your racial or ethnic background. Organizations like the National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235) can help with culturally informed referrals.

- **Talk within your community.** Breaking the silence about eating disorders in communities of color is itself an act of advocacy. Many people in your community may be experiencing similar struggles and may benefit from knowing they are not alone.

- **If you are a clinician,** screen for eating disorders in all patients regardless of race. Examine your own biases. Pursue training in culturally responsive eating disorder treatment.
