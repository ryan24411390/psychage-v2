> **Summary:** Body dysmorphic disorder (BDD) and eating disorders both involve profound distress about the body, but they are distinct conditions with different diagnostic classifications, different cognitive mechanisms, and different treatment approaches. BDD is classified as an obsessive-compulsive spectrum disorder characterized by preoccupation with perceived flaws in physical appearance that are not observable or appear slight to others. Eating disorders involve disturbances in eating behavior and the relationship with food, often (but not always) accompanied by body image concerns. The two conditions can co-occur — and when they do, treatment becomes more complex. This article explains the relationship between BDD and eating disorders, how to distinguish between them, and why accurate differentiation matters for effective treatment.

**Key Facts:**
1. Body dysmorphic disorder affects approximately 1.7–2.9% of the general population and is classified in the DSM-5-TR as an obsessive-compulsive and related disorder, not an eating disorder [1].
2. BDD and eating disorders co-occur at elevated rates: approximately 12–39% of individuals with eating disorders also meet criteria for BDD focused on non-weight/shape concerns [2].
3. The primary focus of BDD is on perceived defects in specific physical features (skin, nose, hair, symmetry), while eating disorders center on body weight, shape, and food-related behavior [1].
4. BDD is treated primarily with high-dose SSRIs and CBT with exposure and response prevention — the same approaches used for OCD, not the same approaches used for eating disorders [3].
5. Misdiagnosis between BDD and eating disorders is common and can lead to inappropriate treatment that worsens outcomes [4].

## Two Conditions That Share a Neighborhood

BDD and eating disorders inhabit overlapping psychological territory. Both involve distress about physical appearance. Both can produce avoidance behaviors, checking behaviors, reassurance-seeking, and significant functional impairment. Both can be accompanied by depression, anxiety, social isolation, and suicidal ideation. From the outside — and sometimes from the inside — they can look remarkably similar.

But the underlying architecture of these conditions is fundamentally different, and understanding that difference is essential for appropriate treatment.

**Body dysmorphic disorder** is defined in the DSM-5-TR as a preoccupation with one or more perceived defects or flaws in physical appearance that are not observable or appear only slight to others [1]. The preoccupation is accompanied by repetitive behaviors (mirror checking, excessive grooming, skin picking, seeking reassurance) or mental acts (comparing one's appearance to others) that the person feels driven to perform. The preoccupation causes clinically significant distress or impairment.

BDD is classified in the obsessive-compulsive and related disorders chapter of the DSM-5-TR — alongside OCD, hoarding disorder, trichotillomania, and excoriation disorder. This classification reflects the condition's core features: intrusive, distressing preoccupations (analogous to obsessions) and repetitive behaviors performed to alleviate that distress (analogous to compulsions).

**Eating disorders**, by contrast, are classified in their own diagnostic chapter and center on disturbances in eating behavior, food relationship, and — in the case of anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and some OSFED presentations — body weight and shape concerns. The behavioral component involves eating (restriction, bingeing, purging) rather than checking, grooming, or appearance-management rituals.

## The Critical Diagnostic Distinction

The key differentiator between BDD and eating disorders is the focus of the body image distress.

In **BDD**, the preoccupation typically centers on specific physical features that are unrelated to overall body weight or shape: the size or shape of the nose, the symmetry of the face, the appearance of the skin (acne, scarring, pigmentation), the hairline, the shape of the jaw, the appearance of the ears, or the muscularity of specific body parts. The perceived flaw is either not observable to others or appears minor — the defining "reality gap" of BDD [1].

In **eating disorders**, the body image disturbance (when present) centers on overall body weight, shape, and size — the desire to be thinner, the fear of weight gain, the perception that one's body is larger than it is. While specific body areas may be the focus (stomach, thighs, arms), the concern is typically about these areas in the context of overall weight and shape, and the behavioral response involves food-related behavior (restriction, purging, exercise for weight control).

This distinction has important clinical implications. A person who is preoccupied with the shape of their nose and engages in compulsive mirror-checking, avoidance of social situations, and excessive camouflaging of the nose (with makeup, hats, specific angles in photographs) has BDD. A person who is preoccupied with the size of their stomach and restricts food intake, exercises excessively, and avoids situations where their body will be visible has an eating disorder. The behavioral response is shaped by the nature of the preoccupation.

However, the distinction is not always clean. The DSM-5-TR includes a specific diagnostic rule: if the body image preoccupation is limited to concerns about body fat or weight, and these concerns are better explained by an eating disorder, then the diagnosis should be the eating disorder, not BDD [1]. BDD should only be diagnosed alongside an eating disorder when the BDD concerns are about features unrelated to weight and shape (e.g., a person with anorexia who also has severe preoccupation with the size of their nose).

## Comorbidity: When Both Conditions Coexist

BDD and eating disorders co-occur at rates that significantly exceed chance. Research by Grant and colleagues (2002) found that approximately 39% of individuals with eating disorders had comorbid BDD focused on non-weight/shape concerns [2]. Conversely, studies of BDD populations find elevated rates of eating disorders, particularly when the BDD involves muscle dysmorphia.

The co-occurrence creates clinical complexity. When a person has both conditions, treating only the eating disorder may leave the BDD untouched — and vice versa. The BDD may maintain body image distress even as the eating disorder improves, potentially triggering relapse. Alternatively, the eating disorder behaviors (restriction, purging) may be partially driven by the BDD concerns, making eating disorder treatment less effective unless the BDD is also addressed.

Research by Phillips and colleagues (2005) found that individuals with comorbid BDD and eating disorders had higher levels of depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation, and functional impairment than those with either condition alone [5]. This underscores the importance of comprehensive assessment that screens for both conditions, rather than assuming that all body image distress in a person with an eating disorder is attributable to the eating disorder.

## Different Brains, Different Treatments

The distinction between BDD and eating disorders is not just academic — it has direct treatment implications because the evidence-based interventions for these conditions differ.

**BDD treatment** follows the OCD treatment model. First-line treatments are:
- **CBT with exposure and response prevention (ERP)**: The person is gradually exposed to situations that trigger appearance-related distress (going out without camouflage, allowing others to see the perceived flaw, reducing mirror-checking) while refraining from compulsive checking, reassurance-seeking, or avoidance behaviors. The goal is to reduce the compulsive behavioral cycle that maintains the preoccupation [3].
- **High-dose SSRIs**: Fluoxetine, fluvoxamine, and other SSRIs are effective for BDD at doses typically higher than those used for depression — often at the upper end of the prescribing range. Research shows that approximately 50–70% of BDD patients respond to adequate SSRI trials [6].

**Eating disorder treatment** follows different models depending on the specific diagnosis:
- **CBT-E** (enhanced cognitive behavioral therapy) for bulimia, BED, and adult anorexia
- **FBT** (family-based treatment/Maudsley approach) for adolescent anorexia
- **IPT** (interpersonal psychotherapy) as an alternative for bulimia and BED
- **DBT** (dialectical behavior therapy) for binge-purge presentations with emotional dysregulation

These approaches differ from BDD treatment in their targets: eating disorder treatment targets food-related behaviors, eating patterns, and weight/shape-related cognitions, while BDD treatment targets appearance-related checking, avoidance, and perceived flaw preoccupations.

When both conditions co-occur, treatment typically needs to integrate elements from both frameworks — an approach that requires a clinician familiar with both conditions.

## Muscle Dysmorphia: The Bridge Between BDD and Eating Disorders

Muscle dysmorphia — a preoccupation with perceived insufficient muscularity, often despite a physique that is objectively muscular — sits at the intersection of BDD and eating disorders in a way that challenges clean diagnostic boundaries.

The DSM-5-TR classifies muscle dysmorphia as a specifier of BDD [1]. However, muscle dysmorphia frequently involves disordered eating behaviors: rigid dietary regimens, excessive protein supplementation, and sometimes restriction or purging in the service of body composition goals. It also involves compulsive exercise patterns that parallel those seen in eating disorders.

Research by Murray and colleagues (2012) has argued that muscle dysmorphia may represent a form of eating disorder rather than BDD, given the prominence of food-related pathology in its presentation [7]. Others maintain the BDD classification based on the prominence of body checking, mirror avoidance, and perceived flaw preoccupation.

What is clear is that muscle dysmorphia affects predominantly (but not exclusively) men, involves significant psychological distress and functional impairment, and often goes unrecognized by clinicians who are not aware of its clinical presentation. It is discussed in greater depth in PSY-EAT-019 of this series.

## Neurobiological Distinctions

Neuroimaging research has begun to identify differences in brain processing between BDD and eating disorders that support their classification as distinct conditions.

In BDD, functional neuroimaging studies by Feusner and colleagues (2010) have found that individuals with BDD show altered processing in visual cortex regions — specifically, enhanced local (detail-focused) visual processing at the expense of global (holistic) visual processing [8]. When viewing faces, individuals with BDD process the fine details (pores, asymmetries, minor contours) more intensely than the overall face. This perceptual style — "seeing the trees but missing the forest" — contributes to the experience of perceived flaws that others cannot see.

In eating disorders, the visual processing distortion is specific to one's own body and involves different brain networks — particularly the insula and parietal cortex (body schema and interoception) rather than the face-processing areas implicated in BDD [9].

These neurobiological differences support the clinical distinction: BDD is fundamentally a disorder of how specific features are visually processed, while eating disorder body image disturbance involves broader disruptions in body representation, interoception, and emotional processing.

## Getting the Right Diagnosis

Misdiagnosis between BDD and eating disorders occurs in both directions and has real consequences.

A person with BDD whose appearance concerns are misinterpreted as an eating disorder may receive inappropriate treatment — dietary rehabilitation and food-focused therapy for a condition that has nothing to do with food. A person with an eating disorder whose weight and shape concerns are misattributed to BDD may receive ERP and high-dose SSRIs without the nutritional rehabilitation and eating-behavior-focused therapy they need.

Accurate diagnosis requires careful assessment of:
- **What is the focus of the body image concern?** Weight/shape = eating disorder. Specific non-weight features = BDD.
- **What behaviors does the concern drive?** Eating-related behaviors = eating disorder. Checking, grooming, camouflaging, reassurance-seeking = BDD.
- **Is there a reality gap?** In BDD, the perceived flaw is not observable or appears slight. In eating disorders, the body image distortion may involve overestimation of body size, but the body itself is typically visible.

Clinicians who are unfamiliar with BDD may miss it entirely, particularly when it co-occurs with an eating disorder. Routine screening for BDD using validated instruments such as the Body Dysmorphic Disorder Questionnaire (BDDQ) is recommended for all individuals presenting with body image distress [10].

> 💡 **Spark:** BDD and eating disorders both involve suffering about the body — but they are different kinds of suffering with different causes and different treatments. Getting the diagnosis right is not a technicality. It is the difference between receiving the help that matches your condition and receiving help that does not.

## What You Can Try

- **Identify the focus of your distress.** Is it primarily about body weight and shape, driving food-related behaviors? Or is it about specific physical features, driving checking and camouflaging behaviors? This distinction is the starting point for accurate diagnosis.

- **Seek assessment from a clinician familiar with both conditions.** Many eating disorder specialists have limited training in BDD, and many OCD specialists have limited training in eating disorders. Look for a clinician who can assess for both.

- **If you have both conditions,** communicate this to your treatment team. Integrated treatment that addresses both BDD and eating disorder symptoms simultaneously produces better outcomes than sequential treatment.
