> **Summary:** Night eating syndrome (NES) is a clinically recognized condition characterized by a significant shift in the circadian pattern of eating — consuming little or nothing during the morning, eating the majority of daily intake in the evening and night hours, and frequently waking from sleep to eat. First described by Albert Stunkard in 1955 and formally operationalized by Kelly Allison and colleagues in 2010, NES sits at the intersection of eating disorders, circadian biology, and sleep medicine. It is distinct from binge eating disorder (the episodes are typically small, not binge-sized), distinct from sleep-related eating disorder (the person is fully awake and aware), and distinct from simple late-night snacking (the pattern is persistent, distressing, and associated with measurable disruptions in melatonin, cortisol, and leptin rhythms). Affecting an estimated 1.5% of the general population and up to 25% of people seeking obesity treatment, NES remains underdiagnosed, often misunderstood as a willpower problem, and responsive to targeted treatments when properly identified.

**Key Facts:**
1. Night eating syndrome was first described by Albert Stunkard at the University of Pennsylvania in 1955, making it one of the earliest recognized eating behavior disorders — yet it was not given proposed diagnostic criteria until 2010 [1].
2. NES is defined by evening hyperphagia (consuming 25% or more of daily intake after the evening meal) and/or nocturnal ingestions (waking to eat at least twice per week), with full awareness during eating episodes [2].
3. Circadian biomarker research has shown that people with NES have delayed rhythms of melatonin, cortisol, leptin, and ghrelin — the eating is not simply a behavioral habit but reflects a measurable phase-shift in the body's internal clock [3].
4. NES prevalence is approximately 1.5% in the general population but rises to 6–16% in obesity treatment settings and up to 25% in bariatric surgery populations, suggesting a strong relationship between disrupted eating timing and metabolic health [4].
5. Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), particularly sertraline, have demonstrated efficacy in reducing NES symptoms in randomized controlled trials, supporting the role of serotonergic dysregulation in the condition [5].

## A Disorder With a 70-Year History and Still No DSM Entry

The story of night eating syndrome is, in part, a story about how slowly clinical recognition moves for conditions that do not fit neatly into existing categories. Albert Stunkard, one of the most influential figures in eating disorders research, first described NES in a 1955 paper studying obese patients who were resistant to conventional weight management. He noticed a consistent pattern: morning anorexia (absence of appetite on waking), evening hyperphagia (heavy eating in the late evening), and insomnia, often accompanied by nocturnal eating with full awareness [1].

Despite Stunkard's description, NES received minimal research attention for decades. It did not fit the emerging clinical profiles of anorexia nervosa or bulimia nervosa. It did not involve the dramatic binge episodes that defined binge eating disorder when it was formally recognized in the 1990s. NES patients were not underweight. They were not purging. They were doing something that, to casual observation, looked like ordinary nighttime snacking — just more of it, more consistently, and with more distress.

The formal operationalization of NES came in 2010, when Kelly Allison and colleagues at the University of Pennsylvania published proposed diagnostic criteria in the *International Journal of Eating Disorders* [2]. These criteria established a clear clinical picture: the core features are evening hyperphagia (25% or more of daily caloric intake consumed after the evening meal) and/or nocturnal ingestions (waking from sleep to eat at least two times per week). The individual must be fully aware during eating episodes. The pattern must have persisted for at least three months. At least three of five associated features must be present: morning anorexia, a strong urge to eat between dinner and sleep, insomnia (at least four nights per week), a belief that eating is necessary to fall asleep or return to sleep, and frequent depressed mood that worsens in the evening [2].

NES was included in the DSM-5 (2013) under Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder (OSFED) as a named example, rather than receiving its own diagnostic category [6]. This partial recognition reflects the ongoing tension between clinical reality — NES is a distinct, measurable condition with a specific biological profile — and diagnostic convention, which has been slow to accommodate eating disorders that do not center on body image disturbance or binge-purge behavior.

## The Circadian Biology of Night Eating

The most compelling evidence that NES is more than a behavioral habit comes from circadian biology research. The human body runs on a network of internal clocks — the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus serves as the master clock, coordinating peripheral clocks in the liver, gut, pancreas, and adipose tissue. These clocks regulate the timing of hunger, satiety, insulin sensitivity, and energy metabolism through rhythmic patterns of hormone release [7].

In people without NES, these rhythms follow a predictable pattern: cortisol peaks in the early morning (promoting wakefulness and appetite), leptin rises in the evening (signaling satiety and preparing the body for overnight fasting), melatonin increases as darkness falls (promoting sleep), and ghrelin — the primary hunger hormone — follows a pattern that aligns appetite with daytime activity [3].

Research by Birketvedt and colleagues (1999), published in *JAMA*, provided the first direct evidence that these rhythms are disrupted in NES. Compared to controls, people with NES showed delayed nocturnal rise of melatonin, attenuated nighttime rise of leptin, and elevated nighttime cortisol [3]. In plain terms: the hormonal signals that should be suppressing hunger and promoting sleep in the late evening are delayed, blunted, or inverted. The person with NES is not simply choosing to eat at night against their body's signals. Their body's signals are telling them to eat at night.

Goel and colleagues (2009) extended this work by documenting altered circadian gene expression in NES, including clock genes such as PER1, PER2, and BMAL1 — the same molecular clockwork that governs circadian rhythms at the cellular level [8]. This finding positions NES within the broader family of circadian rhythm disorders, alongside conditions like delayed sleep phase disorder and shift work disorder, rather than solely within the eating disorder category. It is, in essence, a disorder of when the body expects to eat — not what or how much.

This circadian framing has practical implications. Interventions that target circadian realignment — bright light therapy, chronotherapy, and structured meal timing — have shown promising results precisely because they address the biological mechanism rather than attempting to override it with willpower [9].

## NES Versus Everything It Gets Confused With

NES is frequently confused with three other conditions, and the distinctions matter for treatment.

**NES versus binge eating disorder (BED).** The most common confusion. BED involves discrete episodes of eating objectively large amounts of food in a short period, accompanied by a subjective sense of loss of control [6]. NES episodes are typically not binges. The nocturnal eating is often modest in quantity — a bowl of cereal, a piece of toast, a handful of crackers — consumed calmly, without the frantic quality of a binge episode. People with NES are fully aware of what they are eating and can describe the experience clearly. The distinction is temporal (NES is about when eating occurs) rather than quantitative (BED is about how much). However, NES and BED can co-occur. Approximately 15–20% of people with NES also meet criteria for BED [2].

**NES versus sleep-related eating disorder (SRED).** This is the critical distinction that most popular articles about nighttime eating miss entirely. SRED is a parasomnia — a disorder of arousal from sleep, classified under sleep-wake disorders rather than eating disorders. People with SRED eat during partial arousals from sleep, often with limited or no awareness. They may consume unusual or inedible items. They frequently have no memory of the eating episode the following morning. NES, by contrast, involves full wakefulness and awareness during eating [10]. A person with NES wakes up, knows they are awake, walks to the kitchen, selects food deliberately, eats it, and returns to bed. A person with SRED may be found standing at the stove cooking while still functionally asleep. These are neurologically distinct conditions requiring different treatments.

**NES versus normal late-night snacking.** Not everyone who eats late at night has NES. The distinguishing features are distress, impairment, and persistence. A person who occasionally has a snack before bed because they are hungry is not exhibiting pathology. A person who consistently cannot fall asleep or return to sleep without eating, who consumes a quarter or more of their daily intake after dinner most nights, who experiences morning nausea from nighttime eating, and who feels distressed by this pattern may meet criteria for NES. The clinical threshold is not arbitrary. It reflects the point at which the pattern causes measurable disruption to sleep quality, metabolic health, and psychological well-being [2].

## Psychological and Emotional Dimensions

While the circadian biology of NES is increasingly well-documented, the condition also has significant psychological dimensions that interact with and amplify the biological disruption.

Depression is the most consistent psychological correlate of NES. Allison and colleagues (2005) found that individuals with NES scored significantly higher on measures of depression than matched controls without NES, with evening and nighttime mood deterioration being a particularly characteristic pattern [11]. Many people with NES describe a worsening of mood after dinner — increasing restlessness, anxiety, and sadness that eating temporarily alleviates. The nocturnal eating may function, in part, as a form of self-medication for nighttime distress.

Stress is another significant contributor. NES symptoms often intensify during periods of high psychological stress, consistent with the broader relationship between cortisol dysregulation and eating behavior disruption described in stress-eating research. The evening cortisol elevation documented in NES may represent both a circadian shift and a chronic stress response [3].

The relationship between NES and sleep is bidirectional and self-reinforcing. Disrupted sleep increases hunger hormones and emotional distress. Eating at night disrupts sleep architecture. Poor sleep increases next-day fatigue and mood disturbance, which feeds back into evening eating and insomnia. People with NES often describe a feeling of being "trapped" in this cycle — unable to sleep without eating, unable to eat without disrupting sleep [4].

Shame is a frequently underaddressed psychological dimension. Because nighttime eating is often framed culturally as a failure of discipline, people with NES may spend years concealing the behavior from partners, roommates, and healthcare providers. They may interpret their own experience through the lens of moral failure rather than medical condition — "I just have no self-control" — which prevents them from seeking the diagnosis and treatment that could resolve the pattern [11].

## Treatment: What Works and Why

The evidence base for NES treatment has grown substantially since the early 2000s, with several approaches demonstrating efficacy.

**Pharmacotherapy.** The most robust pharmacological evidence supports sertraline, an SSRI. O'Reardon and colleagues (2006) conducted a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial demonstrating that sertraline significantly reduced NES symptoms, including nocturnal ingestions, evening hyperphagia, and nighttime awakenings — with effects observed as early as week two of treatment [5]. The serotonergic mechanism is relevant because serotonin plays a central role in both satiety signaling and circadian rhythm regulation. By modulating serotonin availability, SSRIs may address both the eating and the circadian components of NES simultaneously.

**Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).** Allison and colleagues developed a CBT protocol specifically adapted for NES (CBT-NES), targeting the behavioral patterns (nighttime eating, morning skipping), cognitive distortions ("I can't sleep without eating"), and mood management skills relevant to the condition. A pilot study demonstrated significant reductions in nocturnal ingestions, evening hyperphagia, and associated distress [12]. CBT-NES typically includes structured meal planning to redistribute caloric intake toward earlier in the day, sleep hygiene education, stimulus control techniques, and cognitive restructuring of beliefs about the necessity of nighttime eating.

**Bright light therapy.** Based on the circadian disruption model, morning bright light therapy has been investigated as a non-pharmacological intervention for NES. The rationale is straightforward: if NES reflects a delayed circadian phase, then advancing that phase with appropriately timed light exposure should reduce evening and nighttime eating urges. Preliminary research supports this approach, though large-scale randomized controlled trials are still needed [9]. Standard light therapy protocols use a 10,000 lux light box for 30 minutes within one hour of waking.

**Progressive muscle relaxation (PMR).** Given the role of evening stress and anxiety in NES, relaxation training has been investigated as a component of treatment. A 2002 study by Pawlow, O'Neil, and Malcolm found that a single PMR session reduced anxiety, salivary cortisol, and hunger in people with NES — suggesting that addressing the stress-arousal component may reduce the drive to eat nocturnally [4].

**Chronotherapy and structured meal timing.** Redistributing food intake toward the morning and midday — eating breakfast even when appetite is absent, ensuring adequate lunch, and front-loading calories — can gradually recalibrate eating rhythms. This approach requires consistency over weeks and is most effective when combined with morning bright light exposure and regular sleep-wake timing [9].

> 💡 **Spark:** Night eating syndrome is not a willpower problem that happens in the dark. It is a circadian system misfiring — a body clock that has shifted its hunger and wakefulness signals to the wrong hours. Recognizing this transforms the experience from personal failure to biological puzzle, and biological puzzles have solutions.

## What You Can Try

- **Track your eating timing for one week.** Without changing anything, record the time and approximate quantity of everything you eat, including nighttime episodes. Note whether you were hungry, anxious, or unable to sleep. This data will clarify whether your pattern fits NES criteria and provide a baseline for any intervention.

- **Eat breakfast within one hour of waking, even without appetite.** Morning anorexia is a hallmark of NES, but eating breakfast — even a small amount — begins the process of redistributing intake toward daytime hours. Start with whatever is tolerable: a piece of toast, a handful of nuts, a glass of milk.

- **Try 30 minutes of bright light exposure within one hour of waking.** Use a 10,000 lux light therapy box or spend time outdoors in natural morning light. This is the most direct non-pharmacological intervention for circadian phase delay.

- **Practice progressive muscle relaxation before bed.** Tense and release each muscle group from feet to forehead over 15–20 minutes. This reduces the cortisol elevation and emotional distress that often precede nocturnal eating episodes.

- **Discuss your pattern with a healthcare provider.** If you consistently eat 25% or more of daily intake after dinner, wake to eat multiple nights per week, and experience distress about the pattern, you may benefit from a formal NES evaluation. The Night Eating Questionnaire (NEQ), developed by Allison and colleagues, is a validated screening tool that your provider can administer.
