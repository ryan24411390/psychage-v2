> **Summary:** Pica and rumination disorder are feeding and eating disorders that exist almost entirely outside public awareness. Pica — the persistent eating of non-nutritive, non-food substances — and rumination disorder — the repeated regurgitation and re-chewing, re-swallowing, or spitting out of food — are clinically recognized conditions in the DSM-5-TR that can cause severe medical complications and significant distress. Neither condition is driven by body image concerns or the desire to control weight. They occur across the lifespan, affect people with and without intellectual disabilities, and are far more prevalent than most clinicians and the general public realize. This article explores what pica and rumination disorder actually involve, who is affected, what drives them, and what effective treatment approaches look like.

**Key Facts:**
1. Pica involves the persistent eating of non-nutritive, non-food substances — such as ice, soil, chalk, paper, or metal — over a period of at least one month, at a developmental stage where such behavior is not expected [1].
2. Prevalence of pica is estimated at 5–28% among individuals with intellectual disabilities and 8–65% during pregnancy, with far less data available for the general population [2].
3. Rumination disorder involves the repeated regurgitation of food for at least one month, which may be re-chewed, re-swallowed, or spit out, without associated nausea, retching, or disgust [1].
4. Rumination disorder in adults is increasingly recognized as underdiagnosed — a 2017 study found a prevalence of 3.1% in a community sample of young adults [3].
5. Both conditions can cause serious medical complications: pica can lead to poisoning, intestinal obstruction, and parasitic infections; rumination disorder can cause malnutrition, dental erosion, and esophageal damage [4].

## Pica: Eating What Is Not Food

Pica is defined in the DSM-5-TR as the persistent eating of non-nutritive, non-food substances over a period of at least one month [1]. The behavior must be inappropriate for the individual's developmental level (mouthing objects is developmentally normal in infants and toddlers, for example) and not attributable to a culturally sanctioned practice. Importantly, pica can co-occur with other eating disorders and with other psychiatric or developmental conditions — but the pica behavior must be sufficiently severe and persistent to warrant independent clinical attention.

The range of substances consumed in pica is extensive and varies by individual. Commonly consumed items include ice (pagophagia), soil or clay (geophagy), starch (amylophagy), chalk, paper, soap, cloth, hair, paint chips, and metal objects. Less common but documented substances include batteries, glass, coins, and sharp objects. The specific substance consumed sometimes provides clinical information: pagophagia (ice craving) is strongly associated with iron deficiency anemia, while geophagy has been observed across cultures and historical periods, sometimes in contexts that suggest mineral supplementation or toxin binding [5].

The medical consequences of pica can be severe. Ingestion of non-food items can cause dental damage, gastrointestinal obstruction or perforation, lead poisoning (from paint chips), parasitic infections (from soil), electrolyte imbalances, and in extreme cases, death from intestinal perforation or toxic substance ingestion. A systematic review by Chatoor and Macaoay (2008) documented cases of emergency surgical intervention for intestinal bezoars — compacted masses of ingested non-food material that can obstruct or rupture the gastrointestinal tract [4].

### Who Develops Pica?

Pica occurs across multiple populations, and the driving factors differ by context:

**Pregnancy.** Pica during pregnancy is remarkably common, with prevalence estimates ranging from 8% to 65% depending on the population studied and the cultural context [2]. Ice and soil/clay are the most commonly consumed substances during pregnancy. The relationship between pregnancy pica and iron deficiency is well-documented but directionally complex — in some cases, iron deficiency appears to drive the pica behavior (and iron supplementation resolves it), while in other cases, the ingestion of clay or starch may itself interfere with iron absorption, creating or worsening the deficiency [6].

**Intellectual and developmental disabilities.** Pica is significantly more prevalent among individuals with intellectual disabilities, with rates estimated at 5–28% in institutional and community settings [2]. In this population, pica may be related to sensory stimulation, limited understanding of food/non-food distinctions, communication limitations, or comorbid psychiatric conditions. The medical risks are compounded by the fact that individuals with intellectual disabilities may have difficulty communicating symptoms of gastrointestinal distress.

**Autism spectrum conditions.** Pica co-occurs with autism at elevated rates, potentially related to sensory-seeking behavior, restricted interests, and executive function differences. A study by Fields and colleagues (2021) found that pica in autistic children was associated with higher levels of overall sensory seeking and with specific oral sensory interests [7].

**Children and adolescents without developmental disabilities.** Pica also occurs in developmentally typical children, where it may be associated with nutritional deficiencies, stressful home environments, neglect, or anxiety. In these cases, pica sometimes represents a self-soothing behavior or a response to environmental deprivation.

**Adults in the general population.** Pica in adults without intellectual disabilities is less well-studied but increasingly recognized. Case reports document pica in the context of iron deficiency, OCD, schizophrenia, autism, stress, and without any identifiable comorbid condition. The stigma associated with eating non-food items means that adults with pica may conceal their behavior for years before seeking or receiving clinical attention.

### What Drives Pica?

The etiology of pica is likely multifactorial, and no single explanation accounts for all presentations. Proposed mechanisms include:

**Nutritional deficiency.** Iron, zinc, and calcium deficiencies have been associated with pica in multiple populations. The relationship is strongest for pagophagia (ice) and iron deficiency, where iron supplementation often resolves the ice cravings within days to weeks [5].

**Sensory reinforcement.** The texture, temperature, crunch, or mouthfeel of non-food substances may provide sensory stimulation that is experienced as rewarding or soothing. This mechanism is particularly relevant in populations with sensory processing differences.

**Emotional regulation.** Pica behavior may function as a self-soothing strategy in the context of stress, anxiety, or environmental deprivation. The rhythmic, oral nature of the behavior has parallels with other self-regulatory behaviors such as thumb-sucking or nail-biting.

**Learned behavior.** In some cases, particularly in childhood, pica may begin through accidental ingestion that is subsequently reinforced by sensory properties or by the social attention it generates.

## Rumination Disorder: The Condition Hidden in Plain Sight

Rumination disorder is defined in the DSM-5-TR as the repeated regurgitation of food over a period of at least one month [1]. The regurgitated food may be re-chewed and re-swallowed, re-chewed and spit out, or simply spat out. The regurgitation is not associated with nausea, involuntary retching, or a diagnosable gastrointestinal condition (such as gastroesophageal reflux disease) that would fully account for the behavior. The condition must also not occur exclusively during the course of another eating disorder.

Rumination disorder has historically been conceptualized as a condition of infancy or of individuals with severe intellectual disabilities. This is incomplete. A 2017 population-based study by Rajindrajith and colleagues found a prevalence of 3.1% in a community sample of young adults — suggesting that rumination disorder in cognitively typical adults is far more common than previously believed [3].

The mechanism of rumination involves a learned, habitual pattern of voluntary (though often only semi-conscious) abdominal contraction that raises gastric content into the esophagus and mouth shortly after eating. Unlike vomiting, rumination is typically effortless and non-forceful. Unlike gastroesophageal reflux, it occurs primarily in the postprandial period (shortly after meals) and can be voluntarily initiated, even if the person does not experience it as fully volitional [8].

### Medical Consequences

The medical consequences of rumination disorder can be significant. Chronic regurgitation exposes the esophagus and oral cavity to gastric acid, leading to dental erosion, esophageal irritation, and potential Barrett's esophagus. Nutritional consequences include weight loss, malnutrition, and electrolyte disturbances. Social consequences are equally important — the behavior can cause embarrassment, social withdrawal, and avoidance of eating with others.

In infants, severe rumination disorder can lead to failure to thrive, malnutrition, and in rare cases, death. In adults, the condition is often misdiagnosed as gastroparesis, GERD, or cyclical vomiting syndrome, leading to years of inappropriate treatment before accurate diagnosis [8].

### Understanding the Psychology

In infants and individuals with intellectual disabilities, rumination is often understood as a self-stimulatory behavior — a learned pattern that provides sensory reinforcement through the oral and gastric sensations associated with regurgitation.

In cognitively typical children and adults, the picture is more nuanced. Research by Murray and colleagues (2019) proposed a biopsychosocial model of rumination disorder in which the behavior develops through an interaction of physiological vulnerability (heightened gastric sensitivity, delayed gastric accommodation), behavioral learning (the regurgitation pattern becomes habitual and self-reinforcing), and psychological factors (stress, anxiety, social avoidance) [9].

Many adults with rumination disorder describe the behavior as semi-automatic — they are aware of it but do not experience it as fully under voluntary control. The regurgitation often occurs without conscious initiation, though it can sometimes be suppressed with effort. Over time, the behavior becomes increasingly habitual and difficult to disrupt without targeted intervention.

## Treatment: What Works

Treatment for pica and rumination disorder differs significantly, reflecting the different mechanisms underlying each condition.

### Pica Treatment

**Nutritional assessment and supplementation** should be the first step for any individual presenting with pica. If a deficiency — particularly iron — is identified, supplementation may resolve the pica behavior without further intervention [5].

**Behavioral interventions** are the primary treatment approach for pica in individuals with intellectual disabilities. Applied behavior analysis (ABA) approaches, including differential reinforcement of alternative behavior and environmental modification (reducing access to non-food items), have the strongest evidence base. A meta-analysis by McAdam and colleagues (2004) found that behavioral interventions produced clinically significant reductions in pica across multiple studies [10].

**CBT-based approaches** may be appropriate for cognitively typical children and adults with pica, addressing the emotional and cognitive factors that maintain the behavior.

### Rumination Disorder Treatment

**Diaphragmatic breathing training** is the primary evidence-based treatment for rumination disorder and is remarkably effective. The technique involves teaching the patient to engage in slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing immediately after meals — a behavior that is physically incompatible with the abdominal contractions that produce regurgitation. Research by Barba and colleagues (2015) found that diaphragmatic breathing training reduced rumination episodes by approximately 74% in a sample of adults with rumination disorder [11].

**Habit reversal therapy,** which combines awareness training (helping the person identify the early sensations and contexts that precede rumination) with competing response training (engaging in an alternative behavior that prevents rumination), has also shown efficacy.

**Biofeedback** — using real-time monitoring of abdominal muscle activity to teach the patient to suppress the contractions that produce regurgitation — is an emerging approach with preliminary evidence of effectiveness.

> 💡 **Spark:** Pica and rumination disorder are not curiosities or footnotes in the eating disorders chapter. They are conditions that cause real suffering in real people — and they respond to treatment when they are correctly identified. The first step is knowing they exist.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are consuming non-food substances**, talk to a healthcare provider about nutritional testing. Iron, zinc, and calcium deficiencies should be assessed as a first step.

- **If you experience repeated, effortless regurgitation after meals**, this may be rumination disorder rather than acid reflux. Ask your gastroenterologist or primary care provider about this possibility, particularly if standard GERD treatments have not been effective.

- **Practice diaphragmatic breathing after meals** if you experience rumination. Breathe slowly and deeply, expanding your abdomen rather than your chest, for 5–10 minutes after eating. This can help interrupt the regurgitation pattern.

- **Track patterns** using Psychage's Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) to identify emotional or situational triggers.

- **Seek specialized care.** Both pica and rumination disorder benefit from clinicians who are familiar with these specific presentations.
