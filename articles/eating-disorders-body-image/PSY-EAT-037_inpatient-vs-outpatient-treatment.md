> **Summary:** Eating disorder treatment is not one-size-fits-all. It exists on a continuum of care — from outpatient therapy (one to two sessions per week while living at home) to full medical hospitalization — with several intermediate levels in between. The appropriate level of care depends on the person's medical stability, psychiatric safety, eating disorder severity, motivation, support system, and response to less intensive treatment. Understanding the different levels of care — what each involves, who it serves, and what transitions between levels look like — helps individuals and families navigate the treatment landscape with clearer expectations. This article maps the full continuum of eating disorder care, explains the clinical criteria that guide level-of-care decisions, and addresses the practical realities of accessing each level.

**Key Facts:**
1. The American Psychiatric Association defines five levels of care for eating disorders: outpatient, intensive outpatient (IOP), partial hospitalization (PHP), residential, and inpatient/medical hospitalization [1].
2. Most individuals with eating disorders can be treated effectively at the outpatient level, with fewer than 10% requiring residential or inpatient treatment [2].
3. Medical instability — including severe bradycardia, dangerous electrolyte imbalances, syncope, or active suicidality — is the primary criterion for inpatient hospitalization [3].
4. Research shows that longer duration of treatment at appropriate levels of care is associated with better outcomes and reduced relapse, but insurance limitations frequently shorten treatment prematurely [4].
5. Step-down from higher to lower levels of care (e.g., residential to PHP to IOP to outpatient) should be gradual, with adequate support at each transition to prevent relapse [5].

## The Continuum of Care

Eating disorder treatment is organized across a continuum of intensity, with each level designed to provide the appropriate amount of structure, supervision, and medical monitoring for the individual's current clinical needs [1].

### Outpatient Treatment
**Structure:** 1–2 sessions per week (therapy, dietitian, and/or psychiatrist). The individual lives at home, attends work or school, and manages their own meals.
**Appropriate for:** Individuals who are medically stable, psychiatrically safe (no active suicidality), able to eat adequately with outpatient support, and have a reasonable support system at home.
**Treatment approaches:** CBT-E, FBT (for adolescents), IPT, DBT, individual nutritional counseling.
**Strengths:** Least disruptive to daily life, allows real-world practice of recovery skills, most widely available, least expensive.
**Limitations:** Limited supervision between sessions, relies on patient's ability to manage meals and resist eating disorder behaviors independently.

### Intensive Outpatient (IOP)
**Structure:** Typically 3–4 days per week, 3–4 hours per session. The individual lives at home but attends the program several times weekly, usually including one or more supervised meals.
**Appropriate for:** Individuals who need more support than weekly outpatient but are medically stable and can manage some meals independently. Often used as a step-down from PHP or as a step-up from outpatient when outpatient alone is insufficient.
**Strengths:** More structure and group support than outpatient, while maintaining daily life activities.

### Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP) / Day Treatment
**Structure:** 5–7 days per week, 6–10 hours per day. The individual attends the program during the day (typically arriving for breakfast and remaining through dinner) but returns home to sleep.
**Appropriate for:** Individuals who are medically stable but unable to manage meals independently, who need daily supervised eating, or who require daily therapeutic contact. Often the step-down from residential treatment.
**Strengths:** Daily meal supervision, intensive therapeutic programming, maintenance of some home life and family connection.

### Residential Treatment
**Structure:** 24-hour care in a non-hospital residential facility, typically for 30–90 days. All meals are supervised, therapeutic programming occurs throughout the day, and the individual lives on-site.
**Appropriate for:** Individuals who are medically stable but unable to manage their eating disorder in a less structured environment, who have not responded to outpatient or PHP treatment, or who need a sustained period of separation from the triggers and routines of their home environment.
**Strengths:** Complete meal supervision, intensive therapeutic community, removal from triggering environments, comprehensive multidisciplinary care.
**Limitations:** Expensive, disruptive to work/school/family, transition back to home life is a high-risk period for relapse, geographic access is limited [6].

### Inpatient/Medical Hospitalization
**Structure:** 24-hour medical care in a hospital setting, with continuous monitoring of vital signs, electrolytes, and cardiac function.
**Appropriate for:** Individuals who are medically unstable — including severe bradycardia (heart rate below 40 bpm), dangerous electrolyte imbalances, active suicidality, severe dehydration, or medical complications requiring acute management. Also appropriate for refeeding initiation in severely malnourished patients who are at risk for refeeding syndrome [3].
**Strengths:** Highest level of medical monitoring, life-saving for medically unstable patients.
**Limitations:** Addresses medical stabilization but not the underlying eating disorder psychopathology — psychological treatment must continue after discharge.

## How Level of Care Is Determined

Clinical guidelines provide criteria for determining the appropriate level of care. The APA guidelines and the Academy for Eating Disorders medical care guidelines identify several dimensions for assessment [1, 3]:

**Medical status.** Heart rate, blood pressure, electrolytes, weight trajectory, and other physical indicators of medical stability. Medical instability requires higher levels of care.

**Suicidality.** Active suicidal ideation with plan or intent requires inpatient psychiatric hospitalization. Passive suicidal ideation may be manageable at lower levels with safety planning.

**Weight status and trajectory.** Severe low weight, rapid weight loss, or failure to gain weight in outpatient treatment may indicate need for higher-level care.

**Motivation and engagement.** Individuals with very low motivation may need the structure of residential or PHP treatment to initiate behavioral change. However, motivation often develops during treatment rather than preceding it.

**Eating disorder behaviors.** Frequency and severity of restriction, bingeing, purging, and compensatory exercise. More severe behaviors that cannot be interrupted in less intensive settings require more structure.

**Environmental factors.** Home environment (supportive vs. triggering), availability of outpatient providers, insurance coverage, geographic access, and family involvement all influence level-of-care decisions.

**Co-occurring conditions.** Comorbid substance use, severe depression, anxiety, PTSD, or personality disorders may require higher levels of care that can address multiple conditions simultaneously.

## The Transition Challenge

One of the most clinically significant aspects of the levels-of-care system is the transition between levels. Research consistently identifies step-down transitions — moving from a more intensive to a less intensive level of care — as high-risk periods for relapse [5].

The transition from residential to home life is particularly challenging. In residential treatment, all meals are prepared and supervised, the environment is controlled, and peers are other individuals in recovery. Returning home means returning to the triggers, routines, and food environment that surrounded the eating disorder — with dramatically less support. Research by Olmsted and colleagues (2005) found that the first month after residential discharge is the highest-risk period for relapse [7].

Best practice guidelines recommend gradual step-down: residential → PHP → IOP → outpatient, with adequate time at each level to consolidate gains before reducing intensity. Unfortunately, insurance limitations frequently truncate this process, pushing patients to lower levels of care before they are clinically ready — a pattern that contributes to the high relapse rates observed in eating disorder treatment.

**Discharge planning** should begin at admission, with clear criteria for stepping down, identified outpatient providers, a structured meal plan for home, and a relapse prevention plan that includes specific triggers, warning signs, and action steps [8].

## Access and Insurance: The Practical Realities

The eating disorder treatment system in many countries is characterized by significant access barriers.

**Cost.** Residential eating disorder treatment in the United States typically costs $500–$2,000 per day, with a 30-day stay ranging from $15,000 to $60,000. Inpatient hospitalization can exceed $3,000 per day. Even outpatient treatment — therapy, dietitian, and psychiatrist — can cost $300–$600 per week without insurance [9].

**Insurance coverage.** Mental health parity laws (such as the US Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act) require that eating disorder treatment be covered at parity with medical conditions, but enforcement is inconsistent and denial of coverage remains common. Insurance companies frequently authorize fewer days at higher levels of care than clinical guidelines recommend, and coverage disputes are a major source of stress for patients and families [4].

**Geographic access.** Eating disorder specialists are concentrated in urban areas, and residential treatment programs are scarce — particularly in rural areas and outside of North America, Western Europe, and Australia. Telehealth has expanded access to outpatient treatment but cannot substitute for in-person residential or inpatient care.

**Waitlists.** Many eating disorder programs, particularly publicly funded programs and specialized services, have waitlists ranging from weeks to months — during which the eating disorder may worsen [10].

## Making the Decision

Choosing the right level of care is a clinical decision that should involve the treatment team, the patient, and (for adolescents) the family. Several principles can guide this decision:

**Start with the least intensive level likely to be effective.** Outpatient treatment is the default starting point for most eating disorders. If outpatient treatment is insufficient after an adequate trial, stepping up to IOP or PHP is appropriate.

**Do not wait until crisis to escalate.** If an individual is deteriorating at their current level of care — losing weight, increasing eating disorder behaviors, becoming more depressed or suicidal — the appropriate response is to step up to a more intensive level rather than waiting for a medical emergency.

**Treatment at a higher level is not failure.** Needing residential or inpatient treatment does not mean the patient has failed at outpatient treatment. It means their eating disorder requires more support than outpatient can provide — just as a broken bone that does not heal with a cast is not the patient's fault.

**Plan for transitions.** Every step down in intensity should be planned, gradual, and supported. Rushing transitions increases relapse risk.

> 💡 **Spark:** The right level of care is not the most intensive or the least disruptive — it is the level that provides enough structure to interrupt the eating disorder while building the skills that will sustain recovery in the real world.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are considering treatment,** start by consulting an eating disorder specialist who can assess your current clinical needs and recommend an appropriate level of care.

- **If outpatient treatment is not working,** talk to your treatment team about stepping up to IOP or PHP. Escalating is a sign of advocating for your recovery, not failing at it.

- **If you are navigating insurance,** organizations like NEDA and the National Alliance for Eating Disorders offer insurance advocacy support and can help with appeals.

- **Plan your transitions.** Before stepping down from a higher level of care, establish your outpatient team, develop a structured meal plan, and create a relapse prevention plan.
