> **Summary:** Athletes are among the highest-risk populations for eating disorders, yet their symptoms are often celebrated as dedication, discipline, and commitment to performance. The intersection of intense physical training, weight-class requirements, aesthetic judging criteria, performance pressure, and a culture that rewards thinness or leanness creates conditions in which disordered eating can develop rapidly and be maintained invisibly. The International Olympic Committee has recognized this through the framework of Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) — a syndrome in which inadequate energy intake relative to exercise expenditure produces cascading health consequences across virtually every physiological system. This article explores the unique risk factors for eating disorders in athletes, the clinical presentation of RED-S, the sports and contexts that carry the highest risk, and how coaches, medical teams, and athletes themselves can intervene early.

**Key Facts:**
1. Eating disorder prevalence in athletes is estimated at 6–45% depending on the sport, gender, and competitive level — consistently higher than in the general population [1].
2. The IOC consensus statement on RED-S (2014, updated 2018) identified relative energy deficiency as a syndrome affecting 10 physiological systems including bone, cardiovascular, endocrine, metabolic, and psychological health [2].
3. Sports that emphasize leanness, weight classes, or aesthetic appearance — including gymnastics, wrestling, distance running, figure skating, ballet, rowing, and diving — carry the highest risk [3].
4. The "female athlete triad" (low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, and low bone density) was the precursor concept to RED-S and remains a critical clinical concern in female athletes [4].
5. Athletes with eating disorders are at elevated risk for stress fractures, cardiac events, heat illness, and performance decline — yet many continue training with active eating disorders due to pressure from coaches and competition schedules [5].

## Why Athletes Are Uniquely Vulnerable

The athletic environment concentrates several of the most potent risk factors for eating disorders into a single context. Understanding these factors is essential for coaches, medical professionals, parents, and athletes themselves.

**Performance-linked body demands.** Many sports directly link body weight or composition to competitive advantage — or at least to the perception of competitive advantage. Weight-class sports (wrestling, boxing, rowing, martial arts) require athletes to make specific weight targets, creating structural incentives for rapid weight manipulation. Aesthetic sports (gymnastics, figure skating, diving, synchronized swimming) incorporate subjective judging that may reward thinner or leaner appearances. Endurance sports (distance running, cycling, triathlon) foster beliefs — not always evidence-based — that lower weight equals better performance.

**Coach influence.** Coaches hold extraordinary power over athletes' careers, team selection, and daily lives. Research by Kerr and colleagues (2006) documented that coach comments about weight, body composition, and eating were among the most commonly reported triggers for eating disorder onset in athletes [6]. A single comment — "you'd be faster if you lost a few pounds" — can initiate a cascade of restriction in a psychologically vulnerable athlete. The power imbalance makes it extraordinarily difficult for athletes to push back against weight-related directives from authority figures on whom their careers depend.

**Personality traits.** Athletes — particularly elite athletes — are selected for traits that overlap significantly with eating disorder risk factors: perfectionism, competitiveness, high pain tolerance, ability to override bodily signals (hunger, fatigue, pain), and discipline around behavioral routines. These traits serve athletic performance but can become the psychological infrastructure of an eating disorder when directed at eating and body control.

**Normalization of extreme behaviors.** Behaviors that would be recognized as disordered in the general population — weighing food, tracking macronutrients obsessively, training through injury, restricting specific food groups, using saunas or excessive clothing to "cut weight" — are normalized and sometimes mandated in athletic culture. This normalization delays recognition by both the athlete and their support network.

**Identity fusion.** Many competitive athletes develop identities that are deeply fused with their sport and their athletic body. When sport-related body ideals become internalized as personal identity, challenges to those ideals (injury, aging, performance plateau, body changes) can trigger eating disorder onset as the athlete attempts to maintain or recapture the body that their identity depends on.

## RED-S: The Body's Response to Energy Deficit

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) is the clinical framework developed by the International Olympic Committee to describe the systemic health consequences of insufficient energy availability in athletes [2]. Energy availability is defined as dietary energy intake minus exercise energy expenditure, normalized to fat-free mass. When energy availability drops below approximately 30 kcal/kg fat-free mass/day — the threshold below which physiological function begins to deteriorate — RED-S can develop.

RED-S replaced and expanded the earlier concept of the "female athlete triad," which focused specifically on the interaction of low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, and bone density loss in female athletes [4]. While the triad remains a valid clinical framework, RED-S recognizes that energy deficiency affects male as well as female athletes and impacts far more than three physiological systems.

The IOC consensus statement identifies 10 systems affected by RED-S:

**Endocrine.** Suppression of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis leads to menstrual dysfunction in women (amenorrhea, oligomenorrhea) and testosterone suppression in men. Thyroid function is reduced. Cortisol is elevated. Growth hormone signaling is disrupted.

**Bone.** Low energy availability is one of the strongest predictors of stress fractures in athletes. The combination of reduced estrogen (or testosterone), elevated cortisol, and inadequate calcium and vitamin D creates an environment in which bone formation decreases while bone resorption increases.

**Cardiovascular.** Energy deficiency can produce bradycardia, reduced cardiac output, and in severe cases, cardiac arrhythmias. Athletes who also purge face additional cardiovascular risk from electrolyte disturbances.

**Metabolic.** Resting metabolic rate decreases. The body shifts toward energy conservation, reducing the efficiency of training adaptations. Paradoxically, the restriction intended to improve performance produces metabolic changes that impair it.

**Psychological.** Depression, anxiety, irritability, reduced concentration, and impaired judgment are documented psychological consequences of RED-S — all of which affect both athletic performance and quality of life.

**Immunological, gastrointestinal, hematological, and growth/developmental** systems are also affected, with consequences including increased illness frequency, iron deficiency, delayed growth in adolescent athletes, and gastrointestinal disturbances.

The cruel irony of RED-S is that the energy deficit typically undertaken in pursuit of better performance produces worse performance — reduced strength, endurance, coordination, and reaction time, along with increased injury risk and recovery time. The athlete restricts to perform better and performs worse because they restrict.

## High-Risk Sports: Where the Danger Concentrates

While eating disorders can develop in any athletic context, research consistently identifies specific sport categories as carrying elevated risk [3]:

**Weight-class sports** (wrestling, boxing, judo, lightweight rowing, weightlifting). The structural requirement to make weight creates direct incentives for rapid weight manipulation — including dehydration, laxative use, sauna use, excessive exercise in sweat-inducing clothing, and severe dietary restriction in the days before weigh-in. Research by Sundgot-Borgen and Torstveit (2004) found that athletes in weight-class sports had among the highest rates of eating disorders of any athletic category [1].

**Aesthetic sports** (gymnastics, figure skating, diving, synchronized swimming, dance, cheerleading). Sports in which appearance is explicitly or implicitly judged create continuous pressure to conform to a body ideal that is often extremely lean and prepubertal in form. The training environment — mirrors, leotards, physical comparison with peers — amplifies body surveillance and body dissatisfaction.

**Endurance sports** (distance running, cycling, triathlon, cross-country skiing). The belief that lower weight improves endurance performance — the "power-to-weight ratio" concept — drives restriction in a population already predisposed to high exercise volume. Distance runners show particularly elevated rates of eating disorders and RED-S.

**Sports with revealing uniforms** (swimming, diving, track and field, volleyball). While the sport itself may not emphasize body weight, the visibility of the body in competition attire increases body self-consciousness and comparison.

## Recognition and Early Intervention

Early identification of eating disorders and RED-S in athletes requires awareness from coaches, athletic trainers, team physicians, parents, and teammates.

**Warning signs in athletes** include: declining performance despite maintained or increased training; recurrent stress fractures or overuse injuries; menstrual irregularity or cessation; weight loss or failure to maintain expected weight during growth periods; changes in mood, concentration, or social behavior; avoiding team meals or eating alone; excessive exercise beyond team training; wearing extra layers during training; dizziness, fainting, or fatigue disproportionate to training load; and preoccupation with body weight, body fat percentage, or food [5].

**The Pre-Participation Physical Examination (PPE)** represents an opportunity for routine screening. The American Academy of Pediatrics and the American College of Sports Medicine recommend incorporating eating disorder screening questions into annual sports physicals. The SCOFF questionnaire or targeted questions about eating behaviors, menstrual function, and body image can identify at-risk athletes before clinical eating disorders develop.

**Creating a culture of nutritional adequacy** is a preventive strategy at the team and institutional level. Research by Martinsen and colleagues (2014) demonstrated that educational interventions for coaches and athletes that emphasized adequate fueling for performance — rather than weight loss — produced reductions in new eating disorder cases [7]. When the team culture frames eating as fuel for performance rather than something to be minimized, the risk environment shifts.

**Referral pathways.** Athletic programs should have established referral pathways to eating disorder specialists, sports dietitians, and mental health professionals who are familiar with the athletic context. An athlete is more likely to accept help from a clinician who understands their sport and does not immediately recommend that they stop training.

## Treatment Considerations for Athletes

Treating eating disorders in athletes involves the same evidence-based approaches used in the general population (CBT-E, FBT for adolescents, nutritional rehabilitation) but with several sport-specific considerations.

**Training modification.** Complete cessation of training is sometimes necessary for medical stability, but it is often experienced by the athlete as catastrophic — confirming their fear that the eating disorder has taken away their sport. When medically appropriate, modified training (reduced volume, intensity, or frequency) may support treatment engagement while allowing the athlete to maintain sport connection [8].

**Team and coach involvement.** With the athlete's consent, involving coaches and team support staff in the treatment plan can create a supportive environment for recovery. Coaches need education about eating disorders, including clear guidance on what they should and should not say about athletes' bodies and eating.

**Return-to-play protocols.** Evidence-based criteria for return to full training after an eating disorder should include: medical stability (normal heart rate, blood pressure, electrolytes), adequate energy availability (confirmed through dietary assessment and weight stability), menstrual resumption or testosterone normalization, psychological readiness (assessed by the treating clinician), and bone density assessment if stress fractures have occurred [9].

**Addressing the sport environment.** If the athletic environment contributed to the eating disorder's development — through coach pressure, weigh-ins, body composition testing, or a culture of weight-based performance optimization — treatment should include advocacy for changing those environmental factors. An athlete who recovers from an eating disorder and returns to the same triggering environment is at high risk for relapse.

Research by Arthur-Cameselle and Quatromoni (2011) found that athletes who recovered from eating disorders frequently cited the support of coaches, teammates, and sports medicine professionals as critical factors in their recovery — but also reported that negative sport experiences (weigh-ins, body-related comments, pressure to lose weight) were among the most common triggers for relapse [10].

The National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) and the IOC have published guidelines for managing eating disorders in athletes that provide frameworks for coaches, athletic trainers, and administrators [11].

> 💡 **Spark:** The athlete who restricts to run faster will eventually be too depleted to run at all. The body that is starved for fuel cannot produce power, cannot repair tissue, cannot maintain bone. Fueling is not the enemy of performance — it is the foundation of it.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are an athlete concerned about your eating,** track your energy level, mood, and recovery — not your weight. If performance is declining despite training, if injuries are recurring, or if you have lost your menstrual period, these are warning signs that warrant medical evaluation.

- **Talk to a sports dietitian.** A dietitian who specializes in sports nutrition can help you optimize fueling for your sport without the restrictive frameworks that increase eating disorder risk.

- **If you are a coach,** never comment on an athlete's weight, body fat percentage, or eating. Focus conversations on performance, recovery, and training quality. Eliminate team weigh-ins unless medically indicated and conducted by medical staff.

- **Use Psychage's Mood Journal** at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) to track connections between training, eating patterns, and emotional wellbeing.

- **Know the helplines.** The National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235) supports athletes and their families.
