> **Summary:** Schools are one of the few settings where nearly every young person can be reached before an eating disorder takes hold. Yet most schools have no formal eating disorder prevention programming, and the programs that do exist vary enormously in their evidence base. Some approaches — particularly cognitive dissonance-based interventions like the Body Project and media literacy programs like Media Smart — have demonstrated meaningful reductions in eating disorder risk factors across multiple randomized controlled trials. Others, including simple psychoeducation about eating disorders, have been shown to be ineffective or even counterproductive. This article examines what the research says about school-based eating disorder prevention, distinguishes between universal and targeted approaches, reviews the strongest evidence-based programs, and offers practical guidance for schools considering implementation.

**Key Facts:**

1. A meta-analysis of 112 eating disorder prevention programs found that only programs using interactive, multi-session formats produced lasting reductions in eating disorder risk factors — single-session lectures and information-only approaches showed no significant effects [1].
2. The Body Project, a cognitive dissonance-based intervention, reduced eating disorder onset by 60% over a three-year follow-up in the largest randomized prevention trial to date [2].
3. Media Smart, an eight-session media literacy program developed in Australia, produced significant reductions in weight and shape concern, dietary restraint, and media internalization in girls aged 13-15, with effects maintained at 12-month follow-up [3].
4. Universal prevention programs (delivered to all students) are most effective before age 15, while targeted programs (delivered to high-risk students) show stronger effects for older adolescents who already show elevated risk factors [4].
5. Teacher-delivered eating disorder prevention is as effective as researcher-delivered prevention when teachers receive adequate training and fidelity support, making school-wide implementation feasible [5].

## Why Schools Are the Right Setting

Eating disorders most commonly emerge during adolescence, and schools are where adolescents spend the majority of their waking hours. This convergence of developmental vulnerability and institutional access makes schools a natural platform for prevention — but only if prevention is done well.

The rationale for school-based prevention rests on several evidence-supported principles. First, eating disorder risk factors — body dissatisfaction, thin-ideal internalization, dietary restraint, and negative affect — are elevated across the general adolescent population, not just among those who go on to develop clinical disorders [6]. This means that universal prevention targeting these risk factors can reach young people before they cross the threshold into clinical illness. Second, schools provide repeated access over time, which is critical because effective prevention requires multiple sessions rather than one-off assemblies. Third, peer influence is one of the strongest predictors of eating disorder risk in adolescence, and school-based programs can leverage peer dynamics for protective rather than harmful effects.

However, the history of school-based eating disorder prevention includes cautionary lessons. Early programs from the 1990s relied on psychoeducation — teaching students facts about eating disorders, their symptoms, and their consequences. These programs not only failed to reduce risk factors but, in several studies, appeared to increase them. Students who learned about purging techniques or the diagnostic criteria for anorexia sometimes reported trying these behaviors for the first time [7]. This finding — that information alone is insufficient and can be harmful — reshaped the field and pushed researchers toward interventions that change attitudes and behavior, not just knowledge.

## Universal vs. Targeted Prevention: Different Tools for Different Populations

Prevention science distinguishes between universal programs (delivered to entire populations regardless of risk level) and targeted programs (delivered selectively to individuals identified as high-risk). In eating disorder prevention, both approaches have a role, but they work differently and are suited to different contexts.

**Universal prevention** is delivered to all students in a given grade or school. Its strength is reach — every student is exposed, including those who would never self-identify as at risk. Universal programs are most effective when they focus on modifiable risk factors like media internalization and body dissatisfaction rather than eating disorder symptoms specifically. They work best with younger adolescents (ages 11-14) who have not yet developed entrenched body image concerns [4]. The trade-off is that universal programs produce smaller average effect sizes because many participants were at low risk to begin with.

**Targeted prevention** is delivered to students who screen positive for elevated risk factors — typically high body dissatisfaction, dietary restraint, or thin-ideal internalization. Because all participants share elevated vulnerability, the program can be more focused and intensive. Targeted programs produce larger effect sizes per participant and are particularly effective for older adolescents (ages 15-18) [1]. The challenge is identification: screening students for eating disorder risk requires careful implementation to avoid stigmatization, and opt-in designs may miss the most at-risk students who do not self-select into programs labeled as being about body image or eating.

The most comprehensive school-based prevention strategies use both: universal programming for younger students to shift the overall culture, and targeted interventions for older students who show elevated risk.

## The Body Project: Cognitive Dissonance in Action

The Body Project, developed by Eric Stice and colleagues at Stanford and later the Oregon Research Institute, is the most extensively studied eating disorder prevention program in the world, with over 20 randomized controlled trials supporting its efficacy [2].

The program is built on cognitive dissonance theory — the psychological principle that people experience discomfort when their actions contradict their beliefs, and that this discomfort motivates attitude change. In the Body Project, participants who have internalized the thin ideal are guided through activities in which they publicly argue against it. They write letters to younger girls about the costs of pursuing thinness. They role-play conversations challenging appearance-focused media messages. They engage in behavioral exercises that require them to act in ways inconsistent with thin-ideal beliefs.

The logic is counterintuitive but empirically robust: by having participants argue against the thin ideal — rather than being told the thin ideal is harmful — the program creates internal motivation for attitude change. Participants experience dissonance between their previous thin-ideal beliefs and their new counter-attitudinal behavior, and they resolve this dissonance by genuinely shifting their attitudes.

The evidence is compelling. In a landmark trial published in the *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, Stice and colleagues randomized 481 female adolescents with body dissatisfaction to the Body Project, a healthy weight management program, an expressive writing control, or an assessment-only control. The Body Project produced significantly greater reductions in thin-ideal internalization, body dissatisfaction, negative affect, and dieting — and these effects persisted at three-year follow-up. Most remarkably, the Body Project group showed a 60% reduction in eating disorder onset compared to the assessment-only group over the follow-up period [2]. No other prevention program has demonstrated this magnitude of effect on actual eating disorder incidence.

The Body Project has been successfully adapted for delivery by peer leaders (trained college students or older high school students), which increases its scalability. Peer-led versions produce effect sizes comparable to clinician-led versions, likely because peer delivery enhances the credibility and relatability of the counter-attitudinal arguments [8].

## Media Smart and Media Literacy Approaches

Media literacy programs take a different but complementary approach: rather than creating cognitive dissonance about the thin ideal, they equip students with the analytical skills to critically evaluate media messages about appearance.

**Media Smart**, developed by Susan Paxton and Simon Wilksch at the University of Melbourne and Flinders University, is the best-studied media literacy program for eating disorder prevention. The program consists of eight 50-minute classroom sessions that teach students to analyze media portrayals of beauty, identify advertising techniques used to manipulate body image, examine the role of digital manipulation in creating unrealistic appearance ideals, and develop a critical stance toward media messages rather than passively absorbing them [3].

In a cluster-randomized trial with over 540 adolescent girls across 36 school classes, Media Smart produced significant reductions in weight and shape concern, dietary restraint, and media internalization compared to control classes. These effects were maintained at 6-month and 12-month follow-ups, suggesting durable changes in how participants processed appearance-related media [3]. Importantly, Media Smart was effective as a universal intervention — it was delivered to entire classrooms, not only to high-risk students.

The mechanism of media literacy programs differs from cognitive dissonance approaches. Rather than changing attitudes about the thin ideal directly, media literacy builds cognitive resilience — the ability to encounter appearance-related media without absorbing its messages as truths about one's own body. This is particularly relevant in the contemporary media environment, where young people encounter hundreds of appearance-related images daily through social media, advertising, and entertainment.

More recent adaptations of media literacy programs have incorporated social media-specific content, teaching students about filters, editing apps, curated self-presentation, and the algorithms that amplify appearance-focused content. These adaptations are still in early evaluation stages but show promise, given that social media use is now the strongest media-related predictor of body dissatisfaction in adolescents [9].

## What Doesn't Work — and What Can Cause Harm

Equally important as knowing what works is understanding what doesn't — and what can inadvertently make things worse.

**Psychoeducation-only programs.** Programs that simply teach students about eating disorders — their types, symptoms, medical consequences, and prevalence — do not reduce eating disorder risk factors. In several studies, psychoeducation-only programs actually increased dieting and purging behaviors among participants, apparently by normalizing these behaviors or providing "how-to" information [7]. This finding is consistent with broader prevention science: knowledge alone rarely changes behavior, and information about harmful behaviors can function as instruction rather than deterrence.

**Scare tactics.** Showing graphic images of medical complications, describing worst-case outcomes, or using shock-value presentations about the dangers of eating disorders is not supported by evidence and may be counterproductive. Fear-based approaches tend to produce short-term emotional reactions that dissipate quickly without changing the underlying attitudes (body dissatisfaction, thin-ideal internalization) that drive eating disorder risk [10].

**One-off assemblies.** Prevention requires repeated exposure to new ideas and skills. A single school assembly, no matter how well-designed, does not produce lasting change. Meta-analytic data consistently show that multi-session programs outperform single-session programs, with a minimum of four sessions needed to produce detectable effects [1].

**Programs that focus on eating disorder symptoms in young children.** For pre-adolescents (under age 12), discussing specific eating disorder behaviors (restriction, purging, binge eating) risks introducing ideas that children would not otherwise encounter. For younger students, prevention should focus on positive body image, media literacy at a developmental level, and emotional regulation skills — without ever naming or describing specific eating disorder behaviors.

## Implementing Prevention: Practical Guidance for Schools

For school administrators, counselors, and teachers considering eating disorder prevention, here are evidence-based recommendations.

**Select programs with published efficacy data.** Specifically, the Body Project and Media Smart have the strongest evidence bases. Both offer implementation manuals, training materials, and fidelity checklists. The Body Project's materials are available free through the Body Project Collaborative, and Media Smart is available through its developers with training support.

**Train facilitators adequately.** Research shows that teacher-delivered prevention is as effective as researcher-delivered prevention — but only when teachers receive structured training and ongoing fidelity support [5]. A half-day workshop followed by observation and feedback during early sessions is the minimum standard. Fidelity monitoring (checking that facilitators are delivering the program as designed) is critical because deviations from the manual reduce effectiveness.

**Match the program to the population.** Use universal programs (Media Smart, school-wide body image curricula) for younger students (grades 6-9) and targeted programs (Body Project) for older students (grades 9-12) who show elevated body dissatisfaction. Consider offering both, sequenced by age.

**Embed prevention in school culture.** Stand-alone programs work better when they are reinforced by school-wide policies: eliminating weight-based teasing, training teachers to avoid appearance-focused comments, removing before-and-after imagery from health class materials, and creating inclusive physical education environments that emphasize enjoyment and competence rather than body shape [11].

**Evaluate outcomes.** Administer brief, validated measures of body dissatisfaction and thin-ideal internalization before and after the program to assess whether it is working in your specific population. The Body Esteem Scale for Adolescents and Adults (BESAA) and the Sociocultural Attitudes Towards Appearance Questionnaire (SATAQ-4) are both suitable for this purpose and can be completed in under 10 minutes.

**Engage parents.** The most effective school-based prevention programs complement their student-facing content with parent information sessions that cover how to model positive body image at home, how to talk about food without moralizing, and how to recognize early warning signs [12].

> 💡 **Spark:** The most effective eating disorder prevention programs do not teach students about eating disorders. They teach students to argue against the cultural forces that create eating disorders. The shift from passive knowledge to active resistance is what makes the difference — and it is a skill that protects young people long after the program ends.

## What You Can Try

If you are a school professional, parent, or community member interested in eating disorder prevention, here are actionable starting points:

**Audit your school's current approach.** Does your health curriculum include eating disorder content? If so, is it evidence-based, multi-session, and interactive — or is it a lecture about symptoms? Many schools discover that their existing content is outdated or counterproductive.

**Propose a pilot program.** Start with a single grade or class. The Body Project can be delivered in four one-hour sessions; Media Smart requires eight sessions. A pilot allows you to build evidence within your school community before seeking resources for broader rollout.

**Advocate for teacher training.** If your school already teaches health or wellness, advocate for professional development specifically focused on eating disorder prevention delivery. Organizations like the National Eating Disorders Association (NEDA) and the Academy for Eating Disorders (AED) offer school-focused training resources.

**Create a school policy on weight talk.** Draft guidelines for staff that explicitly prohibit comments about students' weight, shape, or eating habits — and model the same standard in classrooms, sports, and extracurricular activities.
