> **Summary:** One of the most persistent and destructive myths about eating disorders is that they only affect people in thin bodies. This misconception shapes clinical practice, insurance coverage, public perception, and the internal narratives of millions of people who are suffering but do not "look the part." Research consistently demonstrates that eating disorders occur across the full spectrum of body sizes, and that individuals in average-weight and higher-weight bodies can experience identical — or even more severe — medical complications compared to those who are visibly underweight. The myth that thinness equals eating disorder and fatness equals health is not just scientifically inaccurate; it is a barrier to diagnosis that costs lives.

**Key Facts:**
1. Fewer than 6% of people with eating disorders are medically classified as "underweight," meaning the vast majority of eating disorder cases occur in individuals at average or higher weights [1].
2. Sawyer and colleagues (2016) found that adolescents with atypical anorexia nervosa — who meet all anorexia criteria except low weight — experienced comparable rates of bradycardia, orthostatic instability, and electrolyte disturbances to those with typical anorexia [2].
3. Binge eating disorder, the most common eating disorder worldwide, predominantly occurs in individuals at higher weights and was only formally recognized in the DSM-5 in 2013 [3].
4. Weight-based diagnostic criteria lead to an average treatment delay of 5-7 years, with longer delays for individuals who do not present as underweight [4].
5. Research by Flament and colleagues (2015) demonstrated that eating disorder pathology — restriction, bingeing, purging, body image distortion — is distributed across all weight categories with no correlation between severity of psychological symptoms and body size [5].

## The Myth

The myth is straightforward and deeply embedded: if someone has an eating disorder, you can identify it by their body. Specifically, the cultural prototype of an eating disorder is a visibly emaciated person — usually a young white woman — whose thinness itself serves as the evidence of illness. Under this framework, being thin equals having a problem and not being thin equals not having a problem. The logical extension is that a person in a larger body cannot be restricting, cannot be starving, cannot be dangerously ill. This myth is not stated this explicitly in clinical guidelines, but it operates implicitly in emergency rooms, primary care offices, school counselor conversations, and the internal monologues of people who are starving themselves while being told by the world that they look "healthy" — or worse, that they should keep going.

The myth conflates a single visible presentation with an entire category of psychiatric illness. It reduces eating disorders to their most extreme visual endpoint while rendering invisible the millions of people whose suffering does not fit the expected image.

## Where This Comes From

The association between eating disorders and thinness has roots in both medical history and cultural narrative. When anorexia nervosa was first described by Sir William Gull in 1874 and Charles Lasegue in 1873, its defining feature was emaciation — the dramatic, visible wasting that brought patients to medical attention. For more than a century, the clinical definition of anorexia nervosa required significantly low body weight, codifying the idea that visible thinness was the hallmark of the disorder [6].

This clinical framing merged with cultural narratives about bodies. In Western media and popular culture, eating disorders have been consistently represented through images of extreme thinness — after-school specials, magazine exposures, and celebrity confessions that invariably featured rail-thin women as the face of illness. These representations were not just incomplete; they became the template against which all eating disorder experiences were measured.

The diagnostic system itself reinforced this bias. Prior to the DSM-5 in 2013, individuals who exhibited all the psychological and behavioral features of anorexia nervosa but were not underweight were classified under EDNOS (eating disorder not otherwise specified) — a diagnostic category that was systematically under-researched, under-funded, and under-treated [7]. The message embedded in the diagnostic structure was clear: the "real" eating disorder required thinness. Everything else was a lesser version of the real thing.

Insurance systems, treatment admission criteria, and clinical screening tools all inherited this weight-centric framework. The result was a self-reinforcing cycle: thinness triggered clinical attention, which generated research data, which shaped diagnostic criteria, which determined funding — while everyone else was systematically excluded from the conversation.

## What Research Actually Shows

The evidence dismantling this myth is extensive, consistent, and comes from multiple research traditions.

**Atypical anorexia nervosa** — formally recognized in the DSM-5 under Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder — describes individuals who meet all diagnostic criteria for anorexia nervosa except the low-weight requirement. Research by Sawyer, Whitelaw, Le Grange, Yeo, and Hughes (2016), published in *Pediatrics*, examined medical outcomes in adolescents with atypical anorexia and found that these patients experienced rates of medical complications — including bradycardia (dangerously slow heart rate), orthostatic instability (blood pressure drops on standing), and electrolyte disturbances — that were comparable to patients with typical anorexia nervosa [2]. Some measures of medical severity were actually *worse* in the atypical group, likely because these patients had lost a larger absolute amount of weight before receiving clinical attention.

This finding is critical: a person who has lost a significant percentage of their body weight through severe restriction is medically compromised regardless of where their weight started. A person in a larger body who has lost a large amount of weight rapidly is experiencing the same physiological stress as a person in a thinner body who has done the same — but the first person is far less likely to be identified as ill.

**Epidemiological data** confirms that the weight distribution of eating disorders does not match the cultural stereotype. Flament and colleagues (2015) analyzed community data and found that eating disorder symptoms — food restriction, binge eating, purging, compulsive exercise, body image distortion — are distributed across all weight categories [5]. The psychological severity of an eating disorder cannot be inferred from body size. A person at a statistically average weight can be experiencing restriction as severe, obsessive thoughts as intrusive, and medical consequences as dangerous as someone who is visibly underweight.

**Binge eating disorder (BED)** provides perhaps the most striking evidence against the thin-equals-eating-disorder myth. BED is the single most common eating disorder worldwide, affecting an estimated 1.9% of the global population according to WHO World Mental Health Survey data [3]. It involves recurrent episodes of eating large quantities of food accompanied by a sense of loss of control, marked distress, and no regular use of compensatory behaviors. BED occurs predominantly — though not exclusively — in individuals at higher weights, and it was not formally recognized as a standalone diagnosis until the DSM-5 in 2013. For decades, the most prevalent eating disorder in the world was invisible in the diagnostic manual because it did not fit the thinness prototype.

**Clinical detection studies** reveal the real-world consequences of weight-based diagnostic bias. Sim and colleagues (2013) published research showing that clinicians presented with identical eating disorder symptom profiles were significantly less likely to diagnose an eating disorder when the patient described was at a higher weight [8]. The same behaviors — the same restriction, the same obsessive thoughts, the same compensatory behaviors — were interpreted differently depending on the patient's body size. In some cases, restriction and weight loss in a higher-weight patient were not only undiagnosed but actively praised.

Research by Lebow, Sim, and Kransdorf (2015) found that adolescents with atypical anorexia nervosa had been symptomatic for a significantly longer duration before receiving a diagnosis compared to those with typical anorexia — a delay attributable to clinicians not recognizing the same behaviors as pathological in a non-underweight body [9]. Every month of delayed treatment increases the likelihood of chronicity and worsens long-term prognosis.

## Why This Matters

The myth that eating disorders only affect thin people causes harm that is specific, measurable, and ongoing.

For individuals in average-weight or higher-weight bodies who are experiencing eating disorders, the myth creates a double bind: their internal experience tells them they are suffering, but the cultural narrative tells them their suffering is not real or not serious because they do not look the part. Many report being congratulated for restriction-driven weight loss by friends, family, and even healthcare providers — reinforcement that deepens the illness rather than interrupting it.

At the clinical level, weight-based screening criteria mean that emergency departments, primary care providers, and mental health professionals routinely fail to identify eating disorders in patients who are not underweight. The Academy for Eating Disorders has formally stated that weight should not be used as the primary indicator of eating disorder severity, a position supported by the body of evidence described above [4]. Yet clinical practice has been slow to catch up with this evidence.

At the systemic level, insurance companies and treatment programs that use weight-based admission criteria exclude patients who are medically and psychologically ill but do not meet an arbitrary numerical threshold. This is not just a clinical oversight — it is a structural barrier to care that disproportionately affects people in larger bodies, people of color (who are already less likely to be screened for eating disorders), and anyone whose body does not match the expected image of illness.

The human cost is direct: people die from eating disorders that were never diagnosed because no one thought to look past the number on the scale.

## The More Accurate Picture

Eating disorders are psychiatric conditions defined by patterns of cognition, behavior, and emotional distress — not by body size. A person can be severely restricting food intake at any weight. A person can be experiencing loss-of-control binge episodes at any weight. A person can be engaging in compulsive exercise, purging, or obsessive food rituals at any weight. The medical consequences of these behaviors — cardiac complications, electrolyte imbalances, nutritional deficiencies, bone density loss, gastrointestinal damage — occur across the full body size spectrum.

The accurate clinical picture requires assessing behaviors, cognitions, emotional distress, rate of weight change, and physiological markers — not relying on a visual assessment of body size. A person who has lost a significant amount of weight rapidly needs medical evaluation regardless of their current weight. A person who is experiencing distressing, uncontrollable episodes of binge eating needs clinical attention regardless of their current weight. A person who is spending hours each day consumed by obsessive thoughts about food, body, and control deserves recognition of their suffering regardless of what the scale says.

The more accurate picture is also the more compassionate one. When we decouple eating disorders from the thinness requirement, we create space for everyone who is struggling to be seen, believed, and offered help — including the vast majority of people with eating disorders who were never thin enough to be noticed.

> 💡 **Spark:** The most dangerous thing about this myth is not that it is wrong — it is that it gives eating disorders a place to hide. When the cultural expectation is that eating disorders look a certain way, every person who does not match that image is left without language for their experience, without recognition from their clinicians, and without access to care. Dismantling the thinness myth does not just correct a misconception; it opens the door for millions of people to finally be seen.

## What You Can Try

- **Examine your own assumptions about what eating disorders "look like."** If you have ever thought that someone could not have an eating disorder because of their body size, notice that assumption and question where it came from. Awareness of bias is the first step to interrupting it.

- **If you are experiencing disordered eating behaviors — restriction, bingeing, purging, compulsive exercise — seek evaluation regardless of your body size.** Look for providers who specialize in eating disorders and explicitly practice from a weight-inclusive framework. The Academy for Eating Disorders (aedweb.org) maintains a provider directory.

- **Advocate for weight-inclusive screening in healthcare settings.** If you are a healthcare provider, review your screening practices: are you asking about eating behaviors and body image distress across all patients, or only those who appear underweight? The Academy for Eating Disorders recommends universal screening.

- **Track emotional and behavioral patterns rather than weight.** Psychage's Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) can help you notice connections between emotions and eating patterns without centering weight as the metric.

- **Support organizations working to broaden eating disorder recognition.** The National Alliance for Eating Disorders, NEDA (National Eating Disorders Association), and Project HEAL all advocate for weight-inclusive diagnosis and treatment access.
