> **Summary:** The dominant model of eating disorder prevention — clinician-delivered programs in schools and universities, primarily in high-income Western nations — has produced meaningful evidence but reaches only a fraction of the global population at risk. Eating disorders are not exclusively Western conditions. They emerge across cultures, economic strata, and geographic regions, often shaped by forces that clinical prevention programs cannot address: rapid globalization, media penetration, migration-related cultural shifts, food system changes, and colonial legacies of body ideals. Effective prevention at scale requires community-based approaches — models that embed prevention within existing social structures, train community health workers as frontline agents, adapt interventions to local cultural contexts, and leverage digital tools to extend reach. This article examines global prevention frameworks, the critical Fiji case study that reshaped understanding of cultural vulnerability, community health worker models, cultural adaptation challenges, prevention in low-resource settings, and the emerging role of digital prevention tools.

**Key Facts:**
1. Eating disorders occur in every world region studied, with rising prevalence in rapidly globalizing nations where Western media exposure is increasing — a pattern first documented by Anne Becker in Fiji following the introduction of television [1].
2. The WHO's Mental Health Action Plan 2013-2030 includes eating disorders within its framework for community-based mental health promotion, though specific implementation guidance remains limited [2].
3. Community health worker-delivered mental health interventions have been effective in low- and middle-income countries for depression and anxiety, providing a viable delivery model for eating disorder prevention [3].
4. Cultural adaptation of prevention programs — modifying content, delivery, and framing to match local values and norms — is associated with significantly larger effect sizes compared to direct translation of Western programs [4].
5. Digital eating disorder prevention tools, including app-based and internet-delivered programs, demonstrate effect sizes comparable to face-to-face delivery for body dissatisfaction and disordered eating risk reduction [5].

## Beyond the Clinic: Why Community-Based Prevention Matters

Eating disorder prevention has historically been concentrated in two settings: the clinic and the classroom. Clinician-delivered programs like the Body Project, cognitive-behavioral prevention workshops, and school-based media literacy curricula have accumulated an impressive evidence base. Meta-analyses by Stice and colleagues have identified several programs that reliably reduce risk factors and, in some cases, reduce future eating disorder onset [6].

But these programs share a structural limitation: they reach a narrow slice of the global population. They require trained facilitators, institutional infrastructure, and participants who can access those institutions. They are overwhelmingly developed in, tested in, and disseminated from high-income English-speaking nations — the United States, the United Kingdom, Australia. They assume certain cultural norms about body image, food, gender, and the relationship between the individual and the community.

The global epidemiology of eating disorders demands a broader approach. Systematic reviews by Pike and Dunne (2015) documented clinically significant rates of anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and binge eating disorder across East Asia, South Asia, the Middle East, Latin America, and Sub-Saharan Africa [7]. In many of these regions, there are no eating disorder specialists, no structured prevention programs, and no mental health infrastructure capable of delivering clinical interventions at scale.

Community-based prevention moves the locus of intervention from the clinic to the community — the village, the neighborhood, the faith community, the workplace, the digital platform. It leverages existing social structures, trains non-specialist workers, and adapts to local cultural frameworks rather than importing Western ones wholesale.

## The Fiji Case Study: A Turning Point in Understanding

The most influential study in the global epidemiology of eating disorders was conducted not in a laboratory but on a remote Pacific island. Anne Becker's longitudinal research in Fiji, published in 2004, documented a dramatic increase in disordered eating among ethnic Fijian adolescent girls following the introduction of television to their communities in 1995 [1].

Prior to television, Fiji had cultural norms that actively protected against eating disorders. The traditional Fijian body ideal favored robust, well-fed bodies. Thinness was associated with illness, poverty, and social neglect rather than with beauty or discipline. Communal meals were central to social life, and food sharing was a primary expression of care and status.

Within three years of television introduction — which brought Western programming including shows like Beverly Hills 90210 and Melrose Place — Becker documented a fivefold increase in self-induced vomiting among adolescent girls, a significant rise in pathological attitudes toward eating, and the emergence of a previously absent thin ideal. Qualitative interviews revealed that girls explicitly connected their new body dissatisfaction to the bodies they saw on television: "I want their body. I want their size."

The Fiji study was transformative for several reasons. First, it demonstrated that eating disorders are not genetically Western conditions but can emerge rapidly in any population exposed to thin-ideal media. Second, it revealed that cultural protective factors — robust body ideals, communal eating, non-appearance-based value systems — can be destabilized by media exposure within a single generation. Third, it established that prevention must operate at the community and environmental level, not merely at the individual level. No individual therapy could have prevented the cultural shift that occurred in Fiji; only community-level intervention — media literacy, cultural reinforcement, policy-level media regulation — could address the vector of harm.

Becker's subsequent work has focused on culturally grounded prevention approaches that strengthen existing protective factors within Fijian communities rather than importing Western prevention models [8]. This community-strengthening approach — identifying what already protects and amplifying it — has become a template for prevention in other rapidly globalizing communities.

## The WHO Framework: Global Architecture, Local Gaps

The World Health Organization's Mental Health Action Plan 2013-2030 provides the most comprehensive global framework for community-based mental health promotion. The plan explicitly includes eating disorders within the spectrum of mental health conditions requiring prevention and early intervention, and it calls for integration of mental health services into primary care and community health systems [2].

The WHO framework emphasizes several principles that are directly applicable to eating disorder prevention:

**Task shifting.** Rather than relying on specialist clinicians — who are scarce globally — the WHO advocates training non-specialist health workers, teachers, community leaders, and peer educators to deliver mental health interventions. This model has been successfully applied to depression and anxiety in low- and middle-income countries, with community health workers delivering evidence-based treatments with outcomes comparable to specialist care in some contexts [3].

**Integration with existing systems.** Rather than building parallel mental health infrastructure, the WHO recommends embedding mental health promotion within existing health, education, and social service systems. For eating disorder prevention, this means integrating prevention content into maternal and child health programs, school health curricula, and community nutrition programs that already exist in many low-resource settings.

**Cultural and contextual adaptation.** The WHO explicitly recognizes that mental health interventions developed in high-income contexts cannot be directly transplanted to other settings without adaptation. Intervention content, delivery methods, and theoretical frameworks must be modified to align with local values, languages, social structures, and health beliefs.

However, the gap between the WHO's framework and its implementation for eating disorders specifically is substantial. While the WHO has invested heavily in scaling community-based interventions for depression (mhGAP), suicide prevention, and substance use, eating disorder-specific guidance remains underdeveloped. This reflects the persistent but inaccurate perception that eating disorders are a high-income-country concern — a perception that the epidemiological evidence has thoroughly refuted [7].

## Community Health Workers as Prevention Agents

The community health worker (CHW) model — training local non-professionals to deliver health education and basic interventions within their own communities — has been one of the most successful innovations in global health. CHWs have been instrumental in reducing maternal mortality, increasing immunization rates, and managing chronic disease in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America.

Applying this model to eating disorder prevention is feasible and, in pilot programs, promising. Singla and colleagues' systematic review (2017) found that non-specialist-delivered mental health interventions in low- and middle-income countries produced significant reductions in psychological distress, with effect sizes comparable to specialist-delivered interventions for common mental disorders [3]. The specific application to eating disorders requires training CHWs in several domains: recognizing early warning signs of eating pathology, facilitating community conversations about body image and food culture, delivering media literacy content adapted to local contexts, and providing basic nutritional counseling that avoids the moralization of food.

The strength of the CHW model lies in its cultural embeddedness. A CHW from the local community speaks the local language, understands local food practices, knows the local body ideals, and has credibility within the social network. They are positioned to deliver prevention in a way that feels like community care rather than clinical intervention — which significantly reduces stigma and increases engagement.

## Cultural Adaptation: Beyond Translation

One of the most consistent findings in prevention science is that culturally adapted interventions outperform directly translated ones. Griner and Smith's (2006) meta-analysis found that interventions modified to address the specific cultural context of the target population were approximately four times more effective than interventions that were simply translated into the local language [4].

For eating disorder prevention, cultural adaptation involves much more than linguistic translation. It requires understanding:

**Local body ideals.** The thin ideal is not universal. In many cultures, a larger body is associated with health, wealth, fertility, and beauty. In others, the ideal is somewhere between thin and large. Prevention content that assumes the thin ideal is the primary threat may be irrelevant or even harmful in contexts where the local ideal is different. In some rapidly globalizing communities, multiple body ideals coexist — traditional and Western — creating a different kind of distress than either ideal alone.

**The role of food in social life.** In cultures where food sharing is a primary social currency — where refusing food is a serious social offense, where feeding others is an expression of love and status — prevention approaches that focus on individual food choice may miss the social architecture of eating entirely. Prevention in these contexts may need to focus on preserving communal eating practices as protective factors rather than on modifying individual behavior.

**Gender and power dynamics.** The relationship between gender, body image, and food varies across cultures. In some contexts, women's food intake is controlled by male family members. In others, women are the primary controllers of household food. Prevention approaches must account for these dynamics rather than assuming a universal model of individual food autonomy.

**Spiritual and religious dimensions of eating.** Fasting, dietary laws, food rituals, and body-related spiritual beliefs are central to many cultural and religious traditions. Prevention programs must engage respectfully with these practices rather than pathologizing them.

Successful examples of cultural adaptation include Becker's work adapting prevention for ethnic Fijian communities [8], Ciao and colleagues' adaptation of the Body Project for Latina college students, and emerging work on culturally responsive prevention in East Asian and South Asian communities. Each of these efforts involved extensive community consultation, modification of content and delivery, and testing within the target cultural context.

## Low-Resource Settings: Doing More with Less

In many parts of the world, the resources available for eating disorder prevention are minimal: no eating disorder specialists, no mental health infrastructure, limited educational budgets, and competing health priorities that include infectious disease, malnutrition, and maternal mortality. Prevention in these contexts must be efficient, integrated, and scalable.

Several strategies show promise. First, integrating eating disorder prevention content into existing programs that address nutrition, maternal health, or adolescent development. Rather than creating standalone eating disorder prevention programs, embedding body image and food relationship content within school health curricula or community nutrition programs leverages existing infrastructure. Second, training existing health and education workers — teachers, nurses, midwives, community health volunteers — to recognize and respond to early warning signs. Third, using low-cost media — community radio, SMS messaging, printed materials — to disseminate prevention content.

## Digital Prevention: Scaling Beyond Geography

Digital platforms offer the most scalable pathway for eating disorder prevention worldwide. Internet-based and app-based prevention programs have several advantages: they can reach geographically isolated populations, they reduce stigma through anonymity, they are available on-demand, and they can be adapted to multiple languages and cultural contexts at relatively low marginal cost.

A meta-analysis by Linardon (2020) found that internet-based eating disorder prevention programs produced significant reductions in body dissatisfaction, thin-ideal internalization, dietary restraint, and eating disorder symptoms, with effect sizes comparable to face-to-face delivery [5]. Programs such as Student Bodies (now integrated into the Woebot platform), the online Body Project, and ProYouth (developed in Europe) have demonstrated feasibility and efficacy in multiple countries.

The challenge with digital prevention is ensuring equitable access. While global internet penetration has risen dramatically — over 60% of the world's population had internet access by 2023 — access remains unevenly distributed by income, geography, gender, and age. Mobile-first design, offline functionality, low-bandwidth compatibility, and distribution through community access points (schools, libraries, health clinics) can partially address these gaps.

The emerging use of artificial intelligence in digital prevention — chatbot-based interventions that can provide personalized psychoeducation and early intervention — represents a frontier that could dramatically increase the reach and personalization of prevention, though rigorous evaluation of AI-based eating disorder prevention tools is still in early stages [9][10][11].

> 💡 **Spark:** Eating disorders are not a Western luxury. They are a human vulnerability — one that emerges wherever cultural forces destabilize the relationship between people and their bodies. The Fijian girls who began purging after watching American television were not weak or culturally inferior. They were human, and they were unprotected. Community-based prevention is the work of building that protection — in every community, in every culture, with whatever resources are available.

## What You Can Try

- **Learn about the body ideals in your community.** What is valued? What has changed recently? Understanding the cultural context of body image in your specific community is the first step toward culturally relevant prevention.

- **Support organizations that work on global eating disorder prevention.** The Academy for Eating Disorders, the Strategic Training Initiative for the Prevention of Eating Disorders (STRIPED), and the International Association of Eating Disorders Professionals all support global prevention efforts.

- **If you work in a school, health clinic, or community organization,** explore integrating basic body image and food relationship content into existing programming. The Body Project materials are freely available and have been adapted for diverse populations.

- **Advocate for media literacy education** in your children's schools. The evidence is clear that critical evaluation of appearance-related media messages is protective against body dissatisfaction and disordered eating.

- **Be intentional about the media environment in your home and community.** What messages about bodies and food are being transmitted? What protective messages could be amplified?
