> **Summary:** Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder (OSFED) — formerly known as EDNOS (Eating Disorder Not Otherwise Specified) — is the most commonly diagnosed eating disorder category in many clinical settings, yet it receives a fraction of the public attention directed at anorexia, bulimia, or binge eating disorder. OSFED captures presentations that cause clinically significant distress and impairment but do not meet the full diagnostic criteria for a more specific eating disorder. Far from being a "lesser" diagnosis, OSFED carries medical risks, psychological suffering, and mortality rates comparable to the conditions it sits alongside. This article explores what OSFED actually encompasses, the specific presentations within the category, and why being "in-between" diagnoses does not mean being less deserving of recognition or care.

**Key Facts:**
1. OSFED (previously EDNOS) is the most commonly diagnosed eating disorder in many clinical settings, accounting for an estimated 32–53% of eating disorder diagnoses [1].
2. Mortality rates for OSFED are comparable to those for anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa — this is not a "mild" category of eating disorders [2].
3. The DSM-5-TR specifies five named OSFED presentations: atypical anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa of low frequency or limited duration, binge eating disorder of low frequency or limited duration, purging disorder, and night eating syndrome [3].
4. Atypical anorexia nervosa — in which all criteria for anorexia are met except low body weight — can be as medically dangerous as typical anorexia, with comparable rates of medical complications [4].
5. The shift from EDNOS to OSFED in the DSM-5 was partly intended to reduce the size of this diagnostic "remainder" category by creating binge eating disorder as a standalone diagnosis [3].

## The Diagnostic Category That Holds the Most People

In the landscape of eating disorder diagnoses, OSFED occupies a paradoxical position: it is the category that contains the most people but receives the least recognition. Research by Fairburn and Bohn (2005) found that in many outpatient eating disorder clinics, EDNOS — OSFED's predecessor — was the most common diagnosis, more frequent than anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa combined [1]. Despite this prevalence, the condition has historically been treated as a diagnostic afterthought — a "not quite" category that implied less severity and often translated into less clinical attention and lower treatment priority.

This perception is dangerously wrong. A meta-analysis by Crow and colleagues (2009) found that the standardized mortality ratio for EDNOS was 1.92 — significantly elevated compared to the general population, and comparable in magnitude to the mortality associated with bulimia nervosa [2]. Individuals diagnosed with OSFED experience the same types of medical complications, psychiatric comorbidity, and functional impairment as those diagnosed with more specific eating disorders. The "in-between" label describes a diagnostic position, not a clinical reality.

The DSM-5, published in 2013, made deliberate efforts to reduce the size of the residual eating disorder category. The creation of binge eating disorder as a standalone diagnosis moved a large population out of EDNOS and into a specific diagnostic category. The renaming from EDNOS to OSFED was intended to signal a shift from a vague "not otherwise specified" framing to a more clinically informative approach in which clinicians specify the particular reason the patient does not meet full criteria for another diagnosis [3].

## The Five Named Presentations

The DSM-5-TR identifies five specific presentations within OSFED, each representing a clinically significant pattern that falls just outside the boundaries of a more specific diagnosis [3]:

**Atypical anorexia nervosa.** The individual meets all criteria for anorexia nervosa — persistent energy restriction, intense fear of weight gain or persistent behavior interfering with weight gain, disturbance in body image — except that their weight, despite significant weight loss, falls within or above the medically expected range. This is arguably the most important presentation within OSFED because it directly challenges the assumption that anorexia can be identified by looking at someone's body.

Research by Sawyer and colleagues (2016) at the Royal Children's Hospital in Melbourne found that adolescents with atypical anorexia experienced rates of bradycardia, orthostatic instability, and need for medical hospitalization that were equivalent to or higher than adolescents with typical anorexia [4]. The rate of weight loss — how much and how quickly someone has lost weight — is often more medically significant than the weight at which they arrive. A person who has lost a substantial percentage of their body weight through severe restriction is in medical danger regardless of whether their current weight is classified as "low."

Atypical anorexia is increasingly recognized as the form of anorexia that the clinical system is most likely to miss, precisely because the diagnostic prototype — the visibly emaciated patient — does not match the clinical reality for many individuals with this condition.

**Bulimia nervosa of low frequency and/or limited duration.** This presentation captures individuals who experience binge-purge episodes that meet all the behavioral and psychological criteria for bulimia nervosa but occur less frequently than once per week or have been present for fewer than three months. The reduction in frequency does not necessarily correspond to a reduction in distress or medical risk. A person who binge-eats and purges twice a month may experience severe shame, dental erosion, electrolyte instability, and quality of life impairment.

**Binge eating disorder of low frequency and/or limited duration.** Parallel to the bulimia presentation above, this captures individuals whose binge eating episodes are clinically significant but fall below the frequency or duration threshold for a full BED diagnosis.

**Purging disorder.** This is a distinct presentation in which the individual engages in recurrent purging behavior — self-induced vomiting, laxative misuse, diuretic misuse — to influence body weight or shape, but does not engage in binge eating episodes. Purging disorder is relatively understudied compared to bulimia, but research by Keel and colleagues (2005) has established it as a clinically significant condition with unique features — including a pattern of purging after normal-sized meals rather than objectively large binge episodes [5].

**Night eating syndrome.** Characterized by recurrent episodes of eating after awakening from sleep or by excessive food consumption after the evening meal, night eating syndrome involves distress, awareness during the eating (distinguishing it from sleep-related eating disorder), and is not better explained by binge eating disorder, substance use, or another medical or psychiatric condition. Research by Allison and colleagues (2010) has identified night eating syndrome as affecting approximately 1.5% of the general population, with higher rates in populations with obesity and psychiatric conditions [6].

## Why the "In-Between" Framing Is Harmful

The categorization of OSFED as a residual diagnosis — a catch-all for presentations that do not quite fit elsewhere — has real consequences for people who receive this diagnosis.

**Perceived legitimacy.** Individuals diagnosed with OSFED frequently report feeling that their condition is not "real" or not "serious enough" to warrant attention. Research by Thomas, Vartanian, and Brownell (2009) found that individuals with EDNOS reported lower perceived legitimacy of their illness compared to those with anorexia or bulimia, which was associated with lower rates of help-seeking and longer duration of illness before treatment [7]. The diagnostic label itself — "other specified" — linguistically positions the condition as peripheral rather than central.

**Treatment access.** In many healthcare and insurance systems, treatment authorization is tied to specific diagnoses. OSFED diagnoses have historically been more difficult to use when seeking coverage for intensive treatment programs, residential care, or extended outpatient therapy. While this situation has improved in some jurisdictions, disparities in coverage based on diagnostic specificity persist [8].

**Clinical attention.** Clinicians who are not specialists in eating disorders may unconsciously treat OSFED as a less urgent presentation, delaying referral to specialized care or offering less intensive intervention. This is particularly dangerous for atypical anorexia, where medical instability can develop rapidly in the absence of visible weight loss.

**Research funding.** The fragmented nature of the OSFED category makes it more difficult to study as a unified entity, which means it receives less research funding than more clearly delineated diagnoses. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: less research means less evidence, less evidence means less clinical attention, less clinical attention means less advocacy, and less advocacy means less research.

## Diagnostic Evolution: From EDNOS to OSFED and Beyond

Understanding OSFED requires understanding how eating disorder classification has evolved — and where it may be heading.

The DSM-III (1980) recognized only two eating disorders: anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa. This created an enormous residual category for every other form of clinical eating disturbance. The DSM-IV (1994) introduced EDNOS as a formal category but provided minimal guidance for its use, resulting in a diagnosis that was simultaneously the most common and the least specified [1].

The DSM-5 (2013) represented significant progress. The creation of BED as a standalone diagnosis moved a large portion of the EDNOS population into a specific diagnostic category. The renaming to OSFED encouraged clinicians to specify the particular presentation. And the revision of diagnostic thresholds — for example, reducing the binge-purge frequency criterion for bulimia from twice weekly to once weekly — moved additional individuals from the residual category into specific diagnoses [3].

Research by Machado and colleagues (2013) found that the DSM-5 changes successfully reduced the proportion of patients classified in the residual category from approximately 53% (under DSM-IV) to approximately 29% (under DSM-5) [9]. This is genuine progress — but it means that nearly one in three eating disorder patients still receives a diagnosis that positions their condition as falling between recognized categories.

The future of eating disorder classification may move toward dimensional models — in which eating disorders are understood along spectrums of severity and type rather than as discrete categorical entities. The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) model, increasingly influential in psychiatric classification, proposes organizing eating pathology along dimensions of internalizing distress, compulsivity, and body image disturbance rather than categorical diagnoses [10]. Such an approach would eliminate the concept of "in-between" diagnoses entirely.

> 💡 **Spark:** If your eating disorder does not fit neatly into a diagnostic box, that does not make it less real, less serious, or less deserving of treatment. The boxes were drawn by committees. Your suffering was not designed to conform to their boundaries.

## What You Can Try

If you have been diagnosed with OSFED — or suspect your eating patterns are clinically significant but do not match any specific diagnosis you have encountered:

- **Know that your diagnosis is clinically significant.** OSFED carries medical risks and psychological distress comparable to more recognized eating disorders. You deserve the same quality of care and the same urgency of treatment.

- **Seek an eating disorder specialist, not a generalist.** Specialists are more familiar with OSFED presentations and less likely to minimize or misunderstand your condition. The Academy for Eating Disorders (aedweb.org) provides referral resources.

- **Advocate for yourself in the healthcare system.** If insurance or treatment access is being limited based on your diagnostic category, know that clinical guidelines from the Academy for Eating Disorders and NICE explicitly state that eating disorders of all types warrant specialist assessment and treatment.

- **Connect with others who understand.** Many eating disorder support communities explicitly include OSFED. Organizations like the National Alliance for Eating Disorders and Beat (UK) serve all eating disorder diagnoses.

- **Track your experience using Psychage's tools.** The Clarity Score at [psychage.com/tools/clarity-score](psychage.com/tools/clarity-score) and Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) can help you understand your patterns and communicate them to clinicians.
