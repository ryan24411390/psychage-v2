> **Summary:** Pregnancy involves rapid, visible, and culturally loaded changes to the body — changes that can trigger, intensify, or reactivate eating disorders in individuals who are vulnerable. An estimated 5–8% of pregnant individuals meet criteria for an eating disorder during pregnancy, with additional percentages experiencing subclinical disordered eating. The consequences affect both the pregnant person and the developing fetus: restriction during pregnancy is associated with low birth weight, preterm delivery, and developmental complications, while binge eating is associated with gestational diabetes and macrosomia. Despite these risks, eating disorders during pregnancy are frequently unscreened, undiagnosed, and undertreated — partly because prenatal care rarely includes eating disorder assessment and partly because the cultural celebration of pregnancy can mask the distress of someone whose body changes feel terrifying rather than joyful. This article examines eating disorders across the perinatal period, from preconception through postpartum, and what appropriate care looks like.

**Key Facts:**
1. An estimated 5–8% of pregnant individuals meet criteria for a clinical eating disorder during pregnancy, with rates of subclinical disordered eating even higher [1].
2. Eating disorders during pregnancy are associated with increased risk of miscarriage, preterm birth, low birth weight, cesarean delivery, and postpartum depression [2].
3. For some individuals, pregnancy temporarily improves eating disorder symptoms — the motivation to nourish the baby overrides the eating disorder. For others, pregnancy intensifies symptoms as body changes trigger distress [3].
4. Postpartum is a high-risk period for eating disorder onset or relapse, driven by pressure to "bounce back," body dissatisfaction, hormonal shifts, and sleep deprivation [4].
5. Routine prenatal care rarely includes eating disorder screening, meaning most pregnant individuals with eating disorders are not identified or treated [5].

## Pregnancy and the Eating Disorder Mind

For someone without an eating disorder, the body changes of pregnancy — weight gain, expanding abdomen, breast enlargement, fluid retention, shifting proportions — may be experienced with a range of emotions from pride to discomfort to ambivalence. For someone with an eating disorder, these same changes can feel catastrophic.

Pregnancy demands exactly what eating disorders resist: weight gain, loss of control over body size and shape, increased food intake, and tolerance for a body that is changing rapidly in ways that cannot be controlled. The eating disorder mind, which has organized itself around restriction, control, and resistance to body change, encounters a biological process that requires surrender to change.

Research by Micali and colleagues (2007) identified three patterns of eating disorder course during pregnancy [3]:

**Symptom improvement.** For approximately 50–70% of individuals with pre-existing eating disorders, pregnancy produces a temporary reduction in symptoms. The motivation to protect the baby, the social permission to eat, the clear biological purpose behind weight gain, and hormonal changes may combine to create a period of relative eating disorder remission. However, this improvement is often temporary — symptoms frequently return or intensify postpartum.

**Symptom persistence.** For some individuals, eating disorder behaviors continue unchanged throughout pregnancy. Restriction, purging, or binge eating may persist despite awareness of the risks to the fetus. The eating disorder may be experienced as an addiction — something the person wants to stop but feels unable to control, even with the added motivation of pregnancy.

**Symptom intensification.** For a subset of individuals, pregnancy worsens eating disorder symptoms. The body changes, weight gain, and loss of control over body shape trigger intensified restriction, increased purging, or escalating body image distress. The colloquial term "pregorexia" — while not a clinical diagnosis — describes the pattern of severe dietary restriction during pregnancy driven by fear of pregnancy-related weight gain.

## Risks to the Developing Baby

Eating disorders during pregnancy pose documented risks to fetal development and pregnancy outcomes.

**Restriction and undernutrition** during pregnancy are associated with intrauterine growth restriction, low birth weight, preterm delivery, and small-for-gestational-age infants. Inadequate intake of critical nutrients — including folic acid, iron, calcium, and omega-3 fatty acids — can affect fetal brain development, bone formation, and organ development [2].

**Purging behaviors** (vomiting, laxative use) during pregnancy produce electrolyte disturbances and dehydration that can affect both the pregnant person and the fetus. Repeated vomiting depletes nutrients before they can be absorbed, and the resulting electrolyte imbalances (particularly hypokalemia) pose cardiac risk to both parent and fetus [6].

**Binge eating** during pregnancy is associated with excessive gestational weight gain, gestational diabetes, macrosomia (large-for-gestational-age infants), and increased risk of cesarean delivery. The metabolic consequences of uncontrolled binge eating during pregnancy can affect the infant's long-term metabolic health [7].

**Psychological stress** from eating disorder-related distress during pregnancy is itself a risk factor. Elevated cortisol from chronic stress affects fetal development and is associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, and later behavioral and emotional difficulties in the child.

It is essential to note that many individuals with eating disorders have healthy pregnancies and healthy babies — particularly when the eating disorder is identified and managed with appropriate support. The risks described above are elevated, not inevitable, and early intervention significantly improves outcomes.

## Preconception: The Overlooked Window

For individuals with known eating disorders who are planning pregnancy, the preconception period represents a critical window for intervention.

**Nutritional optimization.** Establishing adequate nutritional status before conception — including replenishing depleted stores of iron, calcium, folic acid, and vitamin D — provides a healthier foundation for pregnancy. A registered dietitian with eating disorder and prenatal expertise can help develop a preconception nutrition plan that addresses both eating disorder recovery and pregnancy preparation.

**Medical assessment.** Individuals with a history of eating disorders should receive medical assessment including bone density evaluation, cardiac screening, hormonal assessment, and nutritional bloodwork before attempting conception. Amenorrhea related to energy deficiency must be resolved before conception can occur, and the presence of amenorrhea should prompt evaluation for an active eating disorder [8].

**Psychological preparation.** The body changes of pregnancy can be anticipated and prepared for in therapy. Developing coping strategies for weight gain, practicing tolerance for body change, and establishing a relapse prevention plan for pregnancy provide psychological scaffolding for the perinatal period.

**Treatment team coordination.** Establishing a prenatal care team that includes an obstetrician/midwife aware of the eating disorder history, a mental health provider, and a dietitian allows for coordinated care from the beginning of pregnancy rather than reactive intervention when problems emerge.

## The Postpartum Period: The Highest-Risk Phase

While pregnancy itself can temporarily improve eating disorder symptoms, the postpartum period is consistently identified as a high-risk window for eating disorder onset, relapse, and intensification.

**"Bounce back" culture.** The cultural pressure to return to pre-pregnancy body size rapidly after birth — reinforced by celebrity postpartum weight loss narratives, social media, and well-meaning comments from family and friends — creates an environment that actively promotes restriction during a period when adequate nutrition is critical for recovery, breastfeeding, and infant care [4].

**Body dissatisfaction.** The postpartum body — which may include residual weight gain, abdominal separation, stretch marks, breast changes, and pelvic floor changes — does not match pre-pregnancy appearance and may never fully return to it. For someone with an eating disorder history, this discrepancy between the body they had and the body they have can trigger intense distress and compensatory behaviors.

**Hormonal shifts.** The dramatic hormonal changes of the postpartum period — including rapid drops in estrogen and progesterone — affect mood, appetite, and body image. These hormonal fluctuations can destabilize eating patterns and emotional regulation.

**Sleep deprivation.** Chronic sleep loss in the postpartum period impairs decision-making, emotional regulation, and impulse control — all of which are relevant to eating disorder behavior. Sleep deprivation also affects hunger and satiety hormones (increasing ghrelin, decreasing leptin), which can disrupt eating patterns [9].

**Social isolation.** New parenthood can be isolating, particularly for individuals who relied on social connection for emotional support. Isolation reduces accountability, increases emotional eating or restriction, and limits access to the social buffering that supports mental health.

**Breastfeeding pressures.** The pressure to breastfeed — and the caloric demands of lactation — create a complex relationship with food for someone with an eating disorder. Some individuals experience breastfeeding as permission to eat adequately; others experience the caloric demands as an opportunity for restriction ("I can restrict and still lose weight because breastfeeding burns extra calories").

## Screening and Identification

The failure to screen for eating disorders during prenatal and postpartum care represents a significant gap in perinatal health.

Standard prenatal care typically assesses weight gain, nutritional intake, and general physical health — but does not include validated eating disorder screening. A pregnant person who is losing weight or gaining inadequately may receive nutritional counseling without being assessed for an eating disorder. A postpartum person who is losing weight rapidly may be congratulated rather than screened.

The SCOFF questionnaire and the EDE-Q can be adapted for perinatal use. Additionally, several perinatal-specific questions have been proposed: Have you ever had an eating disorder? Are you concerned about your weight gain during pregnancy? Have you been restricting food, purging, or exercising excessively since becoming pregnant? Are you distressed about the changes in your body during pregnancy?

Universal screening at the first prenatal visit, with follow-up screening in each trimester and at the postpartum visit, would significantly improve identification rates [5].

## Treatment During and After Pregnancy

Treatment for eating disorders during pregnancy requires careful coordination between obstetric care, mental health treatment, and nutritional support.

**CBT adapted for the perinatal period** can address the specific cognitive distortions that arise during pregnancy and postpartum — fears about weight gain, beliefs about body shape, catastrophic thinking about post-pregnancy body — while maintaining a focus on behavioral change.

**Nutritional rehabilitation** during pregnancy must meet both the eating disorder recovery needs and the enhanced nutritional demands of pregnancy. Meal planning, nutritional supplementation, and regular monitoring by a dietitian are essential.

**Medical monitoring** should include more frequent prenatal visits, regular growth ultrasounds to assess fetal development, and close monitoring of weight gain, nutritional bloodwork, and electrolytes.

**Postpartum planning** should begin during pregnancy. Developing a postpartum support plan that includes continued mental health treatment, nutritional support, practical help with infant care, and strategies for managing "bounce back" pressure can reduce the risk of postpartum relapse [10].

> 💡 **Spark:** Pregnancy does not pause an eating disorder. It reshapes the battlefield. The body that an eating disorder has spent years trying to control is now changing rapidly in ways that serve another life. For the person carrying both a baby and an eating disorder, asking for help is not weakness — it is the bravest form of care for two.

## What You Can Try

- **If you have an eating disorder history and are planning pregnancy,** talk to your treatment team before conceiving. Preconception planning significantly improves outcomes.

- **If you are pregnant and struggling with eating,** tell your prenatal care provider. If they do not have expertise in eating disorders, ask for a referral. The National Alliance for Eating Disorders (1-866-662-1235) can help.

- **If you are postpartum and experiencing intensified body image distress or eating disorder behaviors,** this is a recognized pattern and help is available. You are not failing at parenthood — you are managing a psychiatric condition during one of the most demanding periods of your life.

- **Push back against "bounce back" pressure.** Your body grew a human. It deserves time, nutrition, and compassion — not punishment.
