> **Summary:** Muscle dysmorphia — sometimes called "bigorexia" or "reverse anorexia" — is a form of body dysmorphic disorder in which individuals become preoccupied with the belief that their body is insufficiently muscular or lean, despite often being significantly more muscular than average. This preoccupation drives compulsive exercise, rigid dietary regimens, avoidance of situations where the body might be seen, and significant distress. Muscle dysmorphia predominantly affects men but is not exclusive to any gender. It sits at the intersection of body dysmorphic disorder and eating disorders, involves both exercise and food-related pathology, and carries serious physical and psychological consequences — including anabolic steroid use, social isolation, depression, and suicidal ideation. Despite its prevalence in fitness culture, muscle dysmorphia remains underdiagnosed and undertreated.

**Key Facts:**
1. Muscle dysmorphia is classified in the DSM-5-TR as a specifier of body dysmorphic disorder, under the obsessive-compulsive and related disorders chapter [1].
2. Prevalence estimates range from 6% to 53.6% among regular gym users and competitive bodybuilders, depending on the measurement instrument and population studied [2].
3. Individuals with muscle dysmorphia often have bodies that are objectively more muscular than average, yet perceive themselves as small, weak, or insufficiently developed [3].
4. Anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS) use is significantly more common among individuals with muscle dysmorphia, with rates estimated at 46–58% compared to 7–12% of gym users without the condition [4].
5. Muscle dysmorphia is associated with depression, anxiety, social impairment, occupational dysfunction, and suicidal ideation at rates comparable to other body image disorders [5].

## The Mirror That Shrinks You

The defining psychological feature of muscle dysmorphia is a perceptual and cognitive distortion in which the individual sees their body as smaller, weaker, or less muscular than it actually is. This is the mirror image of the distortion seen in anorexia nervosa, where the individual perceives their body as larger than it is — hence the clinical descriptor "reverse anorexia" proposed by Pope and colleagues in their pioneering 1993 research [3].

But muscle dysmorphia is not simply "anorexia for men who want to be big." It is a distinct condition with its own clinical profile, driven by preoccupation with muscularity and leanness rather than thinness per se. The individual may weigh significantly more than the general population average, have clearly visible musculature, and still experience deep distress about perceived inadequacy.

The behaviors that accompany this distortion are often compulsive in nature. Individuals with muscle dysmorphia may spend 3–5 hours per day exercising, adhere to extremely rigid dietary protocols (often high-protein, highly regimented, with precise meal timing), avoid social situations where their body might be exposed or compared, repeatedly check their muscularity in mirrors or by flexing, and experience severe anxiety or depression when they miss a workout or deviate from their dietary plan [5].

The social and occupational impairment can be profound. People with muscle dysmorphia may decline social invitations because they conflict with training schedules, avoid romantic relationships due to body shame, choose jobs based on proximity to a gym, and spend significant portions of their income on supplements, gym memberships, specialized food, and in some cases, anabolic steroids.

## Who Develops Muscle Dysmorphia?

Muscle dysmorphia predominantly affects men, but it is not exclusively a male condition. Research has documented muscle dysmorphia in women, particularly those involved in bodybuilding, CrossFit, and physique competitions. The condition is most commonly identified in individuals who are actively engaged in resistance training and gym culture, but it can occur in anyone who becomes preoccupied with perceived muscular inadequacy.

Several risk factors have been identified:

**Gender role pressure.** Cultural expectations for male bodies have shifted significantly over the past four decades. Research by Leit, Pope, and Gray (2001) documented that the bodies of male action figures, male models, and male media representations have become increasingly muscular over time — far exceeding what is naturally achievable [6]. Boys and men are exposed to an ever-more-extreme muscular ideal from childhood, creating a comparison gap that mirrors the thin-ideal gap documented for women.

**Childhood experiences.** Research has found that childhood bullying — particularly appearance-based teasing about being thin, small, or weak — is a significant risk factor for muscle dysmorphia. The condition may develop as a compensatory response: the individual pursues size and muscularity to protect against the vulnerability they experienced as a smaller child [7].

**Perfectionism and compulsivity.** Like other body image disorders, muscle dysmorphia is associated with elevated perfectionism, particularly self-oriented perfectionism (setting unrealistically high standards for oneself). The compulsive quality of the exercise and dietary behaviors suggests overlap with OCD-spectrum pathology.

**Social media and fitness culture.** The rise of fitness influencer culture on Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube has created an environment in which extremely muscular physiques — many achieved with the assistance of anabolic steroids, which the influencers may not disclose — are presented as naturally achievable and aspirational. Young men who compare their bodies to these enhanced images experience the same body dissatisfaction dynamics documented in women comparing themselves to thin-ideal imagery.

## The Substance Use Dimension

One of the most medically significant aspects of muscle dysmorphia is its association with anabolic-androgenic steroid (AAS) use. Research by Pope and colleagues has consistently found that AAS use is dramatically more common among individuals with muscle dysmorphia than among gym users without the condition [4].

Anabolic steroids carry serious medical risks including liver damage, cardiovascular complications (cardiomyopathy, atherosclerosis, sudden cardiac death), hormonal disruption (testicular atrophy, infertility, gynecomastia), psychiatric effects (aggression, mania, depression during withdrawal), and dependence. The medical risks of AAS use in the context of muscle dysmorphia are comparable in severity to the medical risks of purging in the context of bulimia — yet they receive far less clinical attention.

The relationship between muscle dysmorphia and AAS use is bidirectional. Muscle dysmorphia may drive individuals toward steroids as a means of achieving a physique that is unattainable naturally. Conversely, AAS use may worsen body dysmorphia through hormonal effects on mood and cognition, and through the experience of muscle loss during off-cycles or cessation, which can trigger intense body image distress [8].

## The Eating Disorder Connection

Muscle dysmorphia occupies a contested diagnostic space between body dysmorphic disorder and eating disorders. The DSM-5-TR classifies it under BDD, but the clinical reality is more complex.

Research by Murray and colleagues (2012) has argued that muscle dysmorphia involves significant eating pathology — rigid dietary rules, macronutrient obsession, food avoidance based on body composition goals, and in some cases, bingeing and purging behaviors in the service of body composition [9]. These behaviors are functionally identical to eating disorder behaviors, even though the goal (muscularity) differs from the goal in typical eating disorders (thinness).

The dietary patterns in muscle dysmorphia can produce nutritional consequences: excessive protein intake can strain kidney function, rigid meal timing can produce social isolation and anxiety, and the cycling between "bulking" (high-calorie eating to build muscle) and "cutting" (severe caloric restriction to reduce body fat) closely parallels the restriction-binge cycle seen in bulimia nervosa.

This diagnostic ambiguity has practical implications: individuals with muscle dysmorphia may present to eating disorder clinicians who are unfamiliar with BDD, or to BDD clinicians who are unfamiliar with the eating pathology component. Comprehensive treatment requires attention to both the body image distortion and the disordered eating behaviors.

## Treatment Approaches

Treatment for muscle dysmorphia draws from both the BDD and eating disorder treatment literature.

**CBT with exposure and response prevention (ERP)** is the primary evidence-based psychotherapy. Treatment involves identifying the compulsive behaviors (excessive exercise, mirror checking, body monitoring) and the avoidance behaviors (avoiding social situations, refusing to be seen without a pump, wearing concealing clothing), then gradually reducing these behaviors through structured exposure exercises. For example, a person might be asked to attend a social event in a regular t-shirt rather than a baggy hoodie, then tolerate the anxiety without engaging in compensatory exercise [10].

**Cognitive restructuring** addresses the distorted beliefs about muscularity and self-worth: "If I'm not big enough, I'm worthless," "Missing one workout will cause me to shrink," "People are judging my size." These beliefs are systematically examined and challenged using evidence-based CBT techniques.

**SSRIs** are used as pharmacological treatment, consistent with the BDD treatment model. Doses are typically at the higher end of the prescribing range.

**Nutritional counseling** addresses the rigid dietary patterns and may involve the same principles used in eating disorder nutrition therapy: developing flexibility around food, reducing food-related rituals, and establishing adequate and balanced nutrition.

**Steroid cessation support** is an essential component for individuals using AAS. Cessation can trigger depression, body image deterioration, and relapse of muscle dysmorphia symptoms, and should be managed with psychiatric and medical support.

> 💡 **Spark:** Muscle dysmorphia is not about being vain at the gym. It is about looking in the mirror and seeing a body that is never enough — no matter how many hours are spent training, how many meals are meticulously planned, or how much the body has already changed. The pursuit of size becomes a prison when the goalpost never stops moving.

## What You Can Try

- **Honest self-assessment.** If your exercise routine causes you significant distress when disrupted, if you avoid social situations because of body dissatisfaction, or if you use appearance-enhancing substances, consider whether your relationship with training has crossed from healthy to compulsive.

- **Take a planned rest day and notice your emotional response.** If missing one day of training produces intense anxiety, guilt, or depression, that emotional response is clinically meaningful and worth exploring with a professional.

- **Seek a clinician familiar with muscle dysmorphia.** Many general therapists and even some eating disorder specialists are unfamiliar with this presentation. Look for clinicians who mention BDD, muscle dysmorphia, or exercise compulsivity in their specialties.

- **Audit your social media.** Unfollow fitness accounts that consistently trigger body dissatisfaction or comparison. Many fitness influencer physiques are achieved with pharmacological assistance that is not disclosed — comparing your natural body to an enhanced one is a rigged comparison.

- **Track your mood around training** using Psychage's Mood Journal at [psychage.com/tools/mood-journal](psychage.com/tools/mood-journal). Patterns of distress linked to exercise, body checking, and dietary rigidity can help you and your clinician understand the condition's maintaining factors.
