> **Summary:** Eating disorders are psychiatric conditions with profound medical consequences. They affect virtually every organ system in the body — from the heart and brain to the bones, kidneys, gastrointestinal tract, and endocrine system. Anorexia nervosa carries the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder, and the medical complications of bulimia and binge eating disorder, while different in mechanism, are equally serious. Many of these complications develop silently, becoming evident only when they reach a dangerous threshold. This article provides a comprehensive overview of the medical impact of eating disorders — not to frighten, but to inform. Understanding what the body goes through is essential for recognizing the urgency of treatment and the importance of ongoing medical monitoring throughout recovery.

**Key Facts:**
1. Cardiac complications are the leading cause of death in anorexia nervosa, with risks including bradycardia, QTc prolongation, and sudden cardiac arrest from electrolyte disturbances [1].
2. Electrolyte imbalances — particularly hypokalemia (low potassium) — can develop rapidly in individuals who purge and can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias [2].
3. Refeeding syndrome — a potentially fatal metabolic complication that can occur when nutrition is reintroduced too rapidly after a period of starvation — requires careful medical management [3].
4. Osteoporosis affects up to 50% of individuals with chronic anorexia nervosa, with bone density loss that may be only partially reversible [4].
5. Medical complications can occur across all eating disorders and all body sizes — they are not limited to individuals who appear visibly underweight [5].

## Cardiovascular Complications: The Most Dangerous System

The cardiovascular system is the organ system most acutely threatened by eating disorders, and cardiac events are the leading cause of death in anorexia nervosa. Understanding these risks is essential for both individuals with eating disorders and the clinicians who treat them.

**Bradycardia** — an abnormally slow heart rate — is one of the most common cardiovascular findings in anorexia nervosa, occurring in up to 95% of individuals with severe restriction. The heart, a muscle, atrophies when the body is deprived of adequate energy, leading to reduced cardiac output, decreased blood pressure, and a resting heart rate that can drop below 40 beats per minute. While the body may adapt to mild bradycardia, severe bradycardia can cause dizziness, syncope (fainting), and in extreme cases, cardiac arrest [1].

**QTc prolongation** — an abnormality in the heart's electrical conduction that increases the risk of dangerous arrhythmias — is found in both anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa. In bulimia, QTc prolongation is often driven by electrolyte imbalances resulting from purging behaviors. A prolonged QTc interval predisposes the heart to torsades de pointes, a type of ventricular tachycardia that can degenerate into ventricular fibrillation and sudden death [2].

**Electrolyte disturbances** are among the most immediately life-threatening complications of eating disorders, particularly in individuals who purge. Hypokalemia (low potassium) is the most dangerous, as potassium is essential for normal cardiac electrical function. Potassium levels can drop rapidly with vomiting or laxative misuse, and hypokalemia can cause muscle weakness, cramping, cardiac arrhythmias, and cardiac arrest. Hyponatremia (low sodium), hypochloremia (low chloride), and metabolic alkalosis from vomiting also contribute to cardiac risk [2].

**Mitral valve prolapse** and **pericardial effusion** (fluid around the heart) have been documented in individuals with anorexia nervosa, reflecting the structural cardiac changes that occur with prolonged malnutrition.

Dr. Philip Mehler, one of the leading medical experts on eating disorder complications, has emphasized that cardiac monitoring — including regular electrocardiograms and electrolyte panels — should be standard practice for all individuals with active eating disorders, regardless of weight or diagnosis [6].

## Gastrointestinal Complications: The Digestive System Under Siege

Eating disorders directly affect the gastrointestinal (GI) system through multiple mechanisms, and GI symptoms are among the most commonly reported complaints in eating disorder patients.

**Delayed gastric emptying** (gastroparesis) is common in anorexia nervosa. When the body is malnourished, the muscles of the stomach and intestines lose tone and motility, causing food to move through the digestive system more slowly. This produces early satiety, bloating, nausea, and abdominal discomfort — symptoms that can paradoxically reinforce restriction by making eating physically uncomfortable [7].

**Constipation** is nearly universal in restrictive eating disorders due to reduced food intake, dehydration, and slowed intestinal motility. In individuals who misuse laxatives, the colon can become dependent on external stimulation, leading to chronic constipation when laxatives are discontinued — a condition sometimes called "cathartic colon."

**Esophageal damage** from recurrent vomiting includes erosive esophagitis, Barrett's esophagus (precancerous changes in the esophageal lining), and in rare but catastrophic cases, Mallory-Weiss tears or Boerhaave syndrome (esophageal rupture) — a surgical emergency with high mortality [6].

**Parotid gland enlargement** — swelling of the salivary glands in front of the ears — is a common physical sign in individuals who purge, caused by repeated stimulation of salivary flow. This swelling is usually painless but cosmetically noticeable and can be a source of distress.

**Pancreatitis** can occur in both restricting and binge-purge presentations, related to nutritional deficiency, metabolic disturbance, or refeeding.

**Superior mesenteric artery (SMA) syndrome** is a rare but serious complication of severe weight loss in which the fat pad that normally cushions the duodenum (the first part of the small intestine) between the aorta and the superior mesenteric artery is depleted, causing the artery to compress the duodenum and obstruct food passage. This condition requires medical intervention and nutritional rehabilitation.

## Skeletal System: Bones That Do Not Recover

Bone health is among the most serious and least reversible complications of eating disorders, particularly anorexia nervosa.

**Osteopenia and osteoporosis** develop when the body is chronically deprived of the calories, calcium, vitamin D, and estrogen (or testosterone) necessary for bone maintenance. Research by Misra and Klibanski (2014) found that up to 50% of individuals with anorexia nervosa have osteoporosis and up to 92% have osteopenia, with some developing stress fractures in their twenties and thirties [4].

The mechanism involves both nutritional deficiency and hormonal disruption. Prolonged malnutrition suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, reducing estrogen production in women and testosterone production in men — hormones that are critical for bone remodeling. Additionally, elevated cortisol levels associated with the stress of starvation directly inhibit bone formation.

What makes this complication particularly devastating is its irreversibility. While bone density may partially improve with weight restoration and hormonal normalization, research indicates that full recovery of bone density is often not achieved, particularly if the eating disorder occurs during the critical period of adolescent bone development when peak bone mass is being established [4]. An individual who develops anorexia nervosa during adolescence may carry increased fracture risk for the rest of their life.

## Endocrine and Reproductive Complications

Eating disorders profoundly affect the endocrine (hormonal) system, with consequences that extend across virtually every hormonal axis.

**Amenorrhea and menstrual irregularity** are among the most well-known endocrine complications. While amenorrhea was removed as a required diagnostic criterion for anorexia nervosa in the DSM-5 (recognizing that the condition affects men and pre-menarchal girls), it remains a common consequence of energy deficiency. The hypothalamus suppresses gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) when energy availability drops below a critical threshold, leading to reduced luteinizing hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), which in turn suppress estrogen production and ovulation [8].

**Thyroid suppression** occurs as an adaptive response to starvation. The body reduces conversion of T4 to the active thyroid hormone T3, resulting in a state sometimes called "sick euthyroid syndrome" or "low T3 syndrome." This suppression reduces metabolic rate — a survival mechanism that also contributes to cold intolerance, fatigue, and constipation.

**Elevated cortisol** — the stress hormone — is consistently found in individuals with anorexia nervosa. Chronic cortisol elevation contributes to bone loss, immune suppression, glucose dysregulation, and muscle wasting.

**Growth hormone resistance** has been documented in anorexia nervosa, with elevated growth hormone levels but reduced IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1), reflecting a paradoxical state in which the body produces growth signals but cannot respond to them due to nutritional deficiency.

**Fertility consequences** can extend into recovery. While menstrual function typically returns with adequate weight restoration, some individuals experience prolonged fertility difficulties. Research suggests that the window of vulnerability is wider than previously believed [8].

## Neurological Complications

The brain is highly metabolically active, consuming approximately 20% of the body's energy. Inadequate nutrition directly impacts brain structure and function.

**Gray and white matter volume loss** has been documented in acute anorexia nervosa using structural MRI. Studies report reductions of 5–20% in gray matter volume during the acute phase of illness [9]. While significant recovery of brain volume occurs with weight restoration, some regional deficits may persist.

**Cognitive impairment** is common during acute malnutrition, affecting concentration, memory, processing speed, and decision-making. These effects can impair the person's ability to engage effectively in psychological treatment — which is one reason nutritional rehabilitation is considered a prerequisite for meaningful psychotherapeutic work [9].

**Peripheral neuropathy** — nerve damage causing numbness, tingling, or weakness in the extremities — can occur with prolonged nutritional deficiency, particularly deficiencies in B vitamins.

**Seizures** can occur in the context of severe electrolyte disturbances, particularly hyponatremia, and during the refeeding period if not managed carefully.

## Refeeding Syndrome: The Danger of Recovery Done Wrong

One of the most important medical complications to understand is one that occurs not during the eating disorder itself but during the early stages of nutritional rehabilitation.

**Refeeding syndrome** is a potentially fatal metabolic complication that can develop when nutrition is reintroduced too rapidly after a period of starvation. During prolonged energy restriction, the body shifts from carbohydrate metabolism to fat and protein metabolism. When carbohydrates are reintroduced, insulin surges drive potassium, phosphate, and magnesium from the bloodstream into cells, causing dangerously low serum levels of these electrolytes [3].

Hypophosphatemia is the hallmark of refeeding syndrome and can cause respiratory failure, cardiac dysfunction, rhabdomyolysis, hemolytic anemia, and death. The NICE guidelines recommend that refeeding in severely malnourished patients begin with reduced caloric intake (often 50–75% of estimated needs), with gradual increases over days to weeks, accompanied by close monitoring of electrolytes and supplementation of phosphate, potassium, magnesium, and thiamine [10].

Refeeding syndrome underscores a critical message: nutritional rehabilitation for severe eating disorders should be medically supervised. It is not simply a matter of "eating more" — it is a medical process that carries its own risks if not managed carefully.

## Complications Across All Body Sizes

A persistent and dangerous misconception is that medical complications of eating disorders are limited to individuals who are visibly underweight. Research has thoroughly dismantled this assumption.

Individuals with atypical anorexia nervosa — who meet all criteria for anorexia except low weight — experience comparable rates of bradycardia, electrolyte disturbances, and orthostatic instability [5]. Individuals with bulimia nervosa, who often maintain a weight in the typical range, are at risk for cardiac arrhythmias, esophageal damage, and dental erosion. Individuals with binge eating disorder may experience metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, sleep apnea, and cardiovascular disease [11].

The rate and extent of weight loss may be more medically significant than the absolute weight at which someone presents. A person who has lost 30% of their body weight through restriction is in medical danger regardless of their starting weight [12].

> 💡 **Spark:** An eating disorder does not wait until someone looks a certain way to damage their body. The heart does not check whether someone meets a weight threshold before it develops an arrhythmia. Medical monitoring is essential for every person with an eating disorder — at every size, every stage, and every diagnosis.

## What You Can Try

- **Request a comprehensive medical assessment.** If you have an active eating disorder, ask your healthcare provider for a complete metabolic panel, electrocardiogram, and assessment of vital signs including orthostatic measurements. This is not optional — it is medically necessary.

- **Understand that some complications are silent.** You may feel physically fine while dangerous electrolyte disturbances or cardiac conduction abnormalities are developing. Regular monitoring catches problems before they become emergencies.

- **If you are beginning nutritional rehabilitation,** ensure it is medically supervised, particularly if you have been severely restricting. Refeeding should be gradual and monitored.

- **Talk to your treatment team about bone density** if you have had an eating disorder for more than 6–12 months, particularly if you have experienced amenorrhea or are under 30 (when peak bone mass is still being established).

- **Track your physical symptoms** alongside your emotional patterns using Psychage's tools at [psychage.com/tools](psychage.com/tools). Reporting physical symptoms to your treatment team can help them monitor your medical status.
