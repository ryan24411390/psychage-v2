> **Summary:** Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) is a feeding and eating disorder that challenges nearly every assumption people hold about what eating disorders look like. It has nothing to do with body image, weight control, or the desire to be thin. Instead, ARFID involves the persistent avoidance or restriction of food based on sensory characteristics, fear of aversive consequences of eating, or apparent lack of interest in food — leading to nutritional deficiency, weight loss, dependence on supplements, or significant psychosocial impairment. First introduced in the DSM-5 in 2013, ARFID is increasingly recognized as a condition that affects children, adolescents, and adults across all demographics. This article explores what ARFID is, how it differs from typical picky eating, and what effective treatment looks like.

**Key Facts:**
1. ARFID was introduced as a formal diagnosis in the DSM-5 in 2013, replacing the narrower pediatric category "feeding disorder of infancy or early childhood" [1].
2. Prevalence estimates for ARFID range from 0.5% to 5% of the general population, with higher rates among children and individuals with autism spectrum conditions and anxiety disorders [2].
3. ARFID has three recognized presentation profiles: sensory-based avoidance, fear of aversive consequences (choking, vomiting, allergic reaction), and lack of interest in eating or low appetite [3].
4. Unlike anorexia nervosa, ARFID does not involve body image disturbance or fear of weight gain — the avoidance is driven by sensory, anxiety-based, or appetite-related factors [1].
5. ARFID can lead to serious medical consequences including malnutrition, growth faltering in children, electrolyte imbalances, and psychosocial impairment including inability to eat in social settings [4].

## The Condition That Breaks the Eating Disorder Mold

Most people think of eating disorders as conditions driven by body dissatisfaction, the desire to control weight, or distorted self-perception. ARFID explodes this framework entirely. A person with ARFID may have no concerns whatsoever about their weight or body shape. They may want to eat more, or different foods, or in social settings — and find themselves unable to.

The DSM-5-TR defines ARFID as an eating or feeding disturbance manifested by persistent failure to meet appropriate nutritional and/or energy needs, associated with one or more of: significant weight loss or failure to achieve expected growth, significant nutritional deficiency, dependence on enteral feeding or oral nutritional supplements, or marked interference with psychosocial functioning [1]. Critically, the disturbance is not better explained by lack of available food, cultural practice, another medical condition (unless the severity exceeds what would be expected from that condition), or another eating disorder such as anorexia nervosa or bulimia nervosa.

Dr. Rachel Bryant-Waugh, one of the leading researchers in ARFID, has identified three primary presentation profiles that often overlap [3]:

**Sensory sensitivity profile.** These individuals avoid foods based on their sensory properties — texture, taste, smell, appearance, or temperature. A common pattern involves restricting to a narrow range of "accepted" foods, often described colloquially as extreme picky eating. However, this is not a preference or a stubbornness issue. Sensory-based ARFID involves genuine, often intense, aversive responses to food characteristics. A person may gag, retch, or experience overwhelming disgust when exposed to foods outside their accepted range. The sensory experience is qualitatively different from what a typical picky eater experiences.

**Fear-based profile.** These individuals avoid eating due to specific fears related to the consequences of eating — choking, vomiting, having an allergic reaction, abdominal pain, or other aversive outcomes. Often, but not always, the fear develops after a traumatic eating experience — a choking episode, a severe vomiting illness, a witnessed allergic reaction. The fear can generalize beyond the original triggering food to broader categories or to eating in general, leading to progressive restriction.

**Low interest/appetite profile.** These individuals simply do not experience hunger or interest in food in the way that most people do. They may forget to eat, feel full after very small amounts, find eating effortful or unpleasant, or experience food as generally unappealing. This presentation can be particularly insidious because the individual may not recognize they have a problem — they are not restricting intentionally, they simply lack the internal cue that drives most people to eat.

Many individuals with ARFID present with a combination of these profiles, and the dominant profile may shift over time or in different contexts.

## ARFID vs. Picky Eating: Where the Line Falls

One of the most important distinctions in ARFID research and clinical practice is the boundary between typical selective eating and clinical ARFID. This distinction matters because it determines whether someone receives appropriate support or is dismissed with advice to "just try new foods."

Picky eating is common, particularly in childhood. Research suggests that up to 50% of parents describe their children as picky eaters at some point during development [5]. Most children who go through a selective eating phase gradually expand their dietary repertoire through natural exposure, developmental maturation, and social modeling. Their picky eating does not typically cause significant nutritional deficiency, growth disruption, or psychosocial impairment.

ARFID differs from typical picky eating in four key dimensions, as outlined by Thomas and Eddy (2019) in their clinical manual for ARFID [6]:

**Severity of restriction.** Individuals with ARFID often eat a very small number of accepted foods — sometimes fewer than 10 or 20 specific items. The restriction is rigid and often extends to specific brands or preparations of foods (e.g., only one brand of chicken nuggets, only a specific texture of bread).

**Nutritional and health consequences.** While a picky eater may eat a limited range of foods but still meet basic nutritional needs, individuals with ARFID frequently develop nutritional deficiencies (iron, zinc, vitamin B12, calcium), experience weight loss or growth faltering, or become dependent on nutritional supplements to maintain adequate intake.

**Persistence and trajectory.** Typical picky eating tends to improve with development. ARFID, left untreated, tends to persist or worsen. Many adults with ARFID report that their restrictive eating patterns have been present since childhood and have not naturally resolved.

**Psychosocial impairment.** ARFID frequently causes significant interference with daily life — inability to eat in social settings (restaurants, friends' houses, school cafeterias), avoidance of travel, relationship strain, workplace difficulty, and social isolation. A study by Zickgraf and colleagues (2019) found that adults with ARFID reported significantly higher levels of social anxiety and eating-related psychosocial impairment than adults with typical selective eating [7].

## The ARFID-Autism Connection

One of the most actively researched areas in ARFID is its overlap with autism spectrum conditions. Sensory processing differences are a core feature of autism, and these differences frequently extend to food — texture, temperature, smell, appearance, and even the sound of certain foods can trigger intense aversive responses in autistic individuals.

Research by Koomar and colleagues (2021) published in the *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* found that sensory-based food avoidance was significantly more prevalent in autistic children than in neurotypical children, with rates that often met or approached ARFID criteria [8]. Importantly, this research also demonstrated that the food avoidance in autistic individuals was not driven by body image concerns or weight-related anxiety — it was rooted in genuine sensory processing differences that made certain food characteristics physically intolerable.

This overlap creates both clinical opportunities and challenges. On the opportunity side, understanding the sensory basis of food avoidance can lead to more effective, personalized interventions — for example, systematic desensitization approaches that gradually introduce new sensory experiences in a structured, low-pressure way, rather than the confrontational "you have to eat it" approach that often worsens avoidance.

On the challenge side, the overlap can lead to diagnostic confusion. An autistic child who eats only five foods may have their restrictive eating attributed solely to autism and never assessed for ARFID — which means they may not receive targeted feeding intervention. Conversely, a child with ARFID and no autism diagnosis may have their food avoidance dismissed as behavioral or oppositional rather than recognized as a feeding disorder with sensory or anxiety-based drivers.

Dr. Kamryn Eddy at Massachusetts General Hospital has been instrumental in advocating for routine ARFID screening in neurodevelopmental clinics, arguing that the high co-occurrence between ARFID and autism warrants systematic assessment rather than assumptions about causation [4].

## Treatment: What Actually Works for ARFID

Treatment for ARFID is still a developing field — the diagnosis is barely a decade old, and large-scale randomized controlled trials are ongoing. However, several approaches have shown promising results in clinical settings and preliminary research.

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for ARFID (CBT-AR)** is the most systematically developed psychotherapy for the condition. Created by Thomas and Eddy at Massachusetts General Hospital, CBT-AR is a structured, 20–30 session treatment that addresses the specific maintaining mechanisms of each ARFID profile [6]. For sensory-based avoidance, the treatment includes graduated food exposure with systematic desensitization. For fear-based avoidance, it incorporates principles from exposure-based anxiety treatments. For low interest/appetite, it uses psychoeducation about hunger cues and behavioral strategies to increase meal structure and volume.

Early outcome data for CBT-AR are encouraging. A case series by Thomas and colleagues (2020) reported that 67% of participants showed clinically significant improvement in ARFID symptoms, with increases in the number and variety of foods consumed and reductions in eating-related distress and avoidance [9].

**Family-based treatment adaptations** have also shown promise for children and adolescents with ARFID. Drawing on the principles of the Maudsley approach to anorexia nervosa — which positions parents as active agents in feeding and recovery — family-based ARFID treatment empowers parents to support their child's eating through structured meals, gradual food introduction, and reduction of mealtime conflict.

**Occupational therapy** approaches, particularly those rooted in sensory integration theory, can be valuable adjuncts for individuals with sensory-based ARFID. These interventions help individuals gradually increase their tolerance for a range of sensory experiences — including food-related sensory input — in a controlled, therapeutic environment.

**Medical nutritional rehabilitation** is often a necessary component of ARFID treatment, particularly for individuals who present with significant malnutrition, growth faltering, or micronutrient deficiencies. This may involve working with a dietitian to optimize nutrition within the person's currently accepted food range while simultaneously working to expand that range through therapeutic intervention.

It is worth noting what does not work for ARFID: pressure, coercion, and "just try it" approaches. Research consistently shows that forcing or pressuring individuals with ARFID to eat non-accepted foods increases anxiety, reinforces avoidance, and damages the therapeutic relationship [10]. Effective ARFID treatment is collaborative, gradual, and respectful of the individual's subjective experience.

## The Adult ARFID Experience

While much of the clinical attention around ARFID has focused on children, a growing body of research is documenting the adult ARFID experience. A study by Zickgraf and Ellis (2018) found that adult ARFID is associated with significant psychosocial burden — including difficulty dating, workplace challenges related to business meals, avoidance of social events involving food, and chronic nutritional concerns [11].

Adults with ARFID often report a lifetime of being dismissed, ridiculed, or punished for their eating patterns. They may have been forced to eat foods that caused genuine distress as children, told they were "too old" for picky eating, or excluded from social activities because of their restricted diet. Many arrive at a clinical setting with deep shame about their eating and skepticism that anyone can help.

Recognition and validation are themselves therapeutic for many adults with ARFID. Learning that their experience has a name, a research base, and effective treatments can be profoundly relieving after years or decades of feeling broken or defective.

> 💡 **Spark:** ARFID is the eating disorder that has nothing to do with wanting to be thin — and that is precisely why it has been invisible for so long. For every person who has been told to "just try new foods" while experiencing genuine distress around eating, there is a diagnosis, there is a scientific understanding, and there is effective help.

## What You Can Try

If you or someone you care about may be experiencing ARFID, here are concrete steps:

- **Recognize that this is a real condition, not a preference.** If food avoidance is causing nutritional concerns, psychosocial impairment, or significant distress, it may meet criteria for ARFID. The first step is recognizing that this is not something you can or should just "get over."

- **Seek assessment from a professional familiar with ARFID.** Many eating disorder clinicians are only trained in anorexia, bulimia, and BED. Look for providers who specifically mention ARFID in their clinical areas. The Academy for Eating Disorders and the Massachusetts General Hospital ARFID clinic maintain referral resources.

- **Start a food journal focused on experience, not calories.** Document what you eat, how it felt (texture, taste, emotional response), and what factors made eating easier or harder. This information is valuable for treatment planning.

- **Build on accepted foods rather than forcing new ones.** Expand from what is already tolerable. If you eat plain pasta, the next step might be pasta with a very small amount of sauce — not a completely novel food. Gradual, systematic exposure works; pressure does not.
