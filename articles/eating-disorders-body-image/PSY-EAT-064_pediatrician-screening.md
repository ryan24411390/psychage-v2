> **Summary:** Pediatricians and primary care providers see children and adolescents more frequently and consistently than any other healthcare professional, positioning them as the most likely medical contact before an eating disorder is formally diagnosed. Yet research consistently shows that eating disorders are under-detected in primary care — fewer than one-third of cases are identified by the treating pediatrician before the patient presents with medical complications or is referred by someone else. This gap is not due to indifference but to a combination of time constraints, lack of training in eating disorder recognition, reliance on visual assessment rather than systematic screening, and discomfort with raising the topic during brief well-child visits. This article reviews validated screening tools suitable for pediatric primary care, explains how to use growth trajectory monitoring as an early detection strategy, discusses communication approaches that increase disclosure, and outlines clear referral criteria and pathways.

**Key Facts:**

1. Only 28.5% of adolescents with eating disorders are identified by their primary care provider prior to specialist referral, despite averaging 2-3 primary care visits during the period of symptom development [1].
2. The SCOFF questionnaire, a five-item screening tool, has a sensitivity of 84.6% and specificity of 89.6% for detecting eating disorders in adolescent populations when used in clinical settings [2].
3. A deviation of one or more major percentile lines on a child's growth chart — either upward or downward — is the earliest detectable medical indicator of disordered eating, often preceding other clinical signs by 6-12 months [3].
4. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends universal screening for eating disorders as part of annual well-child visits beginning at age 11, using brief validated instruments [4].
5. Adolescents are significantly more likely to disclose disordered eating behaviors when asked directly and privately than when questions are embedded in general health questionnaires completed in waiting rooms [5].

## The Detection Gap in Primary Care

Eating disorders have the highest mortality rate of any mental health condition and are treatable, particularly when identified early. Yet primary care — the healthcare setting with the greatest potential for early detection — consistently misses the majority of cases. Understanding why this gap exists is the first step toward closing it.

**Time constraints.** A typical well-child visit lasts 15-20 minutes and must cover developmental milestones, immunizations, school performance, safety, substance use, sexual health, and mental health. Eating disorder screening competes with dozens of other priorities, and without a structured protocol, it is easily deferred or omitted. Providers report that they intend to screen for disordered eating but run out of time, particularly when other pressing issues emerge during the visit [6].

**Reliance on visual assessment.** Many providers implicitly screen for eating disorders by visual impression — looking for obvious thinness, visible malnutrition, or dramatic changes in appearance. This approach misses the majority of eating disorders for several reasons. Anorexia nervosa accounts for only a small fraction of eating disorders; binge eating disorder, bulimia nervosa, and OSFED (other specified feeding or eating disorder) often occur in individuals across a wide range of body sizes. A child with bulimia nervosa may present at a statistically typical size. A child with ARFID may appear small but is dismissed as "always been petite." And a child in the early stages of restriction may not yet show visible changes [7].

**Diagnostic overshadowing.** Patients with eating disorders frequently present to primary care with gastrointestinal complaints (bloating, constipation, abdominal pain), fatigue, dizziness, menstrual irregularity, or mood symptoms — and these presenting complaints may be treated in isolation without investigation of an underlying eating disorder. A 2019 retrospective chart review found that adolescents later diagnosed with eating disorders averaged 4.2 primary care visits for somatic complaints in the 12 months before diagnosis, most of which did not include eating disorder screening [1].

**Discomfort with the topic.** Some providers report hesitancy to ask about eating behaviors, body image, or dieting — particularly with patients in larger bodies, where the provider may worry about implying that the patient should change their eating, or with patients in smaller bodies, where the provider may worry about normalizing or encouraging restriction. This discomfort, while understandable, creates a systematic blind spot.

## Validated Screening Tools for Pediatric Primary Care

Effective screening does not require extensive clinical time. Several brief, validated instruments can be integrated into routine well-child visits.

**The SCOFF Questionnaire.** Developed by Morgan, Reid, and Lacey at St. George's Hospital in London, the SCOFF is a five-item screening tool that takes under two minutes to administer. Each item addresses a core feature of eating disorders: feeling out of control around food, deliberately making oneself sick, believing one is "too fat" when others say otherwise, and food dominating one's life. Two or more positive responses indicate a need for further evaluation. The SCOFF has been validated in adolescent populations with high sensitivity (84.6%) and specificity (89.6%), making it suitable for universal screening in primary care [2]. Its brevity makes it feasible within the constraints of a 15-minute visit, and it can be administered verbally by the provider during the clinical interview.

**The Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q).** The EDE-Q is a 28-item self-report measure that assesses eating disorder psychopathology across four subscales: restraint, eating concern, shape concern, and weight concern. While more comprehensive than the SCOFF, it requires 10-15 minutes to complete and is better suited as a second-stage assessment for patients who screen positive on a brief tool. Adolescent norms have been established, allowing providers to compare individual scores to population-level data [8]. The EDE-Q can be completed by the patient in the waiting room before the visit, maximizing clinical efficiency.

**The Eating Attitudes Test (EAT-26).** The EAT-26 is a 26-item self-report screening tool that identifies individuals with eating attitudes and behaviors associated with eating disorders. It is freely available, has strong psychometric properties in adolescent populations, and includes a behavioral checklist that asks about specific disordered eating behaviors. A score above 20 indicates elevated risk and warrants clinical follow-up [9]. The EAT-26 is widely used in research and clinical settings and can be self-administered.

**The HEADSS/HEADSSS assessment.** While not an eating disorder-specific tool, the HEADSS adolescent psychosocial interview (Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Safety/Suicide) already forms part of many well-adolescent visits. Adding eating-specific questions to this framework — "Have you tried to change your eating patterns or your body in the past year?" "How do you feel about your body?" "Have you ever made yourself vomit or used laxatives to control your weight?" — integrates eating disorder screening into an existing workflow without requiring a separate instrument [4].

## Growth Chart Monitoring as Early Detection

Growth charts are one of the most underutilized tools in eating disorder detection. Every child has an established growth trajectory — their personal curve of height, weight, and other metrics over time. Deviations from this trajectory are among the earliest objective indicators of disordered eating, often preceding self-report, behavioral observation, or clinical presentation by months.

**What to watch for.** A child or adolescent whose growth percentile shifts downward by one or more major lines (for example, from the 50th to the 25th percentile, or from the 75th to the 50th) warrants investigation. This is true regardless of where the absolute value falls — a child who drops from the 90th to the 60th percentile is showing a clinically significant trajectory change even though their absolute values remain statistically typical. Similarly, a child who fails to gain weight or height at the expected rate during a growth period (particularly during puberty, when growth should accelerate) may be nutritionally compromised [3].

**Upward trajectory changes matter too.** While downward shifts receive more attention, rapid increases in growth percentile can indicate binge eating disorder or the metabolic dysregulation associated with binge-purge cycling. Significant upward trajectory changes in the absence of a clear medical explanation (puberty, medication effects) should also prompt screening [10].

**Plot every visit, not just well-child visits.** Growth data collected at sick visits, sports physicals, and urgent care encounters should be entered into the growth chart. A pattern of progressive deviation may only become visible when data points from multiple visit types are connected.

**Use velocity curves.** In addition to static percentile plots, consider growth velocity — the rate of growth over time. A child whose absolute values remain within normal range but whose growth velocity has decelerated may be in the early stages of nutritional restriction. Velocity-based analysis is more sensitive to early change than cross-sectional percentile comparison [3].

## Communication Strategies That Increase Disclosure

Screening tools are only as effective as the clinical interaction in which they are embedded. How a provider asks about eating behaviors profoundly influences whether a patient discloses.

**Ask directly and privately.** Research by Eisenberg and colleagues found that adolescents were three times more likely to disclose disordered eating behaviors when asked directly by a provider during a private clinical encounter than when completing a written questionnaire in a waiting room with parents present [5]. Direct questioning signals that the topic is important and that the provider is a safe person to talk to. Privacy is essential — many adolescents will not disclose restriction, purging, or binge eating in front of caregivers.

**Normalize the conversation.** Frame eating-related questions as routine: "I ask all my patients about their eating and how they feel about their body, because it's an important part of health." This removes the implication that the patient looks like they have an eating disorder, which can trigger defensiveness.

**Use behavioral questions rather than diagnostic labels.** Instead of "Do you have an eating disorder?" — which most patients will deny — ask about specific behaviors: "Have you skipped meals to try to control your weight?" "Have you ever felt out of control while eating?" "Do you exercise even when you're sick or injured?" "Have you tried any diets, cleanses, or food rules in the past year?" Behavioral specificity yields more honest responses than categorical questions [11].

**Avoid weight-focused language.** Do not lead with comments about the patient's weight or size. Saying "You've gained/lost weight" before asking about eating behaviors associates the screening with weight judgment rather than health concern. Focus on behaviors, emotions, and functioning rather than size.

**Ask about social media and peer influences.** For adolescents, questions like "Are any of your friends dieting or trying to change their body?" and "Do you follow any social media accounts about food, fitness, or body transformation?" can open conversations about environmental risk factors and give the adolescent language for concerns they may not know how to articulate directly.

**Respond to disclosure with curiosity, not alarm.** If a patient discloses disordered eating, the provider's response sets the tone for all subsequent care. Express concern without catastrophizing: "Thank you for telling me that. I want to understand more about what's been going on so we can figure out the best next steps together." Avoid visible shock, judgment, or immediately telling the patient what to do — the goal in the moment is to maintain trust and gather information.

## When to Refer and Where

Not every positive screen requires specialist referral, but providers should have clear criteria for when to escalate.

**Refer immediately** if the patient presents with: orthostatic hypotension (heart rate increase greater than 20 bpm or blood pressure drop greater than 20 mmHg on standing), bradycardia (resting heart rate below 50 bpm), electrolyte abnormalities (particularly hypokalemia or hypophosphatemia), syncope, inability to maintain hydration, or acute suicidal ideation [4]. These findings indicate medical instability that requires inpatient or emergency evaluation.

**Refer for outpatient eating disorder evaluation** if the patient screens positive on a validated tool, reports persistent disordered eating behaviors (restriction, purging, binge eating, compulsive exercise), shows growth trajectory deviation without medical explanation, or has menstrual irregularity associated with eating or exercise changes. The referral should be to a provider or program specializing in eating disorders — ideally one that offers family-based treatment (FBT) for adolescents under 18, as FBT is the gold-standard intervention for this population [12].

**Involve the family early.** With appropriate consent and clinical judgment, inform parents or caregivers about screening findings and referral recommendations. Most eating disorder treatment models for adolescents involve the family, and parental buy-in at the point of referral predicts treatment engagement. Frame the referral as precautionary and collaborative: "I want to make sure we get the right support around what I'm seeing, so I'm connecting you with someone who specializes in this area."

**Follow up.** Regardless of whether the patient accepts the referral, schedule a follow-up visit within 2-4 weeks to reassess. Many patients decline initial referral due to ambivalence, denial, or fear — and a follow-up provides a second opportunity for engagement, growth chart reassessment, and relationship building.

> 💡 **Spark:** The pediatrician who catches an eating disorder early is rarely doing something extraordinary. They are doing something ordinary — asking a direct question, plotting a growth point, listening to a patient's answer — and doing it consistently. Detection is not about clinical brilliance. It is about systematic attention applied at every visit, to every patient, regardless of how they look.

## What You Can Try

If you are a pediatrician, primary care provider, or healthcare professional working with children and adolescents, here are actionable implementation steps:

**Integrate the SCOFF into your well-child visit template.** Add the five SCOFF items to your electronic health record (EHR) as a structured assessment that prompts at every well-child visit beginning at age 11. This takes the decision of whether to screen out of the individual visit and makes it automatic.

**Set up growth chart alerts.** Configure your EHR to flag any patient whose growth percentile crosses one or more major lines between visits. This automated monitoring catches trajectory changes that are invisible in single-visit data and reduces reliance on the provider remembering to check.

**Build a referral network.** Identify eating disorder specialists, FBT-trained therapists, and registered dietitians with eating disorder expertise in your area before you need them. Having referral resources pre-identified eliminates a barrier that often delays care after a positive screen.

**Practice the conversation.** Role-play eating disorder screening conversations with colleagues. Comfort with the topic increases with practice, and having rehearsed specific phrases — "I ask all my patients this" and "Thank you for telling me that" — makes the actual clinical encounter smoother and more natural.
