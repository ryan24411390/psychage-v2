> **Summary:** The transition to college represents one of the highest-risk windows for eating disorder onset. Between the ages of 18 and 25, young adults face a unique convergence of vulnerability factors that exist nowhere else in the lifespan: first-time independence over food decisions, unstructured eating environments such as all-you-can-eat dining halls, significant peer drinking culture that disrupts eating patterns, body comparison intensified by shared living spaces, identity instability during a major life transition, and academic stress in an achievement-oriented context. Research indicates that full-threshold eating disorders emerge in approximately 5-10% of college women and 3-4% of college men during their undergraduate years, with subclinical disordered eating affecting a far larger proportion. This article examines the specific risk architecture of the college environment, evaluates the evidence on campus-based prevention programs, and addresses the particular considerations of Greek life, athletics, and marginalized student populations.

**Key Facts:**
1. The transition to college is associated with a significant increase in eating disorder symptomatology, with one longitudinal study finding a 15% increase in disordered eating behaviors during the first semester alone [1].
2. The widely feared "Freshman 15" is largely mythical — average first-year weight change is modest — but the cultural anxiety surrounding it drives restrictive eating behavior in incoming students [2].
3. The Body Project, a cognitive dissonance-based prevention program, has been evaluated in over 50 controlled trials and consistently reduces eating disorder risk factors and future eating disorder onset in college-age women [3].
4. Sorority membership is associated with elevated rates of body dissatisfaction and disordered eating, with research attributing this to the intensified social comparison and appearance norms within Greek organizations [4].
5. "Drunkorexia" — the pattern of restricting food intake before alcohol consumption — affects an estimated 30% of college students who drink, with higher rates among women [5].

## The Perfect Storm: Why College Is a High-Risk Environment

No other developmental transition concentrates as many eating disorder risk factors into a single period as the transition to college. Understanding why requires examining these factors not individually but as an interacting system.

### First-Time Food Independence

For most students, college represents the first time they are fully responsible for what, when, and how much they eat. The structure of family meals — with their regular timing, curated menus, and social accountability — is abruptly replaced by a food environment designed for maximum choice and minimum oversight. The university dining hall, typically operating on a buffet or all-you-can-eat model, presents a quantity and variety of food that can be overwhelming for students who have never had to self-regulate their intake in an unstructured setting.

Research by Levitsky and colleagues at Cornell University found that the college dining environment itself — independent of individual psychological factors — promoted overeating through exposure to variety, social facilitation, and environmental cues [6]. For students who respond to this overeating with guilt and compensatory restriction, the buffet dining model can initiate a restrict-binge cycle that escalates into clinical pathology.

Simultaneously, students living off-campus or in apartments may face the opposite problem: inadequate food access. Food insecurity affects an estimated 36% of college students nationally and is independently associated with increased disordered eating, as the scarcity-abundance cycle of food insecurity mirrors the restrict-binge pattern of bulimia nervosa [7].

### The "Freshman 15" Myth and Its Real Consequences

The cultural narrative of the "Freshman 15" — the belief that students gain 15 pounds during their first year — is one of the most damaging eating disorder risk factors in the college environment, despite being empirically unsupported. A meta-analysis by Vadeboncoeur and colleagues (2015) found that average first-year weight gain is approximately 3-4 pounds — a modest change consistent with the completion of physical maturation in late adolescence [2].

But the myth is psychologically powerful precisely because it is ubiquitous. Incoming students hear about the "Freshman 15" from peers, family, media, and even orientation materials. The anxiety this generates drives preemptive dietary restriction in students who have no clinical need for it. Research by Delinsky and Wilson (2008) documented that fear of the "Freshman 15" predicted increased dietary restraint, body checking, and body dissatisfaction during the first semester — and that these behaviors, once initiated, were difficult to extinguish [1].

The irony is precise: the cultural anxiety about college weight gain produces the restrictive eating behavior that increases the risk of eating disorders. The myth does not reflect reality, but it creates it.

### Body Comparison in Shared Living

College is one of the few environments where adults live in extremely close quarters with same-age peers. Dormitory living means sharing bathrooms, seeing peers dress and undress, observing each other's eating patterns, and being continuously exposed to peer bodies in a way that most post-college adults never experience.

Social comparison theory, as applied to body image by Fitzsimmons-Craft and colleagues (2012), predicts that this proximity intensifies both upward comparison (comparing oneself to peers perceived as more attractive) and body surveillance (habitual monitoring of one's own appearance) [8]. The effects are bidirectional: students who are already prone to social comparison are more affected by close-quarters living, and close-quarters living increases the tendency toward social comparison in students who were not previously prone to it.

### Alcohol and "Drunkorexia"

College drinking culture intersects with eating disorder risk in a pattern that researchers have termed "drunkorexia" — the compensatory restriction of food intake before planned alcohol consumption. The motivations are dual: some students restrict food to enhance the intoxicating effects of alcohol, while others restrict to "save" their intake for alcohol, treating food and alcohol as interchangeable sources of energy to be budgeted.

Burke and colleagues (2010) found that approximately 30% of college students who drink engage in food restriction specifically to compensate for alcohol intake, with significantly higher rates among women and among students with elevated eating disorder symptomatology [5]. The health consequences are acute: alcohol consumed on an empty stomach is absorbed more rapidly, increasing the risk of alcohol poisoning, blackouts, and alcohol-related injury. The psychological consequences are also significant: the pattern reinforces the conceptualization of food intake as something to be minimized and compensated for.

### Academic Perfectionism and Control

Eating disorders are overrepresented among high-achieving individuals, and the college environment amplifies this connection. The student population is self-selected for achievement orientation, and the academic environment rewards perfectionism, discipline, and self-control. For students who experience academic setbacks — a poor grade, rejection from a program, difficulty adjusting to college-level expectations — the body may become a compensatory domain of control.

Bardone-Cone and colleagues' research on perfectionism and eating disorders found that "self-oriented perfectionism" (imposing unrealistically high standards on oneself) was a specific risk factor for eating pathology in college-age populations, particularly when combined with perceived failure to meet those standards [9].

## What Works: Evidence-Based Campus Prevention

### The Body Project

The Body Project, developed by Eric Stice, is the most extensively evaluated eating disorder prevention program in existence. It uses a cognitive dissonance framework: participants engage in verbal, written, and behavioral exercises that challenge the thin ideal, creating psychological dissonance between their endorsement of the ideal and their active arguments against it.

Over 50 controlled trials have demonstrated that the Body Project reduces thin-ideal internalization, body dissatisfaction, negative affect, and disordered eating — and that these effects persist at 2-3 year follow-up. A landmark trial by Stice and colleagues (2008) found that participants in the Body Project had a 60% reduction in future eating disorder onset compared to control participants [3]. The program has been implemented on hundreds of college campuses, is freely available, and can be delivered by trained peer facilitators rather than clinicians.

### Reflections: Body Image Program

The Reflections program, designed specifically for college women in sororities, addresses body image through a cognitive-behavioral framework that combines psychoeducation, cognitive restructuring, and behavioral experiments. Controlled evaluations have demonstrated significant reductions in body dissatisfaction, dietary restraint, and bulimic symptoms among sorority participants [4].

### Screening and Early Intervention

Campus counseling centers that implement routine screening for eating disorders — using validated instruments such as the SCOFF questionnaire or the Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire (EDE-Q) — identify students in the prodromal or early phase of illness who would not otherwise seek help. A study by Lipson and Sonneville (2017) found that only 20% of college students with clinically significant eating disorder symptoms had received any treatment, suggesting that the majority of campus eating disorders are undetected and untreated [10].

## Greek Life: Elevated Risk and Targeted Intervention

Research has consistently identified sorority membership as an independent risk factor for body dissatisfaction and disordered eating. Allison and Park (2004) found that sorority women had significantly higher rates of body dissatisfaction, drive for thinness, and bulimic symptomatology compared to non-sorority college women, even after controlling for pre-existing body image attitudes [4].

The mechanisms are multiple. Sorority recruitment processes often include appearance evaluation. The social environment within sororities tends to emphasize physical appearance and weight management. The close social bonds within sororities create powerful normative influence — when multiple members engage in disordered eating, the behavior is normalized and may even be encouraged.

Fraternity membership carries its own risks, though these have been less extensively studied. Emerging research suggests elevated rates of muscle dysmorphia, supplement abuse, and "drunkorexia" among fraternity men.

Targeted prevention programs for Greek organizations — including peer-led Body Project workshops integrated into new member education — have produced promising results. The key is reaching students during the high-risk period of pledging and new membership, before the social norms of the organization have been fully internalized.

## Reaching Marginalized Students

Eating disorder prevention on college campuses has historically centered the experiences of white, thin, cisgender women. This has left significant gaps.

**Students of color** experience eating disorders at comparable rates to white students but are significantly less likely to be identified, referred, or treated. Prevention programs must explicitly address the intersection of racial identity, body image, and cultural food practices — rather than assuming that all students share the same body ideals or the same relationship with food.

**LGBTQ+ students** face elevated eating disorder risk, with particularly high rates among transgender and gender-nonconforming students for whom body dissatisfaction may be interwoven with gender dysphoria. Prevention programs that fail to address these specific experiences miss a significant portion of the at-risk population.

**Student athletes** represent a high-risk group with unique vulnerability factors, including sport-specific body ideals, performance pressure, and coach or teammate commentary on weight and body composition. The NCAA has developed educational resources for athletic departments, but implementation remains inconsistent.

**First-generation college students** may experience the transition to college more intensely, with fewer resources and support systems, and may face food insecurity at higher rates — all of which elevate eating disorder risk.

Effective campus prevention recognizes that eating disorders are not a homogeneous experience and that prevention strategies must be culturally responsive, identity-affirming, and accessible to the full diversity of the student body.

> 💡 **Spark:** College does not cause eating disorders. But it creates an environment where every major risk factor — food independence, social comparison, identity transition, achievement pressure, alcohol culture, and the toxic myth of the "Freshman 15" — converges simultaneously. Prevention at this stage is not about telling students to eat normally. It is about changing the environment that makes normal eating so difficult.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are a college student:** Recognize the "Freshman 15" for what it is — a myth that drives anxiety and restriction. Your body may change during college, and that is developmentally normal, not a crisis.

- **Establish some structure around eating.** Complete food independence can be overwhelming. Aim for three meals a day at roughly regular times, even if the content varies. Structure reduces the cognitive load of constant food decisions.

- **Be deliberate about your media diet.** Unfollow social media accounts that promote restriction, transformation narratives, or body comparison. Follow accounts that model food variety and body diversity.

- **If you notice disordered eating in a friend or roommate,** say something specific: "I've noticed you're skipping meals a lot, and I'm worried about you." Then offer to help them connect with campus counseling.

- **If your campus does not have an eating disorder prevention program,** advocate for one. The Body Project is free, peer-led, and evidence-based. Any student organization can request training.
