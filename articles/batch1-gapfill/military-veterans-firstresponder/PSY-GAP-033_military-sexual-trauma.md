# Military Sexual Trauma: Prevalence, Impact, and Recovery

> **Summary:** Military sexual trauma (MST) — a term used by the Department of Veterans Affairs to describe sexual assault or repeated, threatening sexual harassment experienced during military service — is one of the most pervasive and underreported problems facing service members and veterans. VA screening data reveals that approximately 1 in 3 women and 1 in 50 men who use VA healthcare screen positive for MST, though researchers consistently note that actual rates are higher due to significant underreporting. The consequences extend far beyond the assault itself: MST survivors experience elevated rates of PTSD, depression, substance use disorders, chronic pain, and suicidality. What makes MST particularly devastating is the context — the perpetrator is often a fellow service member or superior, the institution that should provide safety becomes the site of harm, and the reporting system can itself be retraumatizing. This article examines the scope of MST, the unique barriers survivors face, what the VA offers, and what recovery actually looks like.

**Key Facts:**
- VA universal screening data indicates that approximately 33% of women and 1.6% of men who use VA healthcare report experiences consistent with MST, though actual prevalence is believed to be substantially higher [1].
- Kimerling and colleagues (2010) found that MST was associated with a nearly threefold increase in PTSD risk and more than double the risk of major depressive disorder, independent of combat exposure [2].
- The Department of Defense Annual Report on Sexual Assault in the Military (2022) estimated that 8.4% of active duty women and 1.5% of active duty men experienced unwanted sexual contact in the preceding year — but only 20% of incidents resulted in a formal report [3].
- Turchik and Wilson (2010) identified military culture, chain-of-command reporting structures, and fear of career consequences as the primary barriers to MST disclosure [4].
- Because the majority of military personnel are male, men constitute approximately 40% of all MST survivors despite lower per-capita rates — a fact that is frequently overlooked in policy and treatment [5].

## The Scope of Military Sexual Trauma

The term "military sexual trauma" was created by the VA, not the Department of Defense, and its definition is broader than many people realize. Under 38 U.S.C. 1720D, MST includes "psychological trauma, which in the judgment of a VA mental health professional, resulted from a physical assault of a sexual nature, battery of a sexual nature, or sexual harassment which occurred while the veteran was serving on active duty, active duty for training, or inactive duty training." That last category — sexual harassment — is important. MST doesn't require rape. Persistent, threatening sexual harassment that creates a hostile environment meets the definition and can produce psychological harm that is clinically indistinguishable from that caused by assault [1].

The numbers are staggering. The VA has screened millions of veterans for MST as part of its universal screening initiative, and the findings are consistent year over year: roughly 1 in 3 women and 1 in 50 men who access VA care screen positive [1]. But these figures almost certainly understate the problem. They capture only veterans who use VA healthcare (a subset of all veterans), and they rely on self-report in a clinical setting — which means they're subject to all the disclosure barriers that military culture creates.

The Department of Defense's own data tells a similar story from the active-duty side. The 2022 Annual Report on Sexual Assault estimated that 8.4% of active duty women and 1.5% of active duty men experienced unwanted sexual contact in the prior year [3]. That's not a lifetime prevalence — that's a single year. And the DoD itself acknowledges that only approximately 20% of these incidents result in formal reports, meaning four out of five assaults go unreported within the military system.

Here's a number that challenges the dominant narrative about MST: because the military is approximately 83% male, men constitute roughly 40% of all MST survivors in absolute numbers [5]. Researcher Turchik, in her 2012 analysis, emphasized that the framing of MST as primarily a women's issue — while understandable given the per-capita rates — has created a significant treatment gap for male survivors [5]. Men who experience MST face every barrier that women face, plus an additional layer of stigma rooted in gender norms about masculinity, victimhood, and sexual vulnerability. The result is that male MST survivors are even less likely to disclose, less likely to seek treatment, and more likely to present with substance use disorders and anger rather than the PTSD and depression presentations more commonly recognized in female survivors.

## Why Military Context Makes Everything Harder

Sexual trauma is devastating in any context. But the military environment creates unique conditions that amplify the harm and obstruct recovery in ways that civilian frameworks don't fully capture.

The perpetrator problem is the first and most significant factor. Harned and colleagues (2002) documented that the vast majority of MST perpetrators are fellow service members — people in the survivor's unit, chain of command, or social circle [6]. In civilian sexual assault, survivors can theoretically leave the environment where the assault occurred. In the military, you can't. You may share a barracks with your perpetrator. You may depend on them for mission-critical tasks. You may report to them. This forced proximity transforms the aftermath from a recovery challenge into an ongoing survival situation.

The chain-of-command reporting structure compounds the problem. For most of military history, the only way to report a sexual assault was through the command structure — meaning the survivor's report went to their commander, who might be friends with the perpetrator, who might prioritize unit cohesion over accountability, and who had enormous power over the survivor's career. The Military Justice Improvement Act (2023) transferred prosecution authority for sexual assault cases to independent military prosecutors, but the cultural legacy of command-controlled reporting still suppresses disclosure. Turchik and Wilson (2010) identified the primary barriers to MST reporting as: fear of not being believed, fear of career retaliation, fear of being blamed, and the perception that nothing would happen anyway [4]. Subsequent DoD surveys have confirmed that these perceptions are not unfounded — retaliation against reporters remains documented at rates that undermine institutional credibility.

The "warrior culture" barrier operates differently for men and women but is destructive for both. For women, military culture can frame sexual assault as the price of admission — an attitude captured in the phrase "What did you expect?" that survivors report hearing from peers and leaders [7]. For men, the barrier is even more fundamental: the admission that sexual victimization occurred directly contradicts the warrior identity that military culture constructs. O'Brien, Keith, and Shoemaker (2015) found that male MST survivors were significantly more likely than female survivors to experience identity-related shame — a sense that the assault meant they were weak, failed, or fundamentally unmasculine [7]. This identity shame can prevent disclosure for decades.

Betrayal trauma theory, developed by Jennifer Freyd (1996), provides a framework for understanding why MST is often more psychologically damaging than civilian sexual assault [8]. Freyd argued that when trauma is perpetrated by someone on whom the victim depends for survival — a caregiver, an institution, a system — the psychological impact is compounded by the betrayal of trust. Military service members depend on their institution and their fellow service members for literal survival. MST shatters that dependency relationship in a way that produces what Freyd calls "betrayal blindness" — a dissociative response where the mind suppresses awareness of the betrayal because fully recognizing it would be psychologically overwhelming.

Smith and colleagues (2016) documented another MST-specific phenomenon: moral injury layered on top of sexual trauma [9]. MST survivors frequently report not just the trauma of the assault itself, but moral injury from the institution's response — being disbelieved, being punished for reporting, watching their perpetrator get promoted. The assault is the first wound. The institutional betrayal is the second. And the second wound often proves harder to heal.

## The Psychological Aftermath

The mental health consequences of MST are severe, wide-ranging, and often long-lasting. Kimerling and colleagues (2010), in one of the largest studies of MST outcomes, analyzed data from over 200,000 VA patients and found that MST was associated with a 2.7-fold increase in PTSD risk and a 2.1-fold increase in major depressive disorder risk [2]. These associations held even after controlling for combat exposure, pre-military trauma, and demographic factors. MST was, statistically, a more potent predictor of PTSD than combat.

The PTSD that follows MST has some distinctive features. Compared to combat-related PTSD, MST-related PTSD tends to involve more prominent shame, self-blame, and interpersonal difficulties. Surís and Lind (2008) noted that MST-related PTSD often presents with marked distrust of others, difficulty with intimacy, and emotional numbing in relationships — features that can be misdiagnosed as personality pathology if the clinician doesn't screen for MST [10]. Avoidance behaviors in MST-related PTSD may focus on interpersonal situations, authority figures, or medical settings (particularly gynecological care for women), creating barriers to the very healthcare that could help.

Substance use disorders co-occur with MST at elevated rates. Surís and colleagues (2004) found that MST survivors were 2–3 times more likely to develop alcohol use disorder and 2.5 times more likely to develop drug use disorder compared to veterans without MST exposure [11]. For male survivors especially, alcohol and drug use often precedes mental health treatment-seeking — the substance use is the presenting problem, and the MST history emerges (if at all) only after trust is established with a provider.

Suicidality is the most alarming consequence. Kimerling and colleagues (2016) found that MST was independently associated with suicidal ideation and suicide attempts in both male and female veterans, after adjusting for PTSD, depression, and other risk factors [12]. The pathway from MST to suicidality runs through shame, self-blame, betrayal, and social isolation — each of which MST produces in abundance.

Chronic physical health problems are also consistently documented. MST survivors report higher rates of chronic pain, gastrointestinal disorders, cardiovascular problems, and reproductive health issues compared to veterans without MST exposure. The mechanisms likely involve both the direct physiological effects of stress (HPA axis dysregulation, chronic inflammation) and the behavioral pathways through which trauma disrupts health behaviors.

## VA Treatment and Recovery Pathways

The VA has made MST a stated priority, and the treatment infrastructure — while imperfect — is more developed than many survivors realize.

Every VA healthcare facility is required to have a designated MST coordinator who serves as a point of contact for survivors. MST-related treatment through the VA is available at no cost to veterans, regardless of service connection, disability rating, or whether the MST was formally reported during service. This last point is crucial: because so many MST survivors never reported the assault while serving, the VA explicitly does not require documentation of the event as a precondition for treatment.

Screening is universal. Every veteran who enrolls in VA healthcare is asked two standardized screening questions about MST experiences. The screen is brief and designed to be non-invasive, though some veterans have noted that being asked about sexual trauma in a medical intake setting can be jarring. The purpose of universal screening isn't to provide therapy in the moment — it's to identify survivors and connect them with specialized care.

The evidence-based treatments for MST-related PTSD are the same as those for other forms of PTSD, with important modifications. Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) and Prolonged Exposure (PE) are the two frontline treatments, both of which have been validated in MST populations. Surís and colleagues (2013) conducted a randomized controlled trial comparing CPT to present-centered therapy in female veterans with MST-related PTSD and found that CPT produced significantly greater reductions in PTSD and depression symptoms [13]. PE has similarly strong evidence, though some clinicians note that the exposure component requires careful pacing with MST survivors, for whom the trauma narrative involves betrayal and shame rather than (or in addition to) fear.

Newer approaches are expanding the treatment landscape. EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) has growing evidence for MST-related PTSD. Seeking Safety, an integrated treatment for PTSD and substance use, addresses the common co-occurrence directly. And trauma-sensitive yoga, while not a standalone treatment, has shown promise as an adjunctive intervention that helps survivors reconnect with their bodies — a relationship that MST profoundly disrupts.

For male survivors specifically, the VA has developed targeted programming that addresses the unique barriers men face. Male-only MST treatment groups, when available, can reduce the disclosure barrier by creating an environment where men aren't the only male survivor in the room. Individual therapy with a provider who explicitly names and normalizes male sexual victimization is essential.

Recovery from MST isn't linear, and it doesn't look the same for everyone. Some survivors find that processing the trauma directly through CPT or PE is transformative. Others need to stabilize first — address substance use, establish safety, build basic coping skills — before trauma processing is tolerable. The phased approach recommended by Judith Herman (1992) in her landmark work *Trauma and Recovery* — safety and stabilization, then remembrance and mourning, then reconnection — remains a useful framework, though the phases often overlap rather than proceeding in tidy sequence.

> 💡 **Spark:** MST is not just what happened during the assault. It's the roommate who didn't believe you, the commander who transferred you instead of the perpetrator, the system that chose reputation over your safety. Recovery means healing not just from the trauma, but from the betrayal — and it's possible.

## What You Can Try

**Know your VA benefits.** MST-related treatment through the VA is free, regardless of whether you reported the assault during service, regardless of your discharge status, and regardless of whether you have a service-connected disability rating. Contact your local VA's MST coordinator or call the VA's general information line at 1-800-827-1000 and ask to speak with someone about MST services.

**You don't have to start with trauma processing.** If the idea of talking about what happened feels overwhelming, start with stabilization. A therapist can help you develop coping skills, address sleep problems, and reduce substance use before you ever touch the trauma narrative. Phased treatment is evidence-based and respected.

**Consider who you tell first.** Disclosure is a personal decision, and you don't owe your story to anyone. When you're ready, choosing a first listener matters. A therapist, a crisis counselor (through the Veterans Crisis Line or RAINN), or a trusted friend who you believe will respond without judgment are reasonable first steps. You don't have to start with family members or partners.

**If you're a male survivor, know you're not alone.** Men constitute roughly 40% of MST survivors. The shame and identity disruption you may feel are common responses to an uncommon situation — they don't reflect who you are. VA MST services are available to all genders, and some facilities offer male-specific groups.

**Challenge self-blame actively.** MST survivors frequently carry the belief that they could have or should have prevented what happened. Cognitive Processing Therapy specifically targets these "stuck points." Even before therapy, you can begin to notice when self-blame appears and ask: Would I say this to a fellow service member who told me this happened to them?

**Connect with survivor communities.** Organizations like the Military Rape Crisis Center, Protect Our Defenders, and RAINN's military-specific resources offer information, advocacy, and connection to others who understand. You're not the first person to walk this road, and you don't have to walk it alone.
