# Families of First Responders: Secondary Trauma and Coping

> **Summary:** When a firefighter, police officer, or paramedic walks through the front door after a shift, they don't leave the job on the porch. The residue of what they've witnessed — the cardiac arrest that didn't end well, the child abuse call, the officer-involved shooting — enters the home invisibly and reshapes family life in ways that are only beginning to be understood clinically. Charles Figley coined the term "secondary traumatic stress" in 1995 to describe how close contact with a trauma survivor can produce PTSD-like symptoms in the people who love them. For first responder families, this isn't an abstract concept. It's the partner who can't sleep because they're waiting for the phone to ring, the child who's learned to read a parent's mood before speaking, and the household that runs on hypervigilance even when no one's in danger. This article examines what the research says about how first responder work impacts families and which coping strategies actually protect them.

**Key Facts:**
- Charles Figley's (1995) secondary traumatic stress framework identifies intrusion, avoidance, and arousal symptoms in family members of trauma-exposed professionals — symptoms that mirror PTSD without direct trauma exposure [1].
- A 2019 study by Landers and colleagues found that 35% of first responder spouses reported clinically significant symptoms of secondary traumatic stress, with higher rates among spouses of paramedics and police officers than firefighters [2].
- Divorce rates among law enforcement officers range from 14.5% to 40% depending on the study, with Tuttle and colleagues (2018) finding rates comparable to the general population but with significantly higher marital distress and lower satisfaction scores [3].
- Children of first responders report higher rates of anxiety and behavioral problems during periods of parental shift work, with Bochantin (2016) documenting that children as young as four develop behavioral strategies to manage a parent's occupational stress [4].
- Peer-based family support programs, such as those modeled on Kirschman's (2007) law enforcement family resilience framework, show significant improvements in family communication and stress management compared to no-intervention controls [5].

## The Invisible Transfer: How Trauma Crosses the Threshold

Charles Figley, a psychologist at Tulane University who spent decades studying trauma in military and first responder populations, proposed in 1995 that people in close, empathic relationships with trauma survivors can develop their own set of trauma symptoms — not from witnessing the event, but from absorbing its emotional aftermath [1]. He called this "secondary traumatic stress" (STS), and distinguished it from burnout (which develops gradually from cumulative occupational stress) and compassion fatigue (which he later described as the intersection of STS and burnout). The distinction matters because STS can develop rapidly — sometimes after a single incident relayed by the first responder — and its symptoms cluster around the same intrusion-avoidance-arousal triad that defines PTSD.

For first responder families, the transfer happens through multiple channels. The most obvious is narrative disclosure: the officer who comes home and describes a gruesome scene, or the paramedic who can't stop talking about the pediatric drowning. But Regehr and colleagues (2004) found that even in families where the first responder says nothing about work — the "I don't want to bring it home" strategy — the partner still picks up on behavioral shifts: irritability, emotional flatness, hyperstartle, sleep disruption, and alcohol use [6]. The trauma leaks through behavior, not just words.

Landers and colleagues (2019) conducted one of the most comprehensive assessments of STS in first responder spouses, surveying 352 partners across police, fire, and EMS. Thirty-five percent met the threshold for clinically significant STS, and the strongest predictors weren't the type of critical incidents the first responder experienced but rather the degree of emotional withdrawal the first responder exhibited at home and the spouse's own history of prior trauma [2]. In other words, it's not what happened on the call — it's what happens (or doesn't happen) after the first responder gets home.

This finding has significant clinical implications. It means that family-focused interventions don't necessarily need to process the first responder's operational trauma directly. What they need to address is the relational withdrawal — the emotional shutdown that the first responder uses as a coping mechanism but that the family experiences as abandonment.

## Hypervigilance at Home: When the Switch Doesn't Turn Off

Kevin Gilmartin, a behavioral scientist and former law enforcement officer, described the "hypervigilance biological rollercoaster" in his influential 2002 book *Emotional Survival for Law Enforcement* [7]. During a shift, an officer's sympathetic nervous system is elevated — scanning for threats, making split-second decisions, maintaining heightened awareness of their environment. This state is adaptive on the street. But when the shift ends, the nervous system crashes into a parasympathetic trough: exhaustion, withdrawal, irritability, and a desire to be left alone. The officer who was alert and decisive on patrol becomes a lump on the couch who can't engage with their partner or children.

Gilmartin's model wasn't based on pathology — it described a normal physiological cycle that becomes destructive when it repeats day after day, year after year. The partner learns that the first hour after the shift isn't a time for conversation. The children learn to be quiet. The family organizes itself around the first responder's nervous system state, and over time, a kind of role reversal occurs: the family becomes hypervigilant about the first responder's mood, scanning for cues about whether it's "safe" to engage. This mirrors the hypervigilance the first responder displays on the job, creating a household where everyone is on alert and no one fully relaxes.

The effects on the partner are significant. Karaffa and Koch (2016) found that spouses of law enforcement officers reported lower relationship satisfaction and higher psychological distress than age-matched controls, with emotional unavailability cited more frequently than danger concerns as the primary source of strain [8]. The fear that something bad will happen on the job is real — but the daily reality of living with someone who can't be emotionally present is, for many partners, the more corrosive experience.

Shift work compounds everything. Rotating schedules — particularly those involving night shifts, 48-hour on-call periods, and mandatory overtime — fragment family routines, reduce shared time, and make consistent parenting nearly impossible. Bochantin (2016) interviewed first responder families and documented how children in these households develop remarkably sophisticated strategies for managing uncertainty: checking which uniform the parent is wearing to predict how long they'll be gone, memorizing shift calendars, and evaluating the parent's tone of voice within seconds of arrival to determine the evening's emotional climate [4].

## Children in the Crossfire: Developmental Impacts

Children of first responders grow up in a specific emotional environment that shapes their development in ways that are distinct from both the general population and military families (though overlaps exist). They live with a baseline awareness that a parent might not come home — a fear that is rarely articulated but is constantly present. They witness the emotional toll of the job in their parent's mood, drinking patterns, sleep disruption, and relationship with the other parent. And they absorb the cultural messaging of first responder communities: be tough, don't complain, handle it yourself.

Aamodt and Stalnaker (2006) examined law enforcement families and found that officer's children exhibited higher rates of externalizing behaviors (aggression, defiance) during periods of high paternal work stress, with younger children showing more behavioral dysregulation and older children showing more anxiety and withdrawal [9]. Importantly, the non-deployed parent's mental health was a stronger predictor of child outcomes than the first responder's. When the partner at home is overwhelmed by their own secondary traumatic stress, their capacity to provide stable, attuned parenting diminishes — and the child absorbs the consequences.

Developmental impacts vary by age. Preschool-age children in first responder families may show separation anxiety, regression in toileting or sleep habits, and clinginess — particularly during periods when the parent is on high-risk assignment or has been involved in a critical incident. School-age children often become "parentified," taking on emotional caretaking roles in the household, monitoring the moods of both parents, and suppressing their own needs. Adolescents may respond with externalized rebellion — substance experimentation, rule-breaking — or internalized distress — depression, social withdrawal, academic decline.

The good news, documented by Shakespeare-Finch and colleagues (2014), is that many first responder children also develop notable strengths: high emotional intelligence, adaptive coping under stress, strong problem-solving skills, and a deep sense of community belonging [10]. These aren't inevitable — they emerge most reliably in families where the first responder is emotionally available (even imperfectly), where the other parent has adequate support, and where the family culture allows emotional expression rather than suppressing it.

## What Protects First Responder Families

Ellen Kirschman, a clinical psychologist who spent 30 years working with law enforcement families, developed one of the first comprehensive models of first responder family resilience [5]. Her framework emphasizes four protective factors: informed expectations (understanding the normal emotional cycle of the job before it becomes a crisis), shared coping rituals (regular, predictable activities that reconnect the family), emotional literacy (the ability to name and discuss feelings without pathologizing them), and a peer support network that includes other first responder families.

The peer component is critical. First responder families often feel isolated from civilian friends who can't understand the daily reality of the job. Statements like "I could never be married to a cop" or "How do you handle the worry?" — however well-intentioned — reinforce the sense of being on the outside. Connection with other families who get it — who know the shift schedule, the holiday absences, the post-critical-incident silence — provides a normalizing function that formal therapy often can't replicate.

Clinically, the most effective interventions for first responder families are those that address both the first responder and the partner simultaneously. Couple-based approaches — particularly those informed by Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) — have shown strong results in first responder populations. Johnson and colleagues (2013) found that EFT adapted for first responder couples produced significant improvements in relationship satisfaction and reductions in both the first responder's PTSD symptoms and the partner's secondary traumatic stress [11]. The mechanism is relational: by creating structured opportunities for emotional engagement between partners, EFT interrupts the withdrawal-pursuit cycle that Gilmartin's hypervigilance model predicts.

Department-level programs also matter. Agencies that offer family orientation programs (explaining the hypervigilance cycle, normalizing the partner's experience, and providing resources), regular check-ins with a family support officer, and peer family mentorship programs report higher retention and lower divorce rates than agencies that treat the officer as an isolated unit.

What doesn't work: telling the partner to "be supportive" without acknowledging that support is a two-way street. The partner didn't sign up for emotional caretaking without reciprocity. They signed up for a relationship with a person who happens to have a demanding job — and they deserve the same emotional attentiveness they're expected to provide.

> 💡 **Spark:** The strongest first responder families aren't the ones that never feel the weight of the job. They're the ones that carry it together — where the officer learns to say "That call affected me" and the partner learns to say "Your distance affects me" and both are heard.

## What You Can Try

**Name the hypervigilance cycle.** If you're a first responder, explain Gilmartin's rollercoaster to your partner — not as an excuse, but as a shared map. When your partner understands that your post-shift withdrawal is physiological, not personal, it changes the meaning of the behavior. Agree together on a re-entry ritual: 30 minutes of quiet transition, then deliberate reconnection.

**Check in with the non-first responder partner.** If you're the partner at home, your mental health matters independently. Consider screening for secondary traumatic stress through your own therapist or through resources at FirstRespondersChildren.org. Your stress isn't derivative — it's real, and it deserves its own attention.

**Don't shield children from all information.** Age-appropriate honesty — "Dad had a hard day at work and needs some quiet time before dinner" — gives children an explanation for what they're already observing. Silence forces them to fill in the gaps with their imagination, which is almost always worse.

**Connect with other first responder families.** Organizations like the First Responder Support Network, Code Green Campaign, and local department family auxiliaries provide community that understands without requiring explanation.

**Consider couple therapy with a first responder-informed clinician.** The International Critical Incident Stress Foundation (ICISF) maintains a directory of clinicians trained in first responder family dynamics. Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) has the strongest evidence base for the withdrawal pattern common in these relationships.

**For children showing distress,** school-based counselors and community mental health centers with first responder experience can provide developmentally appropriate support. Play therapy works well for younger children; adolescents often respond better to individual talk therapy with someone outside the family system.
