# Military Children: Deployment, Relocation, and Resilience

> **Summary:** Approximately 1.7 million American children have at least one parent serving in the military, and the average military family moves six to nine times during a service member's career. These children don't just watch their parents go to war — they live inside the architecture of war: the packing boxes, the new schools, the months of wondering whether Mom or Dad is coming home. Research from the RAND Corporation and the Department of Defense shows that while deployment and relocation carry measurable psychological costs — elevated anxiety, behavioral problems, academic disruption — the story isn't one of inevitable damage. Many military children develop remarkable adaptive capacities. The difference between thriving and struggling often comes down to one factor: the functioning of the parent who stays behind. This article examines the developmental impacts of military life by age group, the weight carried by non-deployed caregivers, and the protective factors that help military children not just survive but grow.

**Key Facts:**
- The RAND Corporation's 2019 study of military families found that children with a deployed parent were 11% more likely to use outpatient mental health services and 18% more likely to receive a behavioral health diagnosis than children of non-deployed service members [1].
- A meta-analysis by Card and colleagues (2011) examining 16,500 military children found that parental deployment was associated with small but significant increases in externalizing behavior problems, internalizing symptoms, and poorer peer relationships [2].
- Military children relocate an average of six to nine times between kindergarten and high school graduation, compared to the civilian average of one to two moves [3].
- Flake and colleagues (2009) found that 32% of children ages 3–8 with a deployed parent met clinical threshold for psychosocial problems, and the strongest predictor of child distress wasn't deployment itself but the mental health of the non-deployed caregiver [4].
- The Military Child Education Coalition reports that approximately 80% of military children attend public schools, where staff rarely receive training on the specific challenges these students face [5].

## Growing Up on the Move: What Relocation Does to a Developing Mind

Before a single deployment order is issued, military children already carry a distinctive psychological burden: the relocation cycle. The average military family moves every two to three years. By the time a military child graduates high school, they've attended five to nine different schools. Each move means dismantling a social world — friendships, teachers, sports teams, church groups, the particular geography of home — and rebuilding it from scratch in a place where everyone else already belongs.

Patricia Lester, a psychiatrist at UCLA who directs the FOCUS (Families OverComing Under Stress) program, has spent two decades studying these transitions. Her team's research shows that the impact of relocation varies dramatically by developmental stage [6]. For preschool-age children (3–5), moves disrupt the attachment security that depends on environmental consistency. Young children can't conceptualize why their world keeps changing; they register it as instability, and they respond with regression — bedwetting, clinginess, sleep disruption. They don't have the language to say "I'm afraid everything will keep disappearing," so their bodies say it for them.

School-age children (6–12) bear the social cost most acutely. Between ages 8 and 12, peer relationships shift from activity-based ("we play together") to identity-based ("we belong together"). A military child entering a new school mid-year arrives as a social outsider during precisely the developmental period when belonging matters most. Weber and Weber (2005), in a study of over 600 military adolescents, found that children who had moved three or more times scored lower on measures of social connectedness and school belonging than children who had moved once or not at all [7]. The deficits weren't academic — military children generally perform at or above grade level — but social. They knew how to do the work. They didn't know how to break into the lunch table.

Adolescents (13–18) face a more complex calculus. They're developing identity, romantic relationships, and the early stages of independence, all of which depend on continuity. A move during sophomore year means losing not just friends but the entire context within which the teenager was becoming a person. Some adolescents respond with accelerated maturity — becoming socially adaptable, culturally fluent, capable of reading a new environment quickly. Others withdraw, refusing to invest in relationships they know will be temporary. This self-protective detachment can calcify into a lifelong pattern of emotional guardedness.

But here's where the research complicates the simple damage narrative. The RAND Corporation's 2019 assessment found that while relocation was associated with short-term adjustment difficulties, military children who had been through multiple moves actually showed higher levels of cultural adaptability, independence, and tolerance for ambiguity than their civilian peers after stabilization [1]. The moves cost something real. They also built something real. The challenge for families and clinicians is ensuring the costs don't outweigh the gains.

## Deployment: The Fear That Doesn't Have a Name

Relocation is disruptive. Deployment is existential. When a parent deploys to a combat zone, a child absorbs a fear that most civilian children never encounter: the real possibility that their parent will die. This isn't an abstract anxiety. Military children watch the news. They hear conversations between adults that stop abruptly when they enter the room. They know what the knock on the door means — the chaplain and the casualty notification officer in dress uniform — even if no one has explained it to them.

The deployment cycle has three psychologically distinct phases, each with its own stressors. The pre-deployment phase (weeks to months before departure) is characterized by anticipatory grief, increased family conflict, and what researchers call "emotional cocooning" — the paradoxical tendency for families to pull away from each other before separation, as if practicing for the absence [6]. Children may become irritable, defiant, or clingy during this period, behaviors that often get misread as behavioral problems rather than grief responses.

The deployment phase itself — which for post-9/11 families has averaged 12 to 15 months per deployment, with some families enduring four or five cycles — produces the most sustained psychological strain. Card and colleagues' (2011) meta-analysis, which synthesized data from 16,500 military children across 23 studies, found that during active deployment, children showed significant increases in both internalizing problems (anxiety, depression, withdrawal) and externalizing problems (aggression, defiance, hyperactivity) [2]. The effect sizes were modest — this isn't a population uniformly falling apart — but they were consistent across studies, age groups, and military branches.

Age again modulates the impact. Gorman, Eide, and Hisle-Gorman (2010), using TRICARE medical records for over 640,000 military children, found that children ages 3–8 with a deployed parent had an 11% increase in outpatient mental health visits and a 19% increase in behavioral health diagnoses compared to age-matched children without a deployed parent [8]. Adolescents showed different patterns: higher rates of substance use initiation, earlier sexual behavior, and more disciplinary problems at school. These aren't arbitrary correlations. When the primary authority figure in a household is gone for a year, behavioral boundaries shift.

The reintegration phase — the homecoming — is often the most psychologically complex and the least discussed. The returning parent has been changed by their experience. The non-deployed parent has developed new routines and new authority within the household. The children have adapted to a family structure that no longer includes the deployed parent as a daily presence. When that parent returns, everyone must renegotiate their role simultaneously. Knobloch and Theiss (2012) documented that relational turbulence — conflict, uncertainty, and interference with established routines — actually peaks during the reintegration period, not during deployment itself [9]. The homecoming parade ends. The hard work of reconnection begins.

## The Non-Deployed Parent: The Fulcrum of Everything

If there's a single finding that emerges consistently from military family research, it's this: the best predictor of how a child handles deployment isn't the deployment itself. It's the mental health and functioning of the parent who stays behind.

Flake and colleagues (2009) studied 116 families with children ages 3–8 during wartime deployment and found that 32% of children met clinical threshold for psychosocial difficulties [4]. But when the researchers modeled predictors, the child's distress was more strongly associated with the non-deployed parent's depressive and anxiety symptoms than with any deployment-specific variable — including deployment length, number of deployments, or combat exposure. The non-deployed parent is the emotional filter through which the child experiences everything.

This makes intuitive sense, but the implications are heavy. The non-deployed parent — overwhelmingly mothers, though the number of stay-behind fathers is growing — takes on the full weight of the household during deployment: parenting, finances, home maintenance, medical appointments, school conferences, emotional regulation of frightened children, and management of their own fear and loneliness. They do this without their partner, often without extended family nearby (because the military moved them away from their support networks), and within a military culture that valorizes stoicism and discourages visible struggle.

Lester and colleagues (2010) found that non-deployed caregivers reported rates of depression and anxiety two to three times higher than civilian population norms, with the highest rates among spouses of service members who had been deployed more than once [6]. Yet only a fraction sought treatment. The barriers are structural (inconsistent access to mental health care near installations, long wait times), cultural (fear of being seen as unable to cope, concern that a mental health diagnosis might affect the service member's career), and logistical (who watches the children while a single-functioning parent attends therapy?).

This creates a cruel cycle. The parent most responsible for buffering children against deployment stress is also the parent most likely to be overwhelmed, undertreated, and unsupported. Programs that target child outcomes without addressing caregiver mental health are treating symptoms while ignoring the mechanism.

## What Builds Resilience: Protective Factors That Actually Work

The resilience of military children isn't accidental, and it isn't automatic. Decades of research have identified specific, modifiable factors that tilt outcomes toward adaptation rather than distress.

**Caregiver functioning is the primary lever.** The FOCUS program, developed by Lester and colleagues at UCLA and now implemented across all military branches, is built on this principle. FOCUS provides family-centered resilience training that teaches emotion regulation, communication skills, and shared narrative construction — helping families create coherent stories about deployment that include the hard parts [6]. Randomized trials have shown that FOCUS participation reduces anxiety and depression in both children and non-deployed caregivers, with effects persisting at 12-month follow-up. The program works because it addresses the fulcrum: when the at-home parent functions better, the children function better.

**School-based support makes a measurable difference.** Military children who attend schools with designated military liaison staff, peer mentoring programs for new students, and teacher training on deployment-related behavior show better academic and social adjustment than those in schools without these supports [5]. The Military Child Education Coalition's "Student 2 Student" program, which pairs incoming military students with trained peer mentors, has demonstrated improvements in school connectedness and reduced transition anxiety across 600 participating schools.

**Deployment communication buffers distress.** Children who maintain regular, predictable communication with deployed parents — video calls, letters, recorded bedtime stories — show fewer behavioral symptoms than children with inconsistent contact [8]. The key word is predictable. A weekly video call at a set time gives the child something to hold on to. Unpredictable contact — a call that might come or might not, at any hour — can actually increase anxiety because the child exists in a state of constant anticipation.

**Community connection protects against isolation.** Military children who live on or near installations and participate in youth programs with other military children report higher levels of belonging and peer understanding. There's a specific comfort in being around kids who don't need the experience explained to them — who understand what it means when Dad's deployment gets extended by three months, because their mom's just got extended too.

**Open, age-appropriate family communication about deployment is protective.** Children who are told the truth in language they can handle — "Dad is going to be away for a long time, and his job can be dangerous, and it's okay to feel scared about that" — fare better than children who are either shielded from information or exposed to adult-level fear without processing support [4]. The goal isn't to eliminate the child's anxiety. It's to ensure they don't carry it alone.

> 💡 **Spark:** Military children don't choose this life — and most of them can't articulate what it costs them. But the adaptability they're forced to develop isn't a consolation prize. When supported by a functioning caregiver and a community that understands, it becomes a genuine strength. The kid who's attended seven schools by age 14 knows something about human connection that most adults never learn: how to build it from nothing, again and again.

## What You Can Try

**If you're a non-deployed parent feeling overwhelmed,** your mental health is the single most important factor in your child's adjustment. Military OneSource (1-800-342-9647) provides free, confidential counseling — up to 12 sessions — that doesn't appear in military records. The FOCUS program offers family resilience training at most major installations. Using these isn't a sign of weakness. It's the most protective thing you can do for your children.

**If your child is showing behavioral changes during deployment,** resist the urge to discipline the behavior without addressing the emotion underneath it. A child who's acting out may be communicating fear, grief, or anger they can't articulate. Name the feeling for them: "I wonder if you're feeling angry because you miss Dad." This doesn't excuse the behavior, but it gives the child something to work with besides acting out.

**If you're a teacher or school counselor with military students,** ask at enrollment whether a parent is currently deployed. Train yourself to recognize that the "new kid" who seems disengaged or defiant may be a child in their sixth school who has stopped investing in relationships because they've learned everything is temporary. Connection, not correction, is what they need first.

**If you're a returning service member,** expect reintegration to be harder than you imagined, and don't take it personally if your children seem distant or resistant. They adapted to your absence — that's healthy. Now they need time to adapt to your presence again. Family counseling during the reintegration period isn't a sign that something went wrong. It's recognition that something complicated is happening.

**If you're a clinician working with military families,** screen for caregiver mental health first. A child's symptoms often improve when the non-deployed parent gets support. Programs like FOCUS, MFLC (Military and Family Life Counseling), and installation-based family support centers are referral pathways that don't carry the stigma of a formal mental health diagnosis.
