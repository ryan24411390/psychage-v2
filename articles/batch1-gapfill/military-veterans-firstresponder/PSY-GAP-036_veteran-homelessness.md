# Veteran Homelessness: Mental Health Pathways and Interventions

> **Summary:** On any given night in the United States, roughly 35,000 veterans sleep outside, in shelters, or in places not designed for human habitation. They are disproportionately male, disproportionately Black, and disproportionately affected by PTSD, traumatic brain injury, and substance use disorders. But veteran homelessness isn't a story about individual failure. It's a story about systems — military, medical, and social — that were supposed to catch people and didn't. This article traces the mental health pathways that lead from service to the streets, evaluates the federal programs designed to reverse that trajectory, and examines what the evidence says about what actually works when a nation tries to house the people who fought for it.

**Key Facts:**
- The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) 2023 Annual Homeless Assessment Report counted approximately 35,574 veterans experiencing homelessness on a single night, a 7.4% increase from 2022 [1].
- A landmark study by Tsai and Rosenheck (2015) found that 53% of homeless veterans met criteria for a current mental health disorder and 75% had a lifetime substance use disorder [2].
- The HUD-VA Supportive Housing (HUD-VASH) program has housed over 110,000 veterans since 2008 through the combination of Housing Choice vouchers and VA case management [3].
- Traumatic brain injury affects an estimated 20% of veterans who served in Iraq and Afghanistan, and those with TBI are significantly more likely to experience homelessness than those without [4].
- Between 2010 and 2020, veteran homelessness declined by 55%, representing one of the largest reductions of any subpopulation experiencing homelessness in U.S. history [5].

## From Uniform to Unsheltered: The Pathways

The road from military service to homelessness is rarely a straight line. It's a cascading sequence of losses — housing, employment, relationships, health — in which untreated mental illness almost always plays a central role. Tsai and Rosenheck's (2015) analysis of over 300,000 veterans who used VA homeless services found that more than half met criteria for a psychiatric disorder at the time of assessment, with PTSD, major depression, and psychotic disorders appearing most frequently [2]. Substance use, particularly alcohol dependence and polysubstance use, was present in three-quarters of lifetime histories.

But these clinical realities don't exist in a vacuum. Consider a common trajectory: A combat veteran returns from deployment with undiagnosed PTSD. Sleep disturbance, irritability, and emotional numbing erode the marriage. Alcohol becomes the only reliable way to quiet the hyperarousal at night. The marriage ends. Without a partner's income, housing becomes precarious. The veteran loses the job because concentration and anger are unmanageable. And then — sometimes within months — the person who was trusted with a nation's defense is sleeping in a car.

Traumatic brain injury accelerates every stage of this cascade. Brenner and colleagues (2013) documented that veterans with mild TBI were 2.4 times more likely to report homelessness than those without, even after controlling for PTSD and substance use [4]. TBI compromises executive function — the ability to plan, organize, follow through on tasks, and regulate impulses — which are precisely the cognitive capacities needed to navigate complex bureaucracies, maintain employment, and manage finances. The VA's own disability system, with its voluminous paperwork and byzantine appeals process, is itself an executive function test that many TBI-affected veterans fail.

There's also the discharge factor. Veterans who receive other-than-honorable discharges — often for behavior that was itself a symptom of untreated PTSD or TBI, such as substance use infractions or disciplinary problems — have historically been locked out of VA healthcare entirely. This creates a cruel paradox: the veterans most damaged by their service are the ones most likely to be denied the system built to help them. Legislative reforms have expanded eligibility in recent years, but gaps remain, and many affected veterans don't know their rights have changed [6].

Race compounds the structural vulnerabilities. Black veterans are overrepresented among homeless veterans relative to their share of the veteran population. A 2018 analysis by Metraux and colleagues found that Black veterans were 2.1 times more likely to experience homelessness than White veterans, a disparity that persisted even after controlling for income, disability, and military service characteristics [7]. The intersection of racial discrimination in housing markets, lower household wealth, and concentrated neighborhood disadvantage means that the margin of error between "housed" and "homeless" is thinner for Black veterans from the start.

## What the VA Built: HUD-VASH, SSVF, and GPD

The federal response to veteran homelessness has been, by the standards of American social policy, unusually ambitious. Three programs form the backbone of the effort, each targeting a different segment of the homeless veteran population.

**HUD-VASH** (HUD-VA Supportive Housing) pairs a Housing Choice voucher — which subsidizes rent in the private market — with ongoing VA case management that includes mental health treatment, substance use counseling, and employment support. Since its expansion in 2008, HUD-VASH has distributed over 110,000 vouchers and is credited as the primary driver of the 55% reduction in veteran homelessness between 2010 and 2020 [3]. A randomized controlled trial by Rosenheck and colleagues (2003) demonstrated that the combination of housing voucher plus case management produced significantly better housing outcomes at 36 months than case management alone or standard VA care [8]. The housing-first model underlying HUD-VASH — which provides housing without requiring sobriety or treatment compliance as a precondition — was a philosophically radical departure from earlier approaches that treated housing as a reward for recovery rather than its foundation.

**SSVF** (Supportive Services for Veteran Families) targets a different population: veterans who are at imminent risk of losing housing or who have recently become homeless. SSVF provides rapid re-housing assistance, temporary financial payments for rent and utilities, and connections to VA and community services. It's the prevention arm of the strategy — intervening at the point where the cascade toward chronic homelessness might still be interrupted.

**GPD** (Grant and Per Diem) funds community-based organizations that operate transitional housing programs for veterans. Residents typically stay for up to 24 months and receive structured support including job training, mental health treatment, and life skills development. GPD serves veterans who need more intensive support than SSVF provides but aren't yet ready for independent living through HUD-VASH.

The combined effect of these programs has been measurable. Montgomery and colleagues (2021) analyzed national VA data and found that veterans who received HUD-VASH vouchers spent 86% of follow-up days housed, compared to 52% for those receiving standard VA homeless services [5]. The numbers are real. But they're not complete.

## Where the System Still Fails

Despite the progress, the 2023 Point-in-Time count's 7.4% increase in veteran homelessness was a sobering reminder that structural problems persist [1]. Several fault lines in the system deserve scrutiny.

First, the voucher supply doesn't match the need. HUD-VASH vouchers are distributed based on geographic formulas that don't always align with where homeless veterans actually are. In high-cost housing markets like Los Angeles, San Francisco, and New York City — cities with the largest concentrations of homeless veterans — the fair market rent calculations that determine voucher values often fall below what landlords will accept. A veteran with a voucher in hand may still face months of fruitless apartment searching because no landlord will rent at the allowed rate. O'Connell and colleagues (2020) found that in the 15 highest-cost metro areas, the average time from voucher issuance to housing placement was 178 days, compared to 62 days in lower-cost markets [9].

Second, women veterans remain underserved. Women are the fastest-growing segment of the veteran population, and they experience homelessness through pathways that the current system — designed around the profile of the typical homeless veteran circa 2005 (male, Vietnam-era, substance-dependent) — doesn't always address. Military sexual trauma (MST) is a primary driver of homelessness among women veterans, yet many GPD programs remain male-only or lack trauma-informed programming specific to MST survivors. Gamache and colleagues (2003) documented that women veterans experiencing homelessness report rates of MST above 40%, compared to approximately 23% among women veterans overall [10].

Third, the rural-urban divide is real. VA homeless services are concentrated in urban areas where VA medical centers are located. A veteran in rural Appalachia or the Northern Plains, hours from the nearest VA facility, may have no practical access to HUD-VASH case management, SSVF rapid re-housing, or any of the specialized services that have driven the urban reductions.

And fourth, the numbers themselves may be misleading. Point-in-Time counts, conducted on a single January night, systematically undercount people who are unsheltered in places that outreach teams can't access — including rural areas, vehicles, and encampments in remote locations. The actual number of veterans experiencing homelessness in a given year is estimated to be three to four times the single-night count.

## Rethinking the Approach: What Actually Works

The evidence points toward several principles that distinguish effective interventions from well-intentioned ones that don't move the needle.

**Housing First works — but only with adequate support.** The randomized trial data from Rosenheck and colleagues (2003) and subsequent replication studies consistently show that providing housing without preconditions produces better outcomes than treatment-first models [8]. But "housing first" doesn't mean "housing only." Veterans placed in housing without adequate psychiatric, substance use, and case management support show higher rates of return to homelessness. The magic of HUD-VASH isn't the voucher alone — it's the coupling of the voucher with VA clinical services.

**Peer support makes a measurable difference.** Programs that employ formerly homeless veterans as peer specialists report higher engagement rates and better retention. There's something that happens when a person who once slept under a bridge is the one helping another veteran navigate the housing application — a credibility that no clinician, however skilled, can replicate.

**Prevention is cheaper and more effective than rescue.** SSVF's rapid re-housing model costs a fraction of what HUD-VASH costs per veteran served, and it prevents the trauma and health deterioration associated with actual homelessness. For every dollar invested in preventing a veteran from becoming homeless, multiple dollars are saved in emergency room visits, shelter stays, and incarceration costs that chronic homelessness generates.

**Discharge upgrade advocacy must be part of the mental health toolkit.** Clinicians working with homeless veterans should be trained to identify cases where an other-than-honorable discharge may be attributable to service-connected mental health conditions, and to connect those veterans with legal services that can pursue discharge upgrades. Access to VA care is sometimes one successful appeal away.

The 55% reduction in veteran homelessness between 2010 and 2020 proves something that skeptics doubted: a focused national effort, backed by evidence-based programs and sustained funding, can dramatically reduce homelessness among a defined population. The question now is whether the political will exists to finish the job — or whether the recent increases signal the beginning of a retreat.

> 💡 **Spark:** A veteran's path to homelessness usually isn't a single catastrophic event. It's a series of systems that didn't hold — and recovery depends on building systems that do. Housing isn't the final step in a veteran's recovery. It's the first.

## What You Can Try

**If you're a veteran at risk of losing housing,** contact the National Call Center for Homeless Veterans at 1-877-4AID-VET (1-877-424-3838). This line operates 24/7 and can connect you with SSVF rapid re-housing assistance, HUD-VASH referrals, and local resources. You don't need to be currently homeless to call.

**If your discharge status is blocking VA access,** contact your local VA medical center and ask about the "Other Than Honorable" healthcare eligibility expansion. Since 2017, many behavioral health services have been available regardless of discharge character. Organizations like Swords to Plowshares and the National Veterans Legal Services Program offer free discharge upgrade assistance.

**If you're a clinician or case manager,** screen every veteran client for housing instability, not just those who present as homeless. The question "Have you been worried about losing your housing in the past three months?" catches people in the SSVF-preventable window before they fall through.

**If you're a community member who wants to help,** volunteer with or donate to local Stand Down events — one- to three-day events where homeless veterans can access VA services, housing assistance, employment help, legal aid, and basic necessities in a single location. The National Coalition for Homeless Veterans (nchv.org) maintains a directory of events and organizations by state.

**If you're a family member watching a veteran struggle,** know that you can call the same VA homeless hotline to discuss options and get guidance, even if the veteran isn't ready to engage directly.
