# Secular Spirituality: Connection Without Religion

> **Summary:** Approximately 30% of American adults now identify as "spiritual but not religious" or as having no religious affiliation, according to Pew Research Center's 2023 survey — yet many of these individuals report deep experiences of awe, connectedness, and meaning that traditional psychology has historically categorized as "religious." Secular spirituality refers to the pursuit of transcendence, wonder, and interconnection through non-religious frameworks: nature immersion, philosophical contemplation, meditation, artistic expression, moral commitment, and human relationships. This isn't a lesser version of religious experience. Neuroscience research by Andrew Newberg (2001) and Dacher Keltner (2023) demonstrates that awe and self-transcendence activate the same neural circuits regardless of whether the context is sacred or secular. This article explores the psychology of secular spirituality, examines the evidence for its mental health benefits, and offers practical pathways for cultivating profound meaning without any religious framework.

**Key Facts:**
- Pew Research Center (2023) found that 30% of U.S. adults identify as religiously unaffiliated ("nones"), yet 56% of them describe themselves as spiritual in some way [1].
- Dacher Keltner's research at UC Berkeley (2023) demonstrated that experiences of awe — whether triggered by nature, music, or human kindness — reduce inflammatory cytokines and increase prosocial behavior [2].
- David Yaden and colleagues (2017) found that self-transcendent experiences (STEs) occur across religious and non-religious populations at comparable rates and produce similar psychological benefits [3].
- Lisa Miller's neuroimaging research (2019) at Columbia University showed that spiritual engagement — defined broadly as a sense of connection to something larger — was associated with cortical thickening in brain regions linked to resilience against depression [4].

## Defining Spirituality Beyond Religion

The first challenge in discussing secular spirituality is definitional. For centuries, Western culture treated "spiritual" and "religious" as synonyms. If you had profound experiences of transcendence, meaning, or connection to something larger than yourself, the assumption was that you were having a religious experience — even if you didn't frame it that way. William James, in his landmark *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902), defined the spiritual broadly as "the feelings, acts, and experiences of individual men in their solitude, so far as they apprehend themselves to stand in relation to whatever they may consider the divine" [5]. His definition was deliberately inclusive — "whatever they may consider the divine" left room for non-theistic interpretations.

Contemporary psychology has moved further in this direction. Kenneth Pargament (2007), one of the leading researchers on the psychology of religion and spirituality, defines spirituality as "a search for the sacred," where "sacred" can refer to God, nature, humanity, truth, or any object imbued with extraordinary significance [6]. This definition is useful because it decouples spirituality from institutional religion while preserving its core feature: the pursuit of something that transcends ordinary experience.

For people who don't resonate with the word "sacred," philosopher Andre Comte-Sponville (2006) offers an alternative framework in *The Little Book of Atheist Spirituality*. He argues that spirituality consists of three experiences available to anyone: the experience of mystery (acknowledging what we don't and can't know), the experience of fidelity (commitment to values that matter), and the experience of communion (felt connection to others and to the whole of existence) [7]. No deity required. No scripture consulted.

This isn't about declaring one path superior to another. Religious traditions have sustained billions of people across millennia, and the psychological benefits of religious participation — community, ritual, moral framework, existential comfort — are well-documented. The point is that these benefits aren't exclusive to religion. They arise from specific psychological mechanisms — awe, belonging, meaning, self-transcendence — that can be activated through multiple pathways. The question for secular spiritual seekers isn't "How do I replace religion?" but "How do I access these same fundamental human experiences through the frameworks that feel authentic to me?"

## The Neuroscience of Awe and Self-Transcendence

If secular spirituality seems like a philosophical abstraction, the neuroscience of awe makes it concrete. Dacher Keltner, a psychology professor at UC Berkeley, spent over two decades studying awe — the emotion we feel in the presence of vast things that challenge our existing mental frameworks. His 2023 book *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder, and How It Can Transform Your Life* synthesizes this research and presents a striking finding: awe experiences reduce activity in the default mode network (DMN), the brain's self-referential processing system [2]. When the DMN quiets, the sense of a separate, bounded self temporarily dissolves. People report feeling connected to something larger — nature, humanity, the universe. This is, functionally, what mystics across every tradition have described. And it happens reliably in secular contexts.

Keltner's research showed that nature walks, encounters with moral beauty (witnessing extraordinary courage or compassion), listening to certain music, and contemplating vast ideas all trigger awe responses. The physiological effects are measurable: decreased inflammation (reduced IL-6 cytokine levels), increased vagal tone, and enhanced prosocial behavior. In one study, participants who spent 60 seconds looking up at tall eucalyptus trees subsequently picked up more pens "accidentally" dropped by a researcher than those who looked at a building of comparable height [2]. Awe literally makes people more helpful.

Andrew Newberg, a neurotheologist at Thomas Jefferson University, used SPECT imaging to study the brains of meditating Buddhist monks and praying Franciscan nuns. He found strikingly similar patterns of neural activation: decreased parietal lobe activity (which processes the boundary between self and world) and increased frontal lobe activity (associated with focused attention) [8]. The subjective experiences were described differently — the nuns reported feeling close to God, the monks reported experiencing boundless awareness — but the brain didn't seem to care about the theological framing. The mechanism was the same.

David Yaden and colleagues (2017) at the University of Pennsylvania coined the term "self-transcendent experiences" (STEs) to provide a secular umbrella for these phenomena. Their research found that STEs — moments of decreased self-salience and increased connectedness — occur in approximately 30-40% of the general population, with no significant difference between religious and non-religious individuals in frequency or intensity [3]. The triggers differ (a religious person might experience transcendence during prayer; a secular person might experience it watching a sunset or holding a newborn), but the phenomenology and psychological benefits are comparable.

## Meaning Without Metaphysics: Philosophical Frameworks

One common concern among people leaving religious frameworks is that life without God becomes meaningless. The existential philosophers addressed this head-on — and their answers, while demanding, are remarkably empowering.

Albert Camus (1942) argued in *The Myth of Sisyphus* that the universe is fundamentally indifferent, and that meaning isn't discovered but created through passionate engagement with life [9]. His famous conclusion — "One must imagine Sisyphus happy" — isn't nihilism. It's the radical claim that the human capacity to commit fully to an endeavor, even without cosmic guarantee, is itself the source of meaning. You don't need the rock to reach the top of the hill. You need to push it as though it matters — and in the pushing, it does.

Viktor Frankl (1946), a Holocaust survivor and psychiatrist, made a complementary argument: meaning comes from three sources — creative work, loving relationships, and the attitude we take toward unavoidable suffering [10]. None of these require religious belief. A painter finds meaning in the painting. A parent finds meaning in the child's laugh. A person facing terminal illness finds meaning in how they face it. Frankl's logotherapy has been empirically validated across clinical populations and remains one of the most widely practiced meaning-centered approaches.

More recently, philosopher Susan Wolf (2010) proposed what she calls the "fitting fulfillment" view: meaning arises when subjective passion meets objective value [11]. Activities are meaningful when they engage us deeply and when they connect to something genuinely worthwhile — beauty, truth, justice, knowledge, care for others. Wolf's framework avoids both the trap of pure subjectivism ("anything that makes me feel good is meaningful") and the trap of pure objectivism ("meaning must come from God or cosmic order"). It says: pursue what draws you and choose things that matter beyond yourself.

These frameworks validate what many secular people intuitively feel: that meaning doesn't require metaphysical foundations. It requires engagement, commitment, love, creativity, and honest confrontation with the human condition. These are available to anyone — religious or not.

## Practices for Secular Spiritual Life

The psychological literature suggests several evidence-based practices that cultivate the experiences historically associated with spirituality — awe, transcendence, interconnection, and deep meaning — without requiring religious belief or participation.

Meditation has the strongest evidence base. Jon Kabat-Zinn's Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) program, developed at the University of Massachusetts Medical Center in 1979, deliberately stripped meditation of its Buddhist religious context and reframed it as an attention-training practice [12]. Decades of research confirm its effectiveness: a meta-analysis by Khoury and colleagues (2013) across 209 studies found moderate to large effects on anxiety, depression, and stress [13]. Secular meditation apps and community groups have made the practice accessible to millions who would never set foot in a temple.

Nature immersion is another powerful pathway. The Japanese practice of shinrin-yoku (forest bathing), studied extensively by Qing Li (2010) at Nippon Medical School, produces measurable reductions in cortisol and blood pressure after just 20 minutes of walking in forested environments [14]. Beyond the physiological, nature exposure reliably triggers awe — one of the core spiritual emotions — particularly when the natural environment is vast, complex, or sublime.

Contemplative artistic engagement — deep listening to music, visiting museums with intentional slowness, reading poetry or philosophy — activates the same neural circuits involved in spiritual experience. Pelowski and colleagues (2017) found that profound aesthetic experiences (what they termed "aesthetic awe") produced decreased DMN activity and increased reports of self-transcendence comparable to meditation [15].

Community and ritual also matter. Religious traditions provide built-in community and regular ritual participation, which are protective factors for mental health. Secular people can cultivate equivalents: philosophy discussion groups, community choirs, volunteer organizations, regular dinners with close friends, or secular Sunday assemblies (a movement that began in London in 2013 and has since spread globally). The psychological benefit comes from repeated, shared, meaningful activity — not from the specific content of the activity.

Finally, moral commitment — sustained engagement with causes larger than oneself — provides the sense of purpose that many associate with religious calling. Research by Adam Grant (2013) at Wharton showed that people who see their work as contributing to others' well-being report significantly higher meaning and engagement [16]. You don't need a divine mandate to dedicate yourself to justice, knowledge, healing, or art. The dedication itself generates the experience of calling.

> 💡 **Spark:** Transcendence isn't the property of any belief system. It's a human capacity — as natural as hunger, as available as the sky above you. You don't have to believe anything in particular to be moved by everything that is.

## What You Can Try

**Schedule a weekly awe walk.** Choose a natural setting — a park, waterfront, hillside, or even a tree-lined street — and walk slowly for 20 minutes with the explicit intention of noticing what's vast, beautiful, or surprising. Keltner's research shows that regular awe walks produce cumulative benefits for mood and social connection [2].

**Start a secular contemplative practice.** Download a meditation app (Insight Timer offers non-religious guided meditations) or simply sit quietly for 10 minutes each morning, focusing on your breath. You're training attention, not invoking anything supernatural. Start with five minutes if ten feels daunting.

**Read one existential philosopher.** Try Camus's *The Myth of Sisyphus*, Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning*, or Comte-Sponville's *The Little Book of Atheist Spirituality*. These aren't textbooks — they're invitations to think seriously about meaning without religious assumptions.

**Create or join a meaning-focused community.** This could be a philosophy reading group, a volunteer organization, a community garden, or a monthly gathering with friends centered on honest conversation about what matters. The ritual and regularity are as important as the content.

**Identify your three sources of meaning.** Using Frankl's framework, write down: (1) what you create or contribute, (2) who you love and are loved by, and (3) an attitude or value you hold in the face of difficulty. These three anchors don't require religious grounding — they require honesty.

**Engage deeply with art.** Visit a museum without your phone. Listen to a full album with headphones and closed eyes. Read a poem three times. These aren't leisure activities — they're portals to the same transcendent experiences that religious ritual provides.
