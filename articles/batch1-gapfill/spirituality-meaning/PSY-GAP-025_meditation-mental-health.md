> **Summary:** Meditation has accumulated a substantial evidence base over the past three decades, with meta-analyses showing moderate benefits for anxiety, depression, and pain. But the picture isn't universally positive. Willoughby Britton's research at Brown University has documented that roughly 8% of meditators experience serious adverse effects — including dissociation, panic, depersonalization, and, in rare cases, psychotic episodes. These aren't fringe anecdotes; they're clinically documented outcomes that occur more frequently in people with trauma histories, psychotic spectrum conditions, or dissociative tendencies. This article examines what the best evidence actually tells us about meditation's benefits, where the hype outpaces the data, who's most vulnerable to harm, and how to practice with both eyes open.

**Key Facts:**
- Goyal et al.'s landmark 2014 meta-analysis in *JAMA Internal Medicine*, reviewing 47 trials with 3,515 participants, found meditation produced moderate effect sizes for anxiety (0.38), depression (0.30), and pain (0.33) — comparable to antidepressants for mild-to-moderate depression [1].
- Willoughby Britton's 2017 study in *PLOS ONE* found that 25.4% of regular meditators reported "undesirable experiences" and approximately 8% experienced effects severe enough to warrant clinical attention, including psychosis and dissociation [4].
- A 2020 systematic review by Farias et al. in *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica* identified 55 studies documenting meditation-related adverse events, with anxiety (33%), dissociation (27%), and perceptual disturbances (25%) being the most common [5].
- Hölzel et al.'s 2011 MRI study demonstrated measurable increases in gray matter density in the hippocampus and decreases in amygdala density after just eight weeks of mindfulness training [3].
- The American Psychological Association's 2019 clinical practice guideline conditionally recommends mindfulness-based interventions for depression relapse prevention but notes insufficient evidence to recommend meditation as a first-line treatment for most conditions [8].

## What the Evidence Actually Supports

The scientific study of meditation began in earnest in the 1970s, but it wasn't until the 2000s that the research reached sufficient volume for meaningful meta-analyses. And the meta-analyses tell a more measured story than the popular press tends to convey.

The most rigorous synthesis to date remains Madhav Goyal and colleagues' 2014 meta-analysis published in *JAMA Internal Medicine* [1]. Goyal's team reviewed 18,753 citations, narrowed them to 47 randomized controlled trials involving 3,515 participants, and applied stringent methodological criteria. Their findings: meditation programs showed moderate evidence of improving anxiety (effect size 0.38), depression (0.30), and pain (0.33). For context, those effect sizes are roughly comparable to what you'd see with antidepressant medication for mild-to-moderate symptoms. That's meaningful. But Goyal was equally clear about what the data didn't support: there was low or insufficient evidence that meditation improved stress, positive mood, attention, substance use, eating habits, sleep, or weight. The gap between what meditation can do and what it's popularly claimed to do is considerable.

Jon Kabat-Zinn's Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) program, developed at the University of Massachusetts Medical Center in 1979, remains the most studied meditation protocol. Khoury and colleagues published a 2013 meta-analysis of 209 MBSR and related mindfulness studies and found moderate-to-large effects for reducing anxiety, depression, and psychological distress in clinical populations [2]. But Khoury also flagged significant heterogeneity across studies and noted that when only methodologically rigorous trials were included, effect sizes shrank. The strongest evidence was for depression relapse prevention — Zindel Segal's Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) has been shown to reduce relapse rates by roughly 43% compared to usual care in people with three or more prior depressive episodes [2].

The neuroscience has added biological detail. Britta Hölzel and colleagues at Harvard published a 2011 study using structural MRI that found measurable changes in brain morphology after just eight weeks of MBSR [3]. Participants showed increased gray matter concentration in the left hippocampus (involved in learning and memory), the posterior cingulate cortex (involved in self-referential processing), the temporoparietal junction (involved in perspective-taking), and the cerebellum. They also showed decreased gray matter density in the right amygdala — the brain's primary threat detection structure. These changes correlated with participants' self-reported stress reduction. The brain, it appeared, was physically reorganizing itself in response to sustained attentional practice.

These are real findings. Meditation isn't snake oil. But the leap from "eight weeks of structured mindfulness training reduces anxiety with a moderate effect size" to "meditation is a cure for mental illness" is enormous — and the wellness industry makes that leap daily.

## The Risks Nobody Talks About

In 2017, Willoughby Britton, a clinical psychologist and neuroscientist at Brown University, published a study that made large parts of the meditation world deeply uncomfortable. Britton and her colleagues conducted in-depth interviews with 60 meditation practitioners from multiple traditions — Theravada, Zen, Tibetan — and found that 25.4% reported at least one "meditation-related adverse experience" that was distressing, functionally impairing, or both [4]. About 8% described effects serious enough to require clinical intervention. These weren't beginners who'd misunderstood the instructions. Many were experienced meditators, some with decades of practice.

The taxonomy of adverse experiences was broad and clinically significant. Participants reported anxiety and panic (sometimes during meditation, sometimes hours afterward). Dissociation and depersonalization — the feeling of being detached from one's body or watching oneself from outside. Emotional volatility, including floods of unprocessed grief or rage. Perceptual disturbances: visual and auditory phenomena, distortions of body image, altered sense of time. And in a smaller but clinically alarming subset, psychotic symptoms: paranoid ideation, grandiosity, loss of contact with consensual reality.

Britton's work didn't emerge from nowhere. Contemplative traditions themselves have long recognized these phenomena. Theravada Buddhism describes "dukkha nanas" — stages of insight characterized by terror, misery, and dissolution of the sense of self. Zen teachers speak of "makyo" — hallucinations and disturbing mental states that arise during intensive practice. The problem isn't that these experiences are unknown to meditation traditions. The problem is that secular mindfulness, which stripped meditation of its traditional frameworks and safety structures, also stripped away the language and mentorship for recognizing and managing these states when they arise.

Miguel Farias and colleagues published a systematic review in *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica* in 2020 that brought these concerns to a wider clinical audience [5]. Reviewing 55 studies that documented meditation-related adverse events, they found anxiety (reported in 33% of cases), dissociation (27%), and perceptual disturbances (25%) were the most common categories. Psychotic episodes appeared in approximately 2.7% of documented cases. Farias was blunt in his conclusions: the meditation research field had a systematic bias toward reporting benefits and underreporting harms, partly because most studies simply didn't ask about adverse events.

Ruth Baer, a clinical psychologist at the University of Kentucky and one of the developers of the Five Facet Mindfulness Questionnaire, published a 2019 paper in *Clinical Psychology Review* examining who is most vulnerable [6]. People with active psychotic disorders, dissociative disorders, PTSD with significant dissociative features, or histories of psychosis appear to be at elevated risk. Baer also noted that certain meditation styles — particularly those involving prolonged concentration, body scanning in trauma survivors, or open awareness practices that reduce cognitive filtering — carry higher risk profiles than others.

This isn't anti-meditation. It's anti-recklessness. The same pharmacological research that documents a drug's benefits also documents its contraindications. Meditation deserves — and its practitioners deserve — the same rigor.

## Why the Hype Outpaces the Evidence

Understanding why meditation's risks have been so systematically underreported requires looking at the incentives. The mindfulness industry was valued at approximately $1.2 billion in 2022, with meditation apps alone generating over $200 million in annual revenue [7]. Corporate mindfulness programs have become a $1 billion market. None of these entities have a financial incentive to publicize adverse effects.

The research incentives align similarly. Nicholas Van Dam and colleagues published a critical perspectives paper in *Perspectives on Psychological Science* in 2018 that identified pervasive methodological problems in the meditation literature [7]. These included inconsistent definitions of "mindfulness" across studies (some used it to mean a formal practice, others a personality trait, others a therapeutic protocol), small sample sizes, inadequate control conditions (comparing meditation to a waitlist rather than an active control tells you almost nothing about meditation specifically), and significant publication bias toward positive results.

Van Dam's team also pointed out an uncomfortable cultural dynamic: meditation carries a halo effect that other interventions don't enjoy. When a pharmaceutical company publishes only favorable results, scientists and journalists call it bias. When the meditation research field does the same thing, it tends to get a pass — partly because meditation is coded as "natural" and "ancient," and partly because many researchers are themselves practitioners with personal investment in the practice they're studying.

The American Psychological Association addressed some of these concerns in its 2019 clinical practice guideline [8]. The APA conditionally recommended mindfulness-based interventions for depression relapse prevention, noting moderate confidence in the evidence. But for anxiety, chronic pain, and other conditions where meditation is widely promoted, the APA found the evidence insufficient to issue a recommendation — not because meditation doesn't work, but because the existing studies weren't rigorous enough to say with confidence how well it works, for whom, and under what conditions.

## Practicing With Both Eyes Open

If you've meditated for years without problems, nothing in this article should alarm you. The evidence supports continuing a practice that works. But if you're starting meditation, or recommending it to someone else, or using it in a clinical context, the full picture matters.

Willoughby Britton's team at Brown developed a screening tool called the Meditation Safety Toolbox, designed to help clinicians and meditation teachers identify practitioners at elevated risk for adverse effects [4]. Key risk factors include a history of psychotic episodes, active PTSD with dissociative features, a dissociative disorder diagnosis, and current acute suicidality. Britton doesn't argue these individuals can never meditate — she argues they need modified practices, closer supervision, and clinicians who know what to watch for.

Psychiatrist and meditation teacher Judson Brewer at Brown has written about the importance of matching meditation technique to clinical presentation [9]. Concentration-heavy practices (like single-pointed focus on the breath) may increase anxiety in people who are already hypervigilant. Body scan practices can trigger flashbacks in trauma survivors. Open awareness practices, which reduce cognitive control, may destabilize people prone to dissociation. The idea that one style of meditation fits all mental health presentations is as clinically naive as believing one psychotherapy modality works for every condition.

David Treleaven, a trauma-sensitive mindfulness researcher, published a 2018 framework specifically for adapting meditation to trauma survivors [10]. His core principle is straightforward: any practice that increases interoceptive awareness (awareness of internal body sensations) can reactivate traumatic material stored in the body. Trauma-sensitive modifications include keeping eyes open, using external anchors (sounds, visual objects) instead of body-focused attention, shortening practice periods, and always giving practitioners permission to stop.

> 💡 **Spark:** Meditation isn't inherently healing or inherently harmful. It's a set of attentional techniques that amplify whatever is already present in the mind. For many people, that amplification is therapeutic. For some, it surfaces material they aren't equipped to process alone. Knowing which category you're in isn't weakness — it's wisdom.

## What You Can Try

**Start short and structured.** If you're new to meditation, begin with guided sessions of 5–10 minutes rather than jumping into extended silent practice. MBSR-style programs, typically eight weeks with trained instructors, have the strongest evidence base. Avoid intensive retreats (multiple hours daily, multi-day silent formats) until you have a stable foundation and know how your mind responds.

**Monitor for adverse signals.** During or after meditation, watch for: persistent anxiety that doesn't resolve within an hour, feelings of unreality or detachment from your body, intrusive disturbing imagery, emotional flooding (uncontrollable crying, rage, or terror), or any perceptual disturbances. These don't mean you've "done it wrong." They mean your nervous system is signaling something that deserves clinical attention. Stop the practice and consult a mental health professional familiar with meditation-related experiences.

**Choose your technique deliberately.** If you have a trauma history, consider starting with external-focus practices (listening meditation, walking meditation, or visual focus) rather than body-scan or breath-focused techniques. If you're prone to anxiety, movement-based practices (yoga, tai chi, walking meditation) may be better entry points than still, silent sitting.

**Keep a brief practice log.** Note what you did, for how long, and how you felt afterward. This creates a personal evidence base that helps you identify which techniques help and which destabilize. It also gives you something concrete to share with a therapist or teacher if questions arise.

**Separate meditation from medical treatment.** Meditation can complement professional mental health treatment. It's not a replacement for it. If you're managing depression, anxiety, PTSD, or any other clinical condition, meditation should be one component of a broader treatment plan supervised by a qualified clinician — not the entire plan.
