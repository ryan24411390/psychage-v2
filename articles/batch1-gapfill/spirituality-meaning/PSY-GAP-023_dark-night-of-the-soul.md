> **Summary:** The phrase "dark night of the soul" has been so overused that it's lost its precision. Originally coined by the 16th-century Spanish mystic St. John of the Cross, it described a specific stage in spiritual development — a period of profound emptiness, disorientation, and the collapse of meaning that, paradoxically, precedes deeper insight. Modern transpersonal psychology has revived the concept with clinical rigor, distinguishing genuine spiritual crises from psychiatric conditions that require immediate medical intervention. Not every existential collapse is a dark night, and not every dark night resolves on its own. Some people experiencing this need a therapist; some need a spiritual director; many need both. This article examines what the dark night actually entails, how clinicians can tell the difference between spiritual emergence and psychiatric emergency, and what the evidence says about navigating this territory.

### Key Facts

- **St. John of the Cross (1542-1591) described the "dark night" not as a metaphor for depression** but as a specific contemplative stage in which the soul's attachments to consolation — including spiritual consolation — are stripped away to make room for a deeper, less ego-centered relationship with the divine [1].
- **Stanislav and Christina Grof coined the term "spiritual emergency" in 1989** to describe intense spiritual experiences that overwhelm normal coping mechanisms but carry transformative potential when properly supported [2].
- **David Lukoff's category of "Religious or Spiritual Problem" (V62.89) was added to the DSM-IV in 1994**, providing clinicians with a diagnostic code that acknowledges spiritual distress without pathologizing it [3].
- **A 2017 study by Johnson and Friedman found that 39% of individuals experiencing intense spiritual crises were initially misdiagnosed** with psychotic disorders, bipolar disorder, or schizophrenia — receiving treatments that in some cases worsened their condition [4].
- **Research by Vieten and colleagues (2013) found that fewer than 25% of clinical psychology training programs** include any coursework on spiritual or religious issues in clinical practice [5].

## The Original Dark Night: What St. John of the Cross Actually Meant

The modern use of "dark night of the soul" bears about as much resemblance to its source material as a fortune cookie bears to the Tao Te Ching. When people say they're going through a "dark night," they usually mean they're having a terrible time. But St. John of the Cross, the Carmelite friar who wrote the poem and commentary that gave us the phrase, was describing something far more specific — and, in his framework, far more purposeful [1].

Writing in 1578 from a prison cell in Toledo where he'd been confined by his own religious order, John described two distinct dark nights. The first, the "dark night of the senses," involves the withdrawal of all sensory and emotional consolation from one's spiritual practice. Prayer feels empty. Worship feels mechanical. The joy that once accompanied religious devotion evaporates, replaced by aridity and restlessness. The second, more severe phase — the "dark night of the spirit" — goes deeper. Here, even intellectual certainty about God dissolves. The person experiences what John called "nada" — nothingness — a radical stripping of every framework through which they'd previously understood the divine.

What made John's analysis revolutionary wasn't the description of suffering — mystics had been writing about spiritual anguish for centuries. It was his insistence that this dissolution was purposeful, even necessary. In John's framework, the ego's attachments extend beyond material pleasure into spiritual pleasure. We become attached not just to comfort and status but to our image of ourselves as spiritual people, to the consolations we receive from religious practice, to our certainty about the nature of God. The dark night strips all of it. What remains, according to John, is a relationship with the divine that no longer depends on the ego's mediation — direct, unfiltered, and fundamentally transformed [1].

Modern contemplative scholars like Gerald May, a psychiatrist who spent his later career at the Shalem Institute for Spiritual Formation, have drawn compelling parallels between John's description and the psychological process of ego dissolution that occurs in mature contemplative practice [6]. May's 2004 book *The Dark Night of the Soul* argues that John was describing a psychospiritual process with recognizable stages, predictable emotional features, and — crucially — a trajectory toward integration rather than disintegration.

## Spiritual Emergency vs. Psychiatric Emergency: The Critical Distinction

In 1989, Stanislav Grof, a Czech-born psychiatrist who'd spent decades studying non-ordinary states of consciousness, and his wife Christina published *Spiritual Emergency: When Personal Transformation Becomes a Crisis* [2]. The Grofs introduced a distinction that remains clinically essential: the difference between "spiritual emergence" (a gradual unfolding of spiritual awareness that enriches functioning) and "spiritual emergency" (an overwhelming eruption of spiritual experience that disrupts the ability to function).

The Grofs identified several categories of spiritual emergency: the kundalini awakening, the shamanic crisis, the peak experience that won't resolve, the psychic opening, past-life experiences, possession states, and — directly relevant here — the dark night of the soul. What united these categories was a recognition that the experiences, while genuinely distressing and sometimes dangerous, were not inherently pathological. They were, in the Grofs' framework, natural but overwhelming stages of psychospiritual development that required support rather than suppression.

This distinction matters enormously because the wrong clinical response can cause real harm. Johnson and Friedman's 2017 study examined 74 individuals who self-identified as having experienced a spiritual emergency [4]. Of these, 39% had been diagnosed with a psychotic disorder and treated with antipsychotic medications. While some of these diagnoses may have been appropriate, the researchers found that in many cases, the "psychotic" content was culturally congruent spiritual material — visions, voices, or experiences of unity that are recognized as valid within the person's spiritual tradition. Antipsychotics, in these cases, didn't just suppress symptoms — they aborted a developmental process that, with proper support, might have resolved into growth.

But — and this is equally important — not everything that looks like a spiritual crisis is one. Genuine psychiatric emergencies can present with spiritual content. A person in a manic episode may believe they're having a mystical experience. A person experiencing psychosis may hear voices they interpret as divine communication. A person dissociating after trauma may experience depersonalization that mimics spiritual ego dissolution. Missing a genuine psychiatric emergency because you've framed it as a dark night can be as dangerous as pathologizing a genuine spiritual crisis because you've framed it as psychosis.

David Lukoff, the clinical psychologist who successfully lobbied for the inclusion of "Religious or Spiritual Problem" (V62.89) in the DSM-IV, developed a set of criteria for differentiating spiritual crisis from psychotic disorder [3]. Key indicators of a spiritual crisis (rather than a psychiatric one) include: good pre-episode functioning, the absence of thought disorder (the person can still communicate coherently), an acute onset often triggered by a recognizable spiritual practice or life event, a positive and exploratory attitude toward the experience (even when it's frightening), and the capacity for self-care and reality testing (the person knows they're having an unusual experience).

Red flags suggesting a psychiatric emergency include: deteriorating self-care, inability to maintain reality testing, command hallucinations (voices telling the person to harm themselves or others), grandiosity that escalates rather than resolves, and pre-existing history of psychotic episodes [3]. When in doubt, the responsible approach is to ensure psychiatric safety first and explore spiritual dimensions after acute risk has been addressed.

## The Psychology of Spiritual Crisis: What the Research Shows

The empirical research on spiritual crises, while still developing, reveals several consistent patterns. A 2021 longitudinal study by Greyson and Liester tracked 128 individuals who experienced what they identified as spiritual emergencies [7]. At the 6-month follow-up, 61% reported that the crisis had resulted in significant positive change — greater psychological resilience, increased empathy, reduced materialism, and a more nuanced and less dogmatic relationship with spirituality. However, 22% reported ongoing distress, and 17% reported no significant change in either direction.

The factor that most reliably predicted positive outcomes wasn't the intensity of the experience or the person's prior spiritual practice — it was the quality of support they received during the crisis [7]. Individuals who had access to therapists or spiritual directors who understood the territory and could hold space for the experience without pathologizing it or minimizing it fared significantly better than those whose support systems either panicked or dismissed them.

Vieten and colleagues at the Institute of Noetic Sciences published sobering findings about the preparedness of mental health professionals to navigate this territory [5]. Their 2013 survey of clinical psychology training programs found that fewer than 25% included any formal coursework on spiritual or religious issues. This creates a catch-22: the people most likely to seek help during a spiritual crisis — therapy clients — are least likely to encounter clinicians who can distinguish spiritual emergence from pathology.

Lisa Miller's neuroimaging research at Columbia University adds a biological dimension [8]. Her 2014 study found that individuals with high scores on a measure of spiritual experience showed significantly thicker cortical tissue in precisely the brain regions that thin during depression — the precuneus, the posterior cingulate, and the inferior parietal lobule. This doesn't prove that spiritual crisis leads to cortical thickening, but it suggests that the neural correlates of spirituality and depression are anatomically related, operating in the same brain networks but in opposite directions.

Roberto Assagioli, the Italian psychiatrist who founded psychosynthesis, described spiritual crises as occurring when the "superconscious" breaks through into ordinary awareness faster than the personality can integrate it [9]. His framework remains clinically useful because it normalizes the experience while maintaining clear criteria for when professional intervention is needed: if the person is functioning, processing, and moving through the experience — support the process. If functioning is deteriorating, coherence is fragmenting, or safety is at risk — intervene.

## Navigating the Dark Night: Clinical and Spiritual Guidance

For people currently in what feels like a dark night — the collapse of meaning, the absence of the spiritual consolation they once relied on, the terrifying emptiness — the most honest thing to say is that there's no shortcut through it. But there are ways to move through it more safely.

The Spiritual Emergence Network, originally founded by the Grofs and now operating through various successor organizations, maintains directories of therapists and spiritual directors who specialize in supporting people through spiritual crises [2]. These practitioners bridge the gap between clinical psychology and contemplative wisdom — they can assess for psychiatric risk while also honoring the potentially transformative nature of the experience.

Transpersonal psychotherapy, which explicitly integrates spiritual experience into its clinical framework, has shown particular promise. A 2020 review by Hartelius and colleagues found that transpersonal approaches produced outcomes comparable to CBT for anxiety and depression while additionally improving measures of spiritual well-being, meaning in life, and post-traumatic growth [10]. The advantage of these approaches isn't that they're "better" than standard therapies — it's that they can work with the spiritual content rather than around it.

For those whose dark night occurs within a specific religious tradition, working with a qualified spiritual director who is also psychologically informed can be invaluable. The best spiritual directors don't pathologize the experience or rush to reassure. They've read the contemplative literature, they recognize the dark night as a known stage, and they can offer the specific thing the person most needs: companionship in the darkness without the pressure to perform recovery.

What the dark night is not, however, is an excuse to avoid seeking help for genuine psychiatric symptoms. If you're experiencing suicidal ideation, self-harm impulses, inability to eat or care for yourself, or hallucinations that command you to act — that isn't a spiritual crisis. That's a medical emergency, and it requires immediate professional intervention. The dark night and clinical depression can co-occur, and treating the depression doesn't invalidate the spiritual dimension. They're not competing explanations — they can both be true simultaneously.

> 💡 **Spark:** The dark night doesn't destroy meaning — it destroys your previous container for meaning, so a larger one can form. The emptiness isn't the end of the story. It's the space where the next chapter gets written.

## What You Can Try

**If you're experiencing what feels like a spiritual crisis, these steps can help you navigate it more safely:**

- **Get a differential assessment.** Before interpreting your experience as purely spiritual, rule out medical and psychiatric causes. See a physician to check thyroid function, medication side effects, and neurological issues. See a mental health professional to assess for depression, anxiety, or psychotic spectrum conditions. This isn't about reducing your experience to brain chemistry — it's about ensuring safety.

- **Find a practitioner who speaks both languages.** You need someone who doesn't dismiss the spiritual dimension as delusion and doesn't dismiss the psychological dimension as lacking faith. The Spiritual Directors International directory (sdicompanions.org) and the Association for Transpersonal Psychology can help you find practitioners who bridge these worlds.

- **Maintain basic structure.** During spiritual crisis, the temptation to withdraw from all routine can be strong. Resist it enough to maintain sleep hygiene, regular meals, gentle movement, and at least one consistent human connection. Structure doesn't prevent transformation — it provides the container for it.

- **Reduce spiritual intensity temporarily.** If intense meditation, prayer, or contemplative practice precipitated or is amplifying the crisis, scale back. This isn't spiritual failure — it's responsible self-regulation. You wouldn't run a marathon on a broken ankle.

- **Read accounts from people who've been through it.** Gerald May's *The Dark Night of the Soul*, Thomas Moore's *Dark Nights of the Soul*, and Mirabai Starr's translations of St. John of the Cross can all provide the specific comfort of recognition — the realization that others have traveled this territory and emerged transformed.
