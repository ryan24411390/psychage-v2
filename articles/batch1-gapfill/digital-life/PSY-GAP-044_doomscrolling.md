> **Summary:** You know you should stop scrolling. The news is making you feel worse, not better. And yet your thumb keeps moving, pulled by a force that feels almost gravitational. Doomscrolling — the compulsive consumption of negative news and social media content — isn't a character flaw. It's a predictable collision between ancient neural wiring and modern information technology. This article dissects the neuropsychological mechanisms that make doomscrolling so persistent: negativity bias, uncertainty monitoring, dopamine-driven seeking behavior, and the illusion of preparedness. We examine the surge of research that emerged during the COVID-19 pandemic and offer practical, non-preachy strategies for people who want to stay informed without staying miserable.

## Key Facts

- **Negativity bias causes the brain to allocate more processing resources** to threatening information than to positive information, a trait that served survival but now fuels compulsive news consumption [1].
- **Doomscrolling increased by an estimated 60-70%** during the first year of the COVID-19 pandemic, with downstream effects on anxiety, depression, and sleep quality [4].
- **The dopamine system drives "seeking" behavior**, not pleasure — meaning the neurochemical reward of scrolling comes from the search itself, not from finding anything satisfying [3].
- **Uncertainty intolerance is the strongest psychological predictor** of compulsive news checking, more predictive than trait anxiety or neuroticism alone [5].
- **As little as 14 minutes of negative news exposure** produced measurable increases in anxiety and sadness in a BBC/University of Essex experimental study [2].

## Negativity Bias: The Ancient Wiring

Your brain is not a neutral information processor. It's a threat detection system that happens to also appreciate sunsets.

Negativity bias — the tendency to give more weight to negative information than to positive or neutral information — is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology. Roy Baumeister and colleagues documented this in their landmark 2001 review paper, titled simply "Bad Is Stronger Than Good" [1]. Across domains — learning, memory, impression formation, decision-making — negative stimuli consistently command more attention, are processed more thoroughly, and are remembered more vividly than equivalent positive stimuli.

The evolutionary logic is clear. An ancestor who ignored the rustling in the bushes because the berries tasted great didn't become an ancestor for long. Our brains evolved to prioritize threat, and that prioritization didn't disappear when the threats became headlines instead of predators.

A 2019 study by Marc Trussler and Stuart Soroka at McGill University demonstrated negativity bias in news consumption directly [2]. When given free choice among news stories, participants consistently selected negative stories over positive ones — even when they reported a preference for good news and even when they described the negative stories as less enjoyable. Eye-tracking data confirmed that participants spent longer fixating on negative headlines and returned to them more frequently. The bias operated below conscious awareness.

This is the foundational layer of doomscrolling. You don't choose to fixate on the worst headlines. Your attentional system does it for you, allocating cognitive resources to threatening content with the same automatic efficiency it once allocated to the sound of snapping twigs. The problem is that a news feed contains unlimited "twigs." Your threat detection system was designed for a world with finite dangers. It has no off-switch for infinite ones.

A controlled experiment by Wendy Johnston and Graham Davey at the University of Sussex exposed participants to just 14 minutes of negative television news [2]. Compared to a control group that watched neutral content, the negative news group showed significantly elevated anxiety, sadness, and — critically — a tendency to catastrophize about *their own personal problems*, not just the problems depicted in the news. Negative news doesn't just make you worried about the world. It recalibrates your entire threat assessment system upward.

## The Dopamine Trap: Seeking, Not Finding

Here's a persistent misconception about doomscrolling: that people do it because they enjoy it. They don't. The neuropsychological mechanism is more subtle and more frustrating than simple pleasure-seeking.

Jaak Panksepp, a neuroscientist at Washington State University, spent decades studying what he called the SEEKING system — a dopaminergic circuit that drives exploratory behavior, information foraging, and anticipatory motivation [3]. This is the system that makes you open the refrigerator when you're not hungry, check your phone when you haven't received a notification, and scroll through your feed looking for... something. Panksepp was careful to distinguish seeking from liking. The dopamine release in the SEEKING system is associated with the *search* for reward, not the experience of receiving it.

This distinction is crucial for understanding doomscrolling. Each scroll is a micro-prediction: maybe the next post will contain the piece of information that resolves your uncertainty, explains the crisis, or reassures you that things will be okay. The dopamine system generates a small burst of anticipatory motivation for each scroll. But the resolution never comes — because the feed is infinite and the crises are ongoing. So you keep scrolling, chasing a neurochemical carrot that's always one swipe away.

Kent Berridge at the University of Michigan has refined this model further, demonstrating that "wanting" and "liking" are mediated by distinct neural circuits [3]. You can want something intensely — compulsively, even — without liking it at all. This is the phenomenology of doomscrolling precisely: you want to keep scrolling, you can't seem to stop scrolling, and you don't enjoy a single minute of it.

The infinite scroll design pattern exploits this circuit perfectly. There's no natural endpoint — no last page, no final story. The SEEKING system, which evolved for a world where foraging eventually led to food or shelter, has no mechanism for recognizing that this particular foraging expedition will never yield its reward. So the thumb keeps moving.

## Uncertainty Monitoring: The COVID-19 Lesson

If negativity bias is the fuel and dopamine seeking is the engine, then uncertainty intolerance is the ignition key. And no event in modern history turned that key more decisively than the COVID-19 pandemic.

Between March and December 2020, doomscrolling became a global phenomenon. Bryan McLaughlin, a communications researcher at Texas Tech University, conducted one of the first large-scale studies of the behavior, surveying over 5,000 adults about their news consumption patterns during the pandemic [4]. He found that problematic news consumption — defined as compulsive, difficult to control, and associated with negative emotional consequences — increased by an estimated 60-70% compared to pre-pandemic baselines. Crucially, the strongest predictor of problematic consumption wasn't the amount of time spent online, but the individual's level of uncertainty intolerance.

Uncertainty intolerance (sometimes called intolerance of uncertainty, or IU) is a well-studied psychological construct defined as the tendency to find uncertain situations distressing and to engage in behaviors aimed at reducing uncertainty [5]. Michel Dugas at the Université du Québec en Outaouais identified IU as a transdiagnostic factor underlying generalized anxiety disorder, OCD, and health anxiety — all of which involve compulsive information-seeking as a coping strategy [5].

During the pandemic, uncertainty was ambient and inescapable. Would you get sick? Would your family be safe? Would lockdowns continue? Would the economy recover? Each of these questions drove information-seeking behavior — checking the news, refreshing case counts, scrolling through social media for answers. But the answers weren't available, because the situation was genuinely uncertain. So the checking continued, escalated, and became compulsive.

A 2022 study by Anke Görzig and colleagues at the University of West London tracked the physiological correlates of pandemic doomscrolling [6]. Participants who engaged in extended negative news consumption showed elevated evening cortisol levels, delayed sleep onset, and reduced sleep efficiency — a pattern consistent with hyperarousal. The researchers described a feedback loop: anxiety drives news consumption, news consumption elevates physiological arousal, arousal disrupts sleep, poor sleep reduces emotional regulation capacity, and reduced regulation increases the next day's anxiety. The cycle perpetuates itself.

## The Illusion of Preparedness

There's one more piece to the doomscrolling puzzle, and it's the sneakiest: the belief that staying informed is the same thing as being prepared.

Suzanne Degges-White, a counselor and researcher at Northern Illinois University, has written about what she calls the "illusion of control through information" — the cognitive distortion that consuming more news about a threat somehow reduces the threat [7]. This illusion is psychologically powerful because it contains a grain of truth. In many situations, information *does* improve outcomes. Knowing that a storm is coming helps you board up your windows. Knowing about a disease helps you get vaccinated.

But doomscrolling isn't purposeful information-gathering. It's undifferentiated consumption. And beyond a relatively low threshold of informedness — you know the basic facts, you know what actions to take — additional news consumption provides no practical benefit. It just raises your cortisol.

Robb Willer, a sociologist at Stanford University, has studied how the perception of being "well-informed" functions as a social signal [8]. In many communities, demonstrating awareness of current events carries social currency. Not knowing about the latest crisis feels risky — you might seem uninformed, uncaring, or out of touch. This social pressure creates an additional incentive to keep scrolling, one that has nothing to do with the information itself and everything to do with social belonging.

Research by Mary McNaughton-Cassill at the University of Texas at San Antonio, one of the earliest psychologists to study the mental health effects of news consumption, found that people consistently overestimate the amount of news they need to consume to remain functionally informed [9]. In her studies, participants who consumed 15 minutes of curated news daily were equally informed about current events as those who consumed two or more hours — but reported dramatically lower anxiety levels.

The distinction between being informed and being immersed matters enormously. Informed means you understand what's happening and what actions are available to you. Immersed means you're marinating in the emotional texture of events you cannot influence. One is useful. The other is doomscrolling.

> 💡 **Spark:** Doomscrolling feels like vigilance, but it's actually the opposite. True preparedness is calm, specific, and action-oriented. If you've been scrolling for twenty minutes and haven't identified a single concrete action to take, your nervous system is running a threat detection drill, not gathering intelligence. Close the app. The world will still be there tomorrow.

## What You Can Try

**Name the behavior in the moment.** When you notice you've been scrolling negative content for more than a few minutes, say (silently or aloud): "I'm doomscrolling." This engages the prefrontal cortex and interrupts the automatic SEEKING loop. It sounds almost too simple, but cognitive labeling has demonstrated efficacy in multiple studies on emotion regulation — notably Matthew Lieberman's 2007 fMRI research at UCLA showing that putting feelings into words reduces amygdala activation [10].

**Set an information budget.** Decide in advance how much news you'll consume and from which sources. Fifteen minutes of a reputable news outlet, once or twice daily, is sufficient for most people to stay informed. Put it on your calendar like any other appointment. When it's done, it's done.

**Replace the scroll with a single action.** If you're reading about a crisis and feeling helpless, identify one concrete thing you can do — donate, volunteer, call a representative, check on a friend. Then do it and close the app. Action breaks the uncertainty loop that compulsive monitoring can't.

**Create a "stopping cue."** The infinite scroll removes natural stopping points, so you need to create your own. Some people set a phone alarm. Others use a physical cue — standing up, making tea, stepping outside. The specific cue matters less than its reliability. Pair it with something mildly pleasant to make the transition easier.

**Address the underlying uncertainty intolerance.** If you find that compulsive news checking is part of a broader pattern — checking locks, seeking reassurance, needing to know outcomes before they happen — consider exploring this with a cognitive behavioral therapist. Intolerance of uncertainty is highly treatable with CBT protocols developed by Michel Dugas and colleagues, and reducing it improves doomscrolling along with other anxiety-driven behaviors [5].

**Protect your sleep window.** No news after 9 p.m. (or whatever time falls two hours before your bedtime). Evening news consumption is the most damaging to sleep architecture, and poor sleep amplifies every vulnerability that drives doomscrolling the next day. Charge your phone in another room if you need to.
