> **Summary:** The COVID-19 pandemic didn't invent telehealth therapy — it existed for decades before March 2020 — but the pandemic turned it from a niche option into a mainstream mode of care almost overnight. Now that the emergency waivers have settled and the data is in, telehealth therapy has established itself as a legitimate, effective treatment modality for a wide range of mental health conditions. But it isn't a perfect replacement for in-person care. This article covers what the evidence actually says about telehealth effectiveness, where it falls short, the privacy and licensing complexities you should know about, and practical strategies for getting the most out of virtual sessions.

**Key Facts:**

- A 2021 meta-analysis by Fernandez and colleagues in *Clinical Psychology Review* found that videoconference-delivered CBT produced equivalent outcomes to in-person CBT for depression and anxiety, with a pooled effect size difference near zero [1].
- Before the pandemic, only 1% of outpatient mental health visits in the U.S. were conducted via telehealth; by April 2020, that figure had surged past 50%, according to data from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services [2].
- The American Psychological Association's 2020 telehealth guidelines specify that clinicians must be licensed in the state where the client is physically located at the time of the session — not where the therapist is based [3].
- A 2022 study in *JAMA Network Open* reported that telehealth therapy had a no-show rate of 7.5%, compared to 16.5% for in-person appointments, suggesting that removing transportation barriers improves treatment adherence [4].
- The Psychology Interjurisdictional Compact (PSYPACT) now allows psychologists to practice across 41+ participating states and territories without obtaining separate licenses [5].

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

The question "Does telehealth therapy work?" has been answered about as definitively as clinical research allows — and the answer is yes, with important caveats.

The strongest evidence comes from cognitive-behavioral therapy delivered via video. Fernandez, Nguyen, Engel, and colleagues (2021) conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis of 53 randomized controlled trials comparing videoconference therapy to face-to-face delivery. Across conditions including major depression, generalized anxiety disorder, PTSD, and social anxiety, the outcomes were statistically equivalent [1]. This wasn't a marginal finding — the confidence intervals were tight enough to establish genuine equivalence rather than mere failure to detect a difference.

For depression specifically, the evidence is robust. A landmark randomized trial by Egede and colleagues (2015), funded by the Department of Veterans Affairs, assigned 241 veterans with major depression to either in-person or telehealth CBT. At both post-treatment and 12-month follow-up, there were no significant differences in symptom reduction, treatment satisfaction, or therapeutic alliance ratings [6]. The telehealth group actually showed slightly higher satisfaction scores, though the difference wasn't statistically significant.

The therapeutic alliance — that sense of trust, connection, and collaborative partnership between therapist and client — is often cited as the element most at risk in virtual care. But the data tells a more nuanced story. Norwood and colleagues (2018) reviewed 16 studies examining therapeutic alliance in telehealth versus in-person therapy and found that alliance ratings were comparable across modalities in 12 of the 16 studies [7]. Where differences emerged, they tended to diminish after the first few sessions as both parties adjusted to the medium.

Phone-based therapy, while less studied than video, also shows promise. Mohr and colleagues (2012) found that telephone-administered CBT produced clinically significant improvements in depression, though the effect sizes were slightly smaller than videoconference delivery [8]. Audio-only sessions may work particularly well for clients who find the visual component of video calls anxiety-provoking or for those in areas with poor internet connectivity.

## Where Telehealth Falls Short

For all its advantages, telehealth therapy has real limitations that both clinicians and clients need to take seriously.

**Severe psychiatric conditions present the greatest challenge.** Active psychosis, severe suicidal ideation with a plan, acute manic episodes, and conditions requiring close behavioral observation are difficult to manage safely through a screen. The American Psychiatric Association's 2018 best practices document acknowledges that telehealth can supplement care for these populations but shouldn't serve as the sole mode of treatment during acute episodes [9]. A therapist can't perform a thorough safety assessment when they can only see a person from the chest up.

**Crisis management is inherently more complicated.** When a client discloses active suicidality during an in-person session, the therapist can directly facilitate a safety plan, contact emergency services if needed, and maintain physical presence. In a telehealth session, you might not even know the client's precise location. The APA telehealth guidelines require clinicians to establish an emergency contact and confirm the client's physical address at the start of each session for exactly this reason [3].

**Nonverbal communication is degraded.** Research by Mehrabian (1971) — while often oversimplified — correctly identified that communication is multi-channel. Body posture, fidgeting, breathing patterns, and micro-expressions are all harder to detect on video. Experienced clinicians who work in both modalities report that they sometimes miss subtle affective shifts in virtual sessions that they'd catch in person [7].

**Technology barriers create equity concerns.** A 2021 study from the Journal of the American Medical Association found that older adults, low-income populations, rural communities, and people with limited digital literacy were significantly less likely to use video-based telehealth [10]. The shift to virtual care risks leaving behind precisely the populations who already face the greatest barriers to treatment.

**Certain therapeutic modalities don't translate well.** EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing), exposure therapy requiring in-vivo elements, somatic experiencing, and play therapy with children all lose important components in the virtual setting. Some clinicians have developed modified protocols, but these adaptations haven't been rigorously validated.

## Privacy, Licensing, and the Regulatory Landscape

The regulatory environment around telehealth therapy is still catching up to clinical practice, and the rules matter more than most people realize.

**Privacy and platform security.** Not all video platforms meet the security requirements of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA). Regular Zoom, Skype, and FaceTime are not HIPAA-compliant in their standard configurations. During the pandemic, the Department of Health and Human Services temporarily waived enforcement, allowing clinicians to use non-compliant platforms. That enforcement discretion has since narrowed. Therapists should use platforms specifically designed for telehealth — such as Doxy.me, SimplePractice Telehealth, or Zoom for Healthcare — that offer end-to-end encryption and signed Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) [3].

**Interstate licensing remains a barrier.** Under pre-pandemic rules, a therapist needed to be licensed in every state where they had clients. If you moved from New York to California mid-treatment, your New York-based therapist technically couldn't continue seeing you. The Psychology Interjurisdictional Compact (PSYPACT), which took effect in 2020, created a mechanism for licensed psychologists to practice across participating states. As of 2025, 41 states and territories have joined PSYPACT [5]. Similar compacts exist for counselors (the Counseling Compact) and social workers, though adoption is still growing.

**Insurance reimbursement.** During the public health emergency, most insurers were required to reimburse telehealth sessions at the same rate as in-person visits. Many of those mandates have expired, and reimbursement rates now vary significantly by state and insurer. A 2023 analysis by the Commonwealth Fund found that 38 states had enacted some form of telehealth payment parity law, but the specifics — whether parity applies to audio-only visits, whether it covers out-of-state providers — differ substantially [2].

## Making Telehealth Work: Practical Strategies

The effectiveness of telehealth depends not just on the clinical evidence but on how you set up your environment and engage with the process.

**Create a therapy-worthy space.** Find a private room where you won't be overheard or interrupted. Use headphones — this both protects privacy and improves audio quality. Make sure your face is well-lit (a window in front of you, not behind). Position your camera at eye level so you're making natural eye contact. These small environmental choices signal to your brain that this is therapeutic time, not just another video call.

**Minimize digital distractions.** Close email, silence your phone, and shut unnecessary browser tabs. Research on attention and multitasking by Ophir, Nass, and Wagner (2009) demonstrated that digital distractions degrade cognitive performance and emotional processing [11]. Your therapy session deserves the same undivided attention you'd give it in an office.

**Test your technology in advance.** Nothing derails a therapeutic moment like a frozen screen or dropped connection. Run a tech check before your first session and address any bandwidth issues proactively. If your internet is unreliable, discuss with your therapist whether phone sessions might be a better backup option.

**Communicate what the camera can't convey.** Because your therapist can't see your full body language, you may need to verbalize more. "I'm feeling tense right now" or "My heart is racing as I talk about this" gives your therapist information they'd normally gather through observation.

**Address the "it doesn't feel real" phenomenon.** Some people initially find telehealth less impactful because it lacks the ritual of traveling to a therapist's office. This is a genuine psychological effect, not just a preference. You can create your own pre-session ritual: a short walk, a few minutes of quiet, putting away work materials. Transitions matter.

> 💡 **Spark:** Telehealth didn't lower the bar for therapy — it widened the door. For the millions of people who live far from qualified providers, who can't take two hours off work for a one-hour appointment, who manage physical disabilities that make travel exhausting, the question was never "Is telehealth as good as in-person?" It was "Is telehealth better than no therapy at all?" And the answer to that question isn't even close.

## What You Can Try

If you're considering telehealth therapy — or trying to make your current virtual sessions more effective — here are steps you can take this week.

**Verify your therapist's credentials and platform.** Ask whether they use a HIPAA-compliant telehealth platform with a signed BAA. If they're using a standard consumer video app, that's a red flag worth raising.

**Check your insurance coverage specifics.** Call the behavioral health line on your card and ask whether telehealth sessions are covered at the same rate as in-person visits, whether audio-only sessions are covered, and whether there are any visit limits specific to telehealth.

**If you're searching for a new therapist,** consider providers licensed through PSYPACT or similar compacts, especially if you travel frequently or are considering a future relocation. Psychology Today's therapist directory and the PSYPACT provider lookup both allow you to filter for telehealth availability.

**Give it a real trial.** Research on patient satisfaction with telehealth consistently shows that comfort levels increase after the first two to three sessions [7]. If your first virtual session feels awkward, that's normal — don't write off the modality based on a single experience.

**Discuss the modality openly with your therapist.** Ask them directly: "Do you think our work would benefit from any in-person sessions?" A good clinician will give you an honest answer, and many therapists now offer hybrid models that combine virtual and in-person care based on clinical need.
