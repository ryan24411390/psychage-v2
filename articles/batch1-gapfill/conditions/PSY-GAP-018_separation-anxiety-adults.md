> **Summary:** For decades, separation anxiety was considered strictly a childhood condition — a phase kids were expected to outgrow. The DSM-5, published in 2013, changed that by removing the age-of-onset restriction and formally recognizing that separation anxiety disorder (SAD) can begin in adulthood and persist throughout life. Prevalence estimates suggest it affects 6–7% of adults, making it far more common than previously assumed. Adults with SAD experience intense, persistent anxiety about being separated from attachment figures — typically partners, parents, or children — and this fear goes beyond normal concern. They may refuse work travel, check on loved ones compulsively, or experience physical symptoms when separated. This article examines what adult SAD looks like, why it's so frequently misdiagnosed, and which treatments offer genuine relief.

## Key Facts

- Adult separation anxiety disorder affects approximately 6.6% of adults over their lifetime, based on data from the National Comorbidity Survey Replication [1].
- In 43.6% of adult cases, the onset occurs in adulthood — these aren't all childhood cases that persisted [2].
- Separation anxiety in adults is frequently misdiagnosed as generalized anxiety disorder (GAD), dependent personality disorder, or relationship dysfunction [3].
- Adults with SAD report higher rates of comorbid mood disorders, other anxiety disorders, and suicidal ideation compared to adults with GAD alone [4].
- Cognitive-behavioral therapy adapted for attachment-related anxiety produces significant symptom reduction, with some trials showing 50–70% improvement in severity scores at 12-week follow-up [5].

## The Condition That Was Hidden in Plain Sight

Adult separation anxiety disorder has an unusual history in psychiatry. It's not a recently discovered condition — clinicians observed the pattern for decades. But the diagnostic system itself obscured it. Prior to 2013, the DSM classified separation anxiety disorder as a "disorder usually first diagnosed in infancy, childhood, or adolescence," with an onset criterion requiring symptoms to appear before age 18 [2]. If you were 35 and terrified that your spouse would die in a car crash every time they left for work, if you called them six times during a two-hour meeting and felt physically ill when they didn't answer, if you hadn't spent a night away from them in three years — the DSM had no specific category for what you were experiencing.

Clinicians would typically reach for generalized anxiety disorder, dependent personality disorder, or a relational problem diagnosis. None fit precisely. GAD involves worry across multiple domains; adult SAD centers specifically on attachment figures and separation. Dependent personality disorder involves a pervasive pattern of submissive, clinging behavior motivated by a need to be taken care of; adult SAD is driven by fear of catastrophic loss, not dependency needs per se [3].

The turning point came with data from the National Comorbidity Survey Replication, a landmark epidemiological study led by Ronald Kessler at Harvard. When Kessler and colleagues (2005) assessed for separation anxiety symptoms across the full adult age range — ignoring the childhood-onset restriction — they found a lifetime prevalence of 6.6% [1]. That's higher than panic disorder (4.7%) and comparable to social anxiety disorder (7.1%). Even more striking, 43.6% of adult cases had onset in adulthood, not childhood [2]. These weren't kids who never grew out of it. These were adults developing the condition de novo, often after a significant life event: the death of a loved one, a serious illness in someone close to them, a divorce, or the birth of a child.

Researcher Cynthia Silove at the University of New South Wales had been building the case for adult SAD since the late 1990s. Her team developed the Adult Separation Anxiety Questionnaire and conducted cross-cultural validation studies showing that the construct was robust, measurable, and distinct from both GAD and personality pathology [6]. By the time the DSM-5 task force convened, the epidemiological and psychometric evidence was too strong to ignore. The age restriction was dropped, and adult separation anxiety took its rightful place in the anxiety disorders chapter.

## What Adult Separation Anxiety Looks Like

The core feature is excessive fear or anxiety about separation from attachment figures — people to whom the individual feels deeply bonded. The DSM-5-TR requires at least three of eight symptoms, persisting for six months or more in adults [7]:

Recurrent excessive distress when separation is anticipated or occurs. Persistent worry that harm will come to an attachment figure (accident, illness, death). Persistent worry that an event will lead to separation (getting lost, being kidnapped, having an accident). Reluctance or refusal to go out because of fear of separation. Excessive fear of being alone or without attachment figures. Reluctance to sleep away from home or without an attachment figure nearby. Repeated nightmares involving themes of separation. Physical symptoms (headaches, nausea, vomiting) when separation occurs or is anticipated.

In adults, this plays out in ways that are easy to mislabel. A woman who can't attend a three-day work conference because the thought of being away from her children produces chest-tightening panic. A man who calls his partner twelve times during a normal workday and spends the intervals between calls catastrophizing about car accidents or sudden cardiac events. A retired parent who moves across the country to follow an adult child, not out of desire but out of terror at the distance. A person who hasn't slept alone in a decade and who experiences insomnia, nausea, and racing thoughts on the rare occasions when their partner travels.

The interpersonal consequences are significant. Partners and family members of people with SAD often feel smothered, monitored, or responsible for managing the person's anxiety. Research by Manicavasagar and colleagues (2010) found that adults with SAD reported significantly more interpersonal conflict, greater functional impairment in work and social roles, and lower quality of life compared to adults with GAD matched for overall anxiety severity [3]. The disorder strains the very relationships the person is most afraid of losing.

Comorbidity is the rule, not the exception. The NCS-R data showed that 73.5% of adults with SAD had at least one additional psychiatric disorder [4]. Depression is the most common co-occurring condition, affecting roughly half of adults with SAD. Other anxiety disorders — particularly GAD and panic disorder — are also frequent. And critically, adults with SAD report significantly higher rates of suicidal ideation than adults with other anxiety disorders, even after controlling for comorbid depression [4]. This isn't a "mild" condition that clinicians can safely overlook.

## Why It Develops: Attachment, Adversity, and the Brain

The developmental roots of adult separation anxiety lie at the intersection of attachment theory, life experience, and neurobiology. John Bowlby's attachment framework — developed in the 1960s and '70s — provides the conceptual foundation. Bowlby proposed that humans develop internal working models of relationships based on early caregiving experiences, and that these models shape expectations about the availability and reliability of attachment figures throughout life [8].

Adults who develop SAD disproportionately report insecure attachment patterns, particularly anxious-preoccupied attachment — characterized by hypervigilance to signs of rejection or abandonment and difficulty tolerating the uncertainty inherent in any relationship. However, attachment insecurity alone doesn't cause SAD. The condition typically requires a catalytic event or period that activates the attachment system at high intensity: bereavement, medical trauma, a near-miss accident, relationship instability, or the birth of a child [6].

Neurobiological research on adult SAD is still emerging, but preliminary findings parallel what's known about other anxiety disorders. Functional neuroimaging studies have identified heightened amygdala reactivity to separation-related cues — photographs of loved ones paired with threatening scenarios — along with reduced regulatory activity in the prefrontal cortex [9]. The brain's threat-detection system is, in essence, calibrated too sensitively for separation signals, and the cognitive control system that should modulate these signals isn't operating effectively.

Genetic factors contribute as well. A twin study by Silove and colleagues found moderate heritability for separation anxiety symptoms in adults (approximately 36%), with the remaining variance attributable to non-shared environmental factors [6]. Interestingly, the genetic risk for adult SAD overlaps partially but not completely with the genetic risk for GAD, supporting the clinical distinction between the two conditions.

One pattern clinicians frequently observe: adults with SAD often have histories of childhood separation anxiety that resolved — or appeared to resolve — before reactivating in adulthood under stress. But a substantial minority (the 43.6% with adult onset) develop the condition without any identifiable childhood precursor. For these individuals, the precipitant is typically a vivid confrontation with the possibility of losing someone they love — a partner's cancer diagnosis, a child's serious accident, the sudden death of a peer. The event shatters the assumption of safety and the brain shifts into chronic protective mode.

## Treatment: Building a New Relationship with Uncertainty

Treatment for adult separation anxiety disorder is still a developing field — there are no large-scale randomized controlled trials specifically for adult SAD equivalent to what exists for panic disorder or social anxiety. However, the available evidence, combined with clinical experience, points to several effective approaches.

Cognitive-behavioral therapy adapted for separation anxiety is the first-line recommendation. Therapists working with adult SAD typically focus on three interconnected targets: catastrophic appraisals (the belief that separation inevitably leads to harm), safety behaviors (constant checking, reassurance-seeking, avoidance of separation), and distress tolerance (the capacity to sit with anxiety about a loved one's safety without acting on it) [5].

The cognitive work involves identifying and challenging specific predictions. Not vague anxiety, but concrete catastrophic thoughts: "If I don't check, my partner could have been in an accident and no one will call me." The therapist helps the client evaluate evidence, consider base rates (the actual statistical probability of the feared event), and develop more balanced alternative appraisals. Over time, the client learns to recognize the catastrophic thought as a thought — a product of their anxiety system — rather than as a reliable forecast.

The behavioral component involves graduated reduction of safety behaviors and graduated tolerance of separation. A person who currently calls their partner six times during a workday might reduce to four calls the first week, then three, then two, with the therapist helping them process the anxiety that arises at each step. The key principle, borrowed from exposure therapy, is that anxiety peaks and then subsides when the person doesn't perform the safety behavior — and each time this happens, the brain updates its threat model.

Researcher Mark Pollack at Rush University Medical Center has investigated pharmacological approaches, finding that SSRIs (particularly sertraline and paroxetine) reduce adult SAD symptom severity, with effect sizes comparable to their efficacy in GAD [10]. Combination treatment — CBT plus medication — may be optimal for severe cases, particularly those with comorbid depression. Benzodiazepines are generally avoided due to dependence risks and the importance of emotional processing for long-term improvement.

Couples therapy or family therapy can serve as a valuable adjunct. When SAD has strained a relationship — and it almost always has — involving the attachment figure in treatment helps them understand that the person's behavior stems from a diagnosable condition rather than from manipulation or excessive neediness. It also allows the couple to develop shared strategies for managing separation anxiety that respect both partners' needs.

> 💡 **Spark:** Separation anxiety in adults isn't a sign of emotional immaturity or weakness. It's a real anxiety disorder with identifiable brain mechanisms — and the same brain that learned to treat every separation as a catastrophe can learn, with the right support, that love doesn't require constant proximity to survive.

## What You Can Try

These strategies are grounded in evidence-based approaches and should complement — not replace — professional treatment.

**Track the pattern.** For one week, log every time you experience separation-related anxiety: what triggered it, what you thought would happen, what you did (called, checked, avoided), and what actually happened. This data reveals how often the catastrophic prediction is wrong — which is nearly always.

**Delay safety behaviors.** If you normally check on a loved one immediately when anxiety spikes, wait 10 minutes before acting. Use that time to observe the anxiety — notice where it sits in your body, rate its intensity from 0–10, and see whether it changes on its own. Gradually extend the delay. You're teaching your brain that the urge to check and the need to check are different things.

**Challenge the catastrophic thought.** When the thought "something terrible has happened" arrives, ask yourself: What's the actual probability? How many times have I had this thought before, and how many times was it accurate? What would a trusted friend say about this worry? This isn't about dismissing your feelings — it's about giving your rational mind a voice in the conversation.

**Practice planned separations.** With your attachment figure's knowledge and support, deliberately build brief separations into your routine. Start small — an hour at a coffee shop while they're home — and gradually increase duration and distance. Debrief afterward: Was the feared outcome confirmed or disconfirmed?

**Talk to a clinician who knows this condition.** Adult SAD is underrecognized, so you may need to specifically mention it. The Anxiety & Depression Association of America (adaa.org) can help you find a therapist experienced with anxiety disorders. If your current provider dismisses the concern, consider seeking a second opinion.
