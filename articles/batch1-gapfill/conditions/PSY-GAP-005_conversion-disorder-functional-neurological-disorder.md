> **Summary:** Conversion disorder—increasingly and more accurately called functional neurological disorder (FND)—produces neurological symptoms such as weakness, paralysis, tremor, seizure-like episodes, blindness, or speech difficulty that cannot be explained by a recognized neurological disease. These symptoms are not fabricated, exaggerated, or "all in the person's head." They are genuine neurological symptoms caused by a problem in how the brain functions rather than how it's structured. Advances in neuroimaging over the past two decades have transformed our understanding of FND from a Freudian relic into a neuroscience-grounded condition at the intersection of neurology and psychiatry. The symptoms are as real as those of multiple sclerosis or Parkinson's disease—they just arise from a different mechanism.

**Key Facts:**

1. FND is the second most common reason for outpatient neurology referral, affecting 4-12 per 100,000 people annually, according to epidemiological data from Stone et al. (2010) [1]
2. Functional neurological symptoms are diagnosed based on positive clinical signs (specific neurological examination findings) rather than simply ruling out other diseases—a critical shift in diagnostic approach over the past decade [2]
3. Functional MRI studies by Voon et al. (2010) demonstrated altered connectivity between the amygdala, motor cortex, and supplementary motor area in FND—the brain's emotional processing centers are disrupting voluntary motor control [3]
4. Approximately 70% of people with FND report significant symptom improvement with multidisciplinary treatment combining physiotherapy, psychotherapy, and neurological education, per a prospective study by Nielsen et al. (2015) [4]
5. The average diagnostic delay for FND is 5-7 years, during which patients often undergo unnecessary invasive procedures and accumulate iatrogenic harm from treatments targeting diseases they don't have [5]

## The Symptoms Are Real: Understanding Functional Neurological Disorder

The single most important sentence in this article is this: functional neurological symptoms are real. The weakness is real. The seizures are real. The blindness is real. The tremor is real. These are not symptoms someone is choosing to produce, pretending to have, or imagining. They represent a genuine disruption in how the nervous system functions—a "software problem" rather than a "hardware problem," to use an imperfect but useful analogy.

The term "conversion disorder" dates to Sigmund Freud, who theorized that unconscious psychological conflict was being "converted" into physical symptoms. While the Freudian framework has been largely abandoned, the name persists in the DSM-5-TR alongside the preferred term "functional neurological symptom disorder" [6]. The neurology community increasingly uses "functional neurological disorder" (FND), which describes what the condition is (a disorder of nervous system function) rather than implying a specific psychological mechanism that may or may not be present.

FND symptoms span the full range of neurological function. The most common presentations include:

- **Functional weakness or paralysis:** Loss of strength in one or more limbs, often one-sided, without the pattern of damage you'd see in stroke or nerve injury.
- **Functional seizures (dissociative/psychogenic non-epileptic seizures):** Episodes that resemble epileptic seizures but don't show epileptic activity on EEG. These are the most common type of FND, affecting approximately 20-30% of patients referred to epilepsy monitoring units [7].
- **Functional movement disorders:** Tremor, dystonia (abnormal postures), jerky movements, or gait abnormalities that differ in character from those caused by known neurological diseases.
- **Functional sensory symptoms:** Numbness, tingling, vision changes, or hearing loss without corresponding structural nerve damage.
- **Functional speech and swallowing disorders:** Aphonia (loss of voice), stuttering, or difficulty swallowing.

What distinguishes these symptoms diagnostically is the presence of positive clinical signs—specific examination findings that indicate functional rather than structural origin. Jon Stone and colleagues at the University of Edinburgh have catalogued these signs extensively [2]. Hoover's sign, for example, tests for functional leg weakness: when asked to lift the weak leg, the patient can't—but when asked to push down with the strong leg, the supposedly weak leg generates normal force involuntarily. This isn't evidence of faking. It's evidence that the motor pathway is intact but inaccessible to voluntary control.

This distinction—intact hardware, disrupted software—is the key to understanding FND. The nerve pathways work. The muscles work. The problem lies in the brain's ability to voluntarily access and control these pathways in the normal way.

## The Neuroscience: How Emotions Hijack Motor Control

For decades, FND occupied an uncomfortable no-man's-land between neurology ("There's nothing wrong with your brain") and psychiatry ("It must be psychological"). Neuroimaging has demolished that false dichotomy by revealing exactly what goes wrong in the brains of people with FND.

Valerie Voon, a neuroscientist at Cambridge University, published a landmark functional MRI study in 2010 showing that people with functional motor symptoms have abnormal connectivity between the amygdala (which processes emotion and threat) and motor planning areas including the supplementary motor area and the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex [3]. In simple terms: emotional processing centers in the brain are interfering with the circuits that initiate and control voluntary movement. The motor cortex itself is fine—it can be activated by transcranial magnetic stimulation and produces normal muscle contraction. The problem is upstream, in the networks that should be sending the "move" command.

Mark Edwards, a movement disorder neurologist at St George's Hospital London, has described this as a disorder of the "sense of agency"—the brain's prediction about what it expects the body to do becomes disrupted, and the resulting mismatch between expectation and execution produces symptoms [8]. When you reach for a cup, your brain generates a prediction of movement, sends the command, and monitors whether the outcome matches the prediction. In FND, this prediction system malfunctions—often driven by abnormal attention and arousal states—leading to movements that feel involuntary or to voluntary movements that can't be initiated.

This mechanism explains several puzzling features of FND. Symptoms often fluctuate: they may worsen with attention and improve with distraction, not because the person is faking, but because attention directly modulates the disrupted circuits. Symptoms often begin after physical injury, illness, or surgery—events that alter body-related predictions and create a window of vulnerability. And symptoms frequently co-occur with dissociative experiences, fatigue, and pain, all of which share overlapping neural mechanisms involving altered self-monitoring and interoception.

The role of psychological stress and trauma in FND is real but more nuanced than the Freudian model suggested. Research by Ludwig et al. (2018) found that while adverse life events and psychological distress are significantly more common in FND patients than in controls, approximately 30% of FND patients report no identifiable psychological stressor [9]. FND can develop after physical triggers (injury, illness, surgery, anesthesia) without obvious psychological precipitants. The emerging consensus is that psychological distress is one of several factors—alongside physical events, personality traits, and physiological arousal patterns—that can disrupt the brain's predictive motor control systems.

## The Diagnostic Journey: Why It Takes So Long

People with FND face one of the most difficult diagnostic journeys in medicine. The average time from symptom onset to correct diagnosis is 5-7 years, during which patients accumulate an average of 4-6 incorrect diagnoses and undergo numerous unnecessary investigations and treatments [5].

Several factors contribute to this delay. First, many physicians—both neurologists and psychiatrists—receive minimal training in FND during medical education. A 2019 survey by Lehn et al. found that fewer than 50% of neurology residents felt confident diagnosing FND, and even fewer felt confident explaining the diagnosis to patients [10]. The condition falls into the gap between specialties, owned fully by neither.

Second, the legacy of mind-body dualism in Western medicine means that many clinicians still implicitly operate under the assumption that symptoms must be either "organic" (real, neurological) or "psychological" (not real, psychiatric). When standard neurological tests are normal, the default clinical response has historically been to say "There's nothing wrong with you"—a devastating message for someone experiencing genuine disability. Research by Stone et al. (2005) found that patients with FND rated being told "There's nothing wrong" as one of the most distressing aspects of their diagnostic experience—more distressing than the symptoms themselves [11].

Third, the fear of misdiagnosis runs in both directions. Clinicians worry about missing a "real" neurological disease and having to explain a missed diagnosis later. This leads to repeated testing and specialist referrals that delay the correct diagnosis and, paradoxically, reinforce the patient's belief that something structurally dangerous must be going on.

The diagnostic landscape has improved significantly since Stone, Carson, and colleagues published their influential work on positive diagnostic signs for FND [2]. Current best practice emphasizes making FND a "rule-in" diagnosis based on positive examination findings, explaining the diagnosis transparently and empathetically, and initiating treatment promptly rather than waiting for further exclusionary testing.

## Treatment: Physiotherapy, Psychology, and the Power of Explanation

Effective treatment for FND is multidisciplinary, combining neurological education, specialized physiotherapy, and psychological therapy. The evidence base has grown substantially in the past decade, shifting FND from a condition many clinicians considered untreatable to one with documented pathways to improvement.

**The explanation.** Treatment begins—and often hinges on—how the diagnosis is delivered. Research by Stone and Edwards demonstrates that a clear, credible explanation of FND is itself therapeutic [2][8]. Effective explanation includes: naming the condition ("You have functional neurological disorder"), explaining the mechanism in accessible terms ("Your nervous system is intact, but the signals aren't getting through properly—like a software glitch rather than hardware damage"), validating the reality of symptoms ("These symptoms are real and not your fault"), and providing a framework for recovery ("Because the hardware is intact, recovery is possible with the right rehabilitation").

Patients who receive a clear, positive explanation show better outcomes than those who are simply told what they *don't* have. The explanation shifts the patient's understanding from "mysterious, potentially dangerous disease" to "known condition with a treatment pathway," which alone can reduce the hypervigilance and catastrophizing that fuel symptoms.

**Specialized physiotherapy.** Physical rehabilitation is the cornerstone of FND treatment for motor symptoms. Glenn Nielsen and colleagues at University College London developed and tested a specialized physiotherapy protocol that retrains normal movement patterns using techniques like distraction (performing a secondary task while attempting the affected movement), graded motor imagery, and progressive goal-setting [4]. A prospective cohort study found that 72% of patients showed clinically significant improvement in physical function after 5 days of intensive physiotherapy, with gains maintained at 6-month follow-up.

The key principle is that FND symptoms often improve with automatic rather than effortful movement. When a person with functional leg weakness walks while carrying on a conversation or catching a ball, the affected leg often functions better because attention has been redirected away from the disrupted voluntary control pathway. Physiotherapy exploits this by systematically retraining movement through automatic pathways while gradually reintroducing voluntary control.

**Psychological therapy.** CBT adapted for FND targets the cognitive and emotional factors that maintain symptoms: catastrophic beliefs about the symptoms, avoidance behaviors, hypervigilance toward body sensations, and, where present, the processing of underlying psychological distress or trauma [12]. For patients with functional seizures specifically, a landmark randomized controlled trial by Goldstein et al. (2020)—the CODES trial—demonstrated that CBT plus standard medical care significantly reduced seizure frequency and improved psychological wellbeing compared to standard medical care alone [13].

**Occupational therapy and multidisciplinary programs.** Comprehensive FND treatment programs, such as those at the National Hospital for Neurology and Neurosurgery in London and the Functional Neurological Disorder program at Massachusetts General Hospital, combine physiotherapy, occupational therapy, psychology, and neurology in integrated packages. Outcomes data from these programs show consistent improvement in function, symptom severity, and quality of life [14].

> 💡 **Spark:** FND reveals something profound about the nature of the brain: the line between "neurological" and "psychological" is an artifact of medical specialization, not biology. Your brain doesn't recognize that distinction. Emotions, attention, stress, and motor control share the same neural real estate—and when one system disrupts another, the symptoms are just as real as any caused by a tumor or a lesion.

## What You Can Try

If you've been diagnosed with FND or are experiencing functional neurological symptoms, here are actionable starting points:

**Learn about FND from reliable sources.** FND Hope (fndhope.org) and neurosymptoms.org (created by Jon Stone at the University of Edinburgh) are the two best patient-facing resources. Understanding the mechanism behind your symptoms—that they're real, neurological, and treatable—is itself a therapeutic intervention.

**Find a clinician who specializes in FND.** This is increasingly possible as FND centers expand. The FND Society maintains a list of specialized clinics. When speaking with potential providers, ask: "Do you diagnose and treat functional neurological disorder? Do you work with a multidisciplinary team including physiotherapy and psychology?"

**Practice movement through distraction.** If you have functional motor symptoms, try performing the affected movement while engaged in a secondary task—talking, counting backward, catching a ball. Notice whether the movement improves when your conscious attention is elsewhere. This isn't a trick; it's the same principle rehabilitation physiotherapists use, and it confirms the functional nature of the symptom in a way that can be empowering.

**Track your symptoms in context.** Use Psychage's [Mood Journal](https://psychage.com/tools/mood-journal) to record symptom severity alongside stress levels, sleep, emotions, and activities. FND symptoms typically fluctuate, and identifying patterns gives you information you can use in treatment and daily management.

**Connect with others who have FND.** The isolation of having a condition that many people—including some medical professionals—don't understand can be as disabling as the symptoms themselves. FND Hope's support groups, both online and in-person, connect you with people who share your experience.
