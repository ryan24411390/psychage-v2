> **Summary:** Trichotillomania (hair-pulling disorder) and excoriation disorder (skin-picking disorder) are body-focused repetitive behaviors (BFRBs) classified alongside OCD in the DSM-5. Trichotillomania affects approximately 1–2% of the population; excoriation disorder affects roughly 1.4%. Both conditions involve recurrent, often semi-automatic pulling or picking behaviors that cause visible tissue damage and significant distress. Neither is a "bad habit" — both involve identifiable neurobiological differences in habit-learning circuits, emotional regulation systems, and motor inhibition pathways. Habit reversal training (HRT) and comprehensive behavioral treatment represent the current evidence-based interventions, with emerging pharmacological options showing promise. Understanding these conditions as neurodevelopmental in nature — not as failures of willpower — is essential for effective support and treatment.

**Key Facts:**
- Trichotillomania affects approximately 1–2% of the general population, with typical onset between ages 10–13 and a female-to-male ratio of roughly 10:1 in clinical samples [1].
- Excoriation (skin-picking) disorder affects an estimated 1.4% of the population, with onset typically in adolescence and similar female predominance [2].
- Neuroimaging research by Chamberlain and colleagues (2009) found that individuals with trichotillomania show increased gray matter density in brain regions involved in habit formation (putamen, amygdala) and reduced white matter integrity in tracts connecting frontal cortex to basal ganglia [3].
- Habit reversal training (HRT) produces clinically significant improvement in approximately 60–70% of individuals with trichotillomania, with effects maintained at 6-month follow-up [4].
- Up to 65% of individuals with one BFRB also engage in at least one other body-focused repetitive behavior (nail biting, cheek chewing, skin picking alongside hair pulling), suggesting shared underlying mechanisms [5].

## The Experience from the Inside

A college student sits at her desk studying for an exam. Her left hand drifts to her scalp, fingers threading through hair near the crown. She finds a strand with a particular texture — coarser, perhaps, or with a slight kink — and pulls it out. She examines the root, rolls the bulb between her fingers, and reaches for the next one. Twenty minutes pass. She hasn't looked up from the screen once, and she doesn't realize she's been pulling until she notices the small pile of hairs on her notebook.

This is what "automatic" hair pulling looks like. It's one of two recognized subtypes — the other is "focused" pulling, where the individual deliberately pulls in response to urges, tension, or emotional distress, sometimes experiencing relief or satisfaction afterward [1]. Most people with trichotillomania engage in both types, shifting between automatic and focused pulling depending on context and emotional state.

Excoriation disorder follows a parallel pattern. A person notices an irregularity on their skin — a bump, a scab, a slightly raised area — and begins picking, squeezing, or scraping at it. What starts as a brief inspection becomes a prolonged session that leaves the skin damaged, sometimes bleeding, sometimes scarred. Like hair pulling, skin picking can be automatic (occurring during sedentary activities without full awareness) or focused (driven by the urge to "fix" a perceived imperfection or to manage emotional tension) [2].

Both conditions share core features that distinguish them from casual grooming: the behavior is recurrent and results in noticeable physical damage (bald patches, skin lesions, scarring); repeated attempts to decrease or stop the behavior have failed; and the behavior causes clinically significant distress or impairment in functioning [6]. The physical consequences are real — alopecia, infection, scarring, trichobezoars (hair balls in the gastrointestinal tract from hair swallowing) — and the psychosocial consequences are often equally damaging. People with BFRBs frequently avoid swimming, hairdressers, intimacy, and social situations where the evidence of their behavior might be visible.

The shame runs deep. Nancy Keuthen, co-director of the Trichotillomania Clinic at Massachusetts General Hospital, has documented the profound secrecy that surrounds these conditions [5]. Many individuals go years or decades without telling anyone — including healthcare providers — about their pulling or picking. They develop elaborate concealment strategies: strategic hairstyles, makeup over lesions, long sleeves, hats. The secrecy itself becomes a source of distress, creating isolation that compounds the condition.

## Neuroscience: Why "Just Stop" Doesn't Work

The most common response people with BFRBs hear is some version of "Can't you just stop doing that?" The answer, supported by neuroscience, is that the brain circuitry maintaining these behaviors makes simple cessation extraordinarily difficult — not because of insufficient motivation, but because of how the behaviors are encoded.

Samuel Chamberlain's neuroimaging research at the University of Cambridge has been particularly illuminating. His 2009 study using diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) and voxel-based morphometry found that individuals with trichotillomania show structural brain differences in two key systems [3]. First, increased gray matter density in the striatum (particularly the putamen) — a region central to habit learning and motor program execution. Second, reduced white matter integrity in frontostriatal tracts — the connections between prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia that normally allow conscious control to override habitual behaviors.

Translation: the habit system is overgrown, and the brake system is underconnected. Hair pulling and skin picking become deeply encoded motor programs — like riding a bicycle, but destructive. Once established, these programs can execute with minimal conscious input, which is why automatic pulling and picking happen during reading, driving, watching television, or studying. The behavior doesn't require attention; it's been offloaded to subcortical circuits that run beneath awareness.

Emotional regulation adds another layer. Jon Grant's research group at the University of Chicago has demonstrated that BFRBs are closely linked to difficulties in emotion modulation [7]. Pulling and picking frequently serve regulatory functions — reducing tension, alleviating boredom, managing frustration, or creating a focused, absorptive state that temporarily blocks aversive emotions. Functional MRI during simulated BFRB urges shows hyperactivation in limbic regions (amygdala, anterior insula) and hypoactivation in prefrontal control regions, a pattern consistent with impaired top-down regulation of emotion-driven behavior.

Genetic studies indicate moderate heritability. Novak and colleagues (2009) identified associations between trichotillomania and variations in the SAPAP3 gene, which codes for a postsynaptic scaffolding protein expressed heavily in the striatum [8]. SAPAP3 knockout mice develop compulsive grooming behaviors that cause skin lesions — a striking animal model that further supports the neurobiological basis of BFRBs. Family studies show that first-degree relatives of individuals with trichotillomania have significantly elevated rates of both hair pulling and other BFRBs, with heritability estimates around 40–50%.

The neurotransmitter picture is complex. Unlike OCD, BFRBs show inconsistent response to serotonergic medications. Instead, glutamatergic and dopaminergic systems appear more centrally involved. N-acetylcysteine (NAC), a glutamate modulator available as a dietary supplement, showed promising results in Jon Grant's 2009 double-blind, placebo-controlled trial for trichotillomania — 56% of NAC-treated participants were rated as "much improved" or "very much improved" compared to 16% on placebo [7].

## Treatment: What the Evidence Supports

The strongest evidence base for BFRB treatment belongs to habit reversal training (HRT), a behavioral intervention first developed by Nathan Azrin and Gregory Nunn in the 1970s and subsequently refined through decades of clinical research [4]. HRT has three core components:

**Awareness training** teaches the individual to recognize the early links in the behavioral chain — the urge, the hand movement toward the pulling or picking site, the posture and environmental context associated with episodes. Many people are startled to discover how much of their behavior occurs outside conscious awareness. Awareness training uses self-monitoring (tracking episodes in real-time), video review, and therapist-guided identification of sensory and emotional triggers.

**Competing response training** introduces a physically incompatible behavior to replace the pulling or picking when the urge or early chain link is detected. For hair pulling, common competing responses include clenching the fists, gripping an object, or placing hands flat on a surface. The competing response needs to be maintained for 1–2 minutes or until the urge subsides. It doesn't need to be complex — it needs to be accessible and physically incompatible with the BFRB.

**Social support** enlists a family member or close friend to provide gentle, agreed-upon prompts when they observe the behavior or precursor movements, and to offer encouragement for competing response use. This component reduces the secrecy that maintains the behavioral cycle.

Woods and colleagues (2006) demonstrated in a controlled trial that HRT produced significantly greater improvement than supportive therapy for trichotillomania, with response rates of approximately 63% compared to 28% in the control condition [4]. Effects were maintained at 6-month follow-up.

Comprehensive Behavioral Treatment (ComB), developed by Charles Mansueto and colleagues at the Behavior Therapy Center of Greater Washington, extends HRT by addressing the full ecology of BFRBs [9]. ComB identifies five domains that contribute to pulling/picking: sensory triggers (texture preferences, tactile sensitivity), cognitive factors (permission-giving thoughts like "just one more"), affective triggers (boredom, anxiety, frustration), motor patterns (habitual hand positions), and environmental cues (specific settings, activities). Treatment targets all relevant domains for the individual, creating a comprehensive, personalized intervention plan.

Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) combined with HRT represents another evidence-supported approach. ACT components — mindful awareness of urges without acting on them, acceptance of discomfort, values-based motivation for change — address the emotional regulation dimension that pure HRT may underserve. Woods and colleagues (2006) found that ACT-enhanced HRT produced outcomes comparable to standard HRT, with some evidence for superior long-term maintenance [4].

Pharmacologically, the landscape is mixed. SSRIs, which are first-line for OCD, show limited efficacy for BFRBs. N-acetylcysteine has the strongest controlled trial support [7]. Naltrexone (an opioid antagonist) and olanzapine (an atypical antipsychotic) have shown some benefit in small studies, but neither has robust enough evidence for first-line recommendation. Medication is generally considered adjunctive to behavioral treatment rather than standalone.

## Living With BFRBs: Practical Realities

One of the most important shifts in BFRB treatment over the past decade has been the move away from a purely abstinence-oriented framework. For many individuals, complete cessation of pulling or picking may not be realistic as a treatment goal — and framing anything less than zero episodes as "failure" creates a cycle of discouragement and relapse.

The TLC Foundation for Body-Focused Repetitive Behaviors (formerly the Trichotillomania Learning Center) has been instrumental in promoting a harm-reduction approach alongside traditional treatment goals [10]. Reducing frequency, limiting damage severity, expanding the range of situations where the behavior can be resisted, and improving quality of life are all meaningful outcomes — even if occasional episodes persist.

Relapse is common and expected. BFRBs are chronic conditions for most individuals, more analogous to managing diabetes than to curing an infection. Stressful life transitions, hormonal changes, sleep deprivation, and periods of high emotional demand can all trigger resurgence. Treatment should include relapse prevention planning and the understanding that returning to active use of HRT skills after a setback is not failure — it's the nature of managing a chronic neurobehavioral condition.

> 💡 **Spark:** Your hands aren't betraying you — they're running a program your brain learned long before you had a say in it. BFRBs aren't about discipline or willpower. They're about circuitry. And circuitry, with the right intervention, can be rewired — not to perfection, but to a life where the behavior doesn't run the show.

## What You Can Try

**Start with tracking.** Before trying to change the behavior, understand it. For one week, log every pulling or picking episode you notice: time, location, activity, emotional state, duration, body area targeted, automatic or focused. The TLC Foundation offers free tracking tools (bfrb.org). Patterns will emerge — and patterns are actionable.

**Identify your high-risk contexts.** Most people with BFRBs have specific settings or activities that reliably trigger episodes: reading in bed, watching television, sitting in long meetings, driving. Once identified, these contexts become intervention points. Add physical barriers (fidget tools, bandages on fingertips, textured stickers on pulling sites), modify the environment (better lighting to reduce automatic behavior, moving mirrors), or substitute competing sensory input.

**Try the competing response.** When you notice the urge or the early hand movement, clench your fists gently for 60 seconds. It won't feel like enough. The urge won't immediately disappear. But each successful interruption weakens the automatic chain slightly. Consistency matters more than intensity.

**Seek specialized help.** General therapists often lack training in BFRBs. The TLC Foundation provider directory (bfrb.org) lists clinicians with specific BFRB expertise. Teletherapy has made specialists more accessible regardless of geography.

**Tell someone.** Breaking the secrecy — with a trusted friend, family member, or healthcare provider — reduces the isolation that maintains the shame cycle. You don't need to tell everyone. One person who understands is enough to start.
