> **Summary:** Indigenous healing systems have sustained communities for thousands of years — long before Western psychology formalized its first diagnostic manual. These traditions aren't historical curiosities or "alternative" therapies waiting for scientific validation. They are living, evolving knowledge systems with their own epistemologies, evidence bases, and therapeutic mechanisms. This article examines three Indigenous frameworks — Aboriginal Australian social-emotional wellbeing, Maori Te Whare Tapa Wha, and the Native American Medicine Wheel — and explores how Western psychology is beginning, sometimes clumsily, to engage with these paradigms. We also confront the colonial history that suppressed these traditions and the ethical obligations that come with any cross-paradigm conversation.

## Key Facts

- **Indigenous peoples make up approximately 6.2% of the global population** (476 million people across 90 countries), yet face mental health disparities rooted in colonialism rather than cultural deficiency [1].
- **The Aboriginal concept of social-emotional wellbeing** encompasses connection to land, culture, spirituality, ancestry, family, and community — none of which appear in the DSM-5 [2].
- **Mason Durie's Te Whare Tapa Wha model** (1985) conceptualizes health as a four-walled house: taha wairua (spiritual), taha hinengaro (mental), taha tinana (physical), and taha whanau (family) — if any wall weakens, the whole structure is compromised [3].
- **Joseph Gone's research** with First Nations communities found that cultural interventions — sweat lodges, talking circles, land-based healing — produced therapeutic outcomes comparable to evidence-based Western treatments for depression and PTSD [4].
- **A 2019 Lancet series on Indigenous health** concluded that culturally grounded healing approaches must be central, not supplementary, to mental health services for Indigenous populations [5].

## Deep Dive

### The Colonial Wound: Why This History Matters for Present-Day Mental Health

You can't understand Indigenous healing without first understanding what colonialism did to it. Across every continent where Indigenous peoples lived — which is to say, every continent — European colonial powers systematically suppressed, criminalized, and pathologized Indigenous healing traditions. In the United States, the Indian Religious Freedom Act wasn't passed until 1978. In Australia, the Stolen Generations — Aboriginal children forcibly removed from families and placed in institutions — continued until the 1970s. In Canada, the last residential school closed in 1996. These aren't ancient history. They are within living memory.

The psychological damage of colonialism operates on multiple levels. Gone (2010), a clinical psychologist and enrolled member of the Gros Ventre tribal nation, describes a pattern he calls "colonial disruption" — the systematic dismantling of Indigenous social structures, spiritual practices, languages, and child-rearing traditions that served as the infrastructure for mental health across generations [4]. When you remove the infrastructure, you don't just lose the traditions. You lose the intergenerational transmission of resilience.

Western psychology has been complicit in this process. Early anthropological and psychological research on Indigenous peoples was often extractive — researchers entered communities, collected data that served their own academic careers, and left without providing benefit. Worse, Western diagnostic categories were sometimes used to pathologize cultural practices. Hearing the voices of ancestors, for instance, was categorized as a psychotic symptom rather than a spiritual experience embedded in a coherent cultural framework.

This context matters because any conversation about "integrating" Indigenous healing with Western psychology must reckon with the power differential. These aren't two equal systems coming together in neutral territory. One system was used as a weapon against the other. Acknowledging this isn't performative guilt — it's the minimum prerequisite for honest engagement.

### Three Frameworks: Aboriginal Wellbeing, Te Whare Tapa Wha, and the Medicine Wheel

**Aboriginal Social-Emotional Wellbeing (SEWB)** represents one of the most comprehensive frameworks for understanding health outside the Western biomedical model. Developed and refined by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander scholars and communities, SEWB encompasses seven interconnected domains: body, mind and emotions, family and kinship, community, culture, country (land and place), and spirituality and ancestors [2]. Notice what's present here that's absent from Western models: the land itself as a domain of health. For Aboriginal Australians, connection to country isn't a metaphor. It's a concrete determinant of wellbeing, supported by research showing that Aboriginal people living on or near traditional lands report significantly better mental health outcomes than those in urban displacement [6].

Professor Pat Dudgeon, a Bardi woman and Australia's first Aboriginal psychologist, has led efforts to formalize the SEWB framework within Australian mental health policy. Her work emphasizes that SEWB isn't just relevant for Aboriginal people — it challenges the fundamental individualism of Western psychological models by insisting that health is relational, ecological, and spiritual, not merely the absence of diagnosable pathology [2].

**Te Whare Tapa Wha** ("The House of Four Walls") was articulated by Sir Mason Durie, a Maori psychiatrist, at a Maori Women's Welfare League conference in 1982 and published formally in 1985 [3]. The metaphor is architectural: health is a house with four walls. Taha wairua (spirituality) is the most essential wall — without it, Durie argues, a person is more vulnerable to illness and misfortune. Taha hinengaro (thoughts and feelings) includes cognitive and emotional health but also encompasses the capacity to communicate and think creatively. Taha tinana (physical health) includes bodily care, physical activity, and nutrition. Taha whanau (family) extends beyond the nuclear family to the broader community of belonging.

What makes Te Whare Tapa Wha remarkable isn't just its content but its impact. It's been adopted into New Zealand's national health policy, taught in medical and nursing schools, and used as an assessment framework in clinical practice. A study by Rochford (2004) found that Maori patients assessed using Te Whare Tapa Wha felt more understood and more engaged in treatment than those assessed using standard Western intake procedures [7].

**The Medicine Wheel** varies considerably across First Nations, Metis, and Inuit communities, and it's essential to note that no single version represents all Indigenous peoples of North America. Common elements across many traditions include four quadrants representing physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual dimensions of health, often mapped onto the four cardinal directions and associated with specific colors, seasons, and life stages. The wheel's circular structure communicates something that linear Western models struggle to capture: health isn't a destination you arrive at but a dynamic balance you continuously maintain.

Psychologist Rod McCormick (Mohawk Nation) has documented how Medicine Wheel-based interventions — incorporating ceremony, storytelling, connection to nature, and community participation — produce measurable improvements in depression, substance use, and post-traumatic stress among First Nations participants [8]. His work challenges the assumption that healing requires individual talk therapy in a clinical office.

### Bridging Paradigms Without Collapsing Them

The question of how — or whether — to integrate Indigenous and Western approaches is contested terrain, and the tension is productive rather than something to be resolved.

On one side, researchers like Gone (2021) argue that Indigenous healing practices should be evaluated on their own terms, using methodologies that respect Indigenous epistemologies rather than forcing them into randomized controlled trial frameworks designed for pharmaceutical interventions [9]. A sweat lodge ceremony isn't a pill. Its therapeutic mechanisms involve spiritual experience, community bonding, physical catharsis, and reconnection to cultural identity, operating simultaneously across multiple dimensions. Reducing it to a single measurable outcome variable misses the point entirely.

On the other side, there's a pragmatic argument for developing an evidence base that Western health systems can recognize, because that recognition opens funding, institutional support, and broader access. Maori health researchers have navigated this tension skillfully, publishing research in Western academic journals while using kaupapa Maori methodology — a research framework grounded in Maori values that centers Maori voices and benefits Maori communities [10].

The most promising work happens when practitioners hold both frameworks without collapsing one into the other. In New Zealand, some mental health services offer dual assessment — a standard clinical intake alongside a Te Whare Tapa Wha assessment — allowing clinicians and clients to see where Western and Maori frameworks align and where they diverge. In Canada, several treatment centers for Indigenous communities combine trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy with land-based healing, sweat lodge ceremonies, and elder-guided talking circles.

The key ethical principle: Indigenous healing practices belong to Indigenous communities. Non-Indigenous clinicians can learn from these frameworks, but they shouldn't extract techniques and rebrand them as "holistic therapy" stripped of their cultural context and meaning. That's not integration — it's appropriation. Respectful engagement means following Indigenous leadership, compensating Indigenous knowledge holders, and centering the communities these practices serve.

> 💡 **Spark:** Western psychology asks "What's wrong with you?" Indigenous healing often asks "What happened to your people, and what connections need restoring?" The question you start with shapes everything that follows.

## What You Can Try

These suggestions are offered with a clear distinction: learning from Indigenous wisdom is different from practicing Indigenous ceremony. The latter belongs to specific cultural communities and their protocols.

**Examine your own model of health.** Most of us absorbed the Western biomedical model without realizing it — the idea that health equals the absence of disease in an individual body. Try mapping your own wellbeing using the Te Whare Tapa Wha framework: How is your spiritual life? Your family and community connections? Your mental and emotional state? Your physical health? Where is the weakest wall of your house?

**Notice what your healing practices leave out.** If your mental health approach focuses exclusively on individual thoughts and behaviors — the domain of cognitive behavioral therapy — consider what's missing. Are you attending to your relationships? Your connection to place and nature? Your sense of meaning and purpose? Indigenous frameworks remind us that these aren't luxuries. They're structural.

**Learn about the Indigenous peoples whose land you occupy.** This isn't about guilt. It's about relational accountability. Knowing whose land you're on, what happened to those peoples, and how they're organizing today is a basic form of awareness that can shift how you understand mental health in your own community. Many Indigenous nations maintain websites and cultural centers that welcome visitors.

**If you're a clinician, invest in cultural humility training led by Indigenous practitioners.** Read Gone, Dudgeon, Durie, and McCormick. Attend workshops organized by Indigenous mental health organizations. And critically, examine whether your clinical training included any Indigenous perspectives at all — if it didn't, that absence itself is informative.

**Support Indigenous-led mental health initiatives directly.** Organizations like We Al-Li (Australia), Te Rau Ora (New Zealand), and the Thunderbird Partnership Foundation (Canada) are doing transformative work grounded in Indigenous knowledge systems.
