> **Summary:** More than 100 million people are currently forcibly displaced worldwide — the highest number ever recorded. Behind that statistic are individuals carrying the weight of war, persecution, perilous journeys, and the grinding uncertainty of rebuilding life in an unfamiliar place. Refugee mental health cannot be reduced to a single diagnosis or a single moment of trauma. It's shaped by what happened before departure, during the journey, and — critically — by conditions in the resettlement country. This article examines the full arc of displacement through Derrick Silove's ADAPT model, confronts the limitations of Western trauma frameworks when applied to refugee populations, and centers the agency and resilience that refugees themselves bring to recovery. The evidence is clear: with appropriate support, the vast majority of refugees can and do recover.

## Key Facts

- **Approximately 31% of refugees meet criteria for PTSD and 31% for depression**, though rates vary dramatically depending on the host country's reception conditions [1].
- **Up to 70% of refugees with PTSD respond positively to evidence-based treatment** when they can access it — response rates comparable to non-refugee populations [2].
- **Post-migration stressors** — insecure residency status, detention, poverty, and social isolation — are as strongly associated with mental health outcomes as pre-migration trauma itself [3].
- **Silove's ADAPT model** identifies five psychosocial pillars disrupted by conflict and displacement: safety, bonds and networks, justice, roles and identities, and existential meaning [4].
- **Only 2% of global health funding is allocated to mental health**, and refugees in low- and middle-income host countries often have access to fewer than 1 mental health professional per 100,000 people [5].

## Deep Dive

### The Three Phases of Displacement: Pre-Migration, Transit, and Post-Migration

Understanding refugee mental health requires abandoning the idea that trauma is a single event in the past. For displaced people, the stressors accumulate across three distinct phases, each with its own psychological signature.

**Pre-migration** encompasses the conditions that forced departure: war, genocide, political persecution, sexual violence as a weapon of conflict, torture, witnessing the murder of family members or community members, destruction of homes and livelihoods. The severity and duration of these exposures matter enormously. Mollica and colleagues (1998), working with Cambodian refugees who survived the Khmer Rouge regime, found a dose-response relationship between the number of trauma types experienced and the probability of developing PTSD — those exposed to four or more trauma categories had PTSD rates exceeding 80% [6]. But pre-migration experiences aren't exclusively traumatic. Refugees also carry professional skills, cultural knowledge, multilingual abilities, social networks, and survival capacities honed under extreme conditions.

**The transit phase** — the journey itself — is often the most dangerous period and the least studied. Mediterranean crossings that claim thousands of lives annually. Overland routes through hostile territory. Months or years in transit camps with inadequate food, sanitation, and security. Sexual violence is endemic in many transit contexts, affecting women and girls disproportionately but also men and boys. Unaccompanied minors face the most acute vulnerability during transit, exposed to trafficking, exploitation, and the complete absence of protective adult relationships. A study of refugees arriving in Europe during the 2015 crisis found that 44% had experienced violence during their journey, and those who had were three times more likely to meet PTSD criteria upon arrival [7].

**Post-migration** is where the story gets complicated — and where policy has the most leverage. The conventional assumption is that arriving in a resettlement country ends the trauma. The research emphatically says otherwise. Li, Liddell, and Nickerson (2016) conducted a meta-analysis of 29 studies and found that post-migration stressors — specifically insecure residency status, restricted work rights, detention, isolation, and discrimination — were as strongly associated with depression and PTSD as the pre-migration trauma itself [3]. This is a profound finding. It means that the conditions of resettlement aren't secondary to the original trauma — they're a primary determinant of mental health outcomes.

Detention is particularly damaging. Refugees held in immigration detention facilities — even for relatively short periods — show dramatically elevated rates of depression, anxiety, PTSD, and self-harm compared to refugees processed in the community. A systematic review by von Werthern and colleagues (2018) found that detention was associated with a fourfold increase in mental health symptoms, with effects persisting long after release [8]. Children in detention show developmental regression, sleep disturbance, and attachment disruption that can persist for years.

### Beyond PTSD: The ADAPT Model and the Limits of Western Diagnosis

The Western mental health field's default response to refugee suffering has been to screen for PTSD. This isn't wrong — PTSD is genuinely prevalent — but it's radically incomplete. Derrick Silove, a psychiatrist at the University of New South Wales, developed the ADAPT (Adaptation and Development After Persecution and Trauma) model precisely to address this limitation [4].

ADAPT identifies five psychosocial pillars that conflict and displacement systematically disrupt:

**Safety and security**: The most fundamental human need. Displacement shatters the assumption that the world is safe. Even in resettlement, this pillar remains compromised when refugees face discrimination, hate crimes, or the threat of deportation. Restoring a sense of safety requires more than physical shelter — it requires predictability, legal protection, and social inclusion.

**Bonds and networks**: War and displacement tear apart the social fabric — families separated during flight, communities scattered across countries, elders who held cultural knowledge dying in transit. Rebuilding these networks in a new country is complicated by language barriers, cultural differences, and the loss of shared context. Silove argues that strengthening social bonds should be a central mental health intervention, not a peripheral nicety.

**Justice**: Many refugees have experienced profound injustice — atrocities committed with impunity, perpetrators who face no accountability, asylum systems that disbelieve their testimony. The psychological toll of unaddressed injustice is measurable: Basoglu and colleagues (2005) found that the perceived injustice of torture was a stronger predictor of PTSD than the physical severity of the torture itself [9].

**Roles and identities**: A surgeon who can't practice. A community leader with no community. A parent who can't provide. Displacement strips people of the social roles that give life structure and meaning. This dimension of loss is often invisible to clinicians focused narrowly on trauma symptoms.

**Existential meaning**: How do you make sense of surviving when others didn't? How do you maintain cultural and spiritual practices in a society that doesn't understand them? These aren't philosophical luxuries. They're psychological necessities.

The ADAPT model doesn't replace clinical diagnosis — it contextualizes it. A refugee presenting with insomnia, hypervigilance, and social withdrawal may meet criteria for PTSD, but treating those symptoms without addressing the person's legal limbo, fractured social network, and loss of professional identity will produce limited results.

Cultural idioms of distress add another layer of complexity. Not every culture expresses psychological suffering through the vocabulary of Western psychiatry. Somatic complaints — headaches, chest pain, fatigue — are the primary expression of distress in many non-Western cultures. "Thinking too much" is a widely recognized idiom of distress across African, Southeast Asian, and Latin American cultures that overlaps with but isn't identical to clinical depression. Clinicians who screen only for Western diagnostic categories will systematically undercount and mischaracterize refugee distress.

### Treatment Works — When Refugees Can Access It

Here's the good news that doesn't get enough attention: evidence-based treatment for refugee mental health conditions works. Nosè and colleagues (2017) conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of psychological interventions for refugees and asylum seekers and found that trauma-focused cognitive behavioral therapy (TF-CBT) and narrative exposure therapy (NET) produced large effect sizes for PTSD symptom reduction, with approximately 70% of participants showing clinically significant improvement [2].

Narrative exposure therapy, developed by Maggie Schauer, Frank Neuner, and Thomas Elbert, deserves particular mention because it was designed specifically for survivors of organized violence and can be delivered by trained lay counselors rather than requiring licensed psychologists [10]. In NET, the client constructs a chronological life narrative that places traumatic events within the full context of their life — including moments of joy, competence, and connection. This process helps integrate fragmented trauma memories into a coherent life story. Randomized controlled trials in Uganda, Sri Lanka, and Germany have demonstrated NET's effectiveness with refugee populations.

The WHO's Problem Management Plus (PM+) program offers another scalable approach — a five-session intervention deliverable by trained community members that addresses depression, anxiety, stress, and grief. A randomized trial with Pakistani refugees in Peshawar found that PM+ produced significant reductions in psychological distress that persisted at three-month follow-up [11].

The barrier isn't the availability of effective treatments. It's access. Mental health services for refugees are chronically underfunded, culturally unresponsive, and geographically inaccessible. In many low- and middle-income countries hosting the majority of the world's refugees, there are fewer than 0.5 mental health professionals per 100,000 people [5]. Even in high-income resettlement countries, refugees face language barriers, long waitlists, lack of culturally competent providers, and the fundamental obstacle of prioritizing survival needs — housing, employment, legal status — over mental health treatment.

Task-shifting — training community health workers, cultural brokers, and peer supporters to deliver structured mental health interventions — represents the most promising solution to the access gap. The evidence supports it. Trained lay counselors delivering manualized interventions achieve outcomes that are comparable to those of professional therapists for common mental health conditions.

> 💡 **Spark:** Refugees aren't defined by what was done to them. They're people who survived extraordinary circumstances, crossed borders, learned new systems, and kept going. The clinical question isn't "How damaged are they?" but "What do they need to continue recovering?"

## What You Can Try

**If you're a refugee or displaced person experiencing psychological distress**, know that what you're feeling is a normal response to abnormal circumstances — not a sign of weakness or permanent damage. Effective help exists. Many resettlement agencies offer free mental health services, and some clinics provide services in multiple languages. Ask your caseworker, doctor, or community organization about mental health support specifically designed for refugees.

**If you're supporting a refugee — as a friend, neighbor, volunteer, or sponsor** — the most powerful thing you can offer isn't clinical. It's consistent, nonjudgmental social connection. Help with practical needs: navigating bureaucratic systems, practicing the local language, finding community organizations. Don't press for trauma stories — people will share when and if they're ready. Respect their agency. They've made life-and-death decisions under impossible conditions; they don't need to be managed.

**If you're a clinician working with refugee populations**, invest time in understanding your clients' cultural frameworks for distress and healing. Use the ADAPT model to assess psychosocial functioning beyond diagnostic symptoms. Consider whether your treatment environment — the intake paperwork, the waiting room, the physical setup — communicates safety or replicates institutional power dynamics. Learn about narrative exposure therapy and other approaches specifically developed for refugee populations. Partner with cultural brokers and community organizations. And recognize that treatment for PTSD symptoms will have limited impact if the person's residency status is uncertain, their family is still in danger, or they can't meet basic material needs.

**If you're a policymaker or advocate**, the evidence is unambiguous: post-migration conditions shape mental health outcomes as powerfully as pre-migration trauma. Policies that restrict work rights, prolong legal uncertainty, mandate detention, or limit access to healthcare don't just fail to help refugees — they actively harm them. Every month of legal limbo, every month in detention, is a measurable mental health cost.
