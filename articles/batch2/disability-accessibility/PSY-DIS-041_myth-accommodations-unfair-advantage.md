> **Summary**: The claim that disability accommodations give disabled people an "unfair advantage" over their non-disabled peers is one of the most common and consequential disability myths in education, employment, and public life. Extended test time, flexible scheduling, assistive technology, modified workstations, service animals, and accessible parking are routinely framed as special privileges rather than what they actually are: tools that remove barriers so disabled people can participate on equal terms. Research consistently demonstrates that accommodations do not produce advantage—they reduce disadvantage. Extended test time, for example, does not inflate disabled students' scores above their actual ability; it allows them to demonstrate ability that would otherwise be masked by the disabling condition. The accommodation myth fuels resentment, discourages disclosure, and creates hostile environments for the very people accommodations are designed to support.

## Key Facts

1. **Extended test time—the most commonly contested academic accommodation—does not improve the scores of non-disabled students**, demonstrating that it compensates for disability-related barriers rather than providing a general advantage (Sireci et al., 2005).

2. **Only 39% of employees with disabilities who are legally entitled to workplace accommodations actually request them**, with the primary reason being fear of being perceived as receiving unfair advantages or special treatment (Baldridge & Swift, 2013).

3. **The average cost of a workplace accommodation is $500 or less**, and 59% of accommodations cost nothing at all—yet employers consistently overestimate costs by a factor of 10 or more, fueling resistance (Job Accommodation Network, 2020).

4. **Students who receive disability accommodations do not outperform their non-disabled peers**; they perform at levels consistent with their measured ability, which is precisely what accommodation is designed to achieve (Gregg & Nelson, 2012).

5. **Universal design—building accessibility into systems from the start rather than accommodating after the fact—benefits everyone**: curb cuts help parents with strollers, captioning helps people in noisy environments, and flexible scheduling benefits employees with caregiving responsibilities (Burgstahler, 2015).

## The Myth

Disability accommodations are special privileges that give disabled people an unfair advantage over everyone else. A student who gets extended test time has an easier test. An employee who works from home has a more convenient schedule. A person with an accessible parking placard gets the best spot. These accommodations are not fair—they change the rules for some people and not others, creating a two-tier system where disabled people get benefits that non-disabled people have to earn.

The myth often comes wrapped in the language of fairness: "If everyone else has to take the test in two hours, why should they get three?" "If I have to commute to the office, why do they get to work from home?" "If accommodations were necessary, why didn't they need them before?" These questions sound reasonable. They are not. They rest on a fundamental misunderstanding of what accommodation is and why it exists.

## Where This Comes From

**Zero-sum thinking.** The accommodation myth is rooted in the assumption that fairness is a zero-sum game: if one person gets "more," someone else must be getting "less." This framing treats all people as starting from the same position, so any adjustment for one group must disadvantage another. But disabled people do not start from the same position. They start from behind a barrier that accommodations are designed to remove. Giving someone a ramp does not put them ahead of people who can use stairs. It puts them at the same entrance.

**Invisibility of built-in advantages.** The environments that non-disabled people navigate were designed for their bodies and minds. Desks are at a height that assumes ambulatory users. Tests are timed for neurotypical processing speeds. Office hours assume consistent energy across a workday. These design choices are themselves accommodations—for the majority. They are simply invisible because they are the default. When a disabled person's accommodation is visible and named, it appears as something extra rather than what it is: a counterpart to the unnamed accommodations already built into every system for non-disabled bodies (Dolmage, 2017).

**Suspicion of invisible disability.** The accommodation myth intensifies around invisible disabilities. When a student with ADHD receives extended test time or a worker with chronic pain gets a flexible schedule, observers who cannot see the disability may question whether it is real. "They don't look disabled" becomes a justification for viewing the accommodation as unearned. This suspicion reflects both the visibility myth and a broader cultural distrust of claims to vulnerability.

**Scarcity anxiety.** In competitive environments—college admissions, job markets, limited promotions—accommodation can be perceived as a competitive threat. If a classmate gets extra time and earns a higher grade, has their grade come at my expense? The answer, empirically, is no. But in environments where resources feel scarce, anything that appears to tip the balance generates resentment.

## What Research Actually Shows

**Extended time does not inflate scores.** The research on extended test time is extensive and remarkably consistent. Sireci and colleagues (2005) conducted a meta-analysis of studies examining the effect of extended time on test performance for disabled and non-disabled students. They found that disabled students who received extended time showed score improvements that brought their performance in line with their measured ability—but not above it. Non-disabled students who received the same extended time showed minimal or no improvement. This pattern—differential boost—is the hallmark of a valid accommodation: it helps those who face the barrier and does not advantage those who do not.

**Accommodations level the field, not tilt it.** Gregg and Nelson (2012) tracked academic outcomes for students with learning disabilities who received accommodations throughout their college careers. These students did not outperform their non-disabled peers. Their GPAs, graduation rates, and post-graduation employment rates were consistent with their measured cognitive abilities. Without accommodations, their performance fell significantly below their ability—not because they lacked intelligence or effort, but because the standard testing and learning environment created artificial barriers that obscured their actual capacity.

**Accommodation requests are under-utilized, not abused.** One of the strongest pieces of evidence against the "unfair advantage" narrative is the dramatic underutilization of accommodations. Baldridge and Swift (2013) found that only 39% of employees legally entitled to workplace accommodations actually requested them. The primary barriers were fear of stigma, concern about being perceived as getting special treatment, and worry about career consequences. If accommodations were the advantage the myth claims, disabled people would be clamoring for them. Instead, they avoid requesting them because the social cost is too high.

**Accommodations are inexpensive.** The Job Accommodation Network (2020), funded by the U.S. Department of Labor, analyzed data from thousands of workplace accommodation requests. They found that 59% of accommodations cost nothing—schedule adjustments, task restructuring, permission to take breaks—and the median cost of those that did require expenditure was $500. Meanwhile, employers consistently estimated accommodation costs at $5,000–$10,000, a tenfold overestimate that contributes to resistance. The return on investment for accommodations was substantial: for every dollar spent, employers reported an average of $28.83 in benefits from retained employees, reduced training costs, and increased productivity.

**Universal design benefits everyone.** Perhaps the most compelling refutation of the "unfair advantage" myth is the universal design evidence. Burgstahler (2015) documented that accommodations designed for disabled people consistently benefit the broader population. Curb cuts, originally mandated for wheelchair users, are used daily by parents with strollers, delivery workers with carts, and travelers with luggage. Closed captioning, created for Deaf viewers, is used by 80% of people who watch content with captions—most of them hearing—in noisy environments, while learning a language, or while trying not to wake a sleeping partner. Flexible work schedules, often coded as disability accommodations, improve productivity and satisfaction for all employees.

**Gatekeeping harms more than fraud.** The accommodation system includes documentation requirements—medical verification, diagnostic testing, provider letters—specifically to prevent abuse. Studies of accommodation fraud consistently find that it is rare. Lovett and Lewandowski (2006) examined rates of fraudulent disability documentation in higher education and found that the verification processes effectively screened out invalid requests. The greater problem was not fraud but gatekeeping: overly burdensome documentation requirements that discouraged legitimate requests and delayed needed support. The fear of a few people gaming the system was causing more harm than the gaming itself.

## Why This Matters

**Disclosure deterrence.** When disabled people know that requesting accommodations will trigger suspicion, resentment, or accusations of unfairness, they stop asking. An employee works through pain rather than requesting an ergonomic setup. A student takes tests without extended time and fails rather than being seen as getting an advantage. The myth does not prevent abuse—it prevents access.

**Hostile environments.** When peers and colleagues believe that accommodations are unfair, the accommodated person works in a climate of resentment. Classmates make comments about "special treatment." Coworkers question why someone gets to work from home. The psychological toll of operating in an environment that views your access to participation as an injustice is significant and measurable (Santuzzi et al., 2014).

**Policy erosion.** Public resentment of accommodations translates into political pressure to restrict them. Tighter eligibility criteria, more burdensome documentation, and reduced funding for accommodation programs are all consequences of the myth's influence on policy. When accommodation is framed as advantage, the political appetite for supporting it shrinks.

**Internalized shame.** Disabled people who absorb the myth may feel guilty about using their accommodations—as if every extended-time test, flexible work arrangement, or accessible parking space is something they do not truly deserve. This internalized shame compounds the psychological burden of disability itself and may prevent people from using the very supports that enable their success.

## The More Accurate Picture

Accommodation is equity, not advantage. It is the recognition that standard environments were designed for a specific kind of body and mind, and that people whose bodies and minds work differently need the environment adjusted to access the same opportunities. An accommodation does not make things easier for a disabled person. It makes things as hard for them as they are for everyone else—no more, no less.

The test of a good accommodation is simple: does it allow the disabled person to demonstrate their actual ability? If a student with dyslexia reads at half the speed of their peers due to a neurological condition, extended time does not give them an advantage. It gives them access to the same test—the one that measures knowledge, not reading speed. If an employee with chronic pain can do excellent work but cannot sit at a standard desk for eight hours, a standing desk does not give them a perk. It gives them access to the same job.

Fairness does not mean treating everyone identically. It means giving everyone what they need to participate. And when we design systems that accommodate difference from the start—universal design rather than individual accommodation—everyone benefits.

> 💡 **Spark:** A ramp is not an advantage over stairs. It is access to the same building. Every accommodation works the same way.

## What You Can Try

- **If you receive accommodations**, use them without guilt. They exist because research shows they work, because law requires them, and because you deserve to participate on equal terms. You are not getting an advantage. You are removing a barrier.

- **If a colleague or classmate receives accommodations**, check your assumptions. The fact that you do not need the accommodation does not mean they do not. Fairness means equal access to opportunity, not identical treatment.

- **Advocate for universal design.** Rather than accommodating individuals after systems are built, push for environments that work for diverse bodies and minds from the start—captioned videos, flexible deadlines, multiple formats, adjustable workstations.

- **Learn the actual evidence.** The research on accommodation effectiveness is clear and accessible. Share it when myths arise in conversations, policy discussions, or workplace debates.

- **If you are an employer or educator**, create accommodation cultures, not accommodation procedures. When accommodations are normalized rather than exceptional, the stigma decreases, utilization increases, and the environment improves for everyone.
