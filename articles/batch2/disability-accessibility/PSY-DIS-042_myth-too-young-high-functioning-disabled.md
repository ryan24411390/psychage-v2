> **Summary**: Two of the most common tools used to dismiss disabled people's experiences are age and functioning level. "You're too young to be disabled" suggests that disability belongs only to the elderly. "You're too high-functioning to need help" suggests that disability only counts when it is total. Both statements serve the same purpose: gatekeeping who is allowed to claim a disability identity and access disability support. Research shows that disability occurs across all age groups—one in four young adults aged 18–44 has a disability—and that functioning labels are scientifically unreliable, contextually dependent, and psychologically harmful. A person who appears "high-functioning" in one environment may be collapsing from the effort in another. A twenty-year-old with a chronic illness is no less disabled than a seventy-year-old with the same condition. The gatekeeping myth delays diagnosis, prevents accommodation, and forces disabled people to perform wellness to maintain credibility.

## Key Facts

1. **One in four adults aged 18–44 in the United States has a disability**, contradicting the widespread assumption that disability is primarily an aging-related phenomenon (CDC, 2023).

2. **"High-functioning" and "low-functioning" labels have been rejected by major disability organizations and clinical guidelines** because they reduce complex, variable human experiences to a single binary, ignore context, and correlate poorly with actual support needs (Bottema-Beutel et al., 2021).

3. **The "compensation cost" of appearing high-functioning is measurable**: autistic adults who mask their traits to appear non-disabled report significantly higher rates of burnout, depression, and suicidal ideation than those who do not mask (Hull et al., 2017).

4. **Diagnostic delay caused by age-based dismissal averages 4.5 years for young adults with chronic pain conditions** and up to 7 years for young women with autoimmune diseases, during which treatable conditions worsen and psychological distress accumulates (Sloan et al., 2017).

5. **Functioning labels predict support needs less accurately than individualized assessment**, with studies showing that a person's functioning level in one domain (e.g., verbal communication) does not predict their functioning in another domain (e.g., daily living skills) (Alvares et al., 2020).

## The Myth

There are two versions of this myth, and they often work together:

**"You're too young to be disabled."** Disability is something that happens to old people. Young people are healthy, energetic, and physically capable. If a young person claims disability, they are probably exaggerating, seeking attention, or confusing normal challenges with real impairment. A twenty-five-year-old with a cane must have a sports injury. A teenager requesting accommodations must be looking for an easy way out. Youth and disability are incompatible categories.

**"You're too high-functioning to be disabled."** If you can speak, work, attend school, or live independently, you are not really disabled—or at least not disabled enough to need help. Real disability is severe, obvious, and total. If you can do some things, you can probably do all things with a little more effort. The fact that you are managing proves that you do not need accommodation.

Both versions function as gatekeeping mechanisms: they set criteria for who gets to be believed, supported, and accommodated—and the criteria exclude millions of people whose disabilities are real, documented, and disabling.

## Where This Comes From

**Age-based stereotypes.** The cultural image of disability is overwhelmingly associated with aging. Wheelchairs, walkers, hearing aids, and chronic illness are coded as "elderly" in media, marketing, and public imagination. This association erases the 26% of young adults with disabilities and creates a conceptual barrier: when disability does not match the expected demographic, it becomes harder to recognize and easier to dismiss (CDC, 2023).

**The productivity imperative.** In cultures that equate human worth with productive capacity, being "high-functioning"—able to work, earn, and appear competent—becomes the standard by which disability is judged. If a person can produce, they must not be truly impaired. This logic ignores the enormous hidden cost of productivity-at-all-costs: the person who works a full day and then spends the entire evening recovering. The student who completes assignments but sacrifices sleep, social connection, and physical health to do so.

**Binary thinking about disability.** The functioning-labels myth depends on a binary understanding of disability: you are either disabled (unable to function) or not disabled (able to function). This binary erases the reality that most disabilities exist on a spectrum, fluctuate over time, and affect different domains differently. A person can be highly articulate but unable to manage daily tasks. A person can hold a demanding job but be unable to process sensory input in a grocery store. Functioning is not a single dimension with a single score.

**Survivor bias in visibility.** The disabled people who are most visible—at work, in public, on social media—are by definition those who are currently managing. The millions of disabled people who are at home, in bed, in hospitals, or unable to participate in public life are invisible. This creates a distorted picture where all the disabled people you see seem to be doing fine, reinforcing the impression that disability is not that serious—when in reality, you are only seeing the people whose disability currently allows them to be seen.

## What Research Actually Shows

**Young disability is common.** The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2023) reports that 26% of adults in the United States have a disability, and the distribution across age groups challenges assumptions. Among adults aged 18–44, approximately one in four reports a disability—including mobility limitations, chronic pain, mental health conditions, sensory impairments, and cognitive disabilities. Disability is not a phenomenon of old age that occasionally strikes the young. It is a phenomenon of human life that occurs across the lifespan.

**Functioning labels are scientifically unreliable.** Alvares and colleagues (2020) examined whether functioning labels in autism ("high-functioning" vs. "low-functioning") predicted actual support needs. They found that cognitive ability—the primary basis for functioning labels—correlated poorly with adaptive behavior, mental health status, and quality of life. A person labeled "high-functioning" based on IQ could have severe difficulties with daily living, social connection, and emotional regulation. A person labeled "low-functioning" based on limited verbal communication could have rich inner lives, strong preferences, and the capacity for meaningful relationships. The label told you one thing about the person and implied everything else, usually incorrectly.

**Masking has measurable costs.** Hull and colleagues (2017) studied "camouflaging"—the practice of concealing autistic traits to appear non-disabled—and found that it was associated with significantly elevated rates of depression, anxiety, burnout, and suicidal ideation. The effort required to appear "high-functioning" was itself a source of disability. Participants described camouflaging as exhausting, identity-eroding, and unsustainable—yet they continued because the alternative was being dismissed, excluded, or losing accommodations they desperately needed. The myth creates a trap: appear disabled and be pitied; appear non-disabled and lose support.

**Diagnostic delay harms young people.** When clinicians share the assumption that disability belongs to older populations, young patients presenting with symptoms of chronic illness face systematic delays. Sloan and colleagues (2017) documented that young adults with chronic pain waited an average of 4.5 years from symptom onset to diagnosis, and young women with autoimmune diseases waited up to 7 years. During these delays, conditions worsened, patients accumulated unnecessary tests, and psychological distress—the result of being told repeatedly that nothing was wrong when something clearly was—compounded the physical symptoms. The age-based dismissal was not just an attitude problem. It was a clinical failure with measurable health consequences.

**Bottema-Beutel and colleagues (2021) called for the elimination of functioning labels** in both clinical and everyday language, arguing that they are stigmatizing, inaccurate, and counterproductive. They proposed that support needs should be described specifically and contextually rather than reduced to a global label. Instead of "high-functioning," clinicians should describe what the person can and cannot do in specific contexts. Instead of "low-functioning," clinicians should identify what supports would enable greater participation. This specificity is more accurate, more respectful, and more useful for planning actual support.

**The spoon theory and energy economics.** Christine Miserandino's (2003) "spoon theory"—a now-classic metaphor for living with limited energy—illustrates why functioning labels fail. A person with a chronic illness starts each day with a finite number of "spoons" (units of energy). Every task—getting dressed, commuting, working, socializing—costs spoons. A person who uses all their spoons at work may appear "high-functioning" in the office and be completely depleted at home. The observer who sees only the workplace performance concludes that the person is fine. The person who lives the full cycle knows they are not. Functioning is not a fixed trait—it is a moment-by-moment negotiation with limited resources.

## Why This Matters

**Delayed support.** When young disabled people are told they are "too young" for disability, they delay seeking diagnosis, accommodation, and treatment. Years of preventable suffering pass while they try to meet an impossible standard of wellness that their body or mind cannot sustain.

**Identity confusion.** Disabled people who are told they are "too high-functioning" to be disabled occupy a painful liminal space—too disabled to function without help, too functional to be believed when they ask for it. This identity limbo contributes to depression, anxiety, and a fractured sense of self.

**Burnout and collapse.** The pressure to maintain the appearance of high functioning pushes disabled people past their sustainable capacity. The result is burnout, collapse, and crises that could have been prevented by earlier, less dramatic intervention—accommodation before breakdown rather than treatment after it.

**Systemic access denial.** Functioning labels and age-based dismissal translate into gatekeeping at every level: insurance denials, accommodation rejections, benefit ineligibility, and clinical dismissal. The person who "seems fine" is denied the support that would allow them to actually be fine.

## The More Accurate Picture

Disability does not have an age requirement. A seven-year-old with juvenile arthritis, a twenty-two-year-old with ME/CFS, a thirty-five-year-old with multiple sclerosis, and a seventy-year-old with mobility impairment all have disabilities. The condition is defined by its impact on functioning—not by the age of the person experiencing it.

Functioning is not a fixed trait that can be captured in a label. It is contextual, variable, and multidimensional. A person functions differently at 9 AM than at 9 PM. Differently on a good day than on a flare day. Differently in a quiet room than in a crowded one. The question is not whether someone is "high-functioning" or "low-functioning" but what they need, in this context, to participate.

When we replace labels with specificity—"needs extended time for processing," "requires rest periods during the workday," "uses assistive technology for communication"—we describe what is actually happening. And what is actually happening is always more complex, more human, and more deserving of support than any label can capture.

> 💡 **Spark:** "High-functioning" often means "struggling invisibly." The person who appears to be coping the best may be paying the highest price to do so.

## What You Can Try

- **Drop functioning labels from your vocabulary.** Replace "high-functioning" and "low-functioning" with descriptions of specific strengths and needs. "She communicates verbally but needs support with executive functioning" is more accurate and more useful than either label.

- **Believe young disabled people.** If a young person tells you they have a disability, believe them. Their youth does not make their condition less real, less painful, or less deserving of support.

- **Ask about the full picture.** If someone appears to be coping well, do not assume they are. Ask how they are managing. Ask what it costs. The answer may reveal a level of effort and sacrifice that is invisible from the outside.

- **Advocate for needs-based support.** Push for accommodation systems that assess specific, contextual needs rather than relying on global functioning judgments. What do you need? When do you need it? What helps?

- **If you are a clinician**, take young patients' symptoms seriously. The average diagnostic delay for young adults with chronic illness is years, not months. Early intervention changes outcomes.
