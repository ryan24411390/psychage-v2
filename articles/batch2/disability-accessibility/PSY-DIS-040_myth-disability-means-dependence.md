> **Summary**: One of the most pervasive and damaging myths about disability is the equation: disability equals dependence. This belief frames disabled people as passive recipients of care, incapable of making their own decisions, managing their own lives, or contributing meaningfully to their communities. It drives paternalistic policies, justifies institutional segregation, and erodes the self-determination of millions of people. The reality is radically different. Independence has never meant doing everything alone—for anyone. All human beings exist in networks of interdependence, relying on others for everything from food production to infrastructure to emotional support. What disabled people seek is not the absence of assistance but the presence of choice: the ability to decide how they live, where they live, who helps them, and what their assistance looks like. Research consistently shows that self-determination—having control over one's own life—is the strongest predictor of wellbeing among disabled people, far more important than the amount of help they need.

## Key Facts

1. **Self-determination—the ability to make choices about one's own life—is the single strongest predictor of psychological wellbeing among disabled adults**, more significant than disability severity, income level, or amount of personal assistance received (Wehmeyer & Schwartz, 1998).

2. **The Independent Living Movement, founded by disabled people in the 1970s, redefined independence from "doing everything yourself" to "having control over the assistance you receive"**, a philosophical shift that transformed disability policy worldwide (DeJong, 1979).

3. **Disabled adults who direct their own personal assistance report 40% higher life satisfaction** than those who receive equivalent hours of assistance controlled by service providers or institutions (Beatty et al., 1998).

4. **Approximately 80% of disabled adults live independently in the community**, not in institutions—a figure that has risen steadily since deinstitutionalization but remains poorly recognized by the general public (Braddock et al., 2017).

5. **Paternalistic attitudes—the assumption that disabled people need to be protected from their own decisions—are associated with higher rates of depression, anxiety, and learned helplessness** among disabled people, making well-intentioned overprotection a direct contributor to poor mental health (Lachapelle et al., 2005).

## The Myth

Disabled people are dependent. They rely on others for basic daily tasks, cannot make their own decisions, and would be helpless without the care provided by families, institutions, or the state. Independence—the ability to live your own life, on your own terms—is fundamentally incompatible with disability. The more help a person needs, the less independent they are. The person who can walk is more independent than the person who uses a wheelchair. The person who dresses themselves is more independent than the person who needs assistance with dressing. And independence is what matters—it is the mark of a capable, worthy adult.

This myth is not just wrong. It is the philosophical foundation on which institutional segregation, forced guardianship, denial of reproductive rights, and countless other violations of disabled people's autonomy have been built. It mistakes the need for assistance with the absence of agency, and it mistakes self-sufficiency—an ideal that no human being actually achieves—with human worth.

## Where This Comes From

**The myth of self-sufficiency.** Western culture, particularly American culture, glorifies individual self-sufficiency as the hallmark of adulthood. The "self-made" person who needs no one is the cultural ideal. This narrative is fiction—every person depends on infrastructure, social systems, and other people for survival—but it is powerful fiction, and it positions anyone who visibly relies on others as deficient. Disabled people, whose dependence on assistance is often visible and legible, become the embodiment of what the self-sufficiency myth fears most (Fine & Glendinning, 2005).

**Institutional history.** For most of modern history, disabled people were institutionalized—placed in residential facilities where every aspect of their lives was controlled by staff. They ate what they were given, slept when they were told, and had no say in their own care, relationships, or daily routines. This institutional model reinforced the belief that disabled people needed to be managed rather than supported, creating a feedback loop: people who are never allowed to make decisions appear unable to make decisions, which justifies never allowing them to make decisions.

**Medical model dominance.** The medical model of disability locates the "problem" of disability in the individual's body and frames the solution as treatment, cure, or management. Within this framework, disabled people are patients—passive recipients of expert care—rather than agents of their own lives. The medical model's influence on policy, education, and public perception has entrenched the association between disability and dependence (Oliver, 1990).

**Conflation of physical assistance with decisional control.** The myth's deepest error is conflating two entirely different things: needing physical help (assistance with tasks like dressing, bathing, or mobility) and needing someone else to make your decisions. A person who needs help getting out of bed can still decide what they do once they are up. A person who communicates through assistive technology is still the author of what they communicate. Physical dependence and decisional dependence are separate dimensions, but the myth collapses them into one.

## What Research Actually Shows

**Self-determination predicts wellbeing.** Wehmeyer and Schwartz (1998) conducted a landmark study examining what predicts quality of life among disabled adults. They found that self-determination—defined as the ability to make choices, set goals, and direct one's own life—was the single strongest predictor of overall wellbeing. Crucially, self-determination was independent of disability severity. A person with a significant physical disability who controlled their own life reported higher wellbeing than a person with a mild disability whose life was controlled by others. What mattered was not how much help you needed, but whether you got to decide what that help looked like.

**Consumer-directed assistance transforms outcomes.** The emergence of consumer-directed personal assistance programs—where disabled people hire, train, and manage their own personal assistants rather than receiving agency-assigned care—provided a natural experiment in the effects of autonomy. Beatty and colleagues (1998) found that disabled adults in consumer-directed programs reported 40% higher life satisfaction than those receiving traditional agency-directed services, even when the total hours of assistance were identical. The difference was control. Choosing your assistant, setting your own schedule, and deciding how tasks are performed turns assistance from a dependency into a tool—an extension of the person's own agency.

**The Independent Living philosophy.** The Independent Living Movement, which emerged from the disability rights activism of the 1960s and 1970s, provided the conceptual framework for understanding disability and autonomy. Gerben DeJong (1979) articulated the movement's foundational insight: independence is not about doing things without help. It is about having control over the help you receive. A CEO who has an assistant who schedules meetings, manages correspondence, and handles logistics is not considered "dependent"—they are considered successful. A disabled person whose assistant helps with dressing, meals, and mobility is performing the same function: delegating physical tasks to focus their energy and attention where they choose. The difference in perception is not about the nature of the assistance. It is about who is receiving it.

**Paternalism causes psychological harm.** Lachapelle and colleagues (2005) studied the psychological effects of paternalistic environments—settings where disabled people's choices were limited "for their own good." They found that paternalistic control was associated with increased depression, anxiety, and learned helplessness—the conditioned belief that one's actions do not matter. Disabled people in high-control environments stopped attempting to make choices, not because they lacked the capacity but because repeated denial of their choices taught them that trying was futile. Well-intentioned overprotection produced the very dependence it claimed to be responding to.

**Community living is the norm.** Despite the persistent public image of disability as institutional, the overwhelming majority of disabled adults live in the community. Braddock and colleagues (2017) documented that approximately 80% of disabled people in the United States live independently—in their own homes or apartments, with or without personal assistance, managing their own daily lives. This figure represents a dramatic shift from the institutional era and reflects decades of disability rights advocacy, deinstitutionalization, and the development of community-based support systems. Yet public perception remains anchored to the institutional image, perpetuating the dependence myth.

**Interdependence is the human condition.** Disability studies scholars have proposed replacing the independence/dependence binary with the concept of interdependence—the recognition that all human beings rely on each other, on social systems, and on infrastructure to function. Eva Feder Kittay (1999), in her influential work on dependency and care, argued that human vulnerability is not a deviation from the human condition—it is the human condition. Every person is dependent in some ways and depended upon in others. Disability simply makes this universal reality more visible.

## Why This Matters

**Policy consequences.** The dependence myth justifies policies that restrict disabled people's autonomy—forced institutionalization, involuntary guardianship, denial of the right to marry without losing benefits, and limitations on housing choice. When disabled people are assumed to be incapable of self-direction, policies are designed to manage them rather than support their agency.

**Internalized helplessness.** Disabled people who grow up in environments that assume their dependence may internalize that assumption, developing what psychologists call an external locus of control—the belief that their life is determined by outside forces rather than their own decisions. This learned helplessness is not a feature of disability. It is a product of environments that repeatedly deny disabled people the opportunity to practice self-determination.

**Relationship dynamics.** The dependence myth distorts relationships between disabled people and their families, partners, and support workers. When a parent, partner, or caregiver sees themselves as indispensable and the disabled person as helpless, the relationship becomes controlling rather than supportive. Disabled people may tolerate unhealthy or abusive dynamics because they have been taught that their need for assistance means they cannot afford to lose any source of it.

**Professional practice.** Healthcare providers, social workers, and educators who hold the dependence assumption may set lower expectations, provide fewer choices, and design less ambitious care plans for disabled clients. The assumption of dependence becomes a ceiling on what disabled people are offered—and therefore what they can achieve.

## The More Accurate Picture

Disability may involve needing assistance. It does not involve surrendering your will. The person who directs their personal assistant to help them dress for a job interview is pursuing independence. The person who uses speech-generating technology to advocate for policy change is exercising autonomy. The person who asks for help cooking dinner and then chooses what to eat is making their own decisions. Independence lives in choices, not in the absence of support.

The question that matters is not "how much help does this person need?" but "does this person have control over their own life?" When the answer is yes—when the disabled person chooses where to live, who to love, how to spend their time, and what to do with their one life—then assistance is a resource, not a limitation. And the myth of dependence is revealed for what it always was: a misunderstanding of what it means to be human.

> 💡 **Spark:** Every human being depends on others. The question is not whether you need help—everyone does. The question is whether you get to choose what help looks like. That is the definition of freedom.

## What You Can Try

- **If you are a disabled person**, practice self-advocacy in small daily choices. Directing your own care—choosing what to wear, what to eat, when to go out—is not trivial. It is the foundation of self-determination, and it matters.

- **If you support a disabled person**, ask before helping. Offer assistance rather than imposing it. The difference between "let me do that for you" and "would you like help with that?" is the difference between removing someone's agency and supporting it.

- **Reframe your understanding of independence.** Independence is not doing everything yourself. It is directing your own life. A CEO with an executive assistant is independent. A disabled person with a personal assistant is equally so.

- **Advocate for consumer-directed services.** Support policies that allow disabled people to hire and manage their own personal assistants rather than receiving institutionally assigned care. Self-directed services produce better outcomes by every measure.

- **Watch your language.** Phrases like "confined to a wheelchair" or "dependent on care" frame assistance as imprisonment. A wheelchair is a mobility tool that enables freedom. A personal assistant is a support that enables autonomy. Language shapes perception.
