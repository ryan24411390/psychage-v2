> **Summary:** Asexuality—characterized by a persistent lack of sexual attraction to others—has been systematically misunderstood, pathologized, and erased throughout much of psychiatric and cultural history. Despite affecting an estimated 1% of the population, asexuality was long conflated with sexual dysfunction, hormonal imbalance, or the aftermath of trauma. Contemporary research, led by scholars including Anthony Bogaert at Brock University, has established asexuality as a legitimate sexual orientation characterized by its own internal diversity, community, and spectrum of experience. The removal of distress as a defining criterion in current diagnostic frameworks means that asexuality is not a disorder when it is experienced without personal distress. Understanding asexuality as a valid orientation rather than a pathology is essential for clinical practice, for the wellbeing of asexual individuals, and for a more complete understanding of human sexual diversity.

## Key Facts

1. **Anthony Bogaert's landmark 2004 analysis of the UK National Survey of Sexual Attitudes and Lifestyles estimated that approximately 1% of the population identifies as asexual, making asexuality comparable in prevalence to several well-recognized sexual orientations** (Bogaert, 2004).

2. **The DSM-5 explicitly notes that a self-identified asexual orientation is an exclusion criterion for the diagnosis of Female Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder and Male Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder, formally recognizing that asexuality is not a clinical pathology** (American Psychiatric Association, 2013).

3. **Research published in *Archives of Sexual Behavior* found that asexual individuals who embraced their identity reported comparable levels of psychological wellbeing to sexual individuals, while those who experienced external pressure to be sexual or who internalized the belief that something was wrong with them showed elevated distress** (Brotto et al., 2010).

4. **The asexual spectrum encompasses diverse experiences including demisexuality (sexual attraction only after strong emotional bonding), gray-asexuality (rare or low-intensity sexual attraction), and aromantic asexuality (absence of both sexual and romantic attraction), reflecting the internal diversity of the asexual community** (Carrigan, 2011).

5. **A systematic review in the *Journal of Sex Research* concluded that asexuality is best understood as a sexual orientation rather than a disorder, noting that asexual individuals show stable patterns of low sexual attraction that are consistent over time and not attributable to medical conditions, medications, or psychiatric disorders** (Yule et al., 2014).

## The Myth

The assumption that every human being experiences sexual attraction—and that those who don't must be broken, repressed, traumatized, or hormonally deficient—is so deeply embedded in cultural consciousness that it functions as an invisible default. When asexual individuals disclose their orientation, they commonly encounter a cascade of invalidating responses: "You just haven't met the right person," "Have you had your hormones checked?" "Were you abused?" "You'll grow out of it." Each response, however well-intentioned, communicates the same message: something is wrong with you, and the evidence is that you don't want what everyone is supposed to want.

This pathologization causes measurable harm. Asexual individuals who internalize the belief that their lack of sexual attraction represents a deficiency experience higher rates of anxiety, depression, and identity confusion—not because asexuality itself causes distress, but because the cultural and clinical insistence that it is pathological creates distress.

## Origins of the Myth

The pathologization of asexuality is rooted in compulsory sexuality—the cultural assumption that sexual desire is a universal, essential component of healthy human functioning. This assumption pervades medical, psychological, and popular discourse. Freudian psychoanalysis framed sexual drive (libido) as a fundamental life force whose absence signified deep psychological dysfunction. Masters and Johnson's sexual response cycle implied that healthy adults progress through a reliable sequence of desire, arousal, and orgasm. Evolutionary psychology positioned sexual desire as biologically imperative, suggesting that its absence defied evolutionary logic.

The diagnostic system reinforced these assumptions. Prior to the DSM-5's explicit acknowledgment of asexuality as an exclusion criterion, clinicians routinely diagnosed asexual individuals with Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder (HSDD)—treating a stable orientation as a treatable condition. This diagnostic capture subjected asexual individuals to unnecessary medical investigations, hormonal treatments, and psychotherapy aimed at "restoring" a desire they had never experienced and did not miss (Brotto & Yule, 2017).

Cultural representation compounds the problem. Media narratives overwhelmingly assume that sexual desire is universal and that romantic relationships necessarily include a sexual component. Asexual characters are virtually absent from mainstream media, and when they do appear, they are often portrayed as cold, robotic, traumatized, or awaiting the transformative sexual encounter that will "fix" them. This erasure reinforces the myth that asexuality is an anomaly requiring explanation rather than a natural variation requiring recognition.

## What Research Actually Shows

### Asexuality as a Sexual Orientation

Bogaert's (2004) analysis of the UK National Survey of Sexual Attitudes and Lifestyles provided the first large-scale epidemiological estimate of asexuality's prevalence, finding that approximately 1% of respondents reported never experiencing sexual attraction to anyone. Subsequent studies have produced estimates ranging from 0.4% to 3.3%, depending on how asexuality is defined and measured—a range consistent with the measurement variability seen in estimates of other minority sexual orientations (Bogaert, 2015).

Crucially, Bogaert and subsequent researchers have documented that asexuality shows the hallmarks of a sexual orientation rather than a disorder: it is typically experienced as stable over time, is recognized from an early age by many asexual individuals, is not attributable to life events or medical conditions, and constitutes a core component of identity for those who embrace it (Bogaert, 2012).

### The Asexual Spectrum

One of the most significant developments in asexuality research has been the recognition of the asexual spectrum. Carrigan (2011) and subsequent community-based researchers have documented the diverse experiences gathered under the asexual umbrella:

**Asexual individuals** experience little to no sexual attraction to others, regardless of gender. Some asexual people have positive, neutral, or negative attitudes toward sexual activity itself—the defining characteristic is the absence of attraction, not necessarily the absence of behavior.

**Demisexual individuals** experience sexual attraction only after forming a strong emotional bond. For demisexual people, sexual attraction is not absent but is contingent on relational context in a way that differs from the general population's experience.

**Gray-asexual (gray-ace) individuals** experience sexual attraction rarely, with low intensity, or only under specific circumstances. This category captures experiences that fall between asexuality and more typical patterns of sexual attraction.

**Aromantic asexual individuals** experience neither sexual nor romantic attraction, distinguishing them from romantic asexuals who may desire emotional partnerships without a sexual component.

This spectrum challenges the binary assumption that people are either sexual or asexual and recognizes that human experiences of attraction exist along a continuum with meaningful variation.

### Psychological Wellbeing

Research on the mental health of asexual individuals has produced a finding that directly undermines the pathology myth. Brotto and colleagues (2010) found that asexual individuals who identified comfortably with their orientation showed no deficits in psychological wellbeing compared to sexual individuals. The distress experienced by some asexual people was attributable not to asexuality itself but to external factors: invalidation from partners, family, and healthcare providers; internalized stigma from a hypersexualized culture; and the isolation of belonging to an invisible minority.

Yule and colleagues (2013) found that asexual individuals reported higher rates of suicidal ideation compared to sexual individuals—but that this elevation was associated with minority stress factors (discrimination, invisibility, lack of social support) rather than with asexuality per se. This pattern mirrors findings across other sexual minority populations, where elevated mental health risk reflects the impact of stigma and marginalization rather than intrinsic characteristics of the orientation itself.

### The Diagnostic Distinction

The DSM-5's (APA, 2013) inclusion of asexual self-identification as an exclusion criterion for sexual desire disorders represents a landmark shift. Under current diagnostic guidelines, a person who identifies as asexual and is not distressed by their lack of sexual attraction does not meet criteria for any sexual dysfunction diagnosis. This distinction separates asexuality (an orientation) from HSDD (a disorder characterized by distressing loss of previously present desire) on the grounds that clinically significant distress—not mere deviation from a statistical norm—is required for pathology.

Brotto and Yule (2017) emphasized that this diagnostic clarification has practical implications for clinical encounters. Clinicians who understand asexuality as a valid orientation will not subject asexual patients to unnecessary diagnostic workups, will validate rather than challenge the patient's self-understanding, and will focus treatment on addressing any external stressors rather than attempting to modify the orientation itself.

### Challenges and Ongoing Research

Despite advances in recognition, asexual individuals continue to face significant challenges. Macro-level invisibility means that many asexual people lack community, representation, and language for their experiences—particularly those who recognize their asexuality later in life after years of attempting to conform to sexual norms. Interpersonal challenges arise in mixed-orientation relationships where one partner is asexual and the other is sexual, requiring negotiation of sexual activity, physical intimacy, and relationship expectations.

Przybylo (2019) has documented how compulsory sexuality operates as a systemic force that marginalizes asexual experiences not through overt hostility but through pervasive assumption: the assumption that everyone wants sex, that relationships require sex, that adults who don't want sex are immature, damaged, or in denial. This structural erasure may cause more cumulative harm than explicit prejudice because it is so difficult to name and resist.

## Why This Myth Matters

The pathologization of asexuality causes harm on multiple levels. Individually, asexual people who believe something is wrong with them may undergo unnecessary medical treatments, endure unwanted sexual experiences in an attempt to be "normal," or delay embracing an identity that could provide self-understanding and community connection. Relationally, partners of asexual individuals may frame the relationship through a deficit lens rather than engaging with the reality of their partner's orientation. Clinically, healthcare providers who mistake asexuality for dysfunction may provide inappropriate and potentially harmful interventions.

## The More Accurate Picture

Asexuality is a valid sexual orientation experienced by approximately 1% of the population. It encompasses a spectrum of experiences, is typically stable over time, is not caused by trauma or medical conditions, and is not inherently associated with psychological distress. The distress that some asexual individuals experience is attributable to stigma, invalidation, and the pressure to conform to compulsory sexual norms—not to the orientation itself.

Recognizing asexuality requires expanding our understanding of human sexual diversity beyond the assumption that everyone experiences sexual attraction. Just as the recognition of homosexuality and bisexuality expanded the acknowledged range of who people are attracted to, the recognition of asexuality expands the acknowledged range of how much attraction people experience—including the valid possibility of none.

> 💡 **Spark:** Not wanting what the world insists you should want is not evidence of damage. For the approximately one in a hundred people who experience the world without sexual attraction, the most radical act of self-acceptance may be believing their own experience over the insistence of a culture that cannot imagine it.

## What You Can Try

- **If you think you might be asexual**, explore community resources such as the Asexual Visibility and Education Network (AVEN), which provides information, forums, and community support for people across the asexual spectrum.

- **Learn the vocabulary.** Understanding terms like demisexual, gray-ace, aromantic, and the split attraction model (which distinguishes sexual attraction from romantic attraction) can help clarify experiences that may have previously felt confusing.

- **If a partner, friend, or family member has come out as asexual**, resist the urge to offer explanations or suggest solutions. Instead, ask what their experience is like and what kind of support they want from you.

- **If you are a clinician**, review the DSM-5's exclusion criteria for sexual desire disorders and familiarize yourself with current research on asexuality to ensure that you do not inadvertently pathologize a patient's valid orientation.
