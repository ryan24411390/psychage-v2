> **In Brief**: Religious and spiritual struggles are conflicts, tensions, or negative emotions connected to matters of faith, the sacred, or ultimate meaning. Unlike general stress, these struggles specifically involve the divine, religious community, existential questions, or one's relationship with the transcendent. Research shows such struggles are common—40-50% of people report experiencing them at some point—and they significantly impact mental health, often more strongly than general religious involvement. This article explores different types of spiritual struggles, their psychological impacts, risk and protective factors, and pathways toward resolution or integration.

## Key Facts

- **Surprisingly common**: Population studies find 40-50% of Americans report religious or spiritual struggles, with rates similar across faiths, though content varies by tradition.^1^

- **Mental health impact**: Meta-analysis of 147 studies (n=98,975) found religious struggles associated with higher depression (r=.38), anxiety (r=.35), PTSD (r=.33), and suicidal ideation (r=.27)—effects comparable to major risk factors like abuse or loss.^2^

- **Not the same as non-religiousness**: Atheists and agnostics who never believed report better mental health than those experiencing faith struggles, suggesting struggle itself (not lack of faith) drives distress.^3^

- **Can stimulate growth**: While distressing short-term, longitudinal studies show many people who work through spiritual struggles report increased maturity, deepened faith, greater compassion, and post-traumatic growth.^4^

- **Culturally variable**: The specific content of struggles varies by culture and tradition (karma in Hinduism, salvation in Christianity, ummah in Islam), but the psychological experience of sacred tension is universal.^5^

## Types of Religious and Spiritual Struggles

### The Six-Dimensional Model

Kenneth Pargament and colleagues identify six primary forms:^6^

**1. Divine struggles**: Conflicts with God or the sacred—anger at God, feeling abandoned or punished by God, questioning God's love or existence, theological doubts.

**2. Demonic struggles**: Concerns about evil forces—fear of Satan or demons, spiritual attack, feeling haunted or oppressed by malevolent spiritual entities.

**3. Interpersonal struggles**: Conflicts with religious community—hurt by congregants, anger at religious authorities, feeling judged or rejected, disagreements over beliefs or practices.

**4. Moral struggles**: Wrestling with one's own morality—guilt over moral failures, inability to forgive oneself, feeling unworthy, concern about becoming immoral or sinful.

**5. Struggles of ultimate meaning**: Existential and philosophical concerns—questioning life's meaning, doubting religious teachings, uncertainty about afterlife, inability to find purpose.

**6. Doubt struggles**: Intellectual and faith uncertainties—questioning beliefs, difficulty reconciling faith with reason or science, uncertainty about God's existence, wavering commitment.

These dimensions are measured by validated tools like the Religious and Spiritual Struggles Scale (RSS).^7^

### Life Stage and Context

Different struggles predominate at different times:^8^

**Adolescence**: Identity formation triggers doubt struggles ("Who am I independent of my parents' faith?") and ultimate meaning struggles ("What do I actually believe?").^9^

**Young adulthood**: Moral struggles intensify around sexuality, relationships, lifestyle choices that may conflict with religious teachings.^10^

**Illness and trauma**: Divine struggles emerge ("Why did God let this happen?"), demonic struggles may increase in some traditions, moral struggles about deservingness.^11^

**Loss and grief**: Ultimate meaning struggles ("What's the point?") and divine struggles ("How could a loving God allow this?") peak after bereavement.^12^

**Aging**: Ultimate meaning struggles about legacy, death anxiety intersecting with religious beliefs, potential divine struggles about unanswered prayers or unfulfilled promises.^13^

## Psychological Mechanisms and Impacts

### Why Struggles Affect Mental Health

Several pathways explain the mental health toll:^14^

**Shattered assumptive world**: Religious beliefs often form the foundation of people's understanding of reality. When these beliefs are questioned, it's not just changing an opinion—it's the ground falling away.^15^

**Loss of coping resource**: For religious individuals, faith is a primary coping mechanism. Struggles undermine this resource precisely when it's most needed.^16^

**Social consequences**: Faith communities provide identity, belonging, and support. Struggles can lead to ostracism, family rejection, or voluntary withdrawal—losing entire social networks.^17^

**Moral distress**: Moral struggles generate intense shame and self-condemnation, particularly in traditions emphasizing human sinfulness or strict behavioral codes.^18^

**Existential threat**: Ultimate meaning struggles threaten the entire sense that life has purpose, potentially triggering existential depression or suicidal thinking.^19^

### Longitudinal Research Findings

Multi-year studies reveal complex patterns:^20^

**Short-term distress**: In the immediate wake of struggles, mental health typically declines—increased depression, anxiety, substance use.^21^

**Resolution or integration**: Over 2-5 years, many people either resolve struggles (returning to faith with modified beliefs) or integrate them (accepting doubts as part of mature faith), with mental health recovering.^22^

**Chronic struggle**: For some (roughly 15-20%), struggles persist without resolution, associated with ongoing psychological distress, complicated grief, and difficulty finding meaning.^23^

**Growth through struggle**: Among those who work through struggles, many report positive changes—increased empathy, deeper spirituality, greater tolerance, reprioritized values—consistent with post-traumatic growth.^24^

The difference between paths often depends on social support, psychological resources, and whether struggles are approached with curiosity versus rigidity.^25^

## Specific Struggle Types

### Divine Struggles

Anger at or abandonment by God are among the most distressing:^26^

**"Where were you?"**: Following trauma, many feel God failed to protect them, breaking the implicit covenant that faithfulness ensures safety.^27^

**Theological crisis**: Reconciling belief in an all-powerful, all-loving God with suffering (theodicy problem) creates cognitive dissonance.^28^

**Unanswered prayers**: When fervent prayers go unanswered, it can feel like personal rejection or evidence God doesn't exist.^29^

**Punishment beliefs**: Viewing suffering as divine punishment generates shame, self-blame, and withdrawal from the very religious resources that might help.^30^

Research finds divine struggles predict PTSD symptoms above and beyond trauma severity, and mediate relationships between adverse events and mental health.^31^

### Moral Struggles

Guilt and shame related to perceived moral failures:^32^

**Sexual and relational**: Same-sex attraction, premarital sex, divorce, abortion—behaviors conflicting with religious teachings generate intense moral struggle, particularly when identity and behavior can't be reconciled.^33^

**Self-forgiveness**: Even after confession or ritual atonement, many struggle to accept forgiveness, feeling permanently tainted or unworthy.^34^

**Scrupulosity**: Religious OCD involving obsessive concerns about sin, blasphemous thoughts, or proper ritual performance—extreme form of moral struggle requiring specialized treatment.^35^

**Moral injury**: When religious individuals commit or witness acts violating core values (war, institutional injustice), it creates both moral injury and religious struggle.^36^

### Doubt Struggles

Intellectual and faith uncertainties:^37^

**Science-religion conflict**: Perceived incompatibility between religious teachings and scientific evidence (evolution, cosmology, psychology) triggers doubt, especially for those raised with literal interpretations.^38^

**Historical criticism**: Studying religious texts academically reveals contradictions, human authorship, historical development—threatening beliefs about divine revelation.^39^

**Pluralism**: Recognizing multiple religions make mutually exclusive truth claims raises questions: "How do I know mine is right? Can they all be true? Are any true?"^40^

**Apologetics failure**: When intellectual defenses of faith no longer satisfy, individuals face choice between suppressing doubts or following them where they lead.^41^

### Interpersonal Struggles

Conflicts with religious community:^42^

**Hypocrisy and betrayal**: Observing religious leaders' moral failures or communities failing to embody stated values breeds disillusionment.^43^

**Marginalization**: Feeling judged, excluded, or unwelcome due to identity (LGBTQ+, divorced, questioning), socioeconomic status, race, or political views.^44^

**Authoritarian abuse**: Spiritual leaders using position to control, manipulate, or harm—creating both interpersonal struggle and potential religious trauma.^45^

**Theological disagreements**: Being disciplined or shunned for heterodox beliefs creates forced choice between authentic conviction and community belonging.^46^

## Risk and Protective Factors

### Who's Vulnerable?

Factors increasing likelihood and intensity of struggles:^47^

**Rigid religious upbringing**: Black-and-white thinking, inability to tolerate ambiguity, conflation of cultural and doctrinal elements—makes struggles more destabilizing.^48^

**Insecure attachment**: Both to caregivers and to God—anxious attachment predicts fear of divine abandonment; avoidant attachment predicts suppression of struggles.^49^

**Trauma history**: Abuse, neglect, or other trauma often triggers divine struggles ("Why didn't God protect me?") and complicates healing.^50^

**Low distress tolerance**: Difficulty sitting with uncomfortable emotions intensifies struggle-related distress and urgency to resolve uncertainty.^51^

**Isolated faith**: Practicing alone without community support—struggles feel more shameful and terrifying when hidden.^52^

**High religious commitment**: Counterintuitively, deeply invested individuals experience more distress during struggles—they have more to lose.^53^

### Protective Resources

Factors that buffer distress and facilitate integration:^54^

**Secure attachment**: To God and others—enables maintaining connection despite questions, seeking support rather than hiding struggles.^55^

**Psychological flexibility**: Ability to hold paradox, tolerate ambiguity, revise beliefs as needed without catastrophizing.^56^

**Social support**: Communities that allow questioning, friends who've navigated struggles, therapists or spiritual directors comfortable with doubt.^57^

**Mature faith development**: Movement from literal to symbolic understanding, from external authority to internal discernment, from certainty to faith-with-doubt.^58^

**Quest orientation**: Approaching religion as ongoing search rather than having all answers—normalizes struggle as part of spiritual journey.^59^

**Meaning-making ability**: Skills in constructing coherent narratives that integrate difficulty, finding growth through adversity.^60^

## Pathways Through Struggle

### Resolution Trajectories

Research identifies several common paths:^61^

**Recommitment**: Struggles resolved by returning to faith, often with modified or deepened understanding—"I doubted, wrestled, and my faith emerged stronger."^62^

**Reformation**: Maintaining religious identity while significantly revising beliefs—"I'm still Christian but no longer believe in hell" or "I'm spiritual but not religious."^63^

**Exit**: Leaving religion entirely, identifying as atheist, agnostic, or "none"—finding peace in releasing beliefs rather than perpetually struggling with them.^64^

**Integration**: Living with ongoing tension—"I have doubts and still practice; the questions are part of my faith."^65^

No single path is universally healthier—outcomes depend on fit with personality, values, and social context.^66^

### Therapeutic Approaches

Evidence-based interventions for religious/spiritual struggles:^67^

**Spiritually integrated therapy**: Explicitly addressing religious content rather than treating struggles as purely psychological symptoms.^68^

**R/S Cognitive Therapy**: Examining evidence for and against religious beliefs, identifying cognitive distortions, developing more flexible religious thinking.^69^

**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy**: Accepting doubt as part of human experience, clarifying values, committing to value-consistent action despite uncertainty.^70^

**Meaning-centered approaches**: Logotherapy or narrative therapy helping reconstruct meaning that integrates struggle rather than erasing it.^71^

**Group support**: Faith transition groups, deconversion support, or theological discussion groups providing normalization and community.^72^

### Spiritual Resources

Within religious contexts, several approaches help:^73^

**Lament tradition**: Biblical Psalms, Jewish practices, and other traditions model voicing anger, doubt, and complaint to God—struggles are part of faithful relationship, not its betrayal.^74^

**Apophatic theology**: "Negative theology" emphasizing God's unknowability—makes doubt and uncertainty spiritually valid rather than failures.^75^

**Mystical traditions**: Direct experience of the divine can provide anchor when beliefs falter—"I don't know what I believe, but I know what I've experienced."^76^

**Spiritual direction**: Non-judgmental guides who help explore struggles without pressuring resolution—holding space for questions.^77^

**Contemplative practices**: Meditation, centering prayer, lectio divina—experiential rather than intellectual approaches to the sacred.^78^

## When to Seek Help

### Red Flags

Spiritual struggles warrant professional support when:^79^

- Suicidal ideation or self-harm related to unworthiness, punishment beliefs, or loss of meaning
- Severe depression or anxiety interfering with functioning
- Substance abuse to cope with spiritual distress
- Complete social isolation following religious community exit
- Inability to make decisions due to fear of divine punishment
- Obsessive rituals or scrupulosity symptoms
- Prolonged struggle (2+ years) without any movement or resolution

### Finding Appropriate Support

What to look for in providers:^80^

**For those maintaining faith**: Therapists trained in spiritually integrated approaches, pastoral counselors, spiritual directors who won't pathologize belief.^81^

**For those leaving faith**: Providers who understand religious trauma, support faith transition without agenda to maintain or eliminate belief.^82^

**Essential qualities**: Non-judgmental stance toward patient's religious/spiritual path, knowledge of religious development and faith stages, comfort with existential questions.^83^

Avoid providers who pressure you toward or away from religious beliefs—effective therapy supports your authentic exploration.^84^

## 💫 The Spark

Religious and spiritual struggles aren't signs of weak faith—they're often signs of deepening faith, honest engagement, or necessary transition. The medieval Christian mystic St. John of the Cross called profound spiritual struggle the "dark night of the soul"—not a deviation from the spiritual path but a crucial part of it. Your struggles might be leading you somewhere important.

---

## What You Can Try

### 1. Name the Struggle

Identify which type(s) you're experiencing—divine, moral, doubt, interpersonal, ultimate meaning, or demonic. Naming clarifies what you're actually wrestling with and helps find relevant resources.

### 2. Find Safe Spaces

Seek communities where struggle is normalized—progressive religious communities, faith transition groups, therapy, spiritual direction. Hiding struggles often intensifies them.

### 3. Journal the Questions

Write without censoring—express anger at God, voice doubts, explore heresies. The page is judgment-free space for authentic wrestling.

### 4. Study Faith Development

Read James Fowler's *Stages of Faith* or Richard Rohr's work on spiritual maturity. Understanding struggle as developmental can reduce shame and provide hope.

### 5. Practice Self-Compassion

Treat yourself as you'd treat a friend going through this. Struggles don't mean you're broken, faithless, or bad—they mean you're human and honest.

This article is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical or mental health advice. If you're experiencing thoughts of self-harm, contact the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988). For ongoing struggles affecting functioning or wellbeing, consult a licensed mental health professional with expertise in religious and spiritual issues.
